Growing in Love for God

What the Tradition Has Found — and What You Can Actually Do
This curriculum's mission is to ignite the desire to seek and to point toward the source. This page names what that seeking is ultimately for — not correct doctrine, not membership in a community, not even answered prayer, but love for God himself. Everything else in the curriculum is downstream of this. If these pages have done anything worth doing, they have moved someone a little further along the path this page describes.

The First Principle

Love for a person grows through attentive encounter with that person as they actually are. This is not a principle specific to love for God — it is a structural feature of love as such. The corollary is immediate: anything that increases genuine encounter with God, or removes obstacles to it, or corrects false pictures of him that prevent it, tends to deepen love. Anything that substitutes performance, projection, or religious routine for actual encounter does not.

This gives us the architecture of everything that follows. The question is not "how do I produce more love for God?" That framing has the mechanism backwards, and the tradition is consistent in correcting it. The right question is: "how do I more fully comprehend the love God has already expressed toward me — and how do I remove what is preventing me from seeing it clearly?"

We Love Because He First Loved

1 John 4:19

"We love because he first loved us."

This is the foundational text. Love for God is not self-generated — it is a response to perceived love. Which means the deepening of love for God is not primarily a matter of trying harder to feel more. It is a matter of comprehending more fully what has already been done, said, and given. The limiting factor for most Christians is not insufficient effort. It is insufficient seeing.

Jonathan Edwards made this observation the centre of his account of genuine religion. In Religious Affections he argued that what distinguishes the truly converted from the merely religious is not that one feels more intensely, but that one's love is directed at God's own excellency — his beauty, his character, who he actually is — rather than at his benefits. The saint loves God for himself. The hypocrite loves God for what he provides, and the "love" dissolves whenever the provision stops. The progression from the second to the first is the whole arc of Christian maturity.

Edwards also identified the specific faculty involved. In his theology, genuine perception of divine beauty requires what he called a "new sense" — a spiritual organ given at regeneration — which is why the same gospel that leaves one person unmoved produces in another the response of love and worship. This sense grows through use: through sustained, attentive exposure to the self-revelation of God in Scripture, in worship, in the sacraments, in the community of those who have also begun to see. The more it is exercised, the more clearly God is perceived, and the more naturally love follows.

Correcting False Pictures of God

The most practically undervalued instrument for growing in love for God is probably the correction of false pictures. Sinclair Ferguson, developing a theme from John Owen, argues in Devoted to God and his preaching on 1 John that the fundamental obstacle to love for God in most Christians' lives is not insufficient devotion or inadequate disciplines — it is a quietly distorted image of who God actually is toward them.

The distortions are common and mostly unconscious. The person who secretly believes God tolerates rather than delights in them cannot love him freely — they are relating to a projection rather than to the Person. The person who has unconsciously replaced the Father of Ephesians 1 with a divine auditor checking their performance will find devotion exhausting and assurance impossible, because their picture of God generates fear rather than love. The person whose experience of earthly fathers — absent, demanding, unpredictable, conditional — has silently shaped their image of the heavenly Father will find the language of sonship hollow or even repellent, because the word "father" carries damage the gospel has not yet been able to address.

In each case the remedy is not more effort but more accurate sight. Specifically: sustained exposure to the passages of Scripture that portray God's character most directly and most personally. Several are particularly powerful:

Ephesians 1:3–14 — the Father's electing, adopting, redeeming, sealing love stated as a sustained doxology. The language is not conditional. It is not contingent on the believer's performance. It is grounded entirely in the Father's "kind intention" and "good pleasure," and its security is the seal of the Holy Spirit as the down payment of the inheritance. Reading this slowly, pausing at each phrase, asking "does this describe what I actually believe about how God feels toward me?" — this is one of the most reliable instruments the tradition names for correcting the distortion.

John 17:20–26 — the High Priestly prayer, in which Jesus asks the Father that those who believe through the apostles' witness may be one with the Father and the Son, and that the world may see this. Verse 26 is arresting: "I have made your name known to them, and will make it known, so that the love with which you loved me may be in them." The love the Father has for the Son — the eternal, infinite, Trinitarian love — is what Jesus says will be placed in his people. This is the intended endpoint.

Luke 15:11–32 — the father running, the robe, the ring, the sandals, the feast. Module 2 already treats this, but its specific contribution here is the portrait of a father who honours is restored publicly and extravagantly before the son has finished his prepared speech. The restoration is not contingent on the quality of the repentance. It is the father's response to the son's return. Reading this against whatever false picture of God one carries tends to do specific corrective work that systematic theology alone cannot.

🕊️ When the False Picture Was Formed by Real Damage

For those whose image of God has been damaged not merely by cultural distortion but by specific harm — by a church or pastor who wielded God's authority abusively, by a father whose behaviour made the word "father" a source of pain rather than comfort — the correction described above is real and possible, but it takes longer and requires more than reading. The false picture was formed by experience, not only by doctrine, and experience is what primarily corrects it: the slow accumulation of encounters with the actual character of God, in Scripture, in prayer, in the community of people who embody his care, over time. This is not a character deficiency. It is an injury. And God's response to the injured is characterised throughout Scripture not by impatience but by attentiveness. Isaiah 42:3: "A bruised reed he will not break, and a smoldering wick he will not snuff out."

Bernard's Four Degrees of Love

Bernard of Clairvaux, the twelfth-century abbot, described a progression that maps precisely onto what both Chalmers and Edwards identify as the arc of Christian maturity. He called them the four degrees of love, and they describe a movement that most honest Christians will recognise from the inside:

First degree: We love ourselves for our own sake. This is the starting condition of unregenerate human nature — not necessarily malicious, but self-centred by orientation. The gravitational centre is the self.

Second degree: We love God for our own sake. This is where much Christian life operates, and Bernard is honest about it: many people come to God in need, find him sufficient, and love him for that sufficiency. They love the healing, the forgiveness, the peace, the answered prayer, the sense of belonging and purpose. God is valued as the source of what they genuinely need. This is not nothing — it is the beginning. But it is not yet the love the New Testament describes as characteristic of genuine faith.

Third degree: We love God for his own sake. This is the movement Chalmers describes as the expulsion of inferior loves by a superior one. It arrives as the person's exposure to God — to who he actually is, not merely what he provides — deepens to the point where his excellency, his beauty, his character becomes the object of love in itself. Edwards calls this the decisive test of genuine religion: love directed at God for what he is rather than what he gives. At this point the love does not evaporate when circumstances are hard or God seems distant, because it was never dependent on circumstances or felt presence.

Fourth degree: We love ourselves for God's sake. Bernard considered this the fullest consummation — the state in which even self-love is reordered around love for God, so that the self is loved as God's creature and God's possession rather than as an end in itself. He thought this was largely an eschatological state, only glimpsed rarely in this life. But he named it as the direction of travel.

The practically important movement is from the second degree to the third. Bernard's observation is that this is not achieved by trying harder to feel more love for God, or by suppressing love of what God provides. It is achieved by extended encounter with God himself — by the disciplines and practices that increase exposure to his actual character — until the person finds, as a matter of experience rather than doctrinal commitment, that God is lovely in himself and not merely useful. At that point, Chalmers' mechanism operates: the inferior loves are not fought but outshone.

Owen: Communion with Each Person of the Trinity

John Owen's Communion with God (1657) is probably the most systematic Reformed treatment of the question this page addresses. His central argument is that love for God grows through cultivated personal communion with each person of the Trinity distinctly — not as a general relationship with "God" as an undifferentiated divine presence, but as the Father known as Father, the Son known in his mediatorial person and work, and the Spirit known as the one who applies and seals all of this.

This matters because, Owen argues, different aspects of God's love for us are revealed distinctly through each person, and the appropriate response of love is directed accordingly. To collapse these distinctions into a generic devotion to "God" is to lose the specific content that makes the love compelling at each point.

Communion with the Father: Owen grounds this in the free, electing, adopting love of the Father — the love of 1 John 3:1 ("See what kind of love the Father has given us, that we should be called children of God"). The specific response of love Owen names here is confidence and delight: the experience of a child with a good father, who rests in the relationship without anxiety, who enjoys access without performance. The spiritual discipline he recommends at this point is meditation on the specific, personal love the Father has for you — dwelling on it until it becomes convincing, until the propositional content of Ephesians 1 lands as personal news rather than general theology.

Communion with the Son: Owen grounds this in the grace of the Lord Jesus Christ — his person (fully divine, fully human, in one person), his work (the cross as the measure of the love it expressed), and the ongoing fellowship of his sufferings and resurrection life. The response of love Owen names here is desire and rest: a longing for Christ that finds in him the satisfaction it was always seeking, together with the settled confidence of someone who rests in another's finished work rather than their own. The spiritual discipline: meditation on the cross specifically — on what it cost, what it expressed, and what it secured — until the abstract truth becomes personal recognition.

Communion with the Spirit: Owen grounds this in the comfort, sealing, and unction of the Holy Spirit — the earnest of the inheritance, the one who intercedes with groanings too deep for words, the one who takes what is Christ's and makes it known to the believer. The response of love here is gratitude and responsiveness: treating the Spirit's work with the seriousness and attentiveness his presence deserves, grieving what grieves him, following where he leads. The spiritual discipline: attentiveness to the Spirit's voice in Scripture and prayer, and honest self-examination about whether the person's inner life is showing the fruit of someone who is actually in communion with the Spirit of love, joy, and peace.

⚖️ Is This Tri-theism? — A Clarifying Note

Owen is careful and orthodox throughout. He is not suggesting three separate relationships with three separate beings. He is working within the fully Trinitarian framework of the Westminster Confession: one God in three persons, each fully divine, each distinct. The point is not to divide the Trinity but to engage with the fullness of the self-revelation God has given — in which the Father's free love, the Son's mediatorial grace, and the Spirit's applying work are genuinely distinct aspects of one God's one eternal purpose. A relationship with "God" that engages only one register misses the specific riches of each. The Nicene Creed's careful distinctions are precisely what makes the personal specificity Owen describes possible without collapsing into polytheism.

The Means the Tradition Names

Across the Reformed and Puritan tradition, with remarkable consistency, the following instruments are identified as the appointed means of growing in love for God. They are called means because they are not the cause — God is — but because he has promised to use them. They create conditions; he produces the captivation.

1. Scripture received meditatively, not merely informationally. The Puritans were insistent on the distinction. There is a way of reading Scripture that extracts and moves on — collecting doctrinal points, noting cross-references, completing the day's reading. And there is a way that slows down, dwells on a phrase, asks "what does this say specifically about who God is toward me?" and stays until the heart has been reached. Thomas Watson: "The heart must go along with the duty." John Flavel: "Turn every truth you learn into prayer immediately." The issue is not exposure but attentive, self-applying engagement. Psalm 27:4 names beholding as the orientation — not merely reading about but actually attending to the beauty of the Lord.

2. Specific, named prayer — thanksgiving for particular graces. The spiritual discipline of naming specific things God has done generates consciousness of a personal relationship that generic religious feeling does not. Owen called this the practice of "fresh actings of faith" — repeatedly bringing particular promises into personal contact with particular conditions, and registering what God has actually done there. Gratitude and love are inseparable; you cannot be genuinely and specifically grateful without love being stirred. The movement from "thank you for everything" to "thank you for this specific thing at this specific moment" is the movement from religion to relationship.

3. The Lord's Supper received seriously. Westminster's theology of the sacrament is that the Lord's Supper is a genuine means of grace — the Spirit works through it to strengthen and confirm faith. Edwards preached on this extensively: the Supper is designed specifically to show Christ's love at its most concentrated and most personal, making the abstract truth of the atonement tangible, communal, and recurring. Regular, attentive reception — not rote, not merely habitual, but with deliberate meditation on what is being signified — is one of the tradition's most consistent recommendations for growth in love for God.

4. Obedience that risks something. The whole curriculum's John 7:17 argument applies here with particular force: you learn to love a person by engaging with them, and the engagement that produces the most specific learning is the engagement that costs. The person who obeys at cost — who gives the money, forgives the wound, speaks the truth when silence would be safer — and finds God faithful on the other side, learns something about who God is that no amount of reading produces. Owen again: "Fresh experiences of the love and kindness of God are the only relief against doubts and fears." Experience is built through obedient engagement, not through theological study alone.

5. Suffering received in faith. The tradition consistently testifies that love for God deepens disproportionately in seasons of loss. Not because suffering is intrinsically good, but because suffering strips away the competing loves and forces the question of whether God himself is sufficient when he is all that remains. Keller's observation: in the darkness we have a choice that is not really there in better times — we can choose to serve God just because he is God. The person who makes that choice, in that darkness, discovers a form of love for God that is unavailable in easier times. This is the fourth degree Bernard described — the love that has been freed from its dependence on circumstances, because it has found its object to be sufficient in the worst circumstances it has yet faced.

6. The community of those who already see. You learn to love a person partly through the testimony of others who love them. Hearing a mature Christian describe their specific experience of God's faithfulness — not abstractly but personally — shows you what is there to be seen and loved. This is one of the things the church does at its best that no other institution can replicate: it is a community of people at different stages of seeing the same beauty, and the ones further along help the others to see more clearly. Hebrews 10:24: "Let us consider how to stir up one another to love and good works."

Three Cautions the Tradition Names

Do not try to produce the love directly. The Semi-Pelagian error — assuming that love for God is generated by sufficient human effort — is wrong, and it is pastorally dangerous because it produces the very anxiety it is trying to relieve. The means listed above are not causes; they are conditions. The person who uses these instruments while relying on God to produce the captivation is doing something entirely different from the person who uses them as a programme for generating devotion. The former is a patient; the latter is trying to be their own physician. Ask God for the love you do not yet feel. The asking is itself an act of honesty that he honours.

Do not monitor your love as a measure of spiritual health. Edwards and Ferguson both identify introspective monitoring of one's own love for God as a significant pastoral danger. The person who is constantly checking whether they love God enough will become morbidly self-focused — which is the precise opposite of the outward orientation that love for God requires. The instrument is beholding God; the beholder should not be staring at the quality of their own beholding. If you notice that your love for God feels cold or distant, that is not a verdict but an invitation to return to the means — not to perform love, but to place yourself where love is characteristically given.

Do not mistake the form for the substance. Rote Scripture reading, mechanical prayer, habitual church attendance, dutiful sacramental reception — these do not tend to produce love for God; they tend to produce Pharisaism. The Puritan tradition was insistent: the external form of the disciplines must be inhabited with genuine interior engagement, which is itself a grace worth asking for before each use of a means. The discipline creates the occasion; the heart must actually show up for the meeting.

📚 For Further Reading
  • John Owen, Communion with God (1657; Banner of Truth edition available) — The primary source for this page's treatment of Trinitarian communion. Demanding seventeenth-century prose, but worth the effort. Sinclair Ferguson's Devoted to God (2016) covers much of the same ground in more accessible form and is probably the better entry point.
  • Sinclair Ferguson, Devoted to God: Blueprints for Sanctification (Banner of Truth, 2016) — The most accessible modern treatment of the question this page addresses, drawing heavily on Owen. Ferguson's chapters on communion with each person of the Trinity are direct and pastorally grounded.
  • Jonathan Edwards, Religious Affections (1746; Banner of Truth or Yale editions) — The most thorough account of what genuine love for God looks like, how it differs from its counterfeits, and why the distinctly aesthetic dimension — love for God's beauty in himself — is the decisive test. Heavy going but irreplaceable.
  • Bernard of Clairvaux, On Loving God (c. 1132; freely available online) — The source for the four degrees discussed in this page. Brief, beautiful, and theologically serious despite its medieval setting. The Reformed tradition selectively preserved Bernard on this subject because he got it right.
  • Thomas Chalmers, The Expulsive Power of a New Affection (sermon, 1829; freely available online) — The mechanism by which love for God displaces competing loves: not by suppression but by a superior attraction. Short and essential.
  • C.S. Lewis, Letters to Malcolm: Chiefly on Prayer (1964) — Lewis's most personal account of prayer as an actual relationship rather than a religious performance. Contains some of the most honest and practical material in his writing on what engaging with God actually looks and feels like from the inside.
