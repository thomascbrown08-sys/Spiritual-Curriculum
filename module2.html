The Compelling Story

Why the Gospel Has the Shape of Truth
The Romans 7 cry — "Who will deliver me?" — is now alive in the room. This is exactly the right moment to present the gospel as an answer to a question the group has just felt. The intellectual question "does this story ring true?" is now personally motivated.

The Story No One Would Have Invented

The best human stories — the ones that endure across cultures and centuries — tend to have certain features: a protagonist who suffers, a crisis that seems insurmountable, a resolution from an unexpected direction, a restoration better than the original. C.S. Lewis observed that the gospel shares these qualities with the great myths of human storytelling — but with a crucial and unique difference: it is also rooted in specific history, specific people, a dateable sequence of events. It has the resonance of myth and the specificity of biography simultaneously.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

When Lewis uses the word "myth," he does not mean "false story." He means something like "a story that resonates so deeply it feels like it was always true" — the kind of narrative that appears across cultures in different forms because it is tapping something real about human experience: the hero who dies and rises, the lost one who is found, the exile who comes home. Lewis's point is that the gospel has that same quality of deep resonance — the feeling of a story you recognize even if you've never heard it before — and it is also tied to specific historical events that can be examined. It is the only story, Lewis argued, that satisfies both at once. Whether you find that persuasive is up to you — but it is worth knowing that "myth" is being used here as a compliment, not a dismissal.

The details of the gospel story are not the details a human author designing a religion would choose. A messiah who comes as a servant and dies as a criminal. A triumph that looks like defeat until three days later. A resurrection reported first to women — whose testimony was routinely devalued in first-century Jewish and Roman legal contexts — rather than to the male disciples who would have carried the most credibility as public witnesses. These are the wrong details for an invented story seeking to persuade — which is one reason to take seriously the possibility that the story was not invented but remembered.

⚖️ The "Myth and Fact" Claim — Scoped Carefully

Lewis's framing — that the gospel has the qualities of myth while being rooted in fact — is a compelling apologetic observation, not a proof. Other religious traditions have made similar claims about their own narratives. What is distinctive is the specific combination: the gospel has genuine mythic resonance (death and resurrection of the hero, restoration of what was lost) while being tied to a specific historical figure in a specific time and place whose existence and execution are attested outside the New Testament. The claim being made here is not that the mythic quality proves the gospel true, but that it is worth noticing that the story which most fully satisfies the deep human narrative longing also happens to be the one with the strongest historical anchor.

One honest counterweight: Paul, in 1 Corinthians 1:18–23, says that the word of the cross is foolishness to those who are perishing, and a stumbling block to those who expected a different kind of salvation. The gospel does not, in Paul's account, automatically resonate with prior human expectation — it often offends it. The mythic resonance Lewis describes is real, and many people have experienced it. But it is not universal, and the person for whom the gospel feels alien rather than familiar is not deficient in narrative imagination — they may simply be meeting it with the honesty Paul expected. The argument here is not "this must be true because it feels right," but the more limited and testable claim: this story has a particular combination of qualities worth examining on its own terms.

Lewis's own account of how this dawned on him — over years of resistance, and then in conversation with J.R.R. Tolkien — is worth knowing. He did not find it a quick or comfortable move. His essay Myth Became Fact (collected in God in the Dock) is still one of the most honest accounts of what it felt like to come to this conclusion from the outside.

The Prodigal Son: A Window Into God's Character

Luke 15:20

"But while he was still a long way off, his father saw him and felt compassion, and ran and embraced him and kissed him."

The parable of the Prodigal Son contains an image that rewards close reading. In the ancient Near East, a patriarch running was considered undignified — a deliberate, costly act of condescension. The father runs before the son has finished rehearsing his speech. This single detail is either a profound theological statement about God's character, or an invented flourish so culturally precise and counter-intuitive that it becomes harder to explain as invention than as memory.

The father's actions are not only emotionally generous — they are culturally specific honor restorations. The robe covers the rags of the journey. The ring restores signet authority. The sandals distinguish the son from a servant. The feast publicly reinstates him. This is not a private forgiveness whispered in a corner. It is a public declaration that the shamed one has been received back into full standing.

And the father runs — absorbing the spectacle of the son's undignified return into his own undignified haste — before the son has earned anything. This is the character of God that the gospel displays: not a God who forgives reluctantly once conditions have been met, but a God who is already moving toward the returning child from a long way off.

🌍 Honor-Shame Reading of the Prodigal Son

In the cultural world of the parable, the son's actions were not merely personally foolish — they were a public humiliation of his father, an act of social devastation. The request for his inheritance while his father was still living was equivalent to wishing the father dead. His return would have been anticipated with shame by the whole village. The father's running absorbs that public shame himself — shielding the son from the village's judgment by making his own welcome so extravagant and so public that it reframes the entire narrative before the son has said a word.

For groups from honor-shame cultural backgrounds, this dimension of the parable — not just forgiveness but the active, public restoration of honor — is often the most powerful entry point into understanding what the gospel actually offers. The gospel does not merely pardon; it restores standing. The shamed one is not quietly rehabilitated in private. He is feasted, robed, and publicly received while the whole village watches.

Kenneth Bailey's close reading of this parable (The Cross and the Prodigal) is among the most illuminating pieces of New Testament scholarship available for non-specialists. He draws on forty years of Middle Eastern village life to reconstruct precisely what each gesture in the father's welcome would have meant and cost.

The father's movement also illustrates the Spine's central claim: God's movement precedes and enables ours. The father runs before the son arrives. Grace is already coming toward us before we finish our speech — which is why the curriculum's spine insists that the very desire to seek is already evidence of the Spirit at work.

The Elder Brother: The Other Half of the Parable

Jesus told a parable with three characters, and the church has often focused on two of them. The elder brother — who has stayed, worked, obeyed, and never left — is standing outside the feast at the end of the story, unable to go in. His father comes out to him too, with the same urgency that sent him running down the road. But the elder brother's refusal is harder to move than the prodigal's return. He has not squandered anything. He has kept the rules. And he finds grace for his brother quietly, furiously unbearable.

Henri Nouwen, in his extended meditation on Rembrandt's painting of this parable, observed that many people who have spent their lives in the church find themselves, on honest reflection, in the elder brother's position rather than the prodigal's. Not dissolute. Not obviously far from God. But performing rather than receiving, keeping score rather than resting in love, resentful at the extravagance of grace extended to people who haven't earned it the way they have. The elder brother's father-wound is different from the prodigal's, but it is real: he has never fully believed that the father loves him freely, without the ledger. He has confused earned approval with received love.

The parable does not record whether the elder brother went in. It ends with the father pleading, the door open, the feast in progress. That open ending is almost certainly intentional — and it may be the most personally searching question the parable asks. Not "will you return like the prodigal?" but "will you go in — not because you've earned the right, but because you've been invited?"

🧭 The Spirit Working Through Story — and Through Both Brothers

The Spirit works through narrative. One of his primary modes of illumination is story — he uses the shape of the gospel narrative to awaken recognition and longing in people who may not yet have the theological vocabulary to name what they are responding to (1 Corinthians 2:12–14). Invite the group to notice what they feel when they hear the parable, not only what they think about it. Emotional resonance is not separate from spiritual truth — it is often the Spirit's first point of contact.

Pay attention to which brother the group is drawn to identify with. In many church-adjacent small groups, the elder brother is the more common identification, even if it goes unspoken. The prodigal's story is easier to celebrate from outside; the elder brother's is easier to inhabit from inside. Both are invited in. The father moves toward both.

The Good News as Announcement

John 3:16–17

"For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life. For God did not send his Son into the world to condemn the world, but in order that the world might be saved through him."

The gospel — euangelion in Greek — is literally an announcement of victory. In the Roman world, it described the messenger arriving from the battlefield with news that the war had been won. The listeners do not need to fight the battle; they need to receive the report. N.T. Wright has pressed this point carefully: the gospel is not primarily a set of instructions for how to be saved. It is a proclamation that something has happened — that the king has come, that death has been defeated, that the world has changed — and an invitation to live in the reality of what has been announced.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

Euangelion is the Greek word behind our English word "gospel." It was not a religious word first — it was a political and military one. When a battle was won, a runner was sent back to the city with the euangelion: the announcement that it was over, the enemy was defeated, the people were safe. The runner didn't ask the city to help win the battle. He brought news that the battle was already won. That is the shape of what the New Testament writers are doing when they use this word for the message of Jesus: they are saying an announcement has been made, a victory has been won, and the invitation now is to live in the reality of what has happened — not to earn it, not to produce it, but to receive it. N.T. Wright is a major New Testament scholar who has done more than almost anyone to recover this sense of the word in its original context.

John 3:17 is frequently lost in the shadow of John 3:16, but it carries decisive weight: God did not send his Son to condemn. The purpose of the sending is salvation, not judgment. For people who have primarily encountered Christianity as a system of condemnation — as a voice that names what is wrong with them before it offers anything — this is worth sitting with. The purpose of the announcement is not to establish the charge but to offer the acquittal. The messenger is not the prosecution; he is the herald.

This is also why the news can reach people who are still in the middle of the battle — who do not feel delivered, who do not yet experience the victory the announcement proclaims. The announcement of a war's end reaches the soldier in the field before he has laid down his weapon. It is true before he has acted on it. The gospel's claim is that the decisive battle was won at the cross and vindicated at the resurrection — that the announcement is true whether or not the hearer has fully received it. The invitation is to live in the reality of what has already happened, not to produce the victory by one's own effort.

This is why the Module 1 distinction between good news and good advice matters so much: the gospel's power is not in the quality of the advice it gives but in the reality of what it announces. The question Module 2 puts before the group is not yet "do you believe this is true?" but the prior question: "does this story have the shape of truth? Does it ring with the kind of recognition that great true things produce?"

🕊️ When the Story Doesn't Move You — Or Moves You Wrong

Three different people may be sitting with this module in a posture that none of the main text quite reaches.

If you have heard this story many times and feel nothing: the numbness is worth acknowledging rather than overriding. It may have many causes — overexposure, disappointment, intellectual distance that has become habitual. It is not a sign that the story is false or that you are beyond its reach. It may be a sign that you have been hearing it from the outside for so long that the outside has become your default position. The instruction for this is not "feel more" — it is the one Jesus gives in John 7:17: begin to act on what you know, and see what the movement produces. Feeling sometimes follows action rather than preceding it.

If you are afraid to hope: because you have hoped before and been hurt, the father-running image may feel less like comfort and more like risk. That is an honest and reasonable response to previous disappointment. The parable does not promise that hoping will be painless. It makes a claim about the character of the one you are being invited to hope toward — that he is already moving, that he has seen you from a long way off, that he is running before you have finished your speech. Whether that claim is trustworthy is precisely the question this curriculum is asking.

If "father" is not a consoling word: because your own father was a source of harm rather than safety — this needs to be said plainly: the parable's father is not a universalization of human fatherhood. Jesus is telling his hearers something about God's character by using the image of the best thing a father could possibly be, set against the worst thing a son could possibly do. If the word "father" arrives carrying damage rather than warmth, you are not reading the parable wrong. You are reading it with an honest history. The character being described is worth examining on its own terms, set apart from whatever human fathers have or have not been.

Discussion Questions
  • Is there a part of the gospel story that struck you as particularly unexpected or unlikely — something that seems wrong for a fabricated story to include?
  • Which brother do you identify with more — the prodigal, or the elder brother? What does that tell you?
  • When you heard the story of the father running, what did you feel — before you thought about what it meant?
  • Does it change anything to hear the gospel described as an announcement of something that has already happened, rather than instructions for what you need to do?
  • Is there anything in this story that you find yourself resisting — something that you wish were not true, or that you're not sure you want to be true?
📚 Deep Dive — Module 2

Henri Nouwen, The Return of the Prodigal Son — A sustained meditation on Rembrandt's painting of the parable, exploring all three characters — the prodigal, the elder brother, and the father — with unusual honesty about which one we actually inhabit. One of the most pastorally searching books on this parable available.

N.T. Wright, Simply Good News — On the gospel as announcement rather than advice; one of the clearest short treatments of the euangelion argument available.

Kenneth Bailey, The Cross and the Prodigal — A Middle Eastern New Testament scholar's reading of the parable in its cultural context; essential for understanding the honor-shame dimensions and the precise weight of each gesture.

C.S. Lewis, Myth Became Fact (essay, collected in God in the Dock) — Lewis's own account of why the gospel's mythic resonance combined with historical rootedness matters apologetically, and what it felt like to come to that conclusion from outside.

Timothy Keller, The Prodigal God — A short, accessible treatment of both brothers in the parable; Keller's argument that the elder brother's lostness is as significant as the prodigal's is particularly worth engaging for groups with church backgrounds.
