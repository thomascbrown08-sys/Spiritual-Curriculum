The Resurrection

The Most Disruptive Fact in History
Module 3 examined the general historical credibility of Scripture. Module 4 now addresses the central claim directly — the one that carries the entire weight of the Christian case. Without the resurrection, Paul says, "your faith is futile and you are still in your sins" (1 Corinthians 15:17). This is the load-bearing fact. Everything else in the curriculum depends on it.
1 Corinthians 15:17, 20

"If Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile and you are still in your sins... But in fact Christ has been raised from the dead, the firstfruits of those who have fallen asleep."

The resurrection is not the postscript to the cross. It is the Father's vindication that the cross accomplished what it claimed to accomplish — that the death of Jesus was not the defeat of God's project but the defeat of sin, death, and the dominion that held humanity captive. The distinction matters: a crucified messiah who stays dead is a tragedy. A crucified messiah who rises is a different kind of thing altogether — a new creation breaking into the old, a universe in which death is not the final word about anything.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

Vindication means something more precise than "victory." A verdict of vindication says: the accusation was wrong, the suffering was not the final truth, the one who was condemned has been declared righteous. When the New Testament says the resurrection was the Father's vindication of the cross, it means that what looked like the destruction of everything Jesus stood for was in fact confirmed as exactly right — that God was publicly declaring, through the empty tomb, that the sacrifice counted, the death meant what Jesus said it meant, and death itself had been defeated by it. The resurrection is the verdict on the cross.

The Historical Angle: What Needs Explaining

The resurrection is not presented here primarily as an argument to be won. It is presented as a fact to be reckoned with — something that requires a better explanation than the alternatives have managed to provide.

What the Skeptic Must Account For

The empty tomb was not disputed. The earliest response of Jesus' opponents — preserved in Matthew 28:13 — was not "there is no empty tomb" but "his disciples came by night and stole the body." You do not argue about who took the body if the body is still there. The logic is sound: the counter-story presupposes the absence of a body to counter. It is worth noting, however, that this argument works by inference from a text whose reliability is precisely what is under examination in Module 3 — a circular dependence an alert skeptic will notice. What can be said is that no ancient source, including hostile ones, appears to have argued that the tomb was still occupied. The emptiness is not independently attested; it is the premise shared by every party in the recorded dispute.

The post-resurrection appearances were widespread and public. Paul, writing within twenty-five years of the crucifixion, lists witnesses in 1 Corinthians 15 — including a group of more than five hundred people, most of whom, he notes, are still alive. This is not the language of a legend carefully constructed after all witnesses have died. It is an invitation to go and ask them.

The behavior of the disciples requires explanation. People do not willingly die for things they know to be fabrications. The disciples scattered at the crucifixion — Peter denied knowing Jesus three times. Within weeks, the same people were publicly proclaiming the resurrection in the city where it had happened, before audiences that included people who had been present at the execution, at personal cost that ultimately included martyrdom for most of them. Something happened between the scattering and the proclamation.

The conversion of Paul. Saul of Tarsus was not a sympathetic inquirer. He was actively persecuting the early church, overseeing arrests and executions, a zealous defender of the Judaism he believed the new movement was corrupting. His own account of his conversion — repeated three times in Acts, and referenced in his letters — is of an encounter with the risen Jesus on the road to Damascus. The explanation "he was persuaded by the evidence and changed his mind" does not fit the biographical profile. The explanation he himself gives is that he encountered the person whose existence he had been trying to suppress.

The conversion of James. James, the brother of Jesus, was a skeptic during Jesus' ministry (John 7:5). He became, after the resurrection, one of the key leaders of the Jerusalem church and died as a martyr. The most straightforward explanation — offered by James himself — is that he encountered his risen brother.

⚖️ The Historian's Standard — N.T. Wright

N.T. Wright, in his massive scholarly treatment The Resurrection of the Son of God, argues that historians applying their normal standards of evidence to the resurrection are left with a significant explanatory problem. The alternative theories — hallucination, theft, wrong tomb, spiritual resurrection without a physical body — each fail to account for the full range of evidence. The hallucination theory cannot explain an empty tomb. The theft theory cannot explain the willingness of the disciples to die for what they would have known was a lie. The "wrong tomb" theory was never proposed by ancient critics who had access to the actual locations. Wright concludes that the bodily resurrection of Jesus is the best historical explanation available — not a claim that goes beyond the evidence but a conclusion that the evidence points toward.

This is a minority position among secular historians but a serious scholarly argument, not a piece of popular apologetics. Facilitators should present it as such.

The Existential Angle: What Kind of Universe Do We Live In?

The resurrection is not only a historical claim. It is a metaphysical one. If it happened, we live in a universe where death is not the final word. The broken-cistern problem from Module 1 — the tragedy of good things ending, of the best meal finishing, the applause fading, the relationship eventually failing to carry the full weight of longing — is answered not merely by forgiveness but by the announcement that the brokenness itself is temporary.

Paul describes the resurrection as the "firstfruits" — a term from the harvest that means the first installment of a larger yield. Jesus' resurrection is not a solitary miracle, standing alone as an exception to the normal order. It is the beginning of the new creation — the first emergence of what the entire created order is moving toward. Every longing for something permanent, every grief at loss, every intimation that beauty should last longer than it does — these are not illusions. They are accurate perceptions of a creature made for resurrection life, living in a world still waiting for it.

⚖️ What Does "New Creation" Actually Mean? — An Eschatological Note

The New Testament uses "new creation" language in two distinct but related ways, and conflating them produces unnecessary confusion.

The present dimension: Paul writes in 2 Corinthians 5:17 that "if anyone is in Christ, the new creation has come: the old has gone, the new is here." He is describing something already true about the person who is united to Christ — a present reality, not merely a future one. The Spirit's indwelling, the forgiveness of sins, the ongoing work of transformation: these are new-creation realities already operative.

The future dimension: Romans 8:18–25 describes the whole created order as groaning and waiting for its final liberation — a cosmic renewal not yet complete. Jesus' bodily resurrection is the "firstfruits" of this larger harvest: the first eruption of the final new creation into the present age, the guarantee that what began in the empty tomb will encompass everything.

The relationship between the two is best understood as "already / not yet" — a framework widely shared across Reformed, Arminian, and most evangelical traditions. The new creation has genuinely begun in Christ and in those who belong to him; it has not yet been completed in the world. Both are true. The tension between "already transformed" and "still being transformed" — between the present reality of the Spirit's work and the continuing experience of struggle and incompleteness — is not a sign that something has gone wrong. It is the expected condition of living in the overlap between the old age and the new.

Practically, this means the curriculum's hope is neither naively triumphalist (acting as if the new creation is already fully here and struggle is evidence of insufficient faith) nor bleakly deferred (treating resurrection hope as something relevant only after death). The hope is real and present; it is also genuinely not yet complete. Module 7's signposts of growth and Module 8's obedience-as-door both live in this tension.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

Firstfruits was a harvest term that Jewish readers would have recognized immediately. At harvest time, the first crops to ripen were brought to the temple as an offering — and they served as a preview and guarantee of the full harvest to come. Paul is using this image deliberately: Jesus' resurrection is not a one-off exception to how the universe works. It is the first installment of something that will eventually encompass everything. His resurrection is the guarantee of ours, and the beginning of a new kind of world where death is not permanent. The phrase "new creation" points to the same idea: not a patched-up version of the old world, but a genuine renewal of everything — starting with Jesus, continuing in those who belong to him, and ultimately extending to the whole created order. That process is already underway. It is not yet finished. Both of those things are true at the same time.

The Resurrection and Module 1's Three Registers

Sin as power: The liberation announced in Romans 8 is not merely a past legal verdict. It is a present reality enabled by the Spirit of the risen Christ — the same power that raised Jesus from the dead now working in those who belong to him (Romans 8:11). Liberation is not a promise; it is an ongoing operation.

Sin as guilt: Justification rests on the vindicated righteousness of a risen Lord. The resurrection is the Father's declaration that the sacrifice was accepted, the debt genuinely paid, the verdict real. A dead savior's sacrifice has no ongoing legal standing. A risen one's does.

Sin as shame: The honor-restoration the Prodigal Son story describes finds its ultimate ground in the resurrection. The welcome is not symbolic. The Father who ran down the road is the same Father who raised his Son. The new standing given to the returning child is underwritten by an act of new creation.

The Personal Angle: A Present Person, Not a Historical Conclusion

The risen Christ is not a historical conclusion to be reached. He is a present person to be encountered. The disciples on the road to Emmaus did not conclude from the evidence that Jesus had risen — they walked with him, heard him, and recognized him in the breaking of bread. Thomas did not argue himself into resurrection faith — he encountered the one he had doubted, and the encounter itself was the answer.

This is why the fountain is still flowing. Module 1 describes the God who is a fountain of living waters — and the resurrection is the reason that fountain is not sealed by death. The Spirit whom the risen Christ sends (John 20:22) is the present form of the fountain's availability. The hiddenness that Module 1 acknowledges — the dry seasons when the fountain seems inaccessible — is the hiddenness of a living Person, not the silence of an empty sky. The risen Christ who was encountered in the upper room is the same Christ who is encountered in prayer, in the Word, in the breaking of bread, and in the community of his people.

🕊️ The Resurrection and Assurance

Three kinds of people need to hear the resurrection differently.

The uninformed person hearing the gospel for the first time needs to understand what it is: not a set of doctrines to accept but a person to receive. Romans 10:9 — "if you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved." The confession and the belief are not a transaction securing a legal standing — they are the posture of a person turning toward the risen Christ and saying: I trust you with this.

The doubting believer who wonders whether their faith counted: the resurrection is the answer to this question, but not in the way they might expect. The question "was my faith genuine enough?" is answered by looking not at the quality of the faith but at the faithfulness of the risen One toward whom faith is directed. He who promised is faithful (Hebrews 10:23). The risen Christ does not hold onto us by virtue of the quality of our grip. He holds onto us by virtue of his own.

The repeated-prayer person who has prayed "the prayer" many times and wonders why they feel unsettled: the issue is almost never the inadequacy of the prayer. The risen Christ does not require a correctly worded transaction. He requires a turned heart. If you have turned toward him — even haltingly, even in confusion, even in the middle of significant doubt — the question is not whether you said it right but whether he is faithful to his promise. He is. The call now is not to pray again but to trust the Promiser. "He who calls you is faithful; he will surely do it" (1 Thessalonians 5:24).

The Resurrection and the Community

The risen Christ did not simply appear to individuals. He appeared to communities — to the gathered disciples, to the five hundred, to the early church as it assembled on the first day of the week to break bread in his memory and encounter him in his presence. The resurrection is not only personal. It is the foundation of a new community — the body of Christ, animated by the Spirit of the risen Christ, the visible expression of resurrection life in the world.

This is why Paul cannot describe the resurrection without immediately describing the church. The firstfruits produce a harvest. The new creation that began in the empty tomb is continuing in the community of people who live as if the resurrection is true — who forgive because they have been forgiven, who welcome the shamed because they have been welcomed, who face death without despair because they have staked their lives on the claim that death is not the final word.

Discussion Questions
  • Which of the historical arguments for the resurrection do you find most compelling? Which do you find hardest to dismiss?
  • What would change about your life if the resurrection were certainly true? What would change if it were certainly false?
  • Paul says the resurrection makes the gospel "good news" rather than "good advice" — what does the resurrection add to the gospel's announcement that the cross alone cannot provide?
  • The disciples went from scattering in fear to publicly proclaiming the resurrection within weeks. What is the most plausible explanation for that change?
