The Problem of Evil

The Philosophical Argument, What It Establishes, and What It Cannot Touch
Module 6 addresses the problem of suffering as it is actually experienced — the grief, the silence, the theodicy that lands badly at a graveside. This page is for those who want the philosophical argument examined more carefully. The two pages are companions, not substitutes for each other. If you are in pain right now, start with Module 6. Come here when you are ready to think.

Two Distinct Problems

The "problem of evil" is not one problem. It is at least three, and conflating them produces confusion both in the person raising the objection and in the person trying to respond to it.

The logical problem of evil claims that the existence of God and the existence of evil are logically contradictory — that you cannot hold both simultaneously without formal inconsistency. If God exists and is omnipotent, omniscient, and perfectly good, then evil could not exist. But evil does exist. Therefore God does not.

The evidential problem of evil claims something more modest: that the quantity, distribution, and apparent purposelessness of much suffering constitutes strong evidence against theism. Not a logical proof, but a probability argument: a world with this much apparently gratuitous suffering is far more likely if there is no God than if there is one.

The personal problem of evil — the problem as actually experienced — is not primarily a philosophical argument at all. It is a wound. "Where were you?" "Why did you let this happen?" "I cannot believe in a God who allowed this to my child." This is Job's question, not Bertrand Russell's. It does not need a philosophical answer. It needs what Module 6 tries to provide: the presence of a God who entered the suffering rather than explaining it from outside.

Confusing the third with the first two produces a pastoral disaster: responding to a grieving person with philosophical arguments they did not ask for, addressing a wound as though it were an objection. Keller's advice on this is worth keeping: do not offer theodicy to someone in the middle of acute suffering. Sit with them first. The framework, if it helps at all, helps later — when the raw edge has diminished enough that thinking is possible again.

The Logical Problem

The logical problem of evil was the dominant formulation through most of the twentieth century, associated especially with J.L. Mackie's influential 1955 paper "Evil and Omnipotence." Mackie argued that an omnipotent, omniscient, and wholly good God would eliminate evil entirely — and since evil plainly exists, no such God does. The argument assumed that an omnipotent being is one who can do anything logically possible, that a good being always eliminates evil as far as it can, and that there are no limits to what God can do. On those premises, the existence of evil is indeed logically incompatible with God's existence.

The logical problem is now considered by most professional philosophers — including many atheist philosophers — to have been decisively refuted. The refutation came primarily from Alvin Plantinga, and it turned on a careful examination of what omnipotence can and cannot mean.

Plantinga's Free Will Defense

Alvin Plantinga's response — developed in God and Other Minds (1967) and more fully in God, Freedom, and Evil (1974) — is one of the most important pieces of analytic philosophy of religion in the twentieth century. His argument is carefully technical; what follows is a simplified version that preserves its essential structure.

The logical problem assumes that an omnipotent God could have created a world with no evil. Plantinga's response: this is not obviously true. It depends on what kind of world God was choosing among.

Consider what it means to create beings with genuine free will. A being with genuine free will — not merely the appearance of free choice, but actual libertarian freedom — is one whose choices are genuinely their own and not causally determined by God. But if God creates beings with genuine free will, he cannot simultaneously guarantee that they will always choose rightly. The freedom is what makes the choice genuine; but freedom means the possibility of choosing wrongly. It is logically impossible for God to both give creatures genuine free will and guarantee that they always choose good. The phrase "guarantee free choices" is a contradiction in terms — like "a married bachelor" or "a round square."

Plantinga's formal claim: it is possible that every creaturely essence suffers from "transworld depravity" — that is, every possible free creature God could have made would, if placed in any world with the right conditions for free choice, at some point choose evil. If this is even possible (not certainly true, just logically possible), then there is no possible world God could have created that contains both genuine free creatures and no evil. The logical incompatibility Mackie claimed — between God's existence and evil's existence — dissolves.

This is not a proof that God exists, or that he had good reasons for permitting specific evils. It is a demonstration that the existence of evil does not logically contradict the existence of God. J.L. Mackie himself acknowledged that Plantinga's argument succeeded against the logical problem. Most philosophers now accept this assessment.

⚖️ The Limits of the Free Will Defense

Plantinga's argument works as a logical defense — it establishes that there is no formal contradiction between theism and evil. It does not establish that God had good reasons for every specific evil we observe. The free will defense applies most naturally to moral evil (evil caused by human choices). It applies less obviously to natural evil (earthquakes, diseases, childhood cancers) which are not obviously connected to human freedom. Plantinga has arguments for extending the defense to natural evil, but they are more contested. The evidential problem of evil, addressed below, is a harder challenge than the logical problem — and it is not defeated by the free will defense alone.

The Evidential Problem

Even granting that evil and God are not logically incompatible, the evidential problem presses a different point: the sheer amount and apparent purposelessness of suffering makes theism less probable than it would otherwise be. The philosopher William Rowe formulated this as follows: there exist instances of intense suffering that an omnipotent, omniscient being could have prevented without thereby losing some greater good or permitting some evil equally bad or worse. If there are such "gratuitous" evils — pointless suffering that serves no good purpose even God could have access to — then a wholly good God would have prevented them. But they appear to exist. The argument concludes that therefore there is probably no wholly good God.

The theist's response operates on multiple levels. The most important is epistemic: why should we assume we are in a good position to identify which sufferings are genuinely gratuitous? The philosopher Stephen Wykstra developed this into what he called the "CORNEA" principle — Condition of Reasonable Epistemic Access — which runs roughly as follows.

Suppose you are a small child and your parent takes you to have an injection. The injection hurts and from your perspective serves no obvious purpose. Your worldview tells you: "Someone who loved me would not allow this pain." But you are in a poor epistemic position to evaluate whether this pain serves a good purpose. Your parent's reasons — preventing a disease that would cost you far more pain later — are reasons you cannot access from where you stand.

Wykstra's argument: the gap between human cognitive access and God's is infinitely larger than the gap between a child and a parent. A God who is omniscient and eternal would have access to reasons, connections, and purposes that are, relative to our situation, as invisible as molecular biology is to the child getting the injection. The absence of visible reasons is not evidence that no reasons exist — because we would not expect to be able to see the reasons, given the epistemic distance involved.

Keller states this point more memorably: "If God were small enough to be understood, he wouldn't be big enough to be worshipped." And: "Just because you can't see or imagine a good reason why God would allow something bad to happen doesn't mean there can't be one."

This does not end the conversation. The evidential problem is not defeated by pointing out our epistemic limits — it is moderated. The honest position for the theist is not "I can show you why every evil was necessary" but "there are good philosophical reasons to think the absence of visible reasons is not strong evidence that no reasons exist, given the nature of the being in question." This is a more modest claim. It is also a more honest one.

The Hiddenness of God

The philosopher J.L. Schellenberg developed a related argument that deserves its own treatment: the argument from divine hiddenness. It runs roughly as follows. If a perfectly loving God existed, he would always be open to personal relationship with every person capable of it. A perfect love does not remain hidden from those who seek it. But many people seek God sincerely and find nothing — they experience no sense of divine presence, no response to prayer, no interior confirmation. Non-resistant non-belief exists: people who are not closed to God, who are genuinely looking, who find nothing. A perfectly loving God would not hide from such people. Therefore probably no perfectly loving God exists.

This argument is worth taking seriously because it matches the experience of many earnest people — including many Christians in seasons of what the tradition calls the "dark night of the soul." It is not the objection of a hostile atheist; it is the anguish of someone who has looked and not found, and who wants to know why.

The Christian responses are several. First, the tradition has always held that periods of divine hiddenness are not evidence of divine absence. The Psalms — Psalm 88, Psalm 22, Psalm 10 — are the prayers of people who feel abandoned and who are not being corrected by the text for feeling that way. The darkness is real. The God who is present in the darkness without being felt there is a consistent feature of the tradition's testimony, not an embarrassment to be explained away.

Second, the assumption that a loving God would always make himself felt may itself be questioned. If God's purpose is not simply to make people comfortable but to form them into certain kinds of persons — people capable of genuine trust, genuine love, genuine perseverance — then seasons of hiddenness may be among the means he uses. The parent who does not always make their presence felt is not necessarily less loving than one who is always present; sometimes the tested trust produces something the constant presence could not. This is the argument the cross makes: God's greatest act of love was not felt as love by the one undergoing it.

Third, Schellenberg's argument depends on what "non-resistant" means. It is possible that some people who experience themselves as non-resistant have subtle resistances they are not aware of — not deliberate hostility but habits of attention, patterns of interpretation, prior commitments that shape what they can receive. This is not a charge against those who experience hiddenness; it is an observation about the limits of self-knowledge that the curriculum's discussion of proof-texting and trust-architecture damage elsewhere documents.

What remains after these responses: hiddenness is a real phenomenon, the tradition has always known it, and it is genuinely hard to explain fully. The honest position is not "God always shows up clearly for those who look" — the testimonial record does not support that. It is: "God is present in the dark even when he is not felt there, and the darkness, if endured honestly before him, has sometimes produced people whose faith is more useful than any amount of religious comfort could have made them."

The Cliché of Relationship

The church talks about "a personal relationship with God" more than almost any other phrase. It is in every evangelistic tract, every Sunday sermon introduction, every testimony. And it has become, through sheer repetition, one of the most content-free phrases in popular Christian speech.

What does it actually mean? The people who use it often cannot say. What does it look like in practice? Most Christians, if pressed, have difficulty describing the experience in terms that distinguish it from a general sense of religious comfort or cultural belonging. The phrase promises intimacy; it rarely delivers instruction in what that intimacy actually consists of or how it is built. And for the person outside faith — or the person inside faith but in a dry season — "have a relationship with God" is close to meaningless as a description of what they are supposed to do or experience.

This is worth naming honestly because the hiddenness problem, and the suffering problem, and the question of whether God is real at all, all converge on exactly this gap. If "relationship with God" is a cliché you have heard and not experienced, the question "is there anything actually there?" is entirely reasonable.

Here is what the tradition actually says relationship with God involves — concretely, specifically, in ways that can be tested:

Prayer as conversation, not performance. The psalms model prayer as brutally honest speech to a God who is present — not formulaic petition to an absent authority but the full range of human experience brought into direct address. Lament, anger, confusion, gratitude, desperate pleading, and quiet trust are all represented. The person who brings their actual experience — including the experience of feeling God is absent — into honest speech before him is practicing relationship in the tradition's most basic sense. The relationship is not contingent on feeling God's presence. It is practiced by addressing him whether or not the address feels received.

The Word as the place where God speaks. The Reformed tradition has always insisted that God's primary mode of communication with his people is through Scripture, illumined by the Spirit. The experience of reading a biblical text and finding it address something specific in your actual life — not because you projected it there but because the text reached into you and named something you hadn't articulated — is, in the tradition's account, a genuine mode of encounter. It is not dramatic. It is often quiet. But it is real and it is repeatable.

Obedience as the relational act. John 7:17 — the living experiment this curriculum is built around — describes a specific form of relational knowledge. You do not learn a person's character by thinking about them; you learn it by engaging with them. Acting on what you know, before you have full confidence, is the relational act that generates the knowledge obedience is promised to produce. The person who waits until they have experienced the relationship before they will obey has reversed the order. The relationship deepens through the obedience.

Hidden obedience as the most intimate form. Keller's insight from Walking with God Through Pain and Suffering deserves its full weight here. In ordinary religious life, we serve God with the expectation of receiving something in return — peace, answered prayer, a sense of his presence, the approval of our community. That is not illegitimate; it is normal. But it is not the deepest form of the relationship. The deepest form is discovered only when those things are stripped away — when you continue to obey, to pray, to seek, to do your duties of love to others, even when it does not seem to be paying off in any of those currencies at all. At that point, stripped of every motive except the Person himself, you are finally learning what loving God for himself actually means. The dark season, Keller argues, is not the enemy of the relationship. It is the furnace in which its deepest form is produced.

Through the Furnace: The Relational Logic of Suffering

Tim Keller — Walking with God Through Pain and Suffering

"It is perhaps when we are still in unrelenting darkness that we have the greatest opportunity to defeat the forces of evil. In the darkness we have a choice that is not really there in better times. We can choose to serve God just because he is God. In the darkest moments we feel we are getting absolutely nothing out of God or out of our relationship to him. But what if then — when it does not seem to be paying or benefiting you at all — you continue to obey, pray to, and seek God, as well as continue to do your duties of love to others? If we do that — we are finally learning to love God for himself, and not for his benefits."

p. 247 (verified). Note: the continuation of this quote as cited in Thomas Brown's personal notes reads slightly differently from the published text. Readers who wish to verify should check against a print copy.

This passage — which the author of this curriculum encountered during a period of severe personal darkness and which he credits with opening something that argument could not — gets at something the philosophical theodicy discussion rarely reaches. The problem of evil, as a philosophical argument, is addressed to the intellect. Keller's insight is addressed to the will and the heart.

The philosophical answers to the problem of evil can be correct — and they are, in the sense that no logical contradiction has been established and the evidential arguments do not reach certainty — and still leave the person unmoved. Because the real question underneath the philosophical question is not "how do I reconcile these propositions?" It is "can I trust this God with my life and my suffering?" That is a question of the heart. It is answered not by arguments but by encounter — by the specific, personal, costly experience of bringing your actual suffering into actual relationship with the God who has entered suffering himself.

The incarnation and the cross are God's answer to the problem of evil in a different register than the philosophical one. They do not explain why God permits specific evils. They establish what kind of God is in question when suffering comes: not a distant, impassive deity managing the world from outside, but one who has entered the worst of it, who has himself experienced cosmic abandonment, who has transformed the worst thing that ever happened into the ground of the world's redemption. The cross does not solve the problem of evil. It shows you the character of the God in whose hands the problem rests.

Keller again, on what this means for the person in the dark: suffering produces a kind of love for God that is unavailable in better times. The person who has been stripped of every religious reward and still seeks, still prays, still obeys — has discovered something about the relationship that no comfortable believer has yet found. The darkness is the furnace in which that discovery is made. It is not the last word. But it is not nothing.

The Wounded Healer: What Suffering Equips You to Give

2 Corinthians 1:3–4

"Praise be to the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, the Father of compassion and the God of all comfort, who comforts us in all our troubles so that we can comfort those in any trouble with the comfort we ourselves receive from God."

Paul does not say that suffering produces only personal growth, or only deeper love for God, or only refined character. He says it produces something outward: the capacity to comfort others with the specific comfort you yourself received. The mechanism is worth sitting with. It is not that suffering makes you more generally compassionate — though it may. It is that you are now in possession of something specific: the actual experience of being comforted by God in that particular darkness, and the knowledge of what that comfort felt and tasted like. That is a currency no amount of training, education, or natural empathy can purchase. It has to be suffered for.

This is what Henri Nouwen called "the wounded healer" — a concept he developed from his own experience of depression, loneliness, and deep personal pain, and from his work with severely disabled adults at the L'Arche community. Nouwen observed that the minister who has not suffered — who comes to the suffering of others from behind the safety of professional competence and theological tidiness — can offer information and technique, but something essential is missing. The person who has been in the same pit, or a pit like it, brings something different: presence without flinching, companionship without the anxiety of the unexperienced. They can sit with another human being in genuine darkness without needing to rush them toward the light, because they know from the inside that the darkness is survivable, that God is findable in it, and that the other person does not need to pretend to be further along than they are.

The pattern in church history is striking and consistent.

Charles Spurgeon — arguably the greatest preacher of the nineteenth century, whose sermons are still in print and still read — suffered from severe depression throughout his adult life. He described it with unusual honesty for a public figure of his era: seasons of profound darkness, inability to pray, the felt absence of God, the experience of entering a pulpit he was not sure he believed anything from. And yet his writing and preaching on suffering, on depression, on the Psalms of lament, on persevering faith when feeling produces nothing — has reached more suffering people than almost anything written in the English-speaking Protestant world. His authority to speak on those things was not academic. It was purchased. He had been there, and those who went there after him could feel it in every paragraph.

Amy Carmichael, who spent fifty-five unbroken years in India rescuing children and eventually wrote from a sickbed for the last twenty years of her life, produced devotional material of unusual depth precisely because her confinement stripped away everything except the essential. If — her collection of brief meditations on surrender and suffering — has carried people through experiences she could not have spoken to had she not entered them herself. Her readers knew the difference between someone writing about a country they had visited and someone writing from inside it.

C.S. Lewis wrote The Problem of Pain in 1940 — a careful, intellectually confident account of how suffering fits within a theistic framework. Twenty years later, after the death of his wife Joy to cancer, he wrote A Grief Observed: raw, honest, frequently anguished, none of the confident tidiness of the earlier book. It is the second book that has reached the most people in acute grief. Not because the first is wrong — it is not — but because the second was written from inside the experience rather than looking at it from outside. Many grieving readers have said that The Problem of Pain felt like someone explaining their suffering; A Grief Observed felt like someone who understood it.

The pastoral point that follows from this is one that the person in chronic suffering, disability, or ongoing darkness most needs to hear — and most rarely does:

You are not useless because you are suffering. You may be in preparation.

Not preparation in the sense that there is a plan you need to perform on schedule. Not preparation in the sense that you should stop grieving and start looking for opportunities. But preparation in the sense that what is being forged in you right now — by the very experience you would give anything to be out of — is the exact thing that will one day allow you to walk into a room where someone else is in that experience and be genuinely useful to them in a way no one else in the room can be. The person who has never been in the dark cannot give the dark its name. The person who has been there, and stayed, and found God faithful in it — can.

Bonding sometimes requires a traumatic process. The deepest friendships many people have are with people who knew them in their worst moments, not their best. The comfort that reaches most deeply is often the comfort that comes from someone who did not need to explain how they understood — because you could see that they did. That kind of presence, that kind of comfort, that depth of reach — it cannot be manufactured. It is given to those who have suffered it. Which means that what looks like pure loss from the inside may be, from a wider angle, a slow and painful equipping for a ministry that only you can do.

None of this makes the suffering good in itself, or obligates you to feel grateful for it before you are ready, or means that every suffering is straightforwardly purposeful in this way. The tradition is honest about suffering that remains opaque, that produces no visible fruit, that ends before the ministry it might have equipped. The pastoral observation is not a formula. It is a genuine pattern — attested across centuries and across widely different traditions — that is worth holding in view, especially in the seasons when you cannot see it working.

🕊️ For Those in Active Darkness Right Now

If you are reading this page from inside a season of severe suffering — not as an intellectual exercise but as someone who genuinely wants to know whether there is a God worth trusting — this paragraph is addressed to you directly.

The philosophical material above may be useful later. Right now, what matters more is this: the curriculum you have been reading was built by someone who has been in the place you are describing, who has found the intellectual arguments real but insufficient, and who has — slowly, imperfectly, not without ongoing struggle — found something on the other side of the argument that the argument alone could not give. Not proof. Not the end of suffering. But the beginning of a relationship with a God who turned out to be present in the dark, even when he was not felt there.

If you are struggling with suicidal ideation or are in crisis, please reach out to a pastor, counselor, or crisis service. This curriculum cares about you as a person, not only as a reader. The 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline is available by call or text in the US. The darkness you are in is real; it is not permanent; and you are not its final word.

📚 For Further Study — The Problem of Evil
  • Henri Nouwen, The Wounded Healer (Doubleday, 1972) — Brief and profound: the argument that the minister's own wounds are not an obstacle to serving others but the very source of the capacity to do so. Written from Nouwen's own experience of depression and loneliness. The theological framework for this page's final section.
  • Timothy Keller, Walking with God Through Pain and Suffering (Dutton, 2013) — The primary resource this page draws on for the relational and pastoral dimension. The philosophical section (Part 1) is rigorous; Part 3 (Walking with God in the Furnace) is where the book's deepest pastoral value lies. The chapter on Job is exceptional. Required reading for anyone navigating suffering with any seriousness.
  • Alvin Plantinga, God, Freedom, and Evil (Eerdmans, 1974) — The technical statement of the free will defense. The first half is demanding but accessible to a careful non-philosopher; the second half requires more technical background. Read the first half at minimum.
  • C.S. Lewis, The Problem of Pain (HarperOne, 1940) — The most accessible philosophical treatment of the problem, written before Lewis experienced acute personal grief. Read alongside A Grief Observed (written after his wife's death) to see the difference between thinking about suffering and living inside it.
  • C.S. Lewis, A Grief Observed (HarperOne, 1961) — Raw, honest, and one of the most important things Lewis ever wrote. The intellectual framework of The Problem of Pain confronted with actual grief.
  • D.A. Carson, How Long, O Lord? (Baker, 2nd ed., 2006) — A Reformed scholar's comprehensive biblical treatment of suffering: the problem of evil, lament, God's sovereignty, and the pastoral dimensions. More accessible than Plantinga; more rigorous than popular treatments.
  • Joni Eareckson Tada, A Place of Healing (David C Cook, 2010) — Five decades of quadriplegia and chronic pain narrated by someone who has earned the right to speak on this subject. Not a philosophical treatise — something more valuable: hard-won testimony.
  • J.L. Schellenberg, The Hiddenness Argument (Oxford, 2015) — The most rigorous statement of the argument from divine hiddenness by its originator. Useful for understanding the strongest version of that challenge.
