The Problem of Suffering

Where Is God When It Hurts?
This module is placed here deliberately — after the evidence has been examined and before the call to faithful living is issued. It would be dishonest to invite people into the Christian life without first sitting honestly in the hardest question most of them carry. This module should not be rushed. It is the curriculum's most pastorally demanding session and, for people in genuine pain, potentially its most important.
🧭 Before You Begin: The Facilitator's Own Posture

This module will go badly if it is taught from a position of safety. The facilitator who has answers prepared — who knows ahead of time what they will say to the person who lost a child, or who survived abuse, or who prayed for healing and received silence — will communicate, however unintentionally, that the answers matter more than the person. They do not.

The posture this module requires is what Henri Nouwen called the wounded healer: someone who has been in the dark themselves and speaks from that place, not from above it. The curriculum's theology is sound. But theology spoken from a safe distance to someone in acute pain is not pastoral care — it is a performance of competence that leaves the person more alone than before.

If you have your own experience of suffering and doubt, being honest about it — even briefly — will do more for the group's trust than any preparation of answers. You are not required to have suffered in a particular way. You are only required to be honest about wherever you actually are.

Two Different Problems — and Why the Difference Matters

The problem of suffering is actually two problems, frequently confused, requiring different responses.

The intellectual problem of evil asks: if God is both perfectly good and omnipotent, why does suffering exist? This is a genuine philosophical question with a two-thousand-year history of serious engagement. It deserves a serious answer — and this module offers one, carefully.

The personal problem of suffering asks something different: why is this happening to me, and where is God in it? This is not primarily a philosophical question. It is a cry. And a cry does not primarily need an argument in response. It needs a presence.

The danger of this module — and of well-intentioned theological conversations about suffering generally — is treating the second problem as if it were the first. A person sitting in grief does not primarily need theodicy. They need someone to sit with them in it. The intellectual framework this module offers is not meant to be deployed in the room as a solution to personal anguish. It is meant to give people — including the facilitator — something solid to stand on while they stand with those who are suffering.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

Theodicy is the technical term for any attempt to explain how a good and all-powerful God can allow suffering and evil to exist. The word itself comes from Greek: theos (God) and dikē (justice) — it literally means "the justice of God." Philosophers and theologians have been working on this question for millennia, and the three responses this module offers (free will, soul-making, and the eschatological hope) are the main lines of serious Christian thought on the subject. But the critical distinction the module is drawing is this: the intellectual question ("how can these two things both be true?") and the personal question ("why is this happening to me?") feel like the same question from outside, but from inside they are completely different. The first is a puzzle you work on with your mind. The second is a wound you live with in your body. Answering the first does not heal the second — and trying to do so in the moment can actually make things worse, because it communicates that the person's pain is primarily a problem to be solved rather than an experience to be honored.

What Scripture Does With Suffering

It Honors the Weight of It First

Psalm 88:13–18

"But I, O LORD, cry to you; in the morning my prayer comes before you. O LORD, why do you cast my soul away? Why do you hide your face from me?... You have caused my beloved and my friend to shun me; darkness is my closest friend."

Psalm 88 is unique in the entire Psalter: it is the only psalm that ends in pure darkness, without resolution, without a turn toward praise, without a reassurance that morning is coming. It ends with the word "darkness." The God who inspired this text did not edit it toward comfort. He preserved it — including it in the canonical text used in Israel's worship — as if to say: this experience is real, it belongs here, it does not have to be resolved before it can be spoken to me.

This is the Bible's own word to the person in the room whose experience of suffering has not resolved. The presence of Psalm 88 in Scripture is itself a pastoral act.

Lamentations 3:1–3, 21–23

"I am the man who has seen affliction under the rod of his wrath; he has driven me and brought me into darkness without any light; surely against me he turns his hand again and again, all day long... But this I call to mind, and therefore I have hope: The steadfast love of the LORD never ceases; his mercies never come to an end; they are new every morning."

Lamentations 3 does turn toward hope — but notice when it turns, and from where. The prophet has been in devastation long enough to say "he has driven me into darkness without any light." The hope in verses 21–23 is not cheap. It is the hope of someone who has been all the way down into the darkness and found that the steadfast love of God — the hesed — outlasts even that. The turn is not a denial of the darkness. It is what has been found at the bottom of it.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

Hesed is one of the most important words in the Old Testament, and one of the hardest to translate. It is variously rendered as "steadfast love," "lovingkindness," "mercy," or "covenant faithfulness" — no single English word captures it. What it describes is a love that is bound up with commitment: not merely an emotion God feels but a promise God keeps. It is the love of someone who has made a covenant and will not break it, even when the other party has. When Lamentations 3 says that the hesed of the LORD never ceases, this is not a sentimental reassurance. It is a claim about the character of God made from the bottom of a national catastrophe — the destruction of Jerusalem. The prophet is standing in the rubble and saying: even here, the covenant-keeping love of God is still real. That is either the most deluded or the most remarkable claim in the Old Testament. The book of Lamentations itself is a collection of poems written in response to the destruction of Jerusalem by Babylon in 586 BC — a catastrophe that felt, to the people living through it, like the end of everything God had promised.

It Refuses to Explain It Away

The book of Job is the Bible's most sustained engagement with the problem of suffering — and its most emphatic conclusion is that Job's friends, who offered explanations, were wrong. Their theology was not entirely false. Their application of it was. They took accurate theological principles and used them to explain away an experience that deserved to be honored rather than diagnosed. God's word to them at the end of the book is sharp: "you have not spoken of me what is right, as my servant Job has" (Job 42:7).

Job, who did not explain his suffering but argued with God about it, who demanded an audience, who refused the comfort of tidy answers — Job is the one vindicated. The Bible has room for honest rage at God that the church often does not. This module is an invitation to recover that room.

The Intellectual Response: What We Can Honestly Say

The intellectual problem of evil has several serious responses, none of them complete, all of them worth knowing. The curriculum does not pretend to resolve the problem — it offers honest engagement with what the best Christian thinkers have said.

The Free Will Response

Much of the suffering in human history is the result of human choices — not God's. The capacity to love genuinely requires the capacity to refuse love. A world in which God simply prevents all harm would be a world of automatons, not persons. C.S. Lewis put this point compactly in The Problem of Pain: "Free will, though it makes evil possible, is also the only thing that makes possible any love or goodness or joy worth having." The freedom that makes love possible also makes cruelty possible. This does not account for natural suffering, but it accounts for a significant proportion of human suffering.

⚖️ A Note on the Free Will Defense and Reformed Theology

Lewis's version of the free will defense — and the version most commonly heard in popular apologetics — assumes what philosophers call libertarian free will: the idea that genuine personhood requires the metaphysical ability to have chosen otherwise, and that God cannot prevent moral evil without destroying freedom. This is a useful apologetic frame, particularly for those with no prior theological commitments, and it maps honestly onto the Arminian and broadly Wesleyan traditions.

Reformed theology approaches the question differently. Rather than grounding God's permission of evil in a constraint imposed by human freedom, it distinguishes between God's ordaining and his permitting (WCF V.4), and holds that human moral responsibility is fully compatible with divine sovereignty — a position philosophers call compatibilism. In this frame, the "automatons" objection dissolves differently: genuine personhood and genuine responsibility are preserved not by libertarian freedom but by the nature of the will itself (willing what we will, without external compulsion), even while God providentially governs all things for his holy ends.

Neither tradition claims to fully explain why a good and sovereign God permits particular evils. The honest response from both is that the full answer exceeds what we can currently see — and the eschatological response below is the more fundamentally Christian one: not that we can explain it, but that God in Christ has entered it, and will redeem it.

The Soul-Making Response

The Christian vision of human life is not comfort but transformation — not the preservation of the self as it is but the formation of the self into something genuinely good. Some of the deepest qualities of human character — compassion, courage, perseverance, genuine love — appear to require difficulty in order to develop. This does not mean God authors evil for pedagogical purposes. It means that a world in which nothing costs anything would produce people who are nothing.

🧭 What the Soul-Making Response Cannot Say

The soul-making response is true in a limited sense — and it becomes cruel when offered as the primary frame to someone in acute pain. Do not tell someone whose child has died that God is using this to make them stronger. That may or may not be true; it is almost certainly not what they need to hear in the first month, year, or decade. The soul-making response is a framework for retrospective reflection, not a pastoral response to active grief. Timothy Keller puts this wisely in Walking with God through Pain and Suffering: there is a time for reasoning about suffering and a time for sitting with it; wisdom is knowing which time it is.

The Eschatological Response

The Christian claim is not that suffering is meaningless or that it will be explained. It is that it will be redeemed — that the resurrection is the firstfruits of a new creation in which "he will wipe away every tear from their eyes, and death shall be no more, neither shall there be mourning, nor crying, nor pain anymore, for the former things have passed away" (Revelation 21:4). This is not a denial of the present reality of pain. It is the claim that the present reality of pain is not the final chapter.

Paul makes this claim with unusual force in Romans 8: "I consider that the sufferings of this present time are not worth comparing with the glory that is to be revealed to us." He is not dismissing the present suffering — he has catalogued his own sufferings elsewhere with considerable detail. He is placing it in a frame so large that the ratio is honest: real suffering, against the backdrop of the resurrection's promise, is not the end of the story.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

The word eschatological comes from the Greek eschaton, meaning "last things" or "the end." In theology, it refers to the ultimate future God has promised — the final resolution of all things, the new creation, the world fully set right. The eschatological response to suffering is not saying "it'll all work out eventually" in a vague, hand-waving way. It is making a specific claim grounded in the resurrection: because God raised Jesus from the dead, there is concrete reason to believe that death, suffering, and evil are not the last word about anything. The three responses in this section — free will, soul-making, and eschatological hope — are not meant to be combined into a tidy package that "solves" suffering. Each captures something real, and none is complete on its own. Together they represent what the best Christian thinkers have been able to say honestly. What they cannot do — and what the next section addresses — is replace the presence of a God who entered suffering himself rather than explaining it from a distance.

The Theological Center: God Did Not Stand at a Distance

The Christian answer to the problem of suffering is ultimately not a philosophical argument. It is a person — and specifically, what that person did.

Mark 15:34

"And at the ninth hour Jesus cried with a loud voice, 'Eloi, Eloi, lema sabachthani?' which means, 'My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?'"

On the cross, the eternal Son of God quoted Psalm 22 — the archetypal cry of abandonment, of one who has sought God and found only silence. The one who is God experienced, from the inside, what it is to cry out to God and receive no immediate answer. The incarnation means God did not resolve the problem of suffering by explaining it. He entered it — fully, in its most extreme form. He went all the way to the bottom of human desolation, to godforsakenness itself, to the place where no comfort comes and no answer is given.

This does not answer the question of why suffering exists. But it permanently changes the answer to the question of where God is when suffering happens. He is not watching from a safe distance. He has been there. He knows what it is from the inside.

When the Body Cannot Hear: Trauma and the Capacity for Reception

There is a dimension of suffering that theological frameworks alone do not address, and that every facilitator of this module needs to understand. Researchers in trauma — including Dr. Beth Argot, whose work in this area was presented at Dallas Theological Seminary — have documented a precise neurological mechanism: severe or sustained overwhelming experience does not only produce emotional pain. It disrupts the brain's integration. When a person is overwhelmed beyond their coping capacity, the cortex — the region of the brain responsible for language, reasoning, and the processing of meaning — can go offline. The emotional and physiological survival responses dominate and become, in clinical language, "stuck." The person is not in the past. Neurologically, part of them still is.

The pastoral implication is significant and too often missed. A person who has experienced serious trauma can sit in a small group, hear every word of this module spoken truthfully and well, and find themselves genuinely unable to receive it — not because their theology is deficient or their will is weak, but because the neurological pathway that connects propositional knowledge to relational trust has been disrupted. The brain that once allowed "God loves you" to land as felt reality is the same brain that was overwhelmed by betrayal, violence, or chronic invalidation. And it has learned, at a level below conscious thought, that the world is not safe.

This is not, theologically, the end of the story. The Spirit of God is not limited by damaged neurology — Scripture is full of people whose capacity for trust had been devastated by experience and who were nonetheless reached by God, often in ways that bypassed their ordinary channels of reception (Elijah under the broom tree; Paul on the Damascus road; Mary at the tomb, who did not recognize the risen Christ until he spoke her name). But it is the context in which the church does its work. And it changes what faithful pastoral presence looks like.

The healing pathway that trauma researchers consistently describe has three movements: reconnection with self (the interior world that has been shut down), reconnection with others (in conditions of genuine relational safety), and reconnection with God (which often follows the first two rather than preceding them, because the traumatized person's image of God has been distorted by the trauma itself). The church, understood rightly, is precisely the community in which all three movements can occur — which is why the church's failure to provide safety is so devastating to survivors, and why the church's recovery of its vocation as a community of honest presence and patient love is so urgent.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

The section above is making a claim that may be unfamiliar but is increasingly well-documented: trauma is not only an emotional experience. It is a neurological one — it physically changes how the brain processes information. The cortex (the part of the brain that handles language, reasoning, and making sense of things) can effectively shut down when a person is overwhelmed, leaving the survival systems (fight, flight, freeze) in charge. When this happens repeatedly or severely enough, the brain can get stuck in that protective mode — even years later, even when the danger is gone. This is why someone can sit in a Bible study, hear "God loves you," believe it intellectually, and feel absolutely nothing. The channel between knowing and feeling has been disrupted. It is not a lack of faith. It is an injury. And the section's point about healing through relationship is equally concrete: just as the injury happened through relationships (betrayal, abuse, abandonment), the healing tends to happen through relationships too — safe, patient, non-coercive ones that slowly teach the brain that trust is possible again. This is why the church's calling to be a safe community is not just a nice idea. For some people, it is the difference between being able to receive God's love and not being able to.

🧭 Trauma-Informed Facilitation: What It Looks Like in Practice

A trauma-informed facilitator is not a therapist. The distinction matters: do not attempt to provide clinical care in a small group setting, and do not encourage detailed disclosure of traumatic events in a group context, as this can retraumatize both the disclosing person and listeners. What a facilitator can do is create the conditions in which healing becomes possible.

Safety first. Trauma survivors read relational environments rapidly and below conscious awareness. A facilitator who projects defensiveness, impatience, or the need for a theological outcome will register as unsafe. The posture of genuine curiosity and non-judgment is not just pastoral warmth — it is neurologically significant. The group's emotional temperature is set by the person at the front of the room.

Normalize without minimizing. Naming that "some of us may find it difficult to feel things that we believe cognitively — that the distance between knowing and experiencing is real and not a sign of deficient faith" is a small act with large consequences. It names something many people have never had named, and it removes the shame that compounds the original wound.

Pace matters. Trauma survivors are often hypervigilant to pressure. A facilitator who moves through this material too quickly, or who presses for emotional resolution, will trigger protective shutdown rather than openness. Sit with the hard questions longer than feels comfortable. Silence is not a problem to be fixed.

Refer with care and warmth. If someone discloses significant trauma, the appropriate next step is a private conversation and, where necessary, a warm referral to a qualified Christian counselor — not immediate group processing. Have resources ready. Know what is available in your community. The Trauma Healing Institute (traumahealinginstitute.org) offers trained facilitator certification for church-based healing groups, which is a substantively different context from this curriculum's small group discussion format.

🕊️ For Those Who Have Experienced Suffering at the Hands of Christians or the Church

There is a specific and particularly painful form of suffering that this curriculum must not overlook: the suffering inflicted in God's name. Spiritual abuse, manipulation, coercion, the weaponizing of Scripture against vulnerable people — these are real, they are extensively documented, and they are a significant reason why many people find the fountain feels permanently sealed.

The first thing to be said is: what happened to you was wrong. It is not a theological puzzle to be explained. It is a betrayal of the character of the God this curriculum is about — the God who runs toward the shamed rather than shaming them further, who carries the cost of the return rather than placing it on the one returning.

There is also something worth knowing about why spiritual abuse damages in the particular way it does, because naming it precisely can itself be a small act of liberation. Trauma researchers have documented that damage at the relational level — sustained betrayal of trust, particularly by someone in a position of spiritual authority — disrupts the very neurological pathways through which we receive love, trust others, and experience God as present. Spiritual abuse does not only leave emotional wounds. It can make the experience of God feel neurologically inaccessible, even when the person intellectually believes every word of what they were taught. This is not spiritual failure. It is injury. It can be healed, but it heals through the same pathway it was damaged: slowly, through genuinely safe relationships, over time, as trust is rebuilt with patient and non-coercive people who refuse to use God as leverage.

The second thing is harder, and should only be offered in the presence of genuine pastoral care, not deployed quickly in a group setting: the failure of those who claimed to represent God does not settle the question of who God actually is. The Pharisees represented the God of Israel and killed his Son. The betrayal of representation has a long history. What it cannot do is change the character of the One being misrepresented. But this truth must be held gently over time, in relationship — not offered as an argument to close down a person's pain. If you are facilitating this module and someone discloses institutional harm, do not reach for theology. Reach for presence. The theology can come later, if they want it, when safety has been established.

What We Can and Cannot Promise

A curriculum that offers too much in this area does more damage than one that offers less. Here is what this curriculum will and will not promise, stated plainly:

We can say: suffering is real and its weight is not minimized by faith. We can say God has entered it from the inside and has not stood at a safe distance. We can say the resurrection is the ground for genuine hope that suffering is not the final word. We can say the community of faith is the place where suffering is meant to be borne together rather than alone. We can say that the God of Job vindicated the one who argued honestly more than those who offered explanations.

We cannot say: that God will always tell you why. That the suffering will make sense before it ends, or after. That faith protects you from tragedy. That praying with sufficient faith produces the healing you need. That your suffering is primarily a lesson God is teaching you. That those who suffer more have done something to deserve it.

The honest word, held alongside the hope: some questions will not be answered in this life. The Christian faith does not resolve the problem of suffering — it offers the company of a God who has been inside it, and the promise that he is not finished with the story.

The Church as the Body That Heals: Why Community Is Constitutive, Not Instrumental

Romans 12:15 — "Weep with those who weep" — is perhaps the most practically important instruction in the New Testament for this module. Before theology, before argument: presence. But the curriculum needs to say more than this, because the church's role in healing is deeper than the provision of emotional support.

Trauma, as we have seen, damages primarily at the relational level — it disrupts the capacity to trust, to receive love, and to experience God as safe. This means healing is not primarily an interior transaction between a person and God that community facilitates from the outside. It is a process that happens through relationships of genuine safety and patient love. The church community that shows up faithfully, non-coercively, over a long time — that neither presses for premature resolution nor abandons the person in the darkness — is not providing a service alongside the healing. It is the environment in which healing of this kind actually occurs.

This is deeply congruent with the New Testament's vision of the church as the body of Christ. Paul's body metaphor in 1 Corinthians 12 is not decorative — it means that the suffering of one member is genuinely felt by the others, and that the healing of one member is a corporate project. Galatians 6:2's command to "bear one another's burdens" is written in the context of moral failure and restoration, but its logic extends to suffering: the weight that would crush one person is meant to be distributed across many. The body of Christ is not a collection of individuals who happen to attend the same services. It is a community in which the accumulated weight of human suffering is borne together, in the same way that the One who bore it all bore it bodily.

Practically: a church that provides this environment creates it through specific practices — patient listening without agenda, the recovery of lament in worship, the willingness to name hard things honestly rather than reaching for premature comfort, the provision of ongoing presence through long seasons of suffering rather than a burst of initial response followed by withdrawal, and the explicit training of pastoral caregivers in trauma-informed practices. None of these require clinical expertise. All of them require genuine love that is willing to sit in the dark rather than rushing toward the light. Module 9 develops this further.

Discussion Questions
  • Has there been a time when you experienced suffering and were given an explanation that felt inadequate or even harmful? What would you have needed instead?
  • What do you make of the fact that Psalm 88 — which ends in pure darkness, without resolution — is included in the Bible's book of worship?
  • Does it change anything that the Son of God cried from the cross, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" What does that mean about where God is when we suffer?
  • Have you ever found it easier to know something about God intellectually than to feel it as real and close? What do you think creates that distance — and what has, if anything, bridged it?
  • Where do you need presence more than answers right now — and is there someone in this room who can offer it? What would it look like for this group to be a community that bears that weight together, not just tonight but over time?
📚 Deep Dive — Module 6

Diane Langberg, Suffering and the Heart of God: How Trauma Destroys and Christ Restores (New Growth Press, 2015) — The most theologically and clinically integrated work on trauma from an evangelical perspective. Langberg brings 50 years of clinical experience alongside a deeply Christ-centered theology of suffering. Her central argument — that the cross is the lens through which trauma is transformed, not explained — is exactly the framing this module requires. Essential reading for facilitators, and the single most important resource addition from this revision.

Diane Langberg, When the Church Harms God's People (Brazos, 2024) — Specifically for facilitators who encounter church-hurt in this module. Langberg diagnoses how Christian communities cause harm and offers a constructive vision of what the church could be. Honest, rigorous, and deeply pastoral.

Trauma Healing Institute, Healing the Wounds of Trauma: How the Church Can Help (American Bible Society) — The foundational resource of a globally-deployed, Scripture-based trauma healing curriculum that has been used in 100+ languages. The THI's model — trained facilitators, small-group setting, integration of mental health best practices with biblical truth — is the most mature implementation of the church-as-healing-community vision this module articulates. Facilitators interested in extending this ministry should explore traumahealinginstitute.org.

Beth Argot, "When the Brain Can't Hear God: Understanding Trauma and Disconnection" — A 2025 DTS chapel session available on YouTube. Argot, Arts and Trauma Healing Director at Dallas International University, presents a helpful introduction to the neurological framework underlying this module's trauma section. A useful supplementary resource for facilitators who want a concise, accessible entry point to the neuroscience of trauma before going deeper with Langberg.

Timothy Keller, Walking with God through Pain and Suffering (2013) — The most comprehensive evangelical treatment of the problem of suffering: intellectually serious, pastorally warm, honest about what cannot be said. Keller argues that suffering is not primarily a philosophical problem to be solved but an encounter with a person to be navigated. Essential for facilitators.

C.S. Lewis, A Grief Observed — Lewis's raw journal written after his wife Joy's death. Not a theodicy but an account of what grief actually looks like from the inside, written by someone who had written theodicy and found it insufficient when suffering became personal. One of the most honest books in the Christian tradition.

Philip Yancey, Where Is God When It Hurts? and Disappointment with God — Accessible, honest treatments written from personal experience of disillusionment and recovery. Yancey's willingness to name his own doubt makes him a trustworthy guide.

D.A. Carson, How Long, O Lord? Reflections on Suffering and Evil — Carson's careful biblical-theological treatment. Rigorous where Yancey is accessible; the two complement each other well.

Walter Brueggemann, The Message of the Psalms — On lament as a theological category: the recovery of honest grief as a legitimate form of prayer. Brueggemann's work has rehabilitated the psalms of lament for communities that had marginalized them.

Henri Nouwen, The Wounded Healer — On the pastoral call to minister from one's own wounds rather than from a position of safe competence. Essential reading for facilitators.

Eleonore Stump, Wandering in Darkness — A philosophically rigorous engagement with why God's hiddenness is compatible with his goodness. Demanding but important for those who want to engage the intellectual problem at its most serious level.
