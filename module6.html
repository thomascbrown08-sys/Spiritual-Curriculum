The Problem of Suffering

Where Is God When It Hurts?
This module is placed here deliberately — after the evidence has been examined and before the call to faithful living is issued. It would be dishonest to invite people into the Christian life without first sitting honestly in the hardest question most of them carry. This module should not be rushed. It is the curriculum's most pastorally demanding session and, for people in genuine pain, potentially its most important.
🧭 Before You Begin: The Facilitator's Own Posture

This module will go badly if it is taught from a position of safety. The facilitator who has answers prepared — who knows ahead of time what they will say to the person who lost a child, or who survived abuse, or who prayed for healing and received silence — will communicate, however unintentionally, that the answers matter more than the person. They do not.

The posture this module requires is what Henri Nouwen called the wounded healer: someone who has been in the dark themselves and speaks from that place, not from above it. The curriculum's theology is sound. But theology spoken from a safe distance to someone in acute pain is not pastoral care — it is a performance of competence that leaves the person more alone than before.

Begin by being honest about your own experience of suffering and doubt. The group will trust you more for the honesty than for the answers.

Two Different Problems — and Why the Difference Matters

The problem of suffering is actually two problems, frequently confused, requiring different responses.

The intellectual problem of evil asks: if God is both perfectly good and omnipotent, why does suffering exist? This is a genuine philosophical question with a two-thousand-year history of serious engagement. It deserves a serious answer — and this module offers one, carefully.

The personal problem of suffering asks something different: why is this happening to me, and where is God in it? This is not primarily a philosophical question. It is a cry. And a cry does not primarily need an argument in response. It needs a presence.

The danger of this module — and of well-intentioned theological conversations about suffering generally — is treating the second problem as if it were the first. A person sitting in grief does not primarily need theodicy. They need someone to sit with them in it. The intellectual framework this module offers is not meant to be deployed in the room as a solution to personal anguish. It is meant to give people — including the facilitator — something solid to stand on while they stand with those who are suffering.

What Scripture Does With Suffering

It Honors the Weight of It First

Psalm 88:13–18

"But I, O LORD, cry to you; in the morning my prayer comes before you. O LORD, why do you cast my soul away? Why do you hide your face from me?... You have caused my beloved and my friend to shun me; darkness is my closest friend."

Psalm 88 is unique in the entire Psalter: it is the only psalm that ends in pure darkness, without resolution, without a turn toward praise, without a reassurance that morning is coming. It ends with the word "darkness." The God who inspired this text did not edit it toward comfort. He preserved it — including it in the canonical text used in Israel's worship — as if to say: this experience is real, it belongs here, it does not have to be resolved before it can be spoken to me.

This is the Bible's own word to the person in the room whose experience of suffering has not resolved. The presence of Psalm 88 in Scripture is itself a pastoral act.

Lamentations 3:1–3, 21–23

"I am the man who has seen affliction under the rod of his wrath; he has driven me and brought me into darkness without any light; surely against me he turns his hand again and again, all day long... But this I call to mind, and therefore I have hope: The steadfast love of the LORD never ceases; his mercies never come to an end; they are new every morning."

Lamentations 3 does turn toward hope — but notice when it turns, and from where. The prophet has been in devastation long enough to say "he has driven me into darkness without any light." The hope in verses 21–23 is not cheap. It is the hope of someone who has been all the way down into the darkness and found that the steadfast love of God — the hesed — outlasts even that. The turn is not a denial of the darkness. It is what has been found at the bottom of it.

It Refuses to Explain It Away

The book of Job is the Bible's most sustained engagement with the problem of suffering — and its most emphatic conclusion is that Job's friends, who offered explanations, were wrong. Their theology was not entirely false. Their application of it was. They took accurate theological principles and used them to explain away an experience that deserved to be honored rather than diagnosed. God's word to them at the end of the book is sharp: "you have not spoken of me what is right, as my servant Job has" (Job 42:7).

Job, who did not explain his suffering but argued with God about it, who demanded an audience, who refused the comfort of tidy answers — Job is the one vindicated. The Bible has room for honest rage at God that the church often does not. This module is an invitation to recover that room.

The Intellectual Response: What We Can Honestly Say

The intellectual problem of evil has several serious responses, none of them complete, all of them worth knowing. The curriculum does not pretend to resolve the problem — it offers honest engagement with what the best Christian thinkers have said.

The Free Will Response

Much of the suffering in human history is the result of human choices — not God's. The capacity to love genuinely requires the capacity to refuse love. A world in which God simply prevents all harm would be a world of automatons, not persons. C.S. Lewis put this point compactly: "God could, had He pleased, have been a mechanical Necessity. Instead, He created beings — which is to say, beings capable of love — beings who could choose." The freedom that makes love possible also makes cruelty possible. This does not account for natural suffering, but it accounts for a significant proportion of human suffering.

The Soul-Making Response

The Christian vision of human life is not comfort but transformation — not the preservation of the self as it is but the formation of the self into something genuinely good. Some of the deepest qualities of human character — compassion, courage, perseverance, genuine love — appear to require difficulty in order to develop. This does not mean God authors evil for pedagogical purposes. It means that a world in which nothing costs anything would produce people who are nothing.

🧭 What the Soul-Making Response Cannot Say

The soul-making response is true in a limited sense — and it becomes cruel when offered as the primary frame to someone in acute pain. Do not tell someone whose child has died that God is using this to make them stronger. That may or may not be true; it is almost certainly not what they need to hear in the first month, year, or decade. The soul-making response is a framework for retrospective reflection, not a pastoral response to active grief. Timothy Keller puts this wisely in Walking with God through Pain and Suffering: there is a time for reasoning about suffering and a time for sitting with it; wisdom is knowing which time it is.

The Eschatological Response

The Christian claim is not that suffering is meaningless or that it will be explained. It is that it will be redeemed — that the resurrection is the firstfruits of a new creation in which "he will wipe away every tear from their eyes, and death shall be no more, neither shall there be mourning, nor crying, nor pain anymore, for the former things have passed away" (Revelation 21:4). This is not a denial of the present reality of pain. It is the claim that the present reality of pain is not the final chapter.

Paul makes this claim with unusual force in Romans 8: "I consider that the sufferings of this present time are not worth comparing with the glory that is to be revealed to us." He is not dismissing the present suffering — he has catalogued his own sufferings elsewhere with considerable detail. He is placing it in a frame so large that the ratio is honest: real suffering, against the backdrop of the resurrection's promise, is not the end of the story.

The Theological Center: God Did Not Stand at a Distance

The Christian answer to the problem of suffering is ultimately not a philosophical argument. It is a person — and specifically, what that person did.

Mark 15:34

"And at the ninth hour Jesus cried with a loud voice, 'Eloi, Eloi, lema sabachthani?' which means, 'My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?'"

On the cross, the eternal Son of God quoted Psalm 22 — the archetypal cry of abandonment, of one who has sought God and found only silence. The one who is God experienced, from the inside, what it is to cry out to God and receive no immediate answer. The incarnation means God did not resolve the problem of suffering by explaining it. He entered it — fully, in its most extreme form. He went all the way to the bottom of human desolation, to godforsakenness itself, to the place where no comfort comes and no answer is given.

This does not answer the question of why suffering exists. But it permanently changes the answer to the question of where God is when suffering happens. He is not watching from a safe distance. He has been there. He knows what it is from the inside.

🕊️ For Those Who Have Experienced Suffering at the Hands of Christians or the Church

There is a specific and particularly painful form of suffering that this curriculum must not overlook: the suffering inflicted in God's name. Spiritual abuse, manipulation, coercion, the weaponizing of Scripture against vulnerable people — these are real, they are extensively documented, and they are a significant reason why many people find the fountain feels permanently sealed.

The first thing to be said is: what happened to you was wrong. It is not a theological puzzle to be explained. It is a betrayal of the character of the God this curriculum is about — the God who runs toward the shamed rather than shaming them further, who carries the cost of the return rather than placing it on the one returning.

The second thing is harder, and should only be said in the presence of genuine pastoral care, not in a group setting without significant trust: the failure of those who claimed to represent God does not settle the question of whether God is who he says he is. The Pharisees represented the God of Israel and killed his Son. The betrayal of representation has a long history. What it cannot do is change the character of the One being misrepresented.

This is not an argument to be offered quickly to a wounded person. It is a truth to be held gently, over time, in relationship. If you are facilitating this module and someone discloses institutional harm, do not reach for theology. Reach for presence. The theology can come later, if they want it.

What We Can and Cannot Promise

A curriculum that offers too much in this area does more damage than one that offers less. Here is what this curriculum will and will not promise, stated plainly:

We can say: suffering is real and its weight is not minimized by faith. We can say God has entered it from the inside and has not stood at a safe distance. We can say the resurrection is the ground for genuine hope that suffering is not the final word. We can say the community of faith is the place where suffering is meant to be borne together rather than alone. We can say that the God of Job vindicated the one who argued honestly more than those who offered explanations.

We cannot say: that God will always tell you why. That the suffering will make sense before it ends, or after. That faith protects you from tragedy. That praying with sufficient faith produces the healing you need. That your suffering is primarily a lesson God is teaching you. That those who suffer more have done something to deserve it.

The honest word, held alongside the hope: some questions will not be answered in this life. The Christian faith does not resolve the problem of suffering — it offers the company of a God who has been inside it, and the promise that he is not finished with the story.

The Community as the First Response to Suffering

Romans 12:15 — "Weep with those who weep" — is perhaps the most practically important instruction in the New Testament for this module. Before theology, before argument, before the deep dives: presence. The body of Christ is meant to be the community in which no one suffers alone — where the burden of grief is distributed across many shoulders rather than carried by one. This is not a metaphor. It is the practical shape of what the resurrection community is supposed to look like. Module 9 develops this further, but it begins here.

Discussion Questions
  • Has there been a time when you experienced suffering and were given an explanation that felt inadequate or even harmful? What would you have needed instead?
  • What do you make of the fact that Psalm 88 — which ends in pure darkness, without resolution — is included in the Bible's book of worship?
  • Does it change anything that the Son of God cried from the cross, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" What does that mean about where God is when we suffer?
  • Where do you need presence more than answers right now — and is there someone in this room who can offer it?
📚 Deep Dive — Module 6

Timothy Keller, Walking with God through Pain and Suffering (2013) — The most comprehensive evangelical treatment of the problem of suffering: intellectually serious, pastorally warm, honest about what cannot be said. Keller argues that suffering is not primarily a philosophical problem to be solved but an encounter with a person to be navigated — and that the resources of Christian faith are uniquely suited to that navigation. Essential for facilitators.

C.S. Lewis, A Grief Observed — Lewis's raw journal written after his wife Joy's death from cancer. Not a theodicy but an account of what grief actually looks like from the inside, written by someone who had written theodicy and found it insufficient when the suffering became personal. One of the most honest books in the Christian tradition.

Philip Yancey, Where Is God When It Hurts? and Disappointment with God — Accessible, honest treatments written from personal experience of disillusionment and recovery. Yancey's willingness to name his own doubt makes him a trustworthy guide.

D.A. Carson, How Long, O Lord? Reflections on Suffering and Evil — Carson's own careful biblical-theological treatment. Rigorous where Yancey is accessible; the two complement each other well.

Ron Hutchcraft, various radio programs and A Life That Matters — Hutchcraft's pastoral voice, shaped by significant personal loss, brings a warmth and accessibility to this material that academic treatments often lack. His radio ministry's treatment of suffering and grief is worth exploring as supplementary pastoral material.

Walter Brueggemann, The Message of the Psalms — On lament as a theological category: the recovery of honest grief as a legitimate and important form of prayer. Brueggemann's work has rehabilitated the psalms of lament for contemporary Christian communities that had marginalized them.

Henri Nouwen, The Wounded Healer — On the pastoral call to minister from one's own wounds rather than from a position of safe competence. Essential reading for facilitators of this module.

Eleonore Stump, Wandering in Darkness — A philosophically rigorous engagement with why God's hiddenness is compatible with his goodness. Demanding but important for those who want to engage the intellectual problem at its most serious level.
