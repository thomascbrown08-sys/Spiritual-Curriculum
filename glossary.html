Adiaphora
→ Module 10 · Tactic 1

A Greek word meaning literally "things that make no difference" — used in theology to describe matters neither commanded nor forbidden by Scripture, in which Christians have genuine freedom. The classical tradition developed a three-level framework for theological disagreement built on this category. Dogma (the innermost ring): convictions required for salvation — the Trinity, the full divinity and humanity of Christ, the bodily resurrection. Remove these and the gospel collapses. Doctrine (the middle ring): significant theological positions on which serious, Scripture-honoring Christians have disagreed throughout history — predestination and free will, baptism's mode and subjects, the presence of Christ in the Supper, church governance. Real and worth discussing; not salvation-determining. Adiaphora (the outer ring): worship style, service format, program choices, organizational preferences — matters of Christian liberty where no biblical command governs the specific choice. The Escalation Trap (Module 10, Tactic 1) operates by moving disputes from the outer ring toward the inner ring without naming the move — so that a preference dispute ends up being treated with the gravity of a salvation-level question. The practical test: does a specific biblical command or prohibition directly govern this matter? If not, it almost certainly belongs in the adiaphora category, however strong one's feelings about it.

Belief with Intent
→ The Spine

A pedagogical phrase coined for this curriculum to describe the active, ongoing posture of a person already alive in Christ — not the initial reception of saving faith, but the Spirit-enabled formation life that follows from it. Its home text is 2 Peter 1:3–10: God has already completely gifted (δεδωρημένης, perfect passive — action finished, results standing) everything needed for life and godliness, and the believer is commanded to bring lavish, participatory diligence to growing what has been given. Belief with Intent is the Reformers' fiducia — personal trust and reliance, the third element of faith alongside notitia (knowledge) and assensus (agreement) — lived forward as a continuing orientation of heart and life. It is the description of discipleship, not the mechanism of justification. Crucially: we are held not by the quality of our intention but by the Object toward whom our trust is directed — Christ himself, whose own faithfulness (pistis Christou, Romans 3:22; Galatians 2:16) is the ground of our standing before God. The phrase is not a translation of any single Greek term but a constructed formulation designed to name what active, formation-stage faith looks like in practice. See also: The Spine for the full two-movement distinction and Greek analysis.

Broken Cisterns
→ Module 1

An image from Jeremiah 2:13. A cistern is a human-made container meant to store water — a broken one leaks and cannot hold what it promises. In this curriculum, "broken cisterns" represent the things we turn to for meaning, comfort, or identity that cannot bear the weight we place on them: success, relationships, pleasure, control. The problem is not that these things exist, but that they cannot replace the fountain.

Canon
→ Module 5

The collection of books that the Jewish and Christian communities recognized, over time, as authoritative Scripture — the books that belong in the Bible. The word comes from the Greek kanōn, meaning a measuring rod or standard. The canon is not the product of a single decree or committee decision; it emerged from a long process of communal discernment, in which books were tested by criteria including apostolic origin, consistency with received teaching, and acceptance across the wider church. The Protestant canon contains 66 books; the Catholic and Orthodox canons include additional texts (the Deuterocanonical books) that Protestant traditions classify as Apocrypha.

In this curriculum, the canon matters in a specific way: Module 5 argues that the Bible's willingness to preserve its own failures — prophets whose predictions were not fulfilled, psalms that end in unresolved darkness, narratives that depict the founding figures of Israel with startling honesty — is not evidence against its reliability but evidence for it. A canon shaped by the desire to flatter its tradition would not have kept Psalm 88, or Lamentations, or the book of Job. The decision to keep them is itself a theological statement about the kind of God the canon is describing — one who is not embarrassed by honest darkness.

See also: Deus Absconditus · Lament

Cheap Grace
→ Module 8 · Module 9

Bonhoeffer's term for grace that has been separated from the cost of discipleship: forgiveness without repentance, transformation without the death of what needs to die, following Christ without taking up a cross. This curriculum is explicitly designed to avoid cheap grace by holding the beauty of obedience alongside its cost.

Chief End of Man
→ The Spine · Module 1 · Module 9

The Westminster Shorter Catechism (1647), Question 1, asks: "What is the chief end of man?" The answer: "Man's chief end is to glorify God, and to enjoy him forever."

In plain terms: you were not made primarily to follow rules or avoid punishment. You were made to find your deepest, most lasting satisfaction in God — and that satisfaction, fully received, is the very thing that glorifies him. The glorifying and the enjoying are not two separate duties held in uneasy balance. They are one act.

This claim has deep roots across the Christian tradition. Augustine (fourth century): "You made us for yourself, and our hearts are restless until they rest in you." Jonathan Edwards, in The End for Which God Created the World (1765): "The happiness of the creature consists in rejoicing in God, by which also God is magnified and exalted." C.S. Lewis, in Reflections on the Psalms: praise does not merely express enjoyment — it completes it; the glorifying is the enjoying made full. John Piper, distilling Edwards: "God is most glorified in us when we are most satisfied in him." This last formulation is Piper's own crystallization, not a direct Edwards quotation — but it captures the logic precisely.

The connection to this curriculum's organizing image is direct: the Jeremiah 2:13 diagnosis (broken cisterns) has its full weight only when the positive vision is also named. We are thirsty because we were made for a fountain. Sin is not merely rule-breaking — it is the suicidal exchange of an inexhaustible source of satisfaction for things that cannot hold what we demand of them. The gospel's offer is restoration to the purpose for which we were made: to find our joy in the God who is, as Psalm 37:4 says, infinitely desirable.

Christological Assurance
→ The Spine

The Reformation's answer to the problem of assurance: confidence that rests not on the quality or consistency of our faith, obedience, or transformation, but on the faithfulness of Christ toward whom our faith is directed. We look to Christ, not to the quality of our looking. The strength of our grip on Christ is not what holds us — his grip on us is.

Deceitfulness of Sin
→ Module 1

A phrase from Hebrews 3:13. Sin deceives in a specific way: it promises satisfaction it cannot deliver, hides its own costs, and slowly dulls our perception of what is truly good. This explains why people return to destructive patterns even when they know better. Sin does not usually appear ugly at first — it appears plausible.

Deus Absconditus (The Hidden God)
→ Module 1 · Module 6

A theological term, associated with Luther, for the experience of God's hiddenness — the reality that the God who has revealed himself in Scripture is also, in certain seasons, a God who cannot be found by the seeking soul. This is a canonically attested experience (Psalm 42, Isaiah 45:15, Lamentations 3, Mark 15:34) — not an anomaly. The hiddenness is real and does not disprove the reality of the fountain. It has been inhabited by God himself, from the inside.

Dominion of Sin
→ Module 1

Language Paul uses in Romans to describe sin as a ruling power or master. Sin is not neutral or passive — it claims authority over human lives. This is why the gospel must be an act of liberation, not merely an instruction. A slave does not advise his way to freedom.

Elder Brother
→ Module 2

The third character in the Parable of the Prodigal Son (Luke 15:11–32), whose presence is the most searching element of the story for many people in a church context. The elder brother has never left home, has never squandered his inheritance, has worked faithfully — and yet stands outside, refusing to join the celebration, angry that obedience has not been rewarded in the way he expected.

Henri Nouwen and Timothy Keller both argue that the elder brother represents a form of lostness at least as common as the prodigal's — perhaps more common among religious people. The elder brother's problem is not rebellion but a transactional understanding of the relationship with the father: I have earned this, and I deserve recognition for it. The parable's most haunting detail is that it ends without resolution — we do not know whether the elder brother goes in. Jesus leaves the question open. It is, in effect, the question he is putting to every self-righteous listener in the crowd.

For groups with significant church backgrounds, the elder brother dynamic often goes unspoken. The facilitator's role is to create enough safety for people to identify with it honestly rather than with the more sympathetic prodigal.

See also: Good News vs. Good Advice · Justification

Euangelion
→ Module 2 · Module 1

The Greek word usually translated "gospel" or "good news." In the first-century world, the word had a specific public meaning before Christianity adopted it: a euangelion was a herald's announcement of a victory already won — typically a military or political triumph. The herald did not come to advise the city on how to win; he came to announce that the battle was over and the king had prevailed.

This is the word Paul uses in Romans 1:16 — "I am not ashamed of the euangelion" — and that the Gospel writers use to describe what Jesus proclaimed. Understanding it as announcement rather than advice changes what is being offered. The gospel is not a set of instructions for how to become acceptable to God. It is the proclamation that God has acted in Christ — that the debt has been paid, the enemy defeated, the exile ended, the invitation extended. The appropriate response to an announcement is not effort but trust.

This distinction — gospel as announcement, not advice — runs through Module 1's diagnostic framework and Module 2's presentation of the parable of the prodigal son.

See also: Good News vs. Good Advice · Fiducia

Fiducia
→ The Spine

Personal trust and reliance — the third element of faith in the classical Reformation three-part analysis, alongside notitia (knowledge of the facts) and assensus (agreement that they are true). Fiducia transforms belief into faith: entrusting oneself to Christ, not merely acknowledging facts about him. Important precision: this three-part framework is systematic theology derived from careful reading across the biblical text — it is not a direct lexical analysis of the Greek word pistis itself, which does not neatly divide into three components in the dictionary. The framework is a valid and well-grounded conceptual map; it should be described as such rather than presented as simply "what the Greek word means."

Fountain of Living Waters
→ Module 1

A biblical image from Jeremiah 2:13 describing God himself as the source of life, goodness, and satisfaction. A fountain flows freely and endlessly. In this curriculum, the tragedy of sin is described as turning away from this fountain toward sources that cannot actually sustain life.

Good News vs. Good Advice
→ Module 1 · Module 2

Good advice tells you what to do. Good news tells you what has been done. The gospel is not primarily advice — it is the announcement that God has acted in Christ to liberate, justify, and restore. This distinction matters because the human condition (enslaved, condemned, shamed) is a condition, not merely a performance, and conditions require outside intervention, not better instructions.

Grace
→ Module 1 · The Spine

God's unearned, undeserved gift. In the New Testament, grace is not only a disposition but a power — it reigns (Romans 5:21) and it teaches (Titus 2:12). Grace is not opposed to effort; it is opposed to earning. It is both the source of our forgiveness and the enabling power of our obedience.

Hesed
→ Module 6 · 🕊️ Wounded Pilgrim

One of the most important words in the Old Testament, and among the hardest to translate. English renderings include "steadfast love," "lovingkindness," "mercy," and "covenant faithfulness" — no single word captures the full range. What hesed describes is a love bound up with commitment: not merely an emotion, but a promise held. It is the love of one who has made a covenant and will not break it, even when the other party has.

The word appears at the structural hinge of Lamentations 3 — a book written in the immediate aftermath of Jerusalem's destruction by Babylon, one of the most catastrophic events in Israel's history. The prophet has catalogued devastation in the bleakest terms: darkness, captivity, the sense that God has turned against his own people. And then, at verse 21, the turn: "Yet this I call to mind, and therefore I have hope: because of the LORD's hesed we are not consumed." The claim is not that the catastrophe was not real or that the suffering was less than it appeared. The claim is that the covenant-keeping love of God outlasts even this — and that this is the only ground on which hope is possible when everything else has failed.

For the wounded pilgrim, hesed carries a specific pastoral weight: it is the word that describes a love that does not condition itself on your performance or recovery. It held through Israel's worst failures. It holds now.

Healing Community
→ Module 6 · Module 9 · The Spine

A theological claim about what the church is designed to be — and a distinction about how it does its healing work. The key distinction is between community as instrumental to healing and community as constitutive of it.

If community is instrumental, it is the setting in which individuals pursue healing that could in theory happen elsewhere — a helpful context, but not strictly necessary. If community is constitutive, it is the actual medium through which healing occurs — not just the location but the means. The curriculum, drawing on 1 Corinthians 12 and trauma research (particularly the work of Diane Langberg and Beth Argot), argues for the stronger claim: for people whose capacity for spiritual reception has been damaged by trauma or abuse, the safety of specific relationships and the sustained presence of specific people are not supplementary to healing. They are the pathway.

This does not mean the church replaces clinical care — facilitators are not therapists, and Module 6 is explicit about when to refer. It means the church's ordinary vocation — sustained, non-coercive, honest presence with people in difficulty — is itself a form of grace that clinical care cannot replicate, and that the church has sometimes abdicated.

In practice: no pressure to perform recovery on a schedule. No deadline on grief. Willingness to sit with Psalm 88 week after week, without insisting it resolve into Psalm 103. The healing community is the one that bears what individuals cannot bear alone — which is itself the fulfillment of Galatians 6:2.

See also: Deus Absconditus · Honor Restoration · Module 6's Wounded Pilgrim boxes

Honor Restoration
→ Module 1 · Module 2

The gospel's answer to sin as shame. Drawing on the Prodigal Son, honor restoration means being welcomed back, reinstated, and publicly affirmed — not merely tolerated or forgiven. God's response to shame is not distance or probation but costly, running, restoring love.

Hiding from God
→ Responding · 🕊️ Wounded Pilgrim

The instinct to withdraw from God when sin, failure, or shame make us feel unworthy to approach him. First named in Genesis 3: having eaten the fruit, Adam and Eve hid among the trees and covered themselves with leaves when they heard God walking in the garden. Adam's own words tell the whole story — "I was afraid, because I was naked; and I hid myself." The shame that should have driven him toward the one who could help instead drove him away from that one.

The gospel declares this instinct to be the fundamental misreading of the situation. Our brokenness is not the obstacle to coming to Christ — it is the reason he came. C.H. Spurgeon named the inversion precisely: "The first link between my soul and Christ is, not my goodness, but my badness; not my merit, but my misery; not my standing, but my falling; not my riches, but my need." Every delay rooted in "not yet, not like this — let me become better first" is a version of Adam's leaves: a cover made from things that cannot cover. The call of the gospel is not to present yourself cleaned up, but to come as you are, in your need, to the one whose entire mission is addressed to that need.

Justification
→ Module 1

God's act of declaring a person righteous — fully forgiven and accepted — on the basis of Christ's finished work alone, received through faith alone. The Greek verb dikaioō carries two complementary dimensions that are both present in Paul and are not competing but inseparable. Forensic: a legal declaration — the case is settled, the debt paid, the record cleared — in Christ. This is the Reformation's recovery of Paul's central argument in Romans 3–5: we are not made righteous by what we do but declared righteous by what Christ has done, his righteousness credited to our account. This speaks directly to the experience of guilt. Covenantal: a declaration of full membership in God's covenant people — belonging, acceptance, standing in the family of God. This dimension is equally present in Paul and is not in tension with the forensic but its natural extension: the person who has been cleared is simultaneously welcomed in, not merely acquitted but adopted. This speaks directly to the experience of shame and exclusion. Both dimensions are grounded in Scripture; together they answer both the legal weight of guilt and the relational wound of shame. Justification is the act that settles the record; it is not itself the process of transformation — that is sanctification, the Spirit's ongoing work in the one who has been justified. The two are never separated in a genuine believer's life, but they must be carefully distinguished: our standing before God rests entirely on justification, not on the progress of sanctification.

Lament
→ Module 5 · Module 6 · 🕊️ Wounded Pilgrim

In biblical usage, lament is not simply sadness, complaint, or a crisis of faith. It is a specific and recognizable form of prayer: an honest, often raw expression of pain, confusion, or anger directed toward God rather than away from him. Roughly a third of the Psalter consists of lament psalms, and the form appears throughout the prophets, in Job, and in Lamentations. The pattern typically includes: a cry to God, a description of the suffering, a question (often "why?" or "how long?"), and frequently — though not always — a turn toward trust by the end.

Psalm 88 is the most striking exception: it ends, uniquely among the Psalms, without any turn toward resolution. Its final word is "darkness." This is not an editorial accident. It is a preserved and canonical form of prayer — which means the people who decided what belonged in Scripture looked at a prayer that ends in unresolved darkness and said: this belongs. This is inspired. This is the word of God. That decision is itself a statement about the kind of God the canon is describing: one who receives honest grief without requiring it to be tidied before it is brought to him.

In Western evangelical tradition, lament has sometimes been treated with embarrassment — as a spiritually immature phase to be moved through quickly, as evidence of insufficient faith. The curriculum explicitly rejects this reading. Lament held and sustained, addressed honestly to God across a long season, is one of the deepest forms of trust the Psalms know. It is the prayer of someone who has not stopped believing there is a God worth speaking to — even when no answer comes.

Liberation
→ Module 1 · Module 4

The gospel's answer to sin as power. Liberation means real freedom — not just new information or better advice. In Christ, people are not merely advised to live differently; they are freed from the mastery of sin by a power greater than it: the Spirit of the risen Christ.

Peacemaker
→ Module 10 · Module 9

Jesus calls peacemakers "blessed" and names them "children of God" (Matthew 5:9) — not compromisers or avoiders of conflict, but those who can hold the family frame when everyone around them has forgotten it. In Module 10's usage, a peacemaker is the person in a church conflict who has seen clearly enough to name what is actually happening — who can identify the Enemy's tactical pattern at work in a dispute, and who has the specific relational and conversational tools to address it at the right level. Peacemaking is not neutrality (which refuses to engage the substance) or false harmony (which papers over real wounds without addressing them). It is the harder work of holding both the theological validity of what is being argued and the human reality of who is arguing it — keeping the family frame intact while the argument is engaged honestly. See also: Module 10 for the nine specific peacemaker's moves corresponding to the Enemy's nine tactical patterns.

Purity Spiral
→ Module 10 · Tactic 9

An escalating competition for doctrinal or behavioral purity in which the goalposts keep moving and the center of gravity keeps shifting toward greater strictness — not through theological argument but through competitive signaling. In a community that values seriousness about a standard, demonstrating that seriousness is a social signal of membership and trustworthiness; the person who holds the standard more strictly is more central and more trusted. This creates a structural incentive toward increasing strictness that operates independently of theological conviction and often below conscious awareness. The mechanism is driven by people who genuinely love Scripture and are trying to honor God — which is what makes it difficult to interrupt. Within a generation, positions that would have been considered extreme become the assumed baseline, fellowship becomes impossible with anyone outside the innermost circle, and the community increasingly resembles a closed system. The Novatian schism (3rd century) and 20th-century fundamentalist separatism are the two most fully developed historical examples. The diagnostic question: is this standard drawn from Scripture, or from competition within the community about who is most faithful? See also: Module 10 · Tactic 9.

Prevenient / Enabling Grace
→ The Spine

The prior action of the Holy Spirit that makes our faith possible. Before we take a step toward God, God has already moved toward us. The initial reception of saving faith is itself a gift — not the human initiative to which God then responds. In John 6:44, Jesus says no one can come unless the Father draws them; the drawing precedes and enables the coming. This is why the Spine describes even the desire to seek as already evidence that the Spirit is at work. Our Belief with Intent — the active, formation-stage posture of the one already alive in Christ — is always a response to grace already received, not the cause of grace being given.

Resurrection
→ Module 4

The bodily raising of Jesus Christ from the dead on the third day after his crucifixion — the Father's vindication that the cross accomplished what it claimed. The resurrection is not the postscript to the cross but its confirmation. It is also the firstfruits of the new creation: the first emergence of what the entire created order is moving toward, the ground of the gospel's present-tense claims, and the basis for genuine hope that suffering is not the final word.

Sin as Guilt / Power / Shame
→ Module 1

Three registers in which the New Testament describes the human condition — not three separate problems but three angles on the same condition. Power: bondage, the experience of wanting to change and being unable to sustain it. Guilt: real moral fault that stands on the record. Shame: the sense of being fundamentally defective or unacceptable. The gospel has a specific answer to each: liberation, justification, and honor restoration.

The Trinity
→ Module 1 · The Spine · Module 9

The Christian belief that God is one God who exists eternally as three distinct Persons: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. Not three gods, not one God wearing three masks — one God in three Persons in eternal relationship. This means love, self-giving, and relationship are not things God does; they are what God is. Creation is the overflow of that love. The gospel is the invitation to enter it. The church is the community shaped by it.

Catechumen
→ Module 12

A person receiving formal instruction in the Christian faith in preparation for baptism. In the early church, catechumens typically underwent a structured period of teaching — sometimes lasting years — before being admitted to full membership and the sacraments. The creeds were originally taught to catechumens as summaries of the faith they were being baptized into; the Apostles’ Creed in particular preserves, in condensed form, the baptismal confession of the early Roman church. The catechumenate has been revived in various forms in contemporary liturgical churches (notably in the Roman Catholic RCIA process), but the underlying impulse — that formation precedes full entry, and that the faith is something to be handed on and received rather than individually constructed — is ancient and ecumenically shared.

Contra Mundum
→ Module 12

Latin: "against the world." The phrase associated with Athanasius of Alexandria (c. 296–373 AD), who was exiled five times by Arian emperors for his refusal to compromise the Nicene position that the Son is fully God — "of the same substance" as the Father. At several points during the post-Nicene Arian controversy, Athanasius was among the only major bishops still holding the orthodox position while the majority accommodated imperial pressure. The phrase captures the posture of someone who maintains a conviction it would be professionally and personally safer to abandon. In the curriculum's larger argument, Athanasius is offered as an example of what it looks like to hold something true at real cost — and of how positions that once seemed defeated can prove durable when the pressure changes.

Creed
→ Module 12

From Latin credo, "I believe." A formal statement of shared faith — not a magic formula, not a replacement for personal trust in God, but a voice the whole church has learned to say together. The three great ecumenical creeds of the Western and Eastern church are the Apostles’ Creed (likely 2nd century in essence, though the name is misleading), the Nicene Creed (produced at Nicaea in 325 and expanded at Constantinople in 381), and the Chalcedonian Definition (technically not a creed but a doctrinal statement, 451). The Athanasian Creed is a fourth important document, particularly in the Western tradition. The creeds are not tests designed to exclude people; they are the church’s attempt, across centuries, to say the most important things about God with enough precision that they cannot be quietly twisted into something else.

Ecumenical
→ Module 12

From Greek oikoumene, "the whole inhabited world." An ecumenical council represents the whole church rather than a regional or partisan faction. The seven ecumenical councils recognized by both the Eastern and Western churches (with some variation) include Nicaea I (325), Constantinople I (381), Ephesus (431), and Chalcedon (451) — the four most directly relevant to the creeds. In contemporary usage, "ecumenical" more broadly refers to efforts at Christian unity across denominational lines. The two senses are related: the ecumenical councils were moments when the whole church, or as much of it as could be gathered, spoke together — which is also the aspiration of modern ecumenical dialogue.

Filioque
→ Module 12 · Module 10 · Tactic 2 (Vocabulary Trap)

Latin: "and the Son." The word the Western church added progressively to the Nicene Creed — first appearing in Spain (Council of Toledo, 589 AD), spreading across the Frankish church, and formally adopted by Rome in 1014 so that the line read "the Holy Spirit, who proceeds from the Father and the Son" rather than simply "from the Father." The Eastern church (Constantinople) never agreed to the addition and considers it an unauthorized change to an ecumenically agreed text. The theological question is whether the Spirit’s eternal origin is from the Father alone (the Eastern position) or from the Father and Son together (the Western). The historical consequences have been enormous: the filioque is one of the contributing causes of the Great Schism of 1054, the split between Eastern Orthodoxy and Roman Catholicism that has never been formally healed, leaving over a billion Christians in separated communions partly because of this single word. The filioque controversy is referenced in the curriculum’s Module 10 (Tactic 2, the Vocabulary Trap) as a case study in how theological vocabulary differences can calcify, under the pressure of institutional rivalry and historical circumstance, into permanent institutional division.

Homoiousios
→ Module 12 · Module 11 · Arianism

Greek: "of similar substance." The Arian compromise position at and after the Council of Nicaea (325 AD) — holding that the Son is like the Father in substance, but not identical in nature. It is distinguished from the orthodox homoousios ("of the same substance") by a single Greek letter: iota, the smallest character in the alphabet. The difference between these two words became one of the most consequential distinctions in the history of Christian doctrine. Homoiousios appears to concede much while preserving the Arian position that the Son is ultimately distinct in kind from the Father. The Nicene party recognized the compromise as insufficient: similar substance is not the same substance, and only the same substance could ground the mediatorial role the New Testament assigns to Christ.

Homoousios
→ Module 12 · Module 11 · Arianism

Greek: "of the same substance" or "of one Being." The central term of the Nicene Creed, produced at the Council of Nicaea in 325 AD, affirming that the Son is of the same substance as the Father — not a created being, however exalted, but fully and genuinely God. The word cost decades of controversy: it was opposed at Nicaea by the Arian party, adopted over fierce resistance, then contested through the Arian-backed reign of several post-Constantinian emperors, and finally consolidated at Constantinople in 381. Athanasius spent 17 years in exile rather than sign a formula that would have softened or replaced it. Every time the church says "of one Being with the Father" in the Nicene Creed, it is reaching back across seventeen centuries to receive a word that was paid for at great personal and institutional cost.

Hypostasis
→ Module 12 · Module 11 · Modalism

Greek: a distinct person or subsistence within the one divine being. Developed by the Cappadocian Fathers (Basil of Caesarea, Gregory of Nyssa, Gregory of Nazianzus) in the 4th century to provide precise vocabulary for Trinitarian theology. The formula they arrived at: one ousia (one substance or being), three hypostases (three persons or subsistences). This framework navigated between Modalism (which would say there is only one hypostasis, one subject, appearing in three modes) and tritheism (which would say there are three separate beings, three separate ousiai). The three persons of the Trinity are genuinely, irreducibly distinct; they are not roles or masks. And yet they share one divine being — there are not three Gods. Hypostasis is the word that names the "three" side of the one-and-three; ousia names the "one" side. See also: Ousia.

Ousia
→ Module 12

Greek: substance, or being. In Trinitarian theology, the term that names the unity side of the one-God-three-persons formula. One ousia, three hypostases: one being, three persons. The Father, Son, and Spirit share one divine being, one nature, one substance — they are not three beings who happen to cooperate closely or who have made a covenant with one another. The divine being is one. The confusion in early Trinitarian controversy was partly a translation problem: Latin theologians rendered both ousia and hypostasis with the word substantia ("substance"), collapsing a distinction the Greek was able to maintain. This linguistic confusion contributed to several decades of mutual suspicion between Eastern and Western bishops who were, in many cases, saying the same thing in mutually unintelligible terminology. See also: Hypostasis.

Pneumatomachians
→ Module 12

Greek: "Spirit-fighters." Those who, in the decades between Nicaea (325) and Constantinople (381), accepted the Nicene position on the Son — that he is fully God, "of the same substance" as the Father — but denied the full divinity of the Holy Spirit, treating the Spirit as a subordinate or created being. The Pneumatomachians are sometimes called the "Macedonians" after Macedonius of Constantinople, though his exact views are debated. The Council of Constantinople (381 AD) addressed this position directly, expanding the Nicene Creed’s article on the Spirit: "the Lord, the giver of life, who with the Father and the Son is worshiped and glorified." The statement that the Spirit is worshiped and glorified together with the Father and Son is a statement of co-equal divinity — worship names the nature.

Theotokos
→ Module 12 · Module 11 · Nestorianism

Greek: "God-bearer" or "Mother of God." The title for Mary affirmed at the Council of Ephesus (431 AD), over the objection of Nestorius, bishop of Constantinople. Nestorius argued that Mary should be called Christotokos ("Christ-bearer") rather than Theotokos, because she bore the human Christ, not God himself. The council recognized this as a Christological position, not merely a Marian one: if Jesus is fully God — one person, not two — then the woman who bore him bore God. Theotokos is a statement about the identity of her son, not a statement about Mary’s own divine nature or about Catholic Marian theology more broadly. Protestant readers who encounter this term sometimes read it as a Roman Catholic import; in fact, it is an ecumenical Christological affirmation that predates the Reformation by eleven centuries and is affirmed in the Anglican, Lutheran, and many Reformed traditions.
