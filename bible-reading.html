How to Read the Bible

Reading Honestly, Reading Well, Reading Without Deceiving Yourself
The Bereans in Acts 17 are one of the most quietly remarkable communities in the New Testament. They received Paul's teaching with eagerness — and then went home and checked it against Scripture. They did not accept it because Paul was impressive or because he quoted verses. They examined whether what he said was actually true. Luke calls this "noble." This page is about cultivating that same habit — and about the ways even earnest readers, including this curriculum's author, can get it badly wrong.

A Library, Not a Book

The first thing to understand about the Bible is that it is not a single book in the way a novel is a single book. It is a library — 66 documents written over roughly fifteen centuries, in three languages, across three continents, by approximately forty different authors: kings, fishermen, farmers, poets, priests, scholars, and a tax collector. Its unity — the coherent story of God's redemption of his creation, culminating in Jesus — is not imposed by editorial committee. It is the testimony of a tradition that understood itself to be receiving revelation across generations.

That diversity is not a problem to be overcome. It is a feature. The Bible speaks to every register of human experience precisely because it was written in and through every register of human experience. The psalms sound like psalms and not like Leviticus because they are doing different things. A love poem (Song of Solomon) is not doing what a royal chronicle (Kings) is doing, which is not what a letter to a congregation in crisis (1 Corinthians) is doing. Reading all three with identical expectations produces confusion. Reading each in its own mode produces understanding.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

If you walked into a library and pulled down a random book, you would naturally adjust how you read based on what kind of book it was. A history textbook, a poetry collection, a legal code, a personal letter, and a novel all ask different things of the reader. You would not read a poem the same way you read a tax code, or a letter the same way you read a history. The Bible contains all of those types and more. The skill of reading Scripture well begins with recognizing which type of writing you are in — and adjusting your expectations accordingly.

The Genres and How They Work

Narrative (Genesis through Esther; the Gospels; Acts) — History told with theological purpose. The author selects, sequences, and frames events to make a claim about what God is doing. Not every character's action is endorsed: David's behavior in 2 Samuel is recorded, not approved. The narrative's point is often in what it shows rather than what it states. Ask: what is the author showing us about God, humanity, and the relationship between them? What does this episode contribute to the larger story?

Law and Covenant (Leviticus, Deuteronomy, parts of Exodus) — Instruction given to a specific covenant community at a specific historical moment. Reformed hermeneutics distinguishes ceremonial law (fulfilled in Christ), civil law (given to Israel as a theocratic nation), and moral law (binding across all times and people). Not all commands in the Mosaic law apply to twenty-first century Christians in the same way — but discerning which is which requires understanding the covenant structure and reading Paul's treatment of the law carefully, particularly in Galatians and Romans.

Poetry and Wisdom (Job, Psalms, Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, Song of Solomon) — Poetry uses parallelism, imagery, and heightened language. Proverbs are general principles, not guarantees: "Train up a child in the way he should go; even when he is old he will not depart from it" (Proverbs 22:6) describes what is generally true, not what is always true in every case. Job's friends say many theological-sounding things that God explicitly calls wrong (Job 42:7). Reading poetry as doctrine and proverbs as promises produces both bad theology and unnecessary anguish.

Prophecy (Isaiah through Malachi) — Prophets spoke into specific historical situations, often with future implications. Not all prophecy is prediction; much of it is covenant-lawsuit (God calling Israel to account), comfort, and instruction. When prophecy does predict, the fulfillment may be immediate, near-term, and far-future simultaneously (the "telescoping" of prophecy). The New Testament authors read the prophets as pointing toward Christ, which shapes how Christians read them — but responsible interpretation still requires understanding the original historical setting.

Epistles (Romans through Jude) — Letters written to specific communities addressing specific situations. Paul's argument in Galatians is shaped by the particular crisis of Judaizers insisting on circumcision; Corinthians by the specific mess of a young urban church. The principles are universal; their application emerges from understanding what the original crisis was. Reading a passage in isolation from its letter, and the letter in isolation from its occasion, produces misreadings.

Apocalyptic (Daniel; Revelation; portions of Ezekiel and Zechariah) — Highly symbolic literature using vivid imagery to address present suffering and communicate certainty about God's ultimate purposes. The symbols are often drawn from Old Testament imagery and would have been recognizable to original readers. Reading Revelation as a straightforward newspaper account of twenty-first century events was not the genre its author was working in. The question to ask is always: what is this imagery communicating about God's character and purposes, and how would it have been heard by its original audience under persecution?

Context Is Everything

The single most important habit in Bible reading is reading in context — and the failure to do so is the single most common source of misreading. Context operates at several levels, each one essential:

Immediate context: What comes before and after this verse? Many of the most commonly cited verses mean something different — sometimes the opposite — when read within their surrounding paragraphs. "I can do all things through Christ who strengthens me" (Philippians 4:13) is about contentment in poverty and plenty, not a general promise of achievement. "For I know the plans I have for you" (Jeremiah 29:11) was addressed to Israelites in Babylonian exile — understanding that doesn't negate its application, but it changes it significantly.

Book context: What is this book about, and where does this passage fit in its argument or narrative? Romans 3 is incomprehensible without understanding Paul's argument building from Romans 1. James 2 on "faith without works is dead" sounds like it contradicts Paul until you understand James is addressing a different abuse (cheap profession without transformation) than Paul is refuting (earning justification by works).

Canonical context: How does this passage fit within the whole Bible's story? The Reformed principle of analogia scriptura (Scripture interprets Scripture) holds that unclear or difficult passages should be read in light of clearer passages, and that the whole testimony of Scripture provides the framework for understanding any part of it. A single verse cannot establish a major doctrine alone if the rest of Scripture points the other way.

Within canonical context sits a more specific and enormously productive question: how does this passage relate to Christ? Jesus said explicitly that Moses wrote about him (John 5:46), and after his resurrection he opened the minds of his disciples to understand "that everything written about me in the Law of Moses and the Prophets and the Psalms must be fulfilled" (Luke 24:44). The New Testament reads the Old Testament as a unified story moving toward a single resolution, and Christ is its name. The sacrificial system anticipates him. The Psalms speak in his voice before he arrives. The prophets describe his coming with enough precision that early Jewish readers were themselves developing messianic readings of passages like Isaiah 53 before the first century. Reading any part of Scripture with the question "how does this fit the movement toward Christ?" is not imposing a foreign grid — it is reading the document in the direction it was always already moving.

Charles Spurgeon had a particular gift for this — not the allegorical imagination that finds a hidden spiritual meaning behind every stone and tree, but the typological instinct that recognizes genuine structural resonances: how Adam foreshadows the second Adam, how the Passover lamb prefigures the Lamb of God, how David's suffering in the Psalms becomes the very words of Christ from the cross. Spurgeon's instruction to young preachers on this point is worth knowing: take any text, find the road to Christ, and take it. Not because every text is secretly about him by some interpretive trick, but because the whole Bible is one story and he is where it goes. Reading Scripture with that trajectory in mind does not flatten the diversity of the texts. It illuminates why they are all in the same book.

Historical context: Who wrote this, to whom, when, and why? What was the cultural world of the original readers? This does not relativize the text — it illuminates it. Understanding that "turn the other cheek" was addressed to a culture in which a backhanded blow was a specific gesture of social dominance clarifies what Jesus meant without reducing it to mere social commentary.

The Danger of Proof-Texting

Acts 17:11

"Now these Jews were more noble than those in Thessalonica; they received the word with all eagerness, examining the Scriptures daily to see if these things were so."

Proof-texting is the practice of citing a verse or passage as evidence for a position without engaging the passage in its context. It is one of the most common and most damaging habits in popular Christian discourse — and it operates in two directions, each with its own dangers.

Passive proof-texting: accepting whatever has a verse attached. The assumption is that if someone can cite a Bible verse in support of a claim, the claim is therefore biblical. But a verse can be cited for almost anything if taken out of context. Satan quoted Scripture to Jesus in the wilderness (Matthew 4:6). Slaveholders cited Genesis 9, Ephesians 6, and Philemon to justify the ownership of human beings. Inquisitors cited Luke 14:23 to justify state compulsion. The presence of a proof text is not the same as biblical support. The question is whether the text actually teaches what it is being cited to support — which requires reading the whole passage, in context, in light of the rest of Scripture.

Active proof-texting: finding a verse to confirm what you already believe. This is the more insidious form, because it feels like Scripture study. You begin with a conclusion — a conviction, an experience, an argument that seems persuasive — and then search for biblical support. The verses you find feel like confirmation. What has actually happened is that you have used Scripture as a mirror that reflects what you brought to it, rather than a window that shows you what is actually there.

This second form deserves more honesty than it usually receives. The author of this curriculum is not exempt from it. Anyone with strong convictions and access to a concordance can assemble a biblical case for almost anything. The discipline that prevents it is not having fewer convictions — it is holding them with one hand while using the other to ask: what does the whole of Scripture say about this? Where are the passages that complicate or qualify my reading? What have careful students of this text across the centuries concluded? Why do people I respect disagree with me here?

Proof-texting at its worst becomes a tool of control — a way of making the Bible authorize what one has already decided, and then presenting that authorization as the Word of God rather than one's own interpretation. Module 10 names the "Certainty Weapon" (Tactic 5): confidence deployed not to illuminate but to silence, making questioning feel like faithlessness. Proof-texting is the ammunition the Certainty Weapon fires. The remedy is not less confidence in Scripture — it is more honest engagement with it.

⚖️ Taking a Hypothesis Too Far: The Pride Problem

There is a specific pattern worth naming plainly, because it describes something that happens to people who love Scripture and think carefully about it — which may be the most important people to warn.

You notice something in the text. A connection, a pattern, a reading that illuminates something you hadn't seen before. It is genuinely there. You follow it. It gets richer. You share it. People find it helpful. You follow it further. And at some point — hard to identify, easy to miss — you have moved from insight to system, from observation to over-reading, from seeing something real to seeing everything through that one lens. The lens becomes the grid. Passages that don't fit get forced into it. Complexity gets flattened. What began as discovery has become its own kind of proof-texting.

The antidote is not to stop having insights. It is to hold them loosely enough to be contradicted. The test is simple: when you encounter a passage that sits awkwardly with your framework, is your first instinct to ask what the passage means — or to ask how it fits? If consistently the latter, the framework has become a cage. Ecclesiastes 12:12 offers a warning: "Of making many books there is no end, and much study is a weariness of the flesh." The pursuit of ever-more-elaborate systems is not the same as growing in the knowledge of God. The Puritans called this kind of intellectual pride in theological construction "brain-knowledge" — the occupying of oneself with the form of theology rather than its substance.

One more thing needs saying here, honestly. Intelligence does not protect you from this error. Willingness to be corrected, in the abstract, does not protect you from this error. People who are both intelligent and genuinely open to correction have fallen into serious interpretive errors anyway — not because their method failed them but because the error felt, from the inside, exactly like insight. The corrective that actually worked, in most of these cases, was not a better argument or a more disciplined method. It was God's own sovereign hand — working through a person, a circumstance, a passage encountered at an unexpected moment — bringing the truth home in a way that could not be reasoned around. That is not a reason to abandon careful study. It is a reason to hold careful study with open hands, asking God to do what our best efforts cannot: guard us from errors we cannot yet see.

Be a Berean

The Bereans in Acts 17:11 did something specific: they received Paul's teaching with eagerness, and they examined the Scriptures daily to see whether what he was saying was actually there. Both parts matter equally. They received with eagerness — they were not hostile, defensive, or already decided. They examined daily — they did not take even Paul's word for it without checking.

The Berean posture is the one this curriculum asks of its readers toward itself. Everything written here is written by a fallible person who can misread, over-apply, and bring his own blind spots to the text. The goal of every page is to point toward Scripture and toward the tradition's most careful readings of it — not to replace your engagement with Scripture or to become the authority it is citing. If something here strikes you as wrong, or unclear, or inconsistent with what you read in the Bible: check it. That instinct is not suspicion. It is exactly the posture Luke calls noble.

Practically, the Berean habit involves:

Reading in full. When a verse is cited anywhere, go read the surrounding passage. A five-minute investment prevents the majority of proof-texting errors.

Asking about the other side. Whatever conclusion you have reached from a passage, ask: what would someone who disagrees say about this text? What passages do they cite? Is their reading possible? The habit of steelmanning the opposing interpretation before dismissing it is one of the most productive in theological study.

Noticing when you are surprised. The moments when Scripture says something unexpected — different from what you assumed, harder than you expected, more costly than you planned — are often the moments of genuine engagement. If you never encounter anything surprising in your Bible reading, you may be reading only what you brought.

Tracing a doctrine through the whole Bible. Rather than locating a few proof texts for a position, trace the idea through Scripture from beginning to end. What does the Old Testament establish? How does Jesus engage it? How do the epistles develop it? What does Revelation do with it? This canonical reading produces a much more robust understanding than a set of disconnected citations.

Why You Need Other Readers

"Where there is no guidance, a people falls, but in an abundance of counselors there is safety." (Proverbs 11:14)

The Protestant principle of the priesthood of all believers — that every Christian has direct access to God and the right to read Scripture for themselves — is true and important. It has sometimes been misread to imply that every Christian's reading of Scripture is therefore equally valid, or that the accumulated wisdom of the church across centuries adds nothing to individual reading. That misreading produces a kind of interpretive solipsism in which the isolated modern reader's intuitions about a text are treated as independent of — and potentially superior to — twenty centuries of careful scholarship and pastoral reflection. This is not humility. It is a form of pride.

The reformers who most championed the individual's right to read Scripture — Luther, Calvin, Tyndale — were also the people most saturated in the church fathers, most engaged with one another's theology, most accountable to the tradition even when they departed from it. Luther did not arrive at justification by faith by reading Romans fresh. He arrived there after years of theological training, engagement with the entire tradition, and profound awareness of where he was departing from it and why. The famous "Here I stand" was spoken by a man who had read everything.

For the ordinary Christian navigating Scripture without formal training, the counselors worth attending to include:

Mature readers in your community. A pastor, elder, or experienced Christian whose life demonstrates the fruit of the Spirit and whose reading of Scripture has been tested by time and difficulty is worth more than the most impressive voice you found on the internet yesterday. The person who has sat with a text for decades, applied it in crisis, and been corrected by it carries something that technical knowledge alone does not.

The historic tradition. Commentaries, catechisms, and confessions are the accumulated judgment of the church across centuries — the product of careful people who brought hard questions to the text and recorded what they found. Reading a text the church has discussed for two thousand years in isolation from that discussion is a significant self-limitation. It does not need to be the final word, but it deserves to be a first word.

Readers you disagree with. The most useful check on your reading of a passage is often someone from a different tradition who has read it carefully and arrived somewhere different. Understanding why they arrived there — not to defeat their argument but to understand it — reveals the assumptions you brought to the text that you hadn't noticed. Iron sharpens iron (Proverbs 27:17), but only if you are willing to be sharpened.

🕊️ For Those Who Have Been Burned by Someone's Bible Reading

If someone wielded Scripture against you — to justify abuse, to silence legitimate questions, to force conformity, to shame you for honest doubt — this page is written with you in view. The practices named above (reading in context, checking proof texts, seeking counselors, holding readings accountably) are precisely the practices that are absent when Scripture is weaponized. A person using the Bible as a cudgel is almost always proof-texting: selecting, decontextualizing, deploying.

The abuse of Scripture does not make Scripture unreliable. It makes the abuser accountable for the abuse. The same Bible that was used against you contains the passages about the plank and the speck, about leaders who lord it over others, about shepherds who scatter rather than gather, about those who say "Lord, Lord" but do not do what he says. Those passages are also there. The tradition has always known that Scripture can be misused — which is precisely why the Berean habit, the abundance of counselors, and the accountability of every reader to the whole testimony of Scripture matter so much.

Where to Begin

If you are new to Scripture and feel overwhelmed by its size and complexity, start small and specific rather than trying to read the whole thing at once. The following sequence is practical rather than canonical:

Start with a Gospel. Mark is the shortest and most action-driven. John is the most theologically reflective and the most accessible on the person of Jesus. Luke is the most historically careful. Read one of them slowly — a chapter at a time, with a question: what is Jesus doing here, and what does it show about who he is?

Choose a reliable translation. The differences between good translations are real but not alarming — the main things are plain in all of them. For a fuller guide to what translation philosophy means, which translations have issues worth knowing about, why some verses appear in brackets, and how to compare translations, see the companion page: Choosing a Bible Translation. The short answer: for new readers, the NIV or CSB offers readability without sacrificing accuracy. For serious study, the ESV or NASB stays closer to the original languages.

Then read Romans. It is the most systematic exposition of the gospel in the New Testament. It is also demanding. Expect to be confused by parts of it, especially chapters 9–11. That is normal. Read it anyway. The confusion is part of engaging something genuinely large.

Pray before you read. This is not a ritual. It is the practical act of remembering that you are reading a text the Holy Spirit inspired and illumines (see The Holy Spirit page). Ask for understanding before you begin, and you will be putting yourself in the right posture: receptive rather than acquisitive.

Read a passage more than once. The habit of reading a text twice — once quickly to get the shape of it, once slowly to notice what you missed — reveals more than doubling the number of passages you cover. Depth before breadth is the better discipline, especially early on.

📚 For Further Study — Bible Reading and Hermeneutics
  • Gordon Fee & Douglas Stuart, How to Read the Bible for All Its Worth (Zondervan, 4th ed., 2014) — The most practical and widely used introduction to biblical interpretation available. Covers every major genre with clarity and care. The right starting point for anyone who wants to read Scripture well.
  • Graeme Goldsworthy, According to Plan (IVP, 1991) — A biblical theology that traces the one story of Scripture from creation to new creation. Essential for understanding how the pieces fit together — and why Christ is where the whole story arrives.
  • Charles Spurgeon, Christ in the Old Testament (various editions) — A collection of Spurgeon's sermons on Old Testament passages, each tracing the road to Christ. A practical demonstration of Christocentric reading at its best: not allegorical fancy but genuine typological insight that illuminates both Testaments at once.
  • Kevin DeYoung & Greg Gilbert, What Is the Mission of the Church? (Crossway, 2011) — Not primarily a hermeneutics book, but a model of how to read a set of passages carefully, in context, before drawing conclusions. Useful as a demonstration of good practice.
  • D.A. Carson, Exegetical Fallacies (Baker, 2nd ed., 1996) — A catalog of common errors in reading the New Testament, including the proof-texting patterns described on this page. Demanding but irreplaceable for serious readers.
  • The Gospel Coalition: "How to Read the Bible" — A concise practical guide.
  • Ligonier: "Foundations of Biblical Interpretation" series — Video lectures from R.C. Sproul on hermeneutical method.

Before You Open the Text

Everything on this page is true. The habits described — reading in context, checking proof texts, using the abundance of counselors, holding frameworks loosely — are genuine disciplines worth cultivating. Study them. Practice them. They will serve you.

And they are not enough.

The gap between good method and correct understanding is not closed by adding more method. It is closed, when it is closed, by the God who inspired the text and who is not passive while you read it. The same Spirit who illumines Scripture for understanding (1 Corinthians 2:12–14) is also the one who convicts of error, who brings a passage home to a resisting heart at the right moment, who works through a conversation or a crisis or a sleepless night to accomplish what careful study alone could not. He does not wait for your method to be perfect. He is already at work before you open the page.

What this means practically: the most important thing you bring to Scripture is not intelligence, or hermeneutical training, or even a genuine willingness to be corrected. It is a posture of dependence — the honest acknowledgment that you will err, that you probably already have, and that your only reliable protection against errors you cannot yet see is not your own discernment but God's. The prayer that belongs at the beginning of serious Bible study is not "help me understand this" alone. It is something closer to: correct me. Do not leave me where I am. Whatever it takes.

Psalm 139:23–24 names the posture precisely: "Search me, O God, and know my heart; try me and know my thoughts. And see if there be any grievous way in me, and lead me in the way everlasting." This is not a prayer for insight. It is a prayer for exposure — an invitation for God to find and address what the reader cannot find themselves. It is the prayer of someone who knows that their best efforts at self-examination will miss things that God sees clearly.

📖 A Prayer Before Reading

Lord, do not leave us to ourselves.
With your love, do what it takes to mold us into the image of your Son —
to understand his teaching, and to obey it.
Grant us your discernment,
and guard our hearts from pride and error —
especially the errors we are most certain are not errors.
Correct us gently, or not gently, as you see fit.
We trust your wisdom more than our own.
Amen.

This is not a formula. It is a direction — the posture of someone who has come to the text not to confirm what they already know but to be formed by what God has said, and is still saying, and will say again when we are ready to hear it.
