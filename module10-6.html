The Tradition Trap

"Thus Saith the Committee of 1987"
← Tactic 5  |  Overview  |  Tactic 7 →

The Presenting Dispute

Someone proposes a change — to a program, a practice, a format, a policy. The proposal is reasonable; it has been thought through; there is a case to be made for it from Scripture or pastoral wisdom. The response it receives is not a counter-argument from Scripture or reason. It is a counter-argument from history: "We have always done it this way." Or: "The founders of this church set it up this way for a reason." Or: "When we changed this before, it caused a lot of trouble." The weight being appealed to is not the weight of a biblical command. It is the weight of accumulated institutional habit — the gravity of the past functioning as a theological argument against the present.

This is not inherently wrong. Traditions exist for reasons; those reasons may still be good; wisdom pays attention to what has been learned. The Tradition Trap is not the appeal to history — it is the failure to distinguish between what history requires and what Scripture requires, and the consequent treatment of institutional habit as if it carried divine authority.

The Enemy's Actual Move

The mechanism Jesus identified in Mark 7 is the foundational case. The Pharisees approached him with a critique: his disciples were not following the tradition of the elders in the washing of hands. Jesus did not dispute the tradition's existence or even its historical origins. He disputed its authority: "You leave the commandment of God and hold to the tradition of men" (7:8). The issue was not that the tradition was bad — handwashing has practical merit — but that it had been granted a weight it was not entitled to, and that this weight was being used to void an actual biblical command (the korban controversy in 7:9–13: a tradition had developed by which a person could declare their property "korban" — dedicated to God — while continuing to use it, thereby effectively voiding the commandment to honor father and mother by providing for them).

The Tradition Trap works by the same mechanism: tradition accumulates scriptural-level gravity through habit, repetition, institutional investment, and generational transmission — until the question "is this required by Scripture or by tradition?" is not asked, because it no longer feels necessary to ask. At that point, any reforming impulse — any question that probes the tradition's origins or purposes — looks like an attack on Scripture itself, because the tradition has been fused with Scripture in the community's imagination. The person proposing change is not arguing with the committee of 1987. They are arguing with God.

The Scriptural Frame Being Exploited

Paul's call to "hold to the traditions even as I delivered them to you" (1 Corinthians 11:2) and to "stand firm and hold to the traditions that you were taught by us" (2 Thessalonians 2:15) is genuine — but its referent is specific: the apostolic teaching delivered by Paul, meaning the gospel and its implications as he received and transmitted them. This is not a call to preserve the institutional habits of subsequent generations as if they were apostolic deposit. Colossians 2:8 explicitly warns against the opposite: "See to it that no one takes you captive by philosophy and empty deceit, according to human tradition, according to the elemental spirits of the world, and not according to Christ." The tradition Paul commends and the tradition Paul warns against are not the same thing — but the Tradition Trap merges them.

Counting the Cost

The scribes and Pharisees. The foundational case study is biblical. Jesus' most sustained conflict in the Synoptic Gospels is not with pagans or with obvious sinners but with the tradition-guardians of Israel's institutional religion — people who loved Scripture, who took obedience seriously, who were doing what they believed was right in protecting the tradition. The korban example is worth dwelling on: they had not invented a way to disobey a commandment through defiance. They had invented a way to disobey a commandment while believing they were being especially obedient. The tradition had grown complex enough, and had accumulated enough scriptural gravity, that it could now void the thing it was supposedly protecting. This is the Tradition Trap at full development.

The worship wars of the late 20th century. The introduction of contemporary worship music into Western Protestant and evangelical churches from the 1960s onward produced some of the most sustained and damaging institutional conflict of the century. In retrospect, the vast majority of what was defended as "biblical worship" or "reverent worship" was the musical and aesthetic preference of a particular generation — formed in a particular cultural moment, associated with genuine spiritual experience, and therefore perceived as essential to the encounter with God itself. The tradition was real. The encounter it had mediated was real. The error was the conclusion that the form of the tradition, rather than the substance of the encounter, was what Scripture required. Enormous institutional damage was done on both sides. The question that could have de-escalated it — Is this required by Scripture, or by our tradition? And if it's tradition, is it a good one, a bad one, or simply ours? — was rarely asked early enough.

The Reframe

The three-part question that does the work: Is this required by Scripture, or by our tradition? And if it's tradition — is it a good tradition, a bad one, or simply ours?

The third category — simply ours — is the most practically useful. Many church traditions are neither divinely commanded nor harmful; they are particular, local, generational expressions of faithfulness that have meaning within the community that developed them and need not be imposed on, or used to resist, anyone else. A tradition can be valued, preserved, and passed on without being treated as if it were Scripture — and recognizing that it is "simply ours" makes it possible to hold it with appropriate reverence while remaining open to what the next generation needs.

The Peacemaker's Move

When tradition is being deployed as argument: Ask the clarifying question without embarrassment — "Can we locate this in Scripture specifically? I want to understand whether what you're defending is a biblical requirement or a practice that has worked well for us. Both deserve respect, but they deserve different kinds of respect."

When someone's proposal is being blocked by institutional habit: Reframe the conversation around purpose rather than form. "I hear that [tradition] has been important and formative here. Can we ask together what it was trying to accomplish — what the goal was? Because I think [person proposing change] is actually trying to accomplish the same goal by a different method. If that's right, this might be a conversation about the best method rather than a conflict about the goal."

When you are the keeper of a tradition that feels threatened: The honest question to bring to yourself first: "Is my defense of this tradition coming from Scripture, from genuine wisdom about what has worked and why, or from the fact that this tradition is part of how I understand myself and my community?" All three can be present simultaneously. The goal is not to eliminate the third — traditions are legitimately part of identity — but to be honest about how much weight it is being asked to carry.

When someone says "we've always done it this way": "How far back does 'always' go? And do we know why that choice was made? I'm not saying it was wrong — I genuinely want to understand the reasoning. Because if we can articulate the reasoning, we can evaluate whether it still applies here." This is not disrespectful. It is the kind of inquiry that healthy institutions apply to themselves regularly.

⚖️ Going Deeper: Tradition as Gift and Trap — What the History of Interpretation Teaches

Tradition is not the enemy. The Reformation's battle cry — sola scriptura, Scripture alone — has often been misunderstood as a rejection of tradition per se. It was not. It was a claim about the final authority of Scripture over tradition when the two conflict — a position that presupposes tradition exists, has value, and requires engagement. The Reformers were themselves immersed in tradition; they could not have developed their exegesis without Augustine, Chrysostom, and the patristic commentators. What they rejected was tradition as an independent and equal authority alongside Scripture, not tradition as the accumulated wisdom of the church's engagement with Scripture across centuries.

This more nuanced understanding of tradition is worth recovering, because it allows the church to hold two things simultaneously. First: tradition is a gift. The church that ignores two thousand years of faithful engagement with Scripture in favor of reading it fresh every generation will keep rediscovering the same errors, arriving at the same heresies, and solving the same problems that were already solved — at cost — by those who came before. The creeds, the confessions, the great commentators, the sustained theological reflection of the ecumenical councils: these are resources, not obstacles. A community that treats them seriously will read Scripture better than one that treats each Sunday's interpretation as if it were starting from scratch.

Second: tradition is always provisional. The same Reformation that recovered the gospel by engaging tradition also demonstrated that tradition can err — significantly, on first-order matters, for long periods of time. The assumption that "the church has taught X for centuries" is a strong prima facie argument for X, but it is not a conclusive one. Tertullian taught things that were later rejected as heresy. The medieval church's practice of selling indulgences was old enough to seem traditional. The correct response to tradition is neither reverence that cannot question nor suspicion that cannot receive: it is the same posture the Bereans model in Acts 17:11 — receiving what they were taught with eagerness while examining it against Scripture daily.

The practical question for any community: Can we distinguish between what we do because Scripture requires it, what we do because our tradition has found it wise, and what we do because it is what we have always done? That three-part distinction does not dissolve tradition's value. It clarifies what kind of value each element of tradition actually holds — and makes the community capable of reforming itself without mistaking reformation for faithlessness.
