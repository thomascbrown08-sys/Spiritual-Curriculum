Choosing a Bible Translation

What the Differences Are, Why They Matter, and How Much They Matter
Alistair Begg has a phrase worth memorizing: the plain things are the main things. The central claims of Scripture — who God is, what humanity has done, what Christ accomplished, and how we are to respond — are not in dispute between any legitimate translation. This page is about how to choose wisely among the good options, how to spot the ones that aren't good, and why the whole question is both more interesting and less alarming than it first appears.

The Source Manuscript Question: Why Translations Start in Different Places

Before a translator writes a single English word, there is a prior decision: which ancient manuscripts are you translating from? This question — called textual criticism — sounds arcane but has real practical consequences, including why some Bibles have verses that others omit or put in brackets.

For the Old Testament, the primary source is the Masoretic Text — the Hebrew text preserved by Jewish scribes called the Masoretes, standardized around 900 AD but representing a tradition that goes back much further. Almost all modern Old Testament translations use this as their base. Occasionally they consult the Septuagint (the ancient Greek translation made roughly 250 BC, abbreviated LXX) and the Dead Sea Scrolls (discovered 1947, containing manuscripts a thousand years older than previously available Hebrew texts) to check difficult passages. When translations differ in the Old Testament, it is usually because they've weighed these sources differently.

For the New Testament, the situation is more discussed. There are over 5,000 Greek manuscripts of the New Testament — more than any other ancient document by an enormous margin, which is itself a remarkable fact for those interested in textual reliability. They agree on the vast majority of the text. The disagreements cluster in a small number of passages, and scholars have spent centuries developing criteria for sorting them. The main dividing line in modern translation is between two manuscript traditions:

The Textus Receptus ("received text") — assembled primarily in the sixteenth century by Erasmus and refined by subsequent scholars — underlies the King James Version (1611) and the New King James Version (1982). It draws primarily on later Byzantine manuscripts, which are the most numerous but also the most recent.

The Critical Text (the Nestle-Aland and UBS Greek New Testament) — the scholarly consensus text used by most modern translators — is based on earlier manuscripts, particularly the Alexandrian family, including Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus, both dating to roughly the fourth century. Most modern translations (ESV, NIV, CSB, NASB, NLT) use this as their base.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS: Why More Ancient Usually Means More Reliable

Imagine you are trying to reconstruct an original letter by looking at copies made over several centuries. A copy made fifty years after the original is more likely to reflect the original accurately than one made five hundred years later — not because the later copyist was dishonest, but because each generation of copying introduces the possibility of small errors or additions. Textual critics work like detectives: when an earlier manuscript says one thing and a later one says something fuller or smoother, the general principle is that scribes tend to add explanatory material over time more often than they remove it. A shorter or harder reading is often the more original one. This is why scholars generally prefer earlier manuscripts, even though the later Byzantine manuscripts are far more numerous — popularity is not the same as antiquity.

⚖️ Is the KJV-Only Position Defensible?

Some Christians hold that the King James Version alone is the preserved Word of God and that modern translations are corrupt. This position — KJV-Onlyism — is a recent development (mid-twentieth century) with no historical precedent in the Reformation or Puritan traditions it often claims. The Reformers were committed to returning to the best available manuscripts, not to any single translation. Tyndale produced a different text than the Geneva Bible, which differed from the Bishops' Bible, which differed from what became the KJV — and none of the Reformers treated the differences as evidence of corruption.

The argument that the Byzantine text is more reliable than the Alexandrian manuscripts because it is more widely attested confuses quantity with quality. The Byzantine manuscripts are numerous because they were copied extensively in the medieval Eastern church. The Alexandrian manuscripts are fewer but older. The presence of early papyri (some dating to the second and third centuries) that align with the Alexandrian tradition is significant evidence that its readings are early, not late corruptions.

None of this makes the KJV a bad translation — it is a monument of the English language and doctrinally sound. It makes KJV-Onlyism an untenable position that the text-critical evidence does not support. The page's flag system below treats the KJV as a green-flag translation while noting the relevant caveats.

Translation Philosophy: The Spectrum from Word-for-Word to Thought-for-Thought

Even when two translators are working from identical source manuscripts, they will produce different English if they hold different philosophies about what translation is for. This is the second major source of variation among translations — and unlike the manuscript question, it has no single right answer. It is a genuine trade-off.

The spectrum runs from formal equivalence at one end to dynamic equivalence at the other, with paraphrase beyond that.

Formal equivalence (word-for-word, as closely as English allows) tries to preserve the structure, vocabulary, and even the word order of the original language. The goal is to give readers access to the actual words of the text and to let them do their own interpretive work. The trade-off: Greek and Hebrew sentence structures are often quite different from English, and a formally equivalent rendering can sound stilted or be harder to understand. Representative translations: NASB (most formally equivalent of major translations), ESV, KJV, NKJV, NRSV.

Dynamic equivalence (thought-for-thought) tries to render the meaning of a passage in natural contemporary English rather than tracking the original word order. The goal is readability and comprehension. The trade-off: every act of rendering meaning requires interpretation, and the translator's theological or cultural assumptions shape the result. What reads most naturally is not always most faithful to what the original text actually says. Representative translations: NIV, CSB, NLT.

Paraphrase moves furthest from the original words and most freely into the translator's own idiom. The goal is maximum accessibility and contemporary feel. The trade-off: at this distance from the source, the translator's interpretation is fully embedded in the text — a paraphrase is as much a commentary as a translation. Examples: The Message (Eugene Peterson), The Living Bible (Kenneth Taylor). These can be genuinely illuminating alongside a literal translation, but they should not be used as primary study Bibles.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS: A Translation Is Like a Map

Different maps of the same territory serve different purposes. A topographic map gives you the most precise elevation data but is harder to read quickly. A road map is easier to navigate but loses the terrain detail. A tourist map is the most accessible of all but tells you almost nothing about the actual geography. None of them is the territory itself — they are all representations, and which one is "best" depends on what you are trying to do. A formally equivalent translation is like a topographic map: more work to read, more faithful to the original terrain. A dynamic translation is more like a road map: easier to follow, but with some simplification. A paraphrase is the tourist map: useful as an introduction, misleading as a substitute for the real thing.

The Difficulty of Translation: When Language Refuses to Cooperate

Anyone who has learned a second language knows the experience of encountering a word or phrase that simply has no equivalent in their native tongue. Every language carves up reality differently. Greek makes distinctions English does not. Hebrew has categories English lacks. The process of bridging them involves judgment calls at every step — and sometimes the most honest rendering is a note in the margin admitting that the original does several things at once that English cannot do simultaneously.

Greek tense and aspect. Greek has a tense system significantly richer than English. The aorist tense (roughly, a simple past action) and the perfect tense (a past action with ongoing present results) are often difficult to distinguish in English, and the distinction matters theologically. When Paul writes in Romans 8:1 that "there is therefore now no condemnation" — the "is" translates a present tense built on a verdict that was pronounced definitively in the past. It is not a temporary state; it is a settled reality. When Hebrews 11:1 describes faith as "the substance of things hoped for" (KJV), the word translated "substance" is hypostasis — which can mean substance, foundation, confidence, or assurance. The ESV renders it "assurance," the NASB "certainty," the KJV "substance." Each captures something; none captures everything.

Hebrew wordplay. Hebrew poetry is built on patterns that are simply invisible in translation. Psalm 22:3 says God is "enthroned on the praises of Israel" — but the Hebrew can also be read as "you who inhabit the praises of Israel" or even "you who sit in the holy place of Israel's praises." The ambiguity is part of the text's richness. The translator has to pick one — and in picking, inevitably loses the others.

A single word with multiple resonances. The Greek word monogenēs, used of Jesus in John 1:14 and 3:16, was translated "only begotten" in older versions and "one and only" or "unique" in some modern ones. The older rendering draws on the Nicene Creed's language ("begotten not made"). The newer rendering tries to capture the idea of singular uniqueness. Both are legitimate translations of a word that carries the weight of both ideas. Neither is wrong; neither is complete.

An entire sentence in dispute: Romans 5:12. Paul writes that death spread to all people "because all sinned" — or possibly "in whom all sinned," referring to Adam. The Greek phrase eph' hō is genuinely contested. The Latin Vulgate translated it "in quo" ("in whom"), which supported the Western doctrine of original sin as guilt inherited through Adam. Modern translations render it "because" (ESV, NIV, NASB), following what most Greek scholars consider the more natural reading. Both carry real theological weight. The doctrine of original sin does not stand or fall on this single phrase — it is supported throughout Romans 5 — but the phrase itself illustrates how a two-letter Greek preposition can occupy a footnote in systematic theology.

When One Text Means Several Things — and Both Are True

One of the most remarkable features of the Old Testament is the way it operates at multiple levels simultaneously. A passage can speak directly to its immediate historical situation and also — not instead, but also — point toward a larger future fulfillment. The New Testament authors read it this way, and they were reading their own Scripture. This is not a Christian imposition on Hebrew texts; it is a feature of the texts themselves.

Isaiah 7:14. Isaiah tells King Ahaz that a young woman who is pregnant will bear a son called Immanuel as a sign of deliverance within the king's lifetime. The Hebrew word almah means "young woman" — it does not technically require virginity, though it typically implies it in context. The immediate historical meaning is clear: a child to be born soon, in Ahaz's time, as a sign. When Matthew cites this verse for the virgin birth (Matthew 1:23), he is using the Greek Septuagint, which translated almah as parthenos ("virgin"), and he sees in it a deeper fulfillment in Jesus. These are not two competing interpretations — one right, one wrong. They are two layers of a text that was always carrying more than its immediate historical context. The sign given to Ahaz pointed, in the fullness of time, to the sign given to the world.

Hosea 11:1. "Out of Egypt I called my son" — Hosea is looking back at the Exodus, describing Israel as God's son rescued from slavery. Matthew 2:15 quotes this verse as fulfilled in Jesus's return from Egypt after the flight to avoid Herod. Is Matthew misquoting Hosea? No — he is reading Hosea typologically: Israel was God's son, the Exodus was God's act of rescue, and in Jesus both "son" and "rescue from Egypt" find their deepest meaning. The original meaning is not cancelled; it is fulfilled in a deeper key.

Psalm 22. David's cry — "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" — is the prayer of a man in acute personal suffering. The psalm describes his experience in visceral detail: mocked, surrounded by enemies, bones out of joint. It was a real prayer from a real person in real distress. It is also the psalm Jesus quoted from the cross. When Jesus said those words, he was not just expressing agony — he was consciously entering the pattern of the psalm, identifying himself with the one who suffers to ultimate abandonment and is then vindicated. The psalm ends in triumph and worship, not because David's suffering was somehow not real, but because it was always also pointing somewhere. Translator's note: no English version can capture the way this psalm resonates across centuries without a footnote; the resonance is structural, not linguistic.

⚖️ Typology Is Not Allegory

The multi-layered reading illustrated above is called typology: the identification of historical persons, events, and institutions in the Old Testament that prefigure and find their fulfillment in Christ. It differs from allegory, which finds hidden spiritual meanings behind the plain historical text by treating the historical as merely a code for something else. Typology respects the historical reality of both layers — David really suffered in Psalm 22; that suffering also really points to Christ's — while allegory tends to evacuate the historical of its importance. The Reformed tradition has consistently preferred typology over allegory as the hermeneutical framework for reading the Old Testament's relationship to the New. The church fathers who leaned heavily allegorical (particularly Origen and the Alexandrian school) often read Scripture in ways that disconnected it from its historical grounding — which is precisely why hermeneutical method matters.

Missing Verses, Brackets, and the Doxology

If you grew up reading the King James Bible and then pick up an ESV or NIV, you will notice some familiar passages in brackets or footnotes, or missing entirely. This is not revisionism. It is the honest result of having access to earlier manuscripts that the KJV translators did not have.

Mark 16:9–20 — the "Longer Ending." The oldest and most reliable manuscripts of Mark end at 16:8, with the women fleeing from the empty tomb in fear and saying nothing to anyone. The longer ending (verses 9–20), which includes the commission to handle serpents and drink poison, appears to have been added by a later scribe to provide a more satisfying conclusion. It is not in Codex Sinaiticus or Codex Vaticanus. Modern translations typically include it with a note explaining the manuscript situation. This is not a judgment that the material is false — it is a judgment that it was not original to Mark. The essential resurrection account ends at verse 8; the longer ending does not change any doctrine that is not attested elsewhere.

John 7:53–8:11 — the woman caught in adultery. This passage — "let him who is without sin cast the first stone" — is one of the most beloved in the New Testament. It also does not appear in the oldest manuscripts, appears in different locations in different manuscripts, and has a style that differs from John's. It was almost certainly not original to John's Gospel, though it may well preserve a genuine tradition about Jesus. Again: modern translations bracket it rather than silently omit it, and the note explains why. The story is consistent with everything else Scripture teaches about Jesus; its manuscript status is a separate question.

The Comma Johanneum — 1 John 5:7–8. The KJV includes: "For there are three that bear record in heaven, the Father, the Word, and the Holy Ghost: and these three are one." This is the most explicit Trinitarian formula in the entire Bible — and it is not in any Greek manuscript before the sixteenth century. It appears to have been a marginal note that crept into the Latin text and was then inserted into Erasmus's Greek edition under pressure. Modern translations omit it. The Trinity does not depend on this passage; it is established throughout the New Testament without it. But the Comma Johanneum's absence from the earliest manuscripts is not in serious scholarly dispute.

The Lord's Prayer Doxology — Matthew 6:13. "For thine is the kingdom, and the power, and the glory, forever. Amen." This closing line of the Lord's Prayer — familiar from centuries of liturgical use — is absent from the oldest manuscripts of Matthew. It appears to have been added in the early church, probably drawn from 1 Chronicles 29:11, as a liturgical conclusion for corporate prayer. It appears in the Didache (a first or early second century church manual), which suggests it entered worship practice very early even if it was not original to Matthew's Gospel. Modern translations typically place it in a footnote; the KJV and NKJV include it in the text. Liturgically it is ancient; textually it is an addition. The two facts are not incompatible. It is theologically sound and consistent with everything else Scripture says about God. Whether you say it when you pray the Lord's Prayer is a matter of tradition, not doctrine.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS: Should These Passages Worry Me?

No. Here is why: not one of the passages listed above establishes a doctrine that does not appear elsewhere in Scripture. The resurrection is not in question if Mark 16:8 is the original ending — the empty tomb and the appearances are attested in Matthew, Luke, John, and Paul's letter to the Corinthians. The Trinity is not weakened if the Comma Johanneum is a later addition — Jesus's divine nature and the distinct personhood of Father, Son, and Spirit are established throughout John, Paul, and the rest of the New Testament. The textual questions are genuinely interesting. They do not threaten the substance of what Christians believe. The plain things remain plain regardless of how the contested passages resolve.

A Flag System for Translations

What follows is a practical guide to common translations, using a three-color system: 🔴 red flags indicate translations that contain heresy-level distortions of the text; 🟡 yellow flags indicate translations that should be used with awareness of specific issues; 🟢 no flag indicates translations that are reliable tools in the hands of any reader, whatever their preferences. The goal is not to tell you which translation you should read — it is to give you the information you need to choose wisely.

🔴 Red Flag Translations — Heresy-Level Distortions

The New World Translation (NWT) — produced by the Jehovah's Witnesses, this translation alters key Christological passages to support their non-Trinitarian theology. Most notably: John 1:1 is rendered "the Word was a god" rather than "the Word was God" — a translation not supported by any mainstream Greek scholarship, which reads the anarthrous predicate nominative in a qualitative rather than an indefinite sense. The NWT was not produced by scholars trained in Greek and Hebrew. It is a document of a particular theological agenda, not a translation of the text. Do not use it as a Bible.

The Passion Translation (TPT) — marketed as a fresh translation, it is more accurately described as a theologically loaded paraphrase that adds content not present in any manuscript. Its treatment of the Song of Solomon, for instance, imports charismatic theological concepts into the text without manuscript support. Multiple scholars of Greek have documented specific passages where the TPT's renderings have no basis in the original language. The translator himself has acknowledged that his goal was to "unlock the passion of God's heart" — a goal that produced a document that reflects his interpretation rather than the text. Use it only as devotional commentary, never as a Bible for study.

🟡 Yellow Flag Translations — Use With Awareness

The Message (MSG) — Eugene Peterson's paraphrase is beautifully written and can illuminate familiar passages with fresh eyes. It is also, unambiguously, a paraphrase: Peterson's theological sensibility is embedded in every sentence. Some passages are rendered in ways that soften doctrinal content — propitiation becomes "atonement," specific theological terms are smoothed into more general language. Used alongside a literal translation, it can be genuinely helpful. Used as a primary Bible for study, it will quietly replace the text's categories with Peterson's. Valuable as a companion; inadequate as a primary text.

The Living Bible (TLB) — an older paraphrase by Kenneth Taylor, now largely superseded by the NLT (which is a proper translation, not a paraphrase, produced by a team of scholars). Some editions of the TLB contain dated language and theological imprecisions from the 1970s. Largely of historical interest; the NLT is the better choice for those wanting accessible language.

The New Revised Standard Version (NRSV) — a formally equivalent, scholarly translation with an important caveat: its gender-inclusive language choices occasionally smooth over distinctions in the original text. Specific passages where the Greek uses a masculine generic are rendered in gender-neutral terms, which can create ambiguity about whether the original is making a specific or general claim. It remains a rigorous translation used widely in academic settings. Reliable for study; note the gender-language choices when they occur.

King James Version (KJV) for new readers — the KJV is a doctrinal and literary masterpiece. For new readers of the Bible in the twenty-first century, however, its 1611 English presents a genuine comprehension barrier. Words that once meant one thing now mean another ("prevent" once meant "go before"; "let" once meant "hinder"). The yellow flag here is not theological but practical: if you will not actually read and understand it, a translation you can read is better than a monument you admire from a distance. For experienced readers, the KJV remains excellent.

🟢 Reliable Translations — Organized by Philosophy

Most formally equivalent (word-for-word):

NASB (New American Standard Bible, 2020 update) — the most formally equivalent of widely-used translations. Excellent for careful study where you want to stay closest to the original language. Can read stiffly in places. The 2020 update improves readability while maintaining rigor.

ESV (English Standard Version, 2016) — a slight step toward readability from the NASB while remaining formally equivalent. Wide scholarly respect; used widely in Reformed and evangelical settings. This curriculum's primary reference translation.

KJV / NKJV — the King James tradition. The NKJV updates the KJV's language while retaining its essentially Textus Receptus base. Doctrinally sound; the manuscript question is a nuance, not a crisis.

Balanced translations (between word-for-word and thought-for-thought):

NIV (New International Version, 2011) — the most widely read English translation. Readable, reliable, produced by a large committee of scholars. Some gender-language choices parallel the NRSV; the 2011 update addressed earlier controversies. An excellent primary Bible for most readers.

CSB (Christian Standard Bible, 2017) — aims explicitly at "optimal equivalence," balancing formal and dynamic. Fresh, clear, produced by a capable team. Particularly useful for newer readers who want accuracy with readability.

Most dynamically equivalent (thought-for-thought):

NLT (New Living Translation, 2015) — produced by a team of ninety scholars, not a single translator. Far more reliable than its predecessor the Living Bible. Excellent for new readers or those working through longer narrative sections where comprehension matters most. Some technical theological terms are rendered in ways that require supplemental study.

A practical recommendation for serious study: read a formally equivalent translation as your primary text (NASB, ESV, or NKJV) and keep a readable translation nearby (NIV, CSB, or NLT) for passages where comprehension is a barrier. When in doubt about what a passage means, compare two or three translations — the places where they diverge are often the places where the Greek or Hebrew is genuinely complex, and noticing the divergence is itself a form of textual discovery.

The Plain Things Are the Main Things

After all of the above — manuscripts, philosophy, disputed passages, missing verses, difficult words — it is worth returning to where this page began. Alistair Begg's phrase is both a comfort and a corrective: the plain things are the main things.

Every reliable translation — whether ESV or NIV, whether from the Critical Text or the Textus Receptus, whether formally equivalent or dynamically readable — communicates the same essential truths. God made the world and called it good. Humanity has turned away from God and cannot return by its own effort. Jesus Christ is the eternal Son of God who became fully human, lived without sin, died in the place of sinners, rose bodily from the dead, and will come again. Salvation is by grace through faith in Christ alone. The Spirit indwells believers and is forming them into the image of Christ. The new creation is coming.

None of these truths is in dispute between the NASB and the NIV. None depends on whether the Comma Johanneum is original or whether the longer ending of Mark is authentic. None is threatened by the translation of hypostasis or the rendering of almah. The peripheral questions are genuinely interesting and worth engaging. They should not become the reason someone does not read the Bible they own, or spends more time worrying about which translation is best than actually reading the one they have.

The Bible you will actually read is better than the perfect Bible you are still choosing. Pick up the one in your hand. Open it. And before you do: ask God to do what your method cannot — to correct the errors you will make despite your best efforts, and to bring the truth home in his own time and way. The plain things are waiting, and so is the One who inspired them.

📚 For Further Study — Bible Translation and Textual Criticism
  • Bruce Metzger & Bart Ehrman, The Text of the New Testament (Oxford, 4th ed., 2005) — The standard introductory textbook on New Testament textual criticism. Metzger was a committed Christian; Ehrman has since become a prominent skeptic. The collaboration produces a remarkably balanced account. Demanding but authoritative.
  • Bruce Metzger, A Textual Commentary on the Greek New Testament (UBS, 2nd ed., 1994) — Passage-by-passage explanation of why the critical text makes the decisions it does. Invaluable for understanding specific disputed passages.
  • Leland Ryken, The Word of God in English (Crossway, 2002) — An argument for formal equivalence by a literary scholar. Makes the case that how the Bible is translated matters aesthetically and theologically.
  • Gordon Fee & Mark Strauss, How to Choose a Bible Translation for All Its Worth (Zondervan, 2007) — A companion to the hermeneutics volume. Comprehensive, fair, and aimed at the general reader.
  • BibleGateway.com — Free access to dozens of translations side by side. The single most practical tool for comparing renderings of any passage.
  • BibleHub Interlinear — For those who want to see the Greek and Hebrew beneath any passage, with word-by-word analysis. No language knowledge required to use it productively.
