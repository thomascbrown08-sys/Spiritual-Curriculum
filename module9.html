Living It Out

Faith That Moves the Hands, the Feet, and the Whole Person
Module 9 is the destination of the whole journey. Every prior module has been asking "is this worth my life?" This one asks "what does a life shaped by this actually look like?" It is not a moral summons to try harder — it is a description of what becomes possible when roots grow deep, held honestly alongside an acknowledgment of what it costs and who it is for. This module especially receives what Modules 6 and 7 sent toward it: the community that heals, and the growth that comes through weakness rather than achievement.

The Sermon on the Mount: A Description and a Direction

Matthew 5:3–6

"Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven. Blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted. Blessed are the meek, for they shall inherit the earth. Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they shall be satisfied."

The Beatitudes are not commands to manufacture poverty of spirit, or to perform mourning, or to achieve meekness through willpower. They are descriptions of a kind of person — one who has begun to see reality as God sees it. In the Christian account, this vision is given by the Spirit, not conjured by effort. The Sermon on the Mount describes the shape of a life that has been genuinely transformed by encounter with the Triune God — a life that begins to look, in its texture and direction, like the life of the One who gave the sermon.

But description and direction are not opposites. The Spirit gives this character as gift, and also invites our willing movement toward it. The Beatitudes describe what becomes true of those who walk closely with Christ — and they are therefore a compass, orienting us toward what we are being made into, without demanding that we pretend to have arrived. We are simultaneously becoming and not yet. Both are true at once.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

The Beatitudes are the opening statements of Jesus' most famous teaching, the Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5–7). The word "beatitude" comes from the Latin for "blessed" or "happy." Each one follows the same structure: "Blessed are the [type of person], for they will [receive something]." What is striking about them is who Jesus calls blessed: the poor in spirit, the mourning, the meek, the hungry. These are not the people any culture — then or now — instinctively admires. Jesus is describing a completely different picture of what a flourishing human life looks like: one where vulnerability, honesty about need, and hunger for God's justice are the marks of people who are actually living the way humans were designed to live. The point this module is making is that these are not requirements to fulfill before God will accept you — they are descriptions of what happens to a person who is being transformed from the inside by the Spirit. You do not achieve poverty of spirit as a spiritual exercise. You discover it as the Spirit opens your eyes to how much you need God. The description is also a direction — something to move toward, not a standard to feel crushed by.

Homecoming and Cost: Both Are True

There is a genuine sense in which obedience to Christ is a homecoming — a return to the kind of life human beings were made for, a movement toward the grain of the universe rather than against it. When we forgive, when we love our enemies, when we tell the truth at cost to ourselves, there is often a deep rightness to it that self-serving choices never produce.

But Bonhoeffer — whose work this curriculum draws on — warns with equal force against making the Christian life sound too much like a comfortable return. He calls such teaching cheap grace: grace without cost, discipleship without the cross, transformation without the death of what needs to die. Jesus says: "Take up your cross daily and follow me." The martyrs did not experience obedience as a pleasant homecoming in any comfortable sense. Obedience is both a homecoming and costly — and the cost is not evidence that something has gone wrong. It is part of what makes it real. Bonhoeffer's own summary of the matter, in The Cost of Discipleship, is worth sitting with: when Christ calls a person, he calls them to come and follow — which means leaving where they were and going somewhere they could not have gone alone. That is both the invitation and its cost.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

Dietrich Bonhoeffer was a German pastor and theologian who was executed by the Nazi regime in 1945 for his involvement in resistance efforts. He is not a distant historical figure offering comfortable theology from a safe distance — he wrote about the cost of following Christ and then paid that cost with his life. His distinction between cheap grace and costly grace is one of the most important ideas in modern Christian thought. Cheap grace says: "God forgives, so it doesn't really matter how I live." It takes the gift and ignores the giver. Costly grace says: "God has given everything — the life and death of his Son — and that gift is so valuable that it changes what I live for." Costly grace is not about earning God's favor through suffering. It is about the recognition that real love — the kind the cross represents — always involves giving something up. The Christian life is genuinely a homecoming, a return to what you were made for. And it genuinely costs something, because the things you were holding onto in place of God do not let go easily. Both of those things are true at the same time.

And yet the Westminster Shorter Catechism, asked to name the single chief purpose of human existence, answers: "to glorify God, and to enjoy him forever." Not to endure him. Not merely to serve him. To enjoy him forever — a phrase with its weight on the word forever as much as on the word enjoy. The cost is real and the cross is real. And they are not the last word. The life on the other side of the cross — the life toward which the whole curriculum has been pointing — is one in which the satisfaction we were made for is not a distant hope but an ever-deepening reality. Edwards described it as "rising higher and higher through infinite duration" — not a static arrival but an endless, increasing joy. The homecoming is not a destination. It is the beginning of a journey into a God who is, as Augustine said, our rest.

The Church as Healing Body

Module 6 established something that must not be left behind at the door of Module 9: Christian community is not a resource the church offers to suffering people — it is the environment in which God heals them. This is 1 Corinthians 12's claim about what the body of Christ actually is: "If one member suffers, all suffer together." That is not a sentiment. It is a description of organic life. A body whose members do not feel one another's pain is not more spiritually advanced — it is less alive.

What this looks like in practice is more specific than most community language tends to be. For someone whose capacity for spiritual reception has been damaged — by trauma, by church hurt, by prolonged suffering — purely propositional ministry (however theologically careful) often cannot reach them where they live. What the body can offer instead is something different in kind: sustained presence without pressure to perform recovery; bearing the theological weight on behalf of those who cannot carry it themselves in a given season; long-term companionship through darkness that does not demand resolution on a schedule. This is not an alternative to the gospel. It is the gospel enacted in flesh — the Word becoming present in the community that bears one another's burdens (Galatians 6:2) until the individual can bear their own again.

Practically, this means a community that is not made uncomfortable by protracted grief, that does not interpret unresolved suffering as a failure of faith, that does not rush toward "and then I found peace" before the person in front of them has found it. It means sitting in Psalm 88's darkness with someone, week after week, without insisting that they turn the page. The healing is not the church's to produce. But the conditions for healing — safety, constancy, honest witness, unhurried presence — are the church's to provide.

🧭 When the Group Contains Someone Still in Module 6

A group that has traveled through nine sessions together will almost certainly contain someone who has not moved from suffering into resolution — who is still in Psalm 88's territory at the end of Module 9. This is not a failure of the curriculum or of the facilitator. It is the normal range of human experience.

The most important pastoral move in this session is to make room explicitly for that person — not by singling them out, but by naming in the group discussion that Module 9's vision of community is precisely for people who are not yet through. The community described in 1 Corinthians 12 is not a community of people who have all arrived. It is a community of people at different points in the journey, bearing one another toward where none of them could go alone. That framing keeps the landing pad genuinely open.

Close this final session without forcing closure. Some of the group's most important work may happen in the weeks after the last session, carried by what was said in the room.

The Church: The Visible Shape of Trinitarian Love

The curriculum began with the Triune God — the eternal fellowship of Father, Son, and Spirit, self-giving love overflowing from before the foundation of the world. It ends with the community that is meant to make that love visible.

The church at its best is not an institution. It is the body of Christ — the community of people in whom the Spirit of the risen Christ is at work, shaping them individually and together toward the image of Christ. Romans 12 fills in the practical texture: genuine love that does not perform; patient endurance; blessing those who persecute you; weeping with those who weep; refusing to repay evil for evil; living peaceably as far as it depends on you. These are not natural to fallen human beings — which is precisely why their consistent presence in a community is itself a form of evidence for the transformation that faith produces.

Module 7's counterintuitive signposts of growth — rapid return after failure, less defensiveness, honest acknowledgment of sin without self-justification, increasing compassion for others' failures — are not only individual virtues. They become community culture. A group of people who have collectively internalized that grace is real and ongoing begins to look different from the outside: not a community of those who have mastered the Christian life, but a community of those who keep returning to the fountain, who are not scandalized by one another's weakness, who bear each other's burdens as a matter of course because they know their own weight. This is the visible shape of Trinitarian love — not a performance of virtue, but the overflow of people who have been genuinely met by grace.

John 13:34–35

"A new commandment I give to you, that you love one another: just as I have loved you, you also are to love one another. By this all people will know that you are my disciples, if you have love for one another."

The love between members of the Christian community is not optional, not decorative, not one feature among others. It is the sign — the thing by which the world is supposed to recognize the disciples of Jesus. It is Trinitarian love — the self-giving love of Father, Son, and Spirit — reproduced in human community by the Spirit's work. When the church embodies this, it becomes a living argument for the reality of the God it worships. When it fails to embody it, it becomes an obstacle to faith. Both are true, and the curriculum has been honest about both.

🌍 Romans 12 Community Across Cultures

Romans 12's vision of communal life — weeping with those who weep, bearing one another's burdens, refusing to repay evil for evil — reads differently depending on the cultural lens you bring to it.

In Western individualist contexts, the natural reading is personal: "I will choose to practice these virtues in my own conduct." This is not wrong, but it is thinner than what Paul is describing. He is not writing a personal ethics checklist. He is describing what a community becomes — its shared character, its collective identity, the way it is known from outside.

In high-context, honor-shame, or collectivist cultures, this passage often lands with greater force precisely because the communal frame is already present: the shame of one member is the community's shame; the restoration of one member is everyone's celebration; the obligation to bear one another's burdens is not a voluntary add-on but the basic structure of belonging. This reading does not water down the cost of Romans 12 — it deepens it, because the community's identity is genuinely at stake in how it treats its weakest members.

For groups from diverse cultural backgrounds, naming this explicitly — that Paul's vision is collective by design, not accidentally — often opens the passage in ways that purely individual application does not.

The Embodied Life: Worship That Engages the Whole Person

There is a dimension of "living it out" that neither moral reflection nor community life alone can fully address: the embodied, gathered practice of worship. Faith is not only an intellectual orientation toward true propositions, and it is not only a communal commitment to love one another. It is also — and this matters more than the Western theological tradition has sometimes acknowledged — something that happens in the body.

Scripture, prayer, sung praise, the Lord's Supper: these are not merely cognitive inputs. They are means of grace that engage the whole person — memory, imagination, emotion, sensation, social belonging — not only the rational mind. What recent work on trauma and embodied formation has begun to clarify is something the church has practiced for centuries without always theorizing: that gathered worship creates pathways for encounter with God that purely propositional engagement cannot replicate. A person whose capacity for intellectual reception of spiritual truth has been damaged by suffering or loss may still be reached through a psalm sung together in the dark, through the physical act of communion, through the voice of a community speaking the ancient words over them. The Spirit moves through the whole person, not only through the understanding.

This is not an argument for a particular style of worship. It is an argument for the fullness of it — for gathering practices that do not reduce the encounter with God to the transmission of correct information, but that engage the whole person God made and is remaking. The arts, embodied ritual, communal song, and the deliberate use of silence are not decorative additions to a fully cognitive service. They are the church doing what it has always known how to do: making a space in which the whole human being — not only the mind — can be met by the living God.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

The phrase means of grace is an old theological term for specific practices through which God has promised to meet his people — things like Scripture reading, prayer, communion (the Lord's Supper), baptism, and gathered worship. They are called "means" because they are the channels, not the source — God himself is the source; these are the pathways he has chosen to use. The point of this section is that these practices are not just ways to get information about God into your head. They engage the whole person — your body, your emotions, your memory, your sense of belonging. Think of the difference between reading the words "you are forgiven" in a book and hearing someone you trust say those words directly to you, looking at you, with their hand on your shoulder. The content is the same. The experience is completely different. That difference is what embodied worship creates: it gives God's truth a pathway into parts of you that reading alone cannot reach. The Lord's Supper (also called communion or the Eucharist) — the bread and wine that Christians share in remembrance of Christ's body and blood — is the clearest example: it is theology you can taste. It engages the body in the truth the mind already knows.

⚖️ Formation vs. Encounter: Holding Both

There are two streams of thinking about what worship and embodied practice do. One emphasizes formation: repeated practices shape us over time, reordering our loves and habits beneath the level of conscious decision (James K.A. Smith's argument in You Are What You Love). The other emphasizes encounter: worship is not primarily self-improvement but meeting with the living God, and that encounter can happen suddenly and without the expected preparation.

The Christian tradition holds both. Willard's disciplines (Module 8) and Smith's liturgical formation make the same basic point: the body and its habits matter to spiritual life. And the tradition also insists that the Spirit is not constrained by our formation — he meets people in burning bushes and prison cells and moments of profound darkness when no practice has prepared them for it.

The practical wisdom is: cultivate the practices that create conditions for encounter, without treating those conditions as controls on the Spirit. We prepare the ground. God sends the rain when he wills.

🕊️ For Those Returning After a Long Absence

If this curriculum has awakened something — or re-awakened something — the question of where to go next is real and sometimes frightening. The church that hurt you may still exist in your memory as the primary reference point for what Christian community looks like. It is not the only reference point. Communities exist that practice genuine welcome, genuine honesty, genuine patience with doubt. They are worth looking for.

The Prodigal Son returned to his father's house — not to a different household that happened to look similar. But the house he returned to was the one characterized by a father who runs, who restores, who feasts. That is what you are looking for. You have the right to look, and to decline to return to communities that practice something else.

If you are unsure where to begin: look for a community where the weak are genuinely welcomed, where doubt is tolerated rather than policed, where leadership is accountable to the people, and where the broken-cistern experience is acknowledged rather than suppressed. Look for a community that can sit with Psalm 88 without insisting that everyone reach Psalm 103 on the same schedule. Start there.

And if you are not ready to walk through the door of a gathered community yet — that is also acknowledged here. The Spirit who has brought you this far is not done. The invitation remains open. There is no deadline.

A Final Word

The inside-out sequence of this curriculum is itself a small demonstration of John 7:17. It began with you — with your experience, your longing, your honest sense of the gap between who you are and who you want to be — because that is where the gospel finds people. Not with a syllabus of propositions to be evaluated, but with a recognition: someone has described you accurately, and they are offering a way forward.

By the time a group reaches the evidential material of Modules 3 and 4, the question "can I trust this?" is no longer academic. It is personal. And personal questions receive personal answers — not only arguments, but encounters. That shift — from detached evaluation to personal engagement — is itself the beginning of the movement John 7:17 describes.

The hope is that by the end of this curriculum, each person will have found at least one place where Scripture spoke more truthfully to their experience than they expected, at least one place where the historical reliability of the Bible surprised them, and at least one place where the direction John 7:17 points began to seem not like an evasion of the question but like a genuine answer to it. And — if they have been willing to stay with Module 6 — at least one place where the God of this curriculum sat with them in darkness rather than offering them a premature light.

The cost is real and the homecoming is real. The cross is the shape of the Christian life not because God is indifferent to our flourishing, but because it is the shape of love in a world where love costs something. And on the far side of the cost, there is a life that no amount of self-protection could have given us — the life the Catechism names with quiet precision: to glorify God, and to enjoy him forever. The invitation is open. It has always been open. It is open now — including for those who end this curriculum still in the dark, still carrying the unanswered question, still not yet through. The father who ran is not watching from a fixed point. He is still moving toward you.

Discussion Questions
  • What is one moment from these nine sessions that will stay with you — a passage, an insight, a question you didn't expect? Name it specifically.
  • Where do you find yourself at the end of this curriculum — further in, further out, or somewhere you didn't expect to be? Be honest rather than tidy.
  • The community described in 1 Corinthians 12 bears what individuals cannot bear alone. Is there someone in your life you are currently bearing — or someone who is bearing you? What does that look like?
  • What does the community you want to belong to actually need to be like — not ideally, but practically, for you specifically? What one thing would need to be true of it?
  • What is the first concrete thing you will do differently — or continue doing — as a result of having done this together?
📚 Deep Dive — Module 9

Dietrich Bonhoeffer, The Cost of Discipleship — The classic account of what genuine discipleship costs and why cheap grace is a betrayal of the gospel. Read carefully; Bonhoeffer is demanding and rewarding in equal measure.

James K.A. Smith, You Are What You Love — On how embodied habits and gathered practices form our desires beneath the level of conscious decision; a contemporary articulation of why liturgy and worship matter for the whole person, not only the intellect. A natural companion to Willard (Module 8) and an accessible entry to Smith's larger argument.

Miroslav Volf, Exclusion and Embrace — On the practical meaning of love, forgiveness, and reconciliation in a world where people genuinely harm one another. Some of the most important Christian social ethics of the last thirty years.

Lesslie Newbigin, The Gospel in a Pluralist Society — On what it means to be the church as a sign, foretaste, and instrument of God's kingdom in a culture that has largely moved past it. Chapters 18–20 are especially relevant to Module 9's vision of community as witness.

C.S. Lewis, The Weight of Glory — Particularly "The Weight of Glory" and "Membership": on the glory of ordinary people, the cost and delight of taking them seriously, and what genuine Christian community makes possible.

Beth Argot, "Restoring the Body: Knowing God Fully through Whole-Brain Worship" (DTS Chapel, 2025) — On how embodied, whole-person worship creates pathways for healing and encounter that purely propositional engagement cannot. Available through Dallas Theological Seminary's chapel archive. Pairs naturally with the embodied-life section of this module.
