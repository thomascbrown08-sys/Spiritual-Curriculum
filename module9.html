Living It Out

Faith That Moves the Hands and Feet
Module 9 is the destination of the whole journey. Every prior module has been asking "is this worth my life?" This one asks "what does a life shaped by this actually look like?" It is not a moral summons to try harder — it is a description of what becomes natural when roots grow deep, held honestly alongside an acknowledgment of what it costs.

The Sermon on the Mount: A Description, Not a Ladder

Matthew 5:3–6

"Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven. Blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted. Blessed are the meek, for they shall inherit the earth. Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they shall be satisfied."

The Beatitudes are not commands to try harder to be poor in spirit, or to manufacture mourning, or to become meek. They are descriptions of a kind of person — one who has begun to see reality as God sees it. The vision is given by the Spirit, not manufactured by willpower. The Sermon on the Mount describes the shape of a life that has been genuinely transformed by encounter with the Triune God — a life that begins to look, in its texture and direction, like the life of the One who gave the sermon.

Homecoming and Cost: Both Are True

There is a genuine sense in which obedience to Christ is a homecoming — a return to the kind of life human beings were made for, a movement toward the grain of the universe rather than against it. When we forgive, when we love our enemies, when we tell the truth at cost to ourselves, there is often a deep rightness to it that self-serving choices never produce.

But Bonhoeffer — whose work this curriculum draws on — warns with equal force against making the Christian life sound too much like a comfortable return. He calls such teaching cheap grace: grace without cost, discipleship without the cross, transformation without the death of what needs to die. Jesus says: "Take up your cross daily and follow me." The martyrs did not experience obedience as a pleasant homecoming in any comfortable sense. Obedience is both a homecoming and costly — and the cost is not evidence that something has gone wrong. It is part of what makes it real. Bonhoeffer's own summary of the matter, in The Cost of Discipleship, is worth sitting with: when Christ calls a person, he calls them to come and follow — which means leaving where they were and going somewhere they could not have gone alone. That is both the invitation and its cost.

The Church: The Visible Shape of Trinitarian Love

The curriculum began with the Triune God — the eternal fellowship of Father, Son, and Spirit, self-giving love overflowing from before the foundation of the world. It ends with the community that is meant to make that love visible.

The church at its best is not an institution. It is the body of Christ — the community of people in whom the Spirit of the risen Christ is at work, shaping them individually and together toward the image of Christ. Romans 12 fills in the practical texture: genuine love that does not perform; patient endurance; blessing those who persecute you; weeping with those who weep; refusing to repay evil for evil; living peaceably as far as it depends on you. These are not natural to fallen human beings — which is precisely why their consistent presence in a community is itself a form of evidence for the transformation that faith produces.

John 13:34–35

"A new commandment I give to you, that you love one another: just as I have loved you, you also are to love one another. By this all people will know that you are my disciples, if you have love for one another."

The love between members of the Christian community is not optional, not decorative, not one feature among others. It is the sign — the thing by which the world is supposed to recognize the disciples of Jesus. It is Trinitarian love — the self-giving love of Father, Son, and Spirit — reproduced in human community by the Spirit's work. When the church embodies this, it becomes a living argument for the reality of the God it worships. When it fails to embody it, it becomes an obstacle to faith. Both are true, and the curriculum has been honest about both.

🕊️ For Those Returning After a Long Absence

If this curriculum has awakened something — or re-awakened something — the question of where to go next is real and sometimes frightening. The church that hurt you may still exist in your memory as the primary reference point for what Christian community looks like. It is not the only reference point. Communities exist that practice genuine welcome, genuine honesty, genuine patience with doubt. They are worth looking for.

The Prodigal Son returned to his father's house — not to a different household that happened to look similar. But the house he returned to was the one characterized by a father who runs, who restores, who feasts. That is what you are looking for. You have the right to look, and to decline to return to communities that practice something else.

If you are unsure where to begin: look for a community where the weak are genuinely welcomed, where doubt is tolerated, where the leadership is accountable to the people, and where the broken-cistern experience is acknowledged rather than suppressed. Start there.

A Final Word

The inside-out sequence of this curriculum is itself a small demonstration of John 7:17. It began with you — with your experience, your longing, your honest sense of the gap between who you are and who you want to be — because that is where the gospel finds people. Not with a syllabus of propositions to be evaluated, but with a recognition: someone has described you accurately, and they are offering a way forward.

By the time a group reaches the evidential material of Modules 3 and 4, the question "can I trust this?" is no longer academic. It is personal. And personal questions receive personal answers — not only arguments, but encounters. That shift — from detached evaluation to personal engagement — is itself the beginning of the movement John 7:17 describes.

The hope is that by the end of this curriculum, each person will have found at least one place where Scripture spoke more truthfully to their experience than they expected, at least one place where the historical reliability of the Bible surprised them, and at least one place where the direction John 7:17 points began to seem not like an evasion of the question but like a genuine answer to it. And — if they have been willing to stay with Module 6 — at least one place where the God of this curriculum sat with them in darkness rather than offering them a premature light.

The cost is real and the homecoming is real. The cross is the shape of the Christian life not because God is indifferent to our flourishing, but because it is the shape of love in a world where love costs something. And on the far side of the cost, there is a life that no amount of self-protection could have given us. The invitation is open. It has always been open. It is open now.

Discussion Questions
  • If you were to fully believe everything this curriculum has discussed and act on it — what would be the first thing that would change?
  • Does that change feel more like a homecoming, or more like a cost, or both?
  • What would it mean to inhabit a different story — to live as if the resurrection is true, as if the person in front of you bears the image of God?
  • What does the community you want to belong to look like? And where might you find it?
📚 Deep Dive — Module 9

Dietrich Bonhoeffer, The Cost of Discipleship — The classic account of what genuine discipleship costs and why cheap grace is a betrayal of the gospel. Read carefully; Bonhoeffer is demanding and rewarding in equal measure.

Miroslav Volf, Exclusion and Embrace — On the practical meaning of love, forgiveness, and reconciliation in a world where people genuinely harm one another. Some of the most important Christian social ethics written in the last thirty years.

Lesslie Newbigin, The Gospel in a Pluralist Society — On what it means to be the church as a sign, foretaste, and instrument of God's kingdom in a culture that has largely moved past it.

C.S. Lewis, The Weight of Glory — "The Next Business" and "The Weight of Glory" — on the glory of ordinary people and the cost and delight of taking them seriously.
