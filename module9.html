Living It Out

Faith That Moves the Hands, the Feet, and the Whole Person
Module 9 is the destination of the whole journey. Every prior module has been asking "is this worth my life?" This one asks "what does a life shaped by this actually look like?" It is not a moral summons to try harder — it is a description of what becomes possible when roots grow deep, held honestly alongside an acknowledgment of what it costs and who it is for. This module especially receives what Modules 6 and 7 sent toward it: the community that heals, and the growth that comes through weakness rather than achievement.

The Sermon on the Mount: A Description and a Direction

Matthew 5:3–6

"Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven. Blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted. Blessed are the meek, for they shall inherit the earth. Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they shall be satisfied."

The Beatitudes are not commands to manufacture poverty of spirit, or to perform mourning, or to achieve meekness through willpower. They are descriptions of a kind of person — one who has begun to see reality as God sees it. In the Christian account, this vision is given by the Spirit, not conjured by effort. The Sermon on the Mount describes the shape of a life that has been genuinely transformed by encounter with the Triune God — a life that begins to look, in its texture and direction, like the life of the One who gave the sermon.

But description and direction are not opposites. The Spirit gives this character as gift, and also invites our willing movement toward it. The Beatitudes describe what becomes true of those who walk closely with Christ — and they are therefore a compass, orienting us toward what we are being made into, without demanding that we pretend to have arrived. We are simultaneously becoming and not yet. Both are true at once.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

The Beatitudes are the opening statements of Jesus' most famous teaching, the Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5–7). The word "beatitude" comes from the Latin for "blessed" or "happy." Each one follows the same structure: "Blessed are the [type of person], for they will [receive something]." What is striking about them is who Jesus calls blessed: the poor in spirit, the mourning, the meek, the hungry. These are not the people any culture — then or now — instinctively admires. Jesus is describing a completely different picture of what a flourishing human life looks like: one where vulnerability, honesty about need, and hunger for God's justice are the marks of people who are actually living the way humans were designed to live. The point this module is making is that these are not requirements to fulfill before God will accept you — they are descriptions of what happens to a person who is being transformed from the inside by the Spirit. You do not achieve poverty of spirit as a spiritual exercise. You discover it as the Spirit opens your eyes to how much you need God. The description is also a direction — something to move toward, not a standard to feel crushed by.

Homecoming and Cost: Both Are True

There is a genuine sense in which obedience to Christ is a homecoming — a return to the kind of life human beings were made for, a movement toward the grain of the universe rather than against it. When we forgive, when we love our enemies, when we tell the truth at cost to ourselves, there is often a deep rightness to it that self-serving choices never produce.

But Bonhoeffer — whose work this curriculum draws on — warns with equal force against making the Christian life sound too much like a comfortable return. He calls such teaching cheap grace: grace without cost, discipleship without the cross, transformation without the death of what needs to die. Jesus says: "Take up your cross daily and follow me." The martyrs did not experience obedience as a pleasant homecoming in any comfortable sense. Obedience is both a homecoming and costly — and the cost is not evidence that something has gone wrong. It is part of what makes it real. Bonhoeffer's own summary of the matter, in The Cost of Discipleship, is worth sitting with: when Christ calls a person, he calls them to come and follow — which means leaving where they were and going somewhere they could not have gone alone. That is both the invitation and its cost.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

Dietrich Bonhoeffer was a German pastor and theologian who was executed by the Nazi regime in 1945 for his involvement in resistance efforts. He is not a distant historical figure offering comfortable theology from a safe distance — he wrote about the cost of following Christ and then paid that cost with his life. His distinction between cheap grace and costly grace is one of the most important ideas in modern Christian thought. Cheap grace says: "God forgives, so it doesn't really matter how I live." It takes the gift and ignores the giver. Costly grace says: "God has given everything — the life and death of his Son — and that gift is so valuable that it changes what I live for." Costly grace is not about earning God's favor through suffering. It is about the recognition that real love — the kind the cross represents — always involves giving something up. The Christian life is genuinely a homecoming, a return to what you were made for. And it genuinely costs something, because the things you were holding onto in place of God do not let go easily. Both of those things are true at the same time.

And yet the Westminster Shorter Catechism, asked to name the single chief purpose of human existence, answers: "to glorify God, and to enjoy him forever." Not to endure him. Not merely to serve him. To enjoy him forever — a phrase with its weight on the word forever as much as on the word enjoy. The cost is real and the cross is real. And they are not the last word. The life on the other side of the cross — the life toward which the whole curriculum has been pointing — is one in which the satisfaction we were made for is not a distant hope but an ever-deepening reality. Edwards described it, in Heaven: A World of Love, as being "enraptured with joys that are forever increasing, and yet forever full" — not a static arrival but an endless, increasing joy. The homecoming is not a destination. It is the beginning of a journey into a God who is, as Augustine said, our rest.

The Urgency of the Invitation

This curriculum has been careful, throughout, not to coerce — not to manufacture urgency through fear, not to press the reader toward a decision before the ground has been honestly prepared. That care stands. But honesty requires that the final module not close in a silence about what is actually at stake.

The Christian claim is not only that life with God is better than the alternatives. It is that the choice made in this life — whether to receive or refuse what God has offered in Christ — has consequences that extend beyond this life. Jesus, who spoke more tenderly about the kingdom than anyone in Scripture, also spoke more plainly about judgment than anyone in Scripture. Matthew 25 describes a final separation. The same voice that says "Come to me, all who are weary" also says "Depart from me, I never knew you." These are words from the same mouth. The curriculum would not honor its audience by pretending otherwise. The invitation is not merely aesthetic. The stakes are real. And the gospel described in these modules is not a lifestyle option among others — it is the claim that in Christ, God has done what nothing else could do for the condition all of us are in.

The invitation is real. The cost of refusal is real. The register in which this curriculum holds both is not cold proclamation of terms and not manufactured emotional pressure — it is the register of someone who has received the gift and cannot bear the thought of another person passing it by. If the twelve modules have done what they were designed to do, the reader who has traveled this far knows — at least in part — what is on offer. The door remains open. The invitation is genuine. And the God toward whom this whole curriculum points has been searching for you far longer than you have been looking for him.

Module 6 established something that must not be left behind at the door of Module 9: Christian community is not a resource the church offers to suffering people — it is the environment in which God heals them. This is 1 Corinthians 12's claim about what the body of Christ actually is: "If one member suffers, all suffer together." That is not a sentiment. It is a description of organic life. A body whose members do not feel one another's pain is not more spiritually advanced — it is less alive.

What this looks like in practice is more specific than most community language tends to be. For someone whose capacity for spiritual reception has been damaged — by trauma, by church hurt, by prolonged suffering — purely propositional ministry (however theologically careful) often cannot reach them where they live. What the body can offer instead is something different in kind: sustained presence without pressure to perform recovery; bearing the theological weight on behalf of those who cannot carry it themselves in a given season; long-term companionship through darkness that does not demand resolution on a schedule. This is not an alternative to the gospel. It is the gospel enacted in flesh — the Word becoming present in the community that bears one another's burdens (Galatians 6:2) until the individual can bear their own again.

There is something else that must be said honestly about the community this curriculum describes, and about the suffering that sometimes runs through it. The vision of the body of Christ in Module 6 and here is real — and so is the gap between that vision and what many people have actually experienced. A community formed by imperfect people, in a broken world, will be imperfect. It will sometimes fail you again. That is not a reason to abandon the vision — it is the precise condition under which the vision must be pursued. And it points toward something the Christian tradition has always known: that God does not only work despite our suffering and disappointment; he works through it. Joni Eareckson Tada — who has spent more than five decades navigating life with quadriplegia, and who knows the difference between a tidy theodicy and a hard-won faith — puts it this way: God permits what he hates in order to accomplish what he loves. The community that hurts us, the season that does not resolve, the wound that does not close on our schedule — these are not evidence that God has lost the plot. They are, under his sovereign hand, the very material from which he is conforming us more fully to the image of his Son (Romans 8:29). That process is not gentle in the way we would choose. It is gentle in the way a Father who knows better than we do what we actually need chooses to be — which is sometimes not gentle at all in the short term, and always loving in ways we will eventually be able to see. The call is not to manufacture gratitude for the pain. It is to trust the One who permits it — and to refuse to let the imperfection of his instruments become a reason to abandon his purposes.

Practically, this means a community that is not made uncomfortable by protracted grief, that does not interpret unresolved suffering as a failure of faith, that does not rush toward "and then I found peace" before the person in front of them has found it. It means sitting in Psalm 88's darkness with someone, week after week, without insisting that they turn the page. The healing is not the church's to produce. But the conditions for healing — safety, constancy, honest witness, unhurried presence — are the church's to provide.

🧭 When the Group Contains Someone Still in Module 6

A group that has traveled through the main sequence together will almost certainly contain someone who has not moved from suffering into resolution — who is still in Psalm 88's territory at the end of Module 9. This is not a failure of the curriculum or of the facilitator. It is the normal range of human experience.

The most important pastoral move in this session is to make room explicitly for that person — not by singling them out, but by naming in the group discussion that Module 9's vision of community is precisely for people who are not yet through. The community described in 1 Corinthians 12 is not a community of people who have all arrived. It is a community of people at different points in the journey, bearing one another toward where none of them could go alone. That framing keeps the landing pad genuinely open.

Close this final session without forcing closure. Some of the group's most important work may happen in the weeks after the last session, carried by what was said in the room.

The Church: The Visible Shape of Trinitarian Love

The curriculum began with the Triune God — the eternal fellowship of Father, Son, and Spirit, self-giving love overflowing from before the foundation of the world. It ends with the community that is meant to make that love visible.

The church at its best is not an institution. It is the body of Christ — the community of people in whom the Spirit of the risen Christ is at work, shaping them individually and together toward the image of Christ. Romans 12 fills in the practical texture: genuine love that does not perform; patient endurance; blessing those who persecute you; weeping with those who weep; refusing to repay evil for evil; living peaceably as far as it depends on you. These are not natural to fallen human beings — which is precisely why their consistent presence in a community is itself a form of evidence for the transformation that faith produces.

Module 7's counterintuitive signposts of growth — rapid return after failure, less defensiveness, honest acknowledgment of sin without self-justification, increasing compassion for others' failures — are not only individual virtues. They become community culture. A group of people who have collectively internalized that grace is real and ongoing begins to look different from the outside: not a community of those who have mastered the Christian life, but a community of those who keep returning to the fountain, who are not scandalized by one another's weakness, who bear each other's burdens as a matter of course because they know their own weight. This is the visible shape of Trinitarian love — not a performance of virtue, but the overflow of people who have been genuinely met by grace.

John 13:34–35

"A new commandment I give to you, that you love one another: just as I have loved you, you also are to love one another. By this all people will know that you are my disciples, if you have love for one another."

The love between members of the Christian community is not optional, not decorative, not one feature among others. It is the sign — the thing by which the world is supposed to recognize the disciples of Jesus. It is Trinitarian love — the self-giving love of Father, Son, and Spirit — reproduced in human community by the Spirit's work. When the church embodies this, it becomes a living argument for the reality of the God it worships. When it fails to embody it, it becomes an obstacle to faith. Both are true, and the curriculum has been honest about both.

🌍 Romans 12 Community Across Cultures

Romans 12's vision of communal life — weeping with those who weep, bearing one another's burdens, refusing to repay evil for evil — reads differently depending on the cultural lens you bring to it.

In Western individualist contexts, the natural reading is personal: "I will choose to practice these virtues in my own conduct." This is not wrong, but it is thinner than what Paul is describing. He is not writing a personal ethics checklist. He is describing what a community becomes — its shared character, its collective identity, the way it is known from outside.

In high-context, honor-shame, or collectivist cultures, this passage often lands with greater force precisely because the communal frame is already present: the shame of one member is the community's shame; the restoration of one member is everyone's celebration; the obligation to bear one another's burdens is not a voluntary add-on but the basic structure of belonging. This reading does not water down the cost of Romans 12 — it deepens it, because the community's identity is genuinely at stake in how it treats its weakest members.

For groups from diverse cultural backgrounds, naming this explicitly — that Paul's vision is collective by design, not accidentally — often opens the passage in ways that purely individual application does not.

What Makes a Church a Church

Before the curriculum turns to what can go wrong in the church — which Module 10 addresses with uncomfortable specificity — it is worth establishing clearly what it is we are talking about when we talk about the church at all. "Church" is one of the most overloaded words in Christian speech. It can mean a building, a denomination, a Sunday service, a gathered community, or the entire body of believers across all time and space. The word deserves precision, because the distinction between those meanings matters practically.

The Reformed tradition draws a distinction that has been theologically important for centuries: the visible church and the invisible church. The invisible church is the total company of all those who are genuinely, inwardly united to Christ by saving faith — known fully only to God, spread across all generations and all traditions, the church as it truly is in God's sight. The visible church is the gathered community of those who profess faith in Christ, receive the sacraments, and live together under the Word — the church as it appears in history, with all its imperfections, hypocrisies, and genuine believers mixed together. The parable of the wheat and tares (Matthew 13:24–30) describes the visible church honestly: not everyone in the field is wheat, and the sorting will wait until the harvest.

This distinction is not a license for cynicism about gathered communities. The visible church is where God has promised to work — through preaching, through the sacraments, through the mutual encouragement of believers. The Westminster Confession puts it directly: "out of this visible church there is no ordinary possibility of salvation" (WCF XXV.2) — meaning that the ordinary means of grace God uses operate within gathered, accountable communities, not in a purely invisible privatized faith. The church is not optional; it is God's chosen instrument. The distinction exists not to diminish the visible church but to protect it from two opposite errors: treating it as though every member is certainly saved (which it is not) and dismissing it as worthless because it contains failures (which ignores where God has promised to be present).

A qualification matters here, particularly for those who cannot access — or have been driven from — a large or well-resourced institutional community. The word "ordinary" in the Confession is doing important work: it names how God normally operates, not the limits of his freedom. But more than that: the question of what constitutes a genuine gathering of the church does not require a building, a denomination, or a credentialed minister. Jesus himself set the threshold remarkably low: "Where two or three are gathered in my name, there am I among them" (Matthew 18:20). Two or three. Not a congregation with programmes and a budget, but people gathered around him. Hebrews 10:25 calls us not to forsake "the assembling of ourselves together" — and that assembling can begin as modestly as it must, with whatever people and whatever circumstance is actually available, and still be real.

One further thing is worth naming, against the temptation to despise small or informal gatherings for their lack of theological sophistication or institutional authority. Jesus thanked the Father specifically for hiding wisdom from "the wise and learned" and revealing it to "little children" (Matthew 11:25). God is not bound to speak only through the credentialed and the experienced. He has done his most astonishing work through the young, the marginal, the uneducated, and the overlooked. A small gathering of two or three believers who open Scripture honestly, pray together, and hold one another accountable to what they find — that is not a poor substitute for the church. In the most essential sense, it is the church, present where Christ has promised to be. The larger and more richly gifted community is the fuller expression of what that small gathering already is. It is not a different thing entirely.

The Reformation tradition identified three marks that distinguish a genuine Christian community from one that only uses the name:

The faithful preaching of the Word. The Scriptures are opened, handled honestly, and applied to real life — not used as decoration for a motivational talk, not managed to avoid offence, but allowed to speak with their full weight. Where the Word is silenced or softened into something it does not actually say, the church's first mark is missing.

The right administration of the sacraments. Baptism and the Lord's Supper are practiced as Christ commanded, as covenant signs that embody and confirm the gospel. They are not merely memorials or social rituals — they are means of grace, specific ways God has promised to strengthen and confirm the faith of his people through ordinary, physical elements. The Lord's Supper — bread and wine received together in remembrance of Christ's body and blood until he returns — gives the gathered community a recurring embodied act of what they together believe. It is theology you can taste, solidarity you can see, the same gospel preached in a different register.

The exercise of church discipline. The community takes its shared life seriously enough to address sin, to pursue the restoration of those who have strayed, and to maintain the accountability that makes genuine belonging possible. This mark is the most frequently absent and the most frequently abused — its absence produces communities where anything goes, and its abuse produces the control patterns Module 10 names. Its right exercise — patient, restorative, accountable to Scripture — is the mark of a community that actually believes what it says it believes about sin, grace, and the importance of holiness.

⚖️ The Sacraments: Why They Matter More Than They Sound

The word "sacrament" can sound archaic or even vaguely Catholic to Protestant ears. In Reformed usage it means simply: a physical sign God has appointed to confirm and seal his promises to his people. Two are commanded by Christ — baptism (Matthew 28:19) and the Lord's Supper (1 Corinthians 11:23–26). The classic Reformed understanding is that the sacraments do not earn or add to salvation — justification is by faith alone — but they are genuine means of grace through which the Spirit works to strengthen and confirm the faith of the believer. They are not empty symbols; they are the gospel made physical and shared. The man who receives the Lord's Supper and the man who merely reflects on Christ's death have both encountered the same truth. But one has done so in a way that engages his body, his community, his memory, and his senses alongside his mind. That is not a trivial difference — it is what this module's earlier section on embodied worship was describing.

The sacramental question is also where the marks of the church meet the question of where to be. A gathering that preaches the gospel but never baptizes or takes communion together is missing something the New Testament treats as normal and important. The embodied, communal, repeated practice of these acts is part of what makes a gathered community a church rather than a study group.

The fuller treatment of what to look for in a healthy community — including specific red flags — is on the Finding a Healthy Church supplementary page. Module 10 takes up the question of what happens when the community's life goes wrong.

There is a dimension of "living it out" that neither moral reflection nor community life alone can fully address: the embodied, gathered practice of worship. Faith is not only an intellectual orientation toward true propositions, and it is not only a communal commitment to love one another. It is also — and this matters more than the Western theological tradition has sometimes acknowledged — something that happens in the body.

Scripture, prayer, sung praise, the Lord's Supper: these are not merely cognitive inputs. They are means of grace that engage the whole person — memory, imagination, emotion, sensation, social belonging — not only the rational mind. What recent work on trauma and embodied formation has begun to clarify is something the church has practiced for centuries without always theorizing: that gathered worship creates pathways for encounter with God that purely propositional engagement cannot replicate. A person whose capacity for intellectual reception of spiritual truth has been damaged by suffering or loss may still be reached through a psalm sung together in the dark, through the physical act of communion, through the voice of a community speaking the ancient words over them. The Spirit moves through the whole person, not only through the understanding.

This is not an argument for a particular style of worship. It is an argument for the fullness of it — for gathering practices that do not reduce the encounter with God to the transmission of correct information, but that engage the whole person God made and is remaking. The arts, embodied ritual, communal song, and the deliberate use of silence are not decorative additions to a fully cognitive service. They are the church doing what it has always known how to do: making a space in which the whole human being — not only the mind — can be met by the living God.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

The phrase means of grace is an old theological term for specific practices through which God has promised to meet his people — things like Scripture reading, prayer, communion (the Lord's Supper), baptism, and gathered worship. They are called "means" because they are the channels, not the source — God himself is the source; these are the pathways he has chosen to use. The point of this section is that these practices are not just ways to get information about God into your head. They engage the whole person — your body, your emotions, your memory, your sense of belonging. Think of the difference between reading the words "you are forgiven" in a book and hearing someone you trust say those words directly to you, looking at you, with their hand on your shoulder. The content is the same. The experience is completely different. That difference is what embodied worship creates: it gives God's truth a pathway into parts of you that reading alone cannot reach. The Lord's Supper (also called communion or the Eucharist) — the bread and wine that Christians share in remembrance of Christ's body and blood — is the clearest example: it is theology you can taste. It engages the body in the truth the mind already knows.

⚖️ Formation vs. Encounter: Holding Both

There are two streams of thinking about what worship and embodied practice do. One emphasizes formation: repeated practices shape us over time, reordering our loves and habits beneath the level of conscious decision (James K.A. Smith's argument in You Are What You Love). The other emphasizes encounter: worship is not primarily self-improvement but meeting with the living God, and that encounter can happen suddenly and without the expected preparation.

The Christian tradition holds both. Willard's disciplines (Module 8) and Smith's liturgical formation make the same basic point: the body and its habits matter to spiritual life. And the tradition also insists that the Spirit is not constrained by our formation — he meets people in burning bushes and prison cells and moments of profound darkness when no practice has prepared them for it.

The practical wisdom is: cultivate the practices that create conditions for encounter, without treating those conditions as controls on the Spirit. We prepare the ground. God sends the rain when he wills.

🕊️ For Those Returning After a Long Absence

Hebrews 10:25 — "not forsaking the assembling of ourselves together" — is a real call, and this curriculum does not soften it. God's design for his people has always been plural: a body with many members, a family gathered around a shared table, a community that bears one another's burdens in ways no individual can sustain alone. The gathered life of the church is not an optional supplement to personal faith. It is the environment in which God's purposes for his people are most fully expressed.

That call and the wound that makes it feel impossible can exist in the same person at the same time. Both are real. The Scripture does not retract the command because we have been hurt. But in the same breath as commands about gathering, Scripture offers the repeated call to forgive as you have been forgiven (Ephesians 4:32, Colossians 3:13). Forgiveness is not the same as trust — trust is rebuilt slowly, in genuinely safe conditions, and is not owed on demand. But forgiveness — releasing the debt, refusing to let the wound permanently define the future — is not presented in Scripture as an optional grace for those with the temperament for it. It is the normal posture of someone who has themselves received freely what they did not earn. Carrying unresolved bitterness toward gathered Christian life is carrying exactly the weight Ephesians 4:31–32 names and addresses. The remedy is not "pretend it didn't happen." It is the harder and more honest thing: remember what you have been given, and give accordingly, in whatever measure is possible today.

Hebrews 10:25 is not a condemnation of those who are not yet there. It is a horizon — a direction of travel. The same God who pursues wanderers is not standing at the door of a gathered church tapping his foot. He is moving toward you — and the community he intends for you to find is part of what he is moving you toward, in his time and by his means.

If this curriculum has awakened something — or re-awakened something — the question of where to go next is real and sometimes frightening. Communities exist that practice genuine welcome, honest engagement with doubt, and patient presence in suffering. They are worth looking for.

The Prodigal Son returned to his father's house — not to a different household that happened to look similar. But the house he returned to was the one characterized by a father who runs, who restores, who feasts. That is what you are looking for. You have the right to look, and to decline to return to communities that practice something else in the name of that father.

If you are unsure where to begin: look for a community where leadership is primarily accountable to God and to Scripture — and where that accountability is exercised mutually among the family of believers, rather than being concentrated in an unchecked single voice. Look for a community where the weak are genuinely welcomed, where the broken-cistern experience is acknowledged rather than suppressed, and where Psalm 88 can be sat with honestly without everyone being hurried toward Psalm 103 on someone else's schedule. Start there.

And if you are not yet ready to walk through the door of a gathered community — that is acknowledged here, without pretending readiness is irrelevant. The Spirit who has brought you this far is not done. Hebrews 10:25 will still be true when you are ready to hear it differently than you can today.

Vocation, Work, and the Sacred Ordinary

Colossians 3:23–24

"Whatever you do, work heartily, as for the Lord and not for men, knowing that from the Lord you will receive the inheritance as your reward. You are serving the Lord Christ."

One of the Reformation's most subversive contributions to Western culture was the doctrine of vocation. Medieval Christianity had organized human life into a two-tier hierarchy: the sacred life (monks, priests, nuns — those who had renounced the world for God) and the secular life (everyone else — farmers, merchants, mothers — those whose work was spiritually inferior by definition). The Reformers — Luther especially — read Scripture differently. Every honest occupation, Luther argued, is a calling from God. The milkmaid who tends her cows faithfully serves God as truly as the priest who says Mass. There are no secular jobs, only secular attitudes toward work.

The theological foundation is simple and radical: if God is the Lord of all things, then all things can be done to his glory. Paul's "whatever you do" in Colossians 3:23 is not hyperbole — it is a claim about the scope of God's interest in human activity. The carpenter's trade, the teacher's lesson, the nurse's care, the farmer's planting, the accountant's column — each can be the place where a human being made in God's image brings that image to bear on the world, and does so in conscious awareness of the one who gave them both the capacity and the calling.

This does not make work into a religion. It de-sacralizes false religion and sanctifies the ordinary instead. The person who treats their career as the ultimate source of meaning has made it an idol. The person who treats their work as a gift, a place of service, and a temporary stewardship over something that belongs to God has understood what vocation means. Timothy Keller, who wrote carefully on this in Every Good Endeavor, puts it this way: a job is a vocation only if someone else calls you to do it for them rather than for yourself. Work done in the spirit of service — to colleagues, to customers, to the community, ultimately to God — carries a weight that career-as-self-fulfillment never can. It is not a coincidence that the people history remembers most clearly for their work are those who understood themselves as servants rather than achievers.

Money and generosity belong alongside vocation. The New Testament is specific and unambiguous: you cannot serve both God and money (Matthew 6:24). That is not a prohibition of wealth — Abraham, Job, and David were all wealthy — but a description of what money does when it is trusted as a security system rather than handled as a stewardship. The anxiety that wealth generates when it is treated as the ultimate protection against the future is in direct proportion to how much is being asked of it. Jesus looked at a rich young ruler with love (Mark 10:21) and told him the thing he was holding most tightly was the thing that needed to go. The point was not poverty as a spiritual achievement but release — the freedom that comes from holding lightly what is ultimately not yours. Generosity, practiced consistently and sacrificially, is one of the most reliable ways of reordering the heart's allegiances. You cannot remain in the grip of money while giving it away freely.

John Piper, developing the implication, names what is at stake in how we handle money: the credibility of Christ in the world hangs on it. If Christians live as though the same things the world values are what actually satisfy — accumulating, insuring, consuming, retiring — they will look exactly like people who have a religious interest on the side but who trust in the same things everyone else trusts in. Christ will not look like an all-satisfying treasure. He will look like a lifestyle add-on. The person who gives hazardously, who lives on less than their income permits in order to release more for those who have less and for the causes of the gospel, is saying with their money what they may find difficult to say with words: that Christ is actually worth more than security. That witness, when it is genuine, is among the most powerful apologetics available to ordinary believers. The world notices when someone gives away what the world would hoard.

One Life — Spending and Being Spent

There is a question that this curriculum has built toward without ever stating directly: what will you do with the one life you have been given?

John Piper, in Don't Waste Your Life, tells the story of a man converted in old age who sat weeping at the end of a service saying, through tears: "I've wasted it. I've wasted it." The story gripped Piper as a boy and never let go — not because old-age conversion is tragedy, but because the grief was real. The man had come to see what the life he had lived could have been aimed at, and the recognition of the gap between what it had been aimed at and what it could have been aimed at produced a grief that words could barely carry.

The gospel this curriculum presents is not primarily a rescue operation — though it is that. It is an invitation into the purposes of the God who made everything, for whom everything exists, and who is now calling people to live in conscious alignment with those purposes. That alignment has implications for money and vocation, as the preceding sections name. But it has implications beyond those — for how a life is oriented, for what a person is fundamentally trying to do with the years they have been given. The Westminster Catechism's answer to its first question — to glorify God and enjoy him forever — is not a pious sentiment. It is a comprehensive life direction. Everything else is downstream from it.

The cost is real. Piper puts it plainly: the path of God-exalting joy will cost you your life. Not necessarily in martyrdom — though it may — but in the daily, sustained decision to hold loosely what the world holds tightly, to serve where no one is watching, to risk where comfort counsels staying still, to spend and be spent for people and purposes whose reward is not in this life. This is what Bonhoeffer — already cited in this module — called costly grace: not the endorsement of a life you were already planning to live, but the transformation of what you are planning to live for.

The church's history is the history of ordinary people who understood this, at great personal cost, and through whom God accomplished things that still reverberate. It is worth pausing on three of them.

John Bunyan was a tinker — a mender of pots and pans — in seventeenth-century England who felt called to preach the gospel in defiance of the Conventicle Acts that prohibited unlicensed preaching outside the Church of England. He was imprisoned for twelve years. He could have been released at any time if he agreed to stop preaching. He did not agree. His wife was heavily pregnant at the time of his arrest. One of his daughters was blind. The separation, he wrote, was "as the pulling of the flesh from my bones." And yet from that prison he wrote The Pilgrim's Progress — one of the most widely read books in the history of the English language, still in print after 350 years, still forming the faith of ordinary Christians who have never heard of Bunyan but who know Christian and Faithful walking toward the Celestial City. The cost was real. What came through it was also real. He chose faithfulness over comfort and did not waste his life.

William Tyndale gave his life for a single project: to translate the Bible into English so that ordinary English people — the ploughboy, he said — could read Scripture for themselves without depending on a priest to interpret it. He was hunted across Europe by Henry VIII's agents for ten years. He was eventually betrayed, imprisoned, and strangled and burned at the stake in 1536. His dying words, according to witnesses, were: "Lord, open the King of England's eyes." Within four years of his death, Henry VIII mandated that an English Bible be placed in every church in England — in large part a Bible composed of Tyndale's translation. The prayer was answered after the martyr who prayed it was already dead. Every English-speaking Christian who has opened a Bible since owes an unacknowledged debt to a man who counted the cost and decided the ploughboy's access to Scripture was worth his life.

Perpetua of Carthage was a young North African noblewoman, twenty-two years old and nursing an infant, arrested during the Severan persecution in 203 AD for refusing to offer sacrifice to the Roman emperor. She was offered her freedom repeatedly, including by her own father, who begged her to renounce her faith for the sake of the child. Her prison diary — still extant — is among the most extraordinary documents from the early church: a first-person account of visions, of refusing compromise, of handing her infant to her family and choosing death. She was executed in the arena at Carthage. Her account helped form the church's theology of martyrdom for centuries afterward and her name is still recited in the oldest surviving eucharistic prayers of the Western church — more than 1,800 years after she died in a Roman arena for a faith she had held for perhaps two years. She did not waste her life.

These are not stories designed to make ordinary believers feel inadequate. Most people are not called to martyrdom. But they are stories of what becomes possible when a person is freed from the enchantment of comfort and security long enough to act from a different set of values — and of the kind of impact that can come from one human life fully surrendered to a purpose larger than itself. The question that follows every one of these accounts is the same: what are you doing with the one life you have?

⚖️ Avoiding the Avoidance Ethic

One of Piper's sharpest observations in Don't Waste Your Life is about what he calls the "avoidance ethic" — the reduction of the Christian life to a list of things not done. The avoidance ethic asks: what's wrong with this? What's wrong with this movie, this purchase, this weekend, this level of comfort? When the primary question is avoidance, the result is a life defined by negatives — not this, not that — and a person who looks, from the outside, almost indistinguishable from a disciplined secular person. There are no visible adultery, theft, or dishonesty; but there is also no visible love, sacrifice, or generosity that makes Christ look like a treasure.

The better question is positive: how will this help me love God more, or show others that God is worth loving? The difference between those two questions — the negative and the positive — often determines the difference between a life that is morally clean but spiritually thin, and a life that is actively making people glad in God. Both questions matter. But the positive one is primary.

🕊️ When Competitive Vocation Is Closed — Disability, Illness, and Faithful Limitation

The Reformation doctrine of vocation, taken as written, can feel like a word addressed to people who have the health, capacity, and circumstance to pursue meaningful work. For those living with disability, chronic illness, or conditions that have narrowed or closed the door to conventional employment, the language of calling can cut rather than comfort — another standard to fail by, another vision of flourishing that excludes you.

This page cannot be honest and leave that experience unnamed.

The first thing to say is that Scripture's picture of vocation is wider than competitive employment. The call to work heartily as for the Lord (Colossians 3:23) was addressed to enslaved people, people without agency over their occupation, people for whom "career" was not a concept that applied. The "whatever you do" is genuinely whatever — including the work of endurance, the work of prayer, the work of being present to another person in pain, the work of maintaining trust in God through darkness.

The second thing to say is that the disabled, the chronically ill, and those the world would classify as unproductive have occupied a specific and honored place in Scripture's account of how God works. Paul's thorn in the flesh — whatever it was — was not removed, because God's power was made perfect in weakness (2 Corinthians 12:9). Fanny Crosby, blind from infancy, wrote more than 8,000 hymns — including "Blessed Assurance" — from the very limitation that the world would have named a disqualification. Joni Eareckson Tada has spent more than fifty years as a quadriplegic and has become one of the most theologically serious and pastorally useful voices on suffering in the modern church. These are not stories of limitation overcome. They are stories of limitation inhabited — and of God doing through the limitation what he could not have done without it.

The third thing is the most important: the primary call of the Christian life is not to be productive. It is to love God with all of oneself and to love one's neighbor as oneself (Matthew 22:37–39). Neither of these requires a functional body, a competitive resume, or an income. They require a willing orientation of the whole person — whatever that person's condition — toward God and toward others. A person who cannot work may still pray with greater discipline and depth than a person who can. May still be the one whose honest presence in suffering communicates to another person that they are not alone. May still be the recipient of care in ways that give the community the opportunity to practice the love that is its defining mark. May still, by the faithfulness of their endurance, preach a sermon that no pulpit could deliver.

There is a fourth thing worth saying, directly to the person who feels useless: your suffering may be the very thing that most qualifies you to reach someone else. Paul writes in 2 Corinthians 1:4 that God "comforts us in all our troubles so that we can comfort those in any trouble with the comfort we ourselves receive from God." This is not a platitude. It is a description of a specific mechanism: the person who has been in the darkness has something to give that no amount of cheerful competence can replicate. They can sit with another person in the dark without flinching, without rushing toward resolution, without the anxiety of someone who has never been there. That capacity — to be present in pain without needing to fix it — is formed precisely by having endured what the other person is enduring. It cannot be acquired any other way. The suffering that felt like pure loss may be the very thing God is using to equip you for a ministry no seminary could have trained you for.

The currency of the kingdom is not productivity. It is faithfulness. And faithfulness is available to everyone who breathes.

A Final Word

The inside-out sequence of this curriculum is itself a small demonstration of John 7:17. It began with you — with your experience, your longing, your honest sense of the gap between who you are and who you want to be — because that is where the gospel finds people. Not with a syllabus of propositions to be evaluated, but with a recognition: someone has described you accurately, and they are offering a way forward.

By the time a group reaches the evidential material of Modules 3 and 4, the question "can I trust this?" is no longer academic. It is personal. And personal questions receive personal answers — not only arguments, but encounters. That shift — from detached evaluation to personal engagement — is itself the beginning of the movement John 7:17 describes.

The hope is that by the end of this curriculum, each person will have found at least one place where Scripture spoke more truthfully to their experience than they expected, at least one place where the historical reliability of the Bible surprised them, and at least one place where the direction John 7:17 points began to seem not like an evasion of the question but like a genuine answer to it. And — if they have been willing to stay with Module 6 — at least one place where the God of this curriculum sat with them in darkness rather than offering them a premature light.

🧭 What Has Just Happened in This Room

Before you close the final session, it is worth pausing to name — at least for yourself — what this curriculum has actually been doing across all nine modules.

It has not been a Christian self-improvement program. It has been returning, again and again, to the gospel — at a new angle each time, applied to a real problem each time. Module 1 brought the gospel to bear on the diagnosis of the human condition. Module 2 on the shape of the story itself. Modules 3–5 on the question of trust. Module 6 on suffering, which is where trust is tested most severely. Modules 7 and 8 on what growth actually looks like when the gospel is the engine rather than willpower. Module 9 on the community that embodies it. Every session, the gospel was the answer — not a new answer, but the same announcement encountered at greater depth and applied to a more specific wound.

This means two things are worth naming as the room closes. For believers: the gospel you received at the beginning is not the entry-level content you are meant to graduate from. It is the home you were always meant to return to, and you will spend the rest of your life discovering that it is larger than you knew. The fact that you still need it — still return to it, still find that it meets you — is not a sign of spiritual immaturity. It is the sign of a faith that knows where the fountain is.

For anyone who came into this curriculum from outside faith — or who ends it still not quite sure — something has been happening in this room that is worth naming plainly. Every time a believer returned to the gospel for help with a real problem, and found it adequate, you were watching the gospel work. Not as an argument. As a demonstration. Paul describes a moment like this in 1 Corinthians 14:24–25: an unbeliever comes into a gathering of God's people, hears the truth spoken plainly, and finds it naming what they know to be true about themselves. "God is really among you," he says. That moment — the moment of recognition — is not manufactured. It happens when the gospel is being applied honestly to real things by real people. If something like that happened in your group across these sessions, it is not an accident. It is the curriculum working exactly as it was designed.

The cost is real and the homecoming is real. The cross is the shape of the Christian life not because God is indifferent to our flourishing, but because it is the shape of love in a world where love costs something. And on the far side of the cost, there is a life that no amount of self-protection could have given us — the life the Catechism names with quiet precision: to glorify God, and to enjoy him forever. The invitation is open. It has always been open. It is open now — including for those who end this curriculum still in the dark, still carrying the unanswered question, still not yet through. The father who ran is not watching from a fixed point. He is still moving toward you.

📚 Deep Dive — Module 9

Dietrich Bonhoeffer, The Cost of Discipleship — The classic account of what genuine discipleship costs and why cheap grace is a betrayal of the gospel. Read carefully; Bonhoeffer is demanding and rewarding in equal measure.

John Piper, Don't Waste Your Life (Crossway, 2003) — The book behind the "One Life" section of this module. Piper's central argument: a life without a single, all-embracing passion for the supremacy of God in all things is a wasted life — and the wasted life is not a tragedy in the dramatic sense but in the quiet, drifting sense that most of us are at greater risk of. Accessible, urgent, and practically aimed at every stage of life.

Timothy Keller, Every Good Endeavor (Dutton, 2012) — The most careful theological treatment of vocation available at a popular level. Keller works through the Reformation doctrine of calling, the problem of work in a fallen world, and what it means to do ordinary work as sacred service. Belongs alongside this module's section on vocation and the sacred ordinary.

James K.A. Smith, You Are What You Love — On how embodied habits and gathered practices form our desires beneath the level of conscious decision; a contemporary articulation of why liturgy and worship matter for the whole person, not only the intellect. A natural companion to Willard (Module 8) and an accessible entry to Smith's larger argument.

Miroslav Volf, Exclusion and Embrace — On the practical meaning of love, forgiveness, and reconciliation in a world where people genuinely harm one another. Some of the most important Christian social ethics of the last thirty years.

Lesslie Newbigin, The Gospel in a Pluralist Society — On what it means to be the church as a sign, foretaste, and instrument of God's kingdom in a culture that has largely moved past it. Chapters 18–20 are especially relevant to Module 9's vision of community as witness.

C.S. Lewis, The Weight of Glory — Particularly "The Weight of Glory" and "Membership": on the glory of ordinary people, the cost and delight of taking them seriously, and what genuine Christian community makes possible.

Beth Argot, "Restoring the Body: Knowing God Fully through Whole-Brain Worship" (DTS Chapel, 2025) — On how embodied, whole-person worship creates pathways for healing and encounter that purely propositional engagement cannot. Available through Dallas Theological Seminary's chapel archive. Pairs naturally with the embodied-life section of this module.
