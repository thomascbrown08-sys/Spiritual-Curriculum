Signposts of Growth

Seeing God's Faithfulness Over Time
Having established that the story is compelling, the document credible, the resurrection real, and the problem of suffering honestly faced — the group is ready for the bridge question: what does following this actually produce in a life? This module transitions from the evidential toward the applied.
🧭 The Pivot — What Just Changed, and Why It Matters for Both Audiences

Module 7 is the hinge of the curriculum. Everything before this module made the case; everything from here applies it. That transition changes what your job as a facilitator looks like — and it changes it in a specific way that is worth naming before you begin.

In Modules 3–5, your primary task was to engage questions honestly and let evidence do its work. From Module 7 onward, your primary task is different: it is to return believers to the gospel as the solution to real problems in their actual lives. Not to give them better strategies or techniques. Not to call them to try harder. But to point them, again and again, to what Christ has already done — and to show how deeper trust in that finished work is what produces genuine change.

This matters because the gospel that saves is the same gospel that sanctifies. Believers do not graduate from it; they go deeper into it. When you bring a struggling believer back to the cross as the answer to their anger, their fear, their persistent sin — you are not repeating yourself. You are helping them discover that the gospel they received at the beginning is larger than they knew, and that it meets them exactly where they are now.

And here is the thing to hold in the back of your mind: if there is anyone in your group who is not yet a believer — who is still watching, still evaluating — they are about to see something that no argument in Module 3 could give them. They are going to see faith in Christ actually working in a person they know. They will watch someone return to the gospel for help with a problem they also carry, and find it adequate. That is not a superficial moment. It is the most persuasive kind of evangelism: the gospel demonstrating in real time what it claims to do. You do not need to manage your room into two conversations. Return believers to the gospel faithfully, and both audiences are being served.

Fruit as Expression, Not Measure

Galatians 5:22–23

"But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, self-control; against such things there is no law."

The Spirit's fruit is exactly that — fruit. You do not manufacture it. You abide in the vine, and fruit is the natural direction of a living connection. This distinction is one of the most practically important in the curriculum: growth is the natural expression of genuine faith, not the measure by which we verify whether our faith is real.

A note for those in traditions shaped by the charismatic and Pentecostal stream: the New Testament also describes gifts of the Spirit — including healing, prophecy, tongues, and others named in 1 Corinthians 12–14 — that many Christians experience as ongoing and active in the life of the church. Whether and how these gifts operate today is a question different Christian traditions answer differently, and this curriculum does not attempt to settle it. What every tradition agrees on is that the Spirit is actively at work, and that his work is recognizable — both in the fruit Paul describes in Galatians 5 and in the forms of service and supernatural activity Paul describes in 1 Corinthians 12. The two lists are not competing accounts of the same thing; they are complementary dimensions of the same Spirit's presence in a community.

If growth is the measure of genuine faith, then inconsistency — which Paul describes as his own normal experience in Romans 7 — becomes evidence against one's salvation. But if growth is the natural expression of genuine faith, then inconsistency is a call to return to the vine, not a verdict on whether we were ever truly connected. The root precedes and produces the fruit. Assurance rests on the root — on Christ's faithfulness — not on the consistency of our output.

A nuance worth naming: fruit is not the ground of assurance, but Scripture does treat it as a genuine sign of what the root is producing. Hebrews 12:14 and 1 John 3:14 both appeal to the pattern of a person's life as an indicator of real transformation — not as the thing that saves, but as evidence of the saving work already underway. The difference matters pastorally: the person whose inconsistency is the normal struggle of genuine faith does not need their fruit assessed; they need their eyes directed to Christ. But the person who shows no movement whatsoever, no desire, no returning — the 1 John letters suggest that absence is worth examining honestly, not to produce despair, but to ask whether the root is actually there to produce from. The goal is always to press deeper into the root, not to perform the fruit.

What Growth Actually Looks Like

The signposts of genuine growth are rarely dramatic. They tend to be quiet changes over time: a reduction in reactivity, an increase in patience under pressure, a greater capacity to love difficult people, a growing honesty with oneself, an ability to sit with suffering without being destroyed by it. These are observable — by the person experiencing them and by those around them.

2 Peter 1:5–8 describes a progression — faith to virtue to knowledge to self-control — not as a ladder to climb but as a description of a life that is moving. The direction matters more than the speed. A person who is measurably more patient than they were five years ago, whose anger has lost some of its edge, who finds themselves capable of genuine compassion that did not come naturally before — that person is seeing the Spirit's fruit emerging in the slow time of a real life.

The Counterintuitive Signposts: What Duguid Saw

But here the curriculum must do something harder than describe the observable progress, because that description — however true — can quietly reinstall a performance framework through the back door. If the signpost of growth is that I am measurably more patient, the question becomes: am I patient enough? Have I grown enough? And the person who honestly cannot identify measurable improvement sits in silence or shame.

Barbara Duguid, drawing on John Owen's rigorous account of mortification, poses a question that reorients the entire picture: "Perhaps our greatest problem is not the reality of our sin, but our unbiblical expectations of what Christian growth should look like. What if growing in grace is more about humility, dependence, and exalting Christ than it is about defeating sin?"

This is not quietism. It is a precise observation about what the Spirit is primarily producing in a life, and it is grounded in Paul's own testimony. In 2 Corinthians 12, Paul prayed three times for the thorn to be removed. It was not removed. The reason given was so that the power of Christ might rest on him — and specifically through the weakness, not despite it. The weakness remained by design. The maturity Paul arrived at was not the maturity of someone who had overcome a limitation. It was the maturity of someone who had stopped trusting his own resources entirely.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

Several terms in this section carry specific weight. Mortification is an old word for the deliberate, ongoing effort to weaken the power of sin in your life — not by sheer willpower, but by bringing sin honestly before God and relying on the Spirit's help to fight it. Think of it less as "killing sin" and more as "keeping sin in the light where it can't grow in the dark." John Owen was a seventeenth-century English pastor and theologian who wrote what many consider the most rigorous treatment of this subject ever produced. Barbara Duguid is a contemporary author who took Owen's difficult material and made it pastorally accessible — particularly her insight that growth often looks less like conquering sin and more like becoming more honest about it and more dependent on Christ in the middle of it. Quietism — which the text says this is not — is the idea that we should just be passive and let God do everything, making no effort ourselves. Duguid's point is not passivity. It is that the goal of the effort is different from what we assume: the goal is deeper dependence on Christ, not the comfortable feeling of having arrived. Paul's thorn in the flesh (2 Corinthians 12:7) — whatever it was specifically, Paul does not say — was something painful that he asked God to remove three times, and God said no. The "no" was not cruelty. It was the means by which Paul discovered that God's grace was more valuable than the absence of the problem.

2 Corinthians 12:9–10

"But he said to me, 'My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness.' Therefore I will boast all the more gladly of my weaknesses, so that the power of Christ may rest upon me... For when I am weak, then I am strong."

The practical implication is significant. The signposts of genuine spiritual maturity are not always the ones we reach for instinctively. They may include:

  • A more rapid return to Christ after failure — not a longer interval between failures, but a shorter interval between failure and running back.
  • A deeper, less defended honesty about the condition — the person who can name their sin without minimizing it is further along than the person who needs to protect themselves from the sight of it.
  • A growing distaste for the sin itself, not merely its consequences — grieving what it does to the relationship with Christ rather than merely regretting its costs.
  • A decrease in spiritual self-congratulation — the mature believer is less likely to compare themselves favorably to others, not more.
  • An increase in genuine compassion for others in their failures, born of clear-eyed knowledge of one's own — which is exactly what the thorn produced in Paul's letters.

None of these feel like progress from the inside. All of them are. And all of them have this in common: they exalt Christ rather than the self, because they are descriptions of a person increasingly aware of their need for him rather than increasingly aware of their own sufficiency.

The Essential Balance: Mortification Is Still Real Effort

Duguid's reframe must not be used to license passivity. Owen — whose work underlies her book — is unambiguous: the mortification of sin requires deliberate, Spirit-dependent effort. The call to "reckon yourselves dead to sin" (Romans 6:11) is a real command, addressed to real people who have not yet felt it to be experientially true. The paradox Owen identifies is that this effort is designed to keep sin clearly before us — in the light, named, brought to Christ — rather than to produce the comfortable feeling that we are winning. Genuine mortification often increases the felt weight of sin before it reduces it, because the Spirit is opening eyes that were previously closed. This is not regression. It is the work. The signposts in the previous section and the active effort described here are not alternatives. They are the same life seen from two angles.

🕊️ When the Growth Is Not Visible — and the Thorn Was Not Removed

If you have been following Christ for years and the honest answer to "how have you grown?" is silence or worse — if the same struggles are still present, if the fruit seems absent, if the experiment described in Module 8 has not yielded the results you were hoping for — this section is written for you specifically.

The thorn was not removed. The grace was sufficient. Those are not the same promise, and it matters enormously that Paul came, over time, to prefer the second. The preference was not produced by the thorn going away. It was produced by discovering that the grace was genuinely there in the middle of the thorn remaining. That discovery is itself a signpost of profound growth — one that would not have been available if the prayer had been answered the way Paul first asked.

The question worth sitting with is not "why hasn't this changed?" but "what has the Spirit been producing in me through the fact that it hasn't?" The answer may surprise you.

🧭 The Spirit as Agent of Growth — and How to Frame Discussion

2 Corinthians 3:18 describes the process: "we all, with unveiled face, beholding the glory of the Lord, are being transformed into the same image from one degree of glory to another. For this comes from the Lord who is the Spirit." The agent is the Spirit. The signposts of growth are signposts of his work. Encourage the group to notice them with gratitude rather than claim them as personal achievement.

Practically: the framing of discussion questions matters here more than anywhere else in the curriculum. "How have you grown?" places participants in the position of auditing their progress — and will produce either performance or shame. Questions shaped by this module's reframe sound different: "Where have you found yourself more dependent on Christ this year, even if that dependence was produced by failure?" "Where has your inability to change something become the occasion for grace rather than the evidence against you?" These open the same territory but orient it toward Christ rather than toward the self's record.

Discussion Questions
  • Where have you found yourself more dependent on Christ recently — even if, especially if, that dependence was produced by failure rather than by success?
  • Is there a struggle you have had for a long time that has not resolved? Looking at it through Duguid's lens: what might the Spirit have been producing in you through its persistence that victory would not have produced?
  • What does the phrase "my grace is sufficient for you" mean if the thorn is not removed? Have you ever experienced the sufficiency of grace in conditions where the problem remained?
  • Is there someone you know whose maturity and depth of character has clearly been produced by suffering and persistent struggle rather than by visible triumph? What does their life look like — and what does it suggest about what the Spirit is doing?
📚 Deep Dive — Module 7

Barbara Duguid, Extravagant Grace: God's Glory Displayed in Our Weakness (2013) — The book from which this module's central reframe is drawn. Duguid writes with unusual pastoral honesty about the ongoing experience of indwelling sin in mature Christian life, and grounds her argument in Owen's mortification while making it accessible. The most important book on this module's theme and essential reading for facilitators.

John Owen, The Mortification of Sin (1656; modern editions available) — Owen's original treatment, demanding and precise. His central insight — that the goal of mortification is not to feel like you are winning but to keep sin clearly before Christ — is the theological root of Duguid's reframe. Chapter 2 alone is worth the investment.

Eugene Peterson, A Long Obedience in the Same Direction — On the pilgrim life as sustained journey rather than dramatic experiences. Peterson's reading of the Psalms of Ascent as a curriculum for the long walk complements this module's argument about the slow time of genuine growth.

Dallas Willard, Renovation of the Heart — On the whole-person transformation the Spirit is working; Willard's emphasis on training rather than trying provides the active-effort counterbalance to Duguid's reframe.
