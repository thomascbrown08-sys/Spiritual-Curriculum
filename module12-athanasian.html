The Athanasian Creed

The Trinity at Maximum Precision
← Return to Module 12: We Believe · Also available: The Chalcedonian Definition
Advanced Material

The Athanasian Creed is the most philosophically demanding of the three great creeds — a sustained, precise exposition of Trinitarian doctrine that does not attempt to make the subject accessible so much as it attempts to make it accurate. It should be approached as advanced material, which is exactly why it is offered here as a linked sub-page rather than part of the main Module 12 text. Readers who have worked through the Apostles' Creed, the Nicene Creed, and the Chalcedonian Definition will be better prepared to receive what this document is trying to do.

Despite its name, the Athanasian Creed was almost certainly not written by Athanasius of Alexandria. It first appears in Latin in the 5th or early 6th century — after Athanasius' death in 373 — and shows no signs of the Greek-language context in which Athanasius wrote. It was attributed to him because of its vigorous defense of Nicene Trinitarianism, which suited his reputation. The actual author is unknown. What is certain is that it represents the most extended and precise creedal statement of Trinitarian theology in the Western tradition.

Why It Exists

The Nicene Creed established the essential structure: one God in three persons, the Son fully divine, the Spirit worshiped with Father and Son. But it left significant room for confusion about how exactly the three persons relate to one another — how God can be both one and three without either collapsing into one person (Modalism) or becoming three gods (tritheism). The Athanasian Creed attempts to express this with more precision than any creed before or since, using philosophical categories that were the standard tools of late-antique thought.

It also presses harder on Christology than the Nicene Creed did — its second major section summarizes the Chalcedonian settlement in creedal form, affirming the full humanity and full divinity of Christ in one person.

The Full Text

The Athanasian Creed (Quicunque vult) Whosoever will be saved, before all things it is necessary that he hold the catholic faith.

Which faith, except every one do keep whole and undefiled, without doubt he shall perish everlastingly.

And the catholic faith is this: That we worship one God in Trinity, and Trinity in Unity; neither confounding the persons, nor dividing the substance.

For there is one person of the Father, another of the Son, and another of the Holy Spirit.
But the Godhead of the Father, of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit, is all one: the glory equal, the majesty coeternal.

Such as the Father is, such is the Son, and such is the Holy Spirit.
The Father uncreated, the Son uncreated, and the Holy Spirit uncreated.
The Father incomprehensible, the Son incomprehensible, and the Holy Spirit incomprehensible.
The Father eternal, the Son eternal, and the Holy Spirit eternal.
And yet they are not three eternals but one eternal.
As also there are not three uncreated, nor three incomprehensibles, but one uncreated and one incomprehensible.

So likewise the Father is almighty, the Son almighty, and the Holy Spirit almighty; and yet they are not three almighties, but one almighty.

So the Father is God, the Son is God, and the Holy Spirit is God;
and yet they are not three Gods, but one God.
So likewise the Father is Lord, the Son Lord, and the Holy Spirit Lord;
and yet not three Lords, but one Lord.

For like as we are compelled by the Christian verity to acknowledge every person by himself to be God and Lord, so are we forbidden by the catholic religion to say: there are three Gods or three Lords.

The Father is made of none, neither created nor begotten.
The Son is of the Father alone; not made nor created, but begotten.
The Holy Spirit is of the Father and of the Son; neither made, nor created, nor begotten, but proceeding.

So there is one Father, not three Fathers; one Son, not three Sons; one Holy Spirit, not three Holy Spirits.
And in this Trinity none is afore, or after another; none is greater, or less than another; but the whole three persons are coeternal together and coequal.

So that in all things, as aforesaid, the Unity in Trinity and the Trinity in Unity is to be worshipped.
He therefore that will be saved must thus think of the Trinity.

Furthermore it is necessary to everlasting salvation that he also believe faithfully the incarnation of our Lord Jesus Christ.

For the right faith is that we believe and confess that our Lord Jesus Christ, the Son of God, is God and man; God, of the substance of the Father, begotten before the worlds; and man of the substance of his mother, born in the world…

Who, although he is God and man, yet he is not two, but one Christ; one, not by conversion of the Godhead into flesh, but by taking of the manhood into God; one altogether; not by confusion of substance, but by unity of person.

For as the reasonable soul and flesh is one man, so God and man is one Christ; who suffered for our salvation, descended into hell, rose again the third day from the dead; ascended into heaven, sits at the right hand of the Father, God Almighty; from whence he shall come to judge the living and the dead. At whose coming all men shall rise again with their bodies; and shall give account of their own works. And they that have done good shall go into life everlasting; and they that have done evil into everlasting fire.

This is the catholic faith, which except a man believe faithfully and firmly, he cannot be saved.

Understanding the Structure

The creed divides into two major sections:

Part One (Trinitarian) works through an extended parallel structure: Father, Son, and Spirit each possess the full divine attributes — each is uncreated, incomprehensible, eternal, almighty, God, Lord. And yet they are not three of each of these things. There is one eternal, one uncreated, one almighty, one God, one Lord. The creed drives this point with relentless repetition, which is not careless but deliberate: every reader who has thought carefully about this territory knows that the temptation toward Modalism (collapsing three into one) or tritheism (treating three as simply three) is constant. The repetition closes each exit in turn.

The key formula it arrives at: "We are compelled by the Christian verity to acknowledge every person by himself to be God and Lord; so we are forbidden by the catholic religion to say there are three Gods or three Lords." Both things are simultaneously true. The creed does not pretend to explain how. It insists that both must be held.

The technical terms for the persons' distinguishing relationships: the Father is "made of none, neither created nor begotten"; the Son is "of the Father alone, not made nor created, but begotten"; the Spirit is "of the Father and of the Son, neither made nor created nor begotten, but proceeding." These distinctions — ingenerate, begotten, proceeding — are the church's careful attempt to name what distinguishes the three persons while maintaining the unity of the one substance. They do not explain the inner life of God. They mark the boundary of what can be said.

Part Two (Christological) summarizes the Chalcedonian settlement: fully God, fully human, one person, not by converting the Godhead into flesh but by taking the manhood into God, not by confusion of substance but by unity of person. The analogy offered — as soul and body are one human being, so God and human are one Christ — is an analogy, not an explanation. It points toward the kind of thing being described without claiming to have described it fully.

The Damnatory Clauses

On the Opening and Closing Lines

The Athanasian Creed opens: "Whosoever will be saved, before all things it is necessary that he hold the catholic faith — which faith, except every one do keep whole and undefiled, without doubt he shall perish everlastingly." It closes: "This is the catholic faith, which except a man believe faithfully and firmly, he cannot be saved."

These are the famous "damnatory clauses" that have made the Athanasian Creed controversial — and that have led many churches to use it rarely or to omit these lines when they do. They deserve honest engagement rather than embarrassed silence.

What should we make of them? Several things are worth noting. First: the faith being described is not a complex theological examination. The creed's affirmations are the ones at the center of Christianity — the Trinity and the incarnation. The stakes are the same as they would be for any denial of the gospel's core. Second: the clauses are framing statements, not a license to damn specific individuals — they establish what the catholic faith is, not who is or is not saved. Third: the church that produced these clauses also produced Athanasius, who spent seventeen years in exile rather than compromise on one Greek word, and whose pastoral correspondence shows a man who cared deeply about individual souls. The severity of the framing language reflects the severity of what was at stake in the controversies — not a punitive spirit but a conviction that truth about God matters enormously.

Whether to retain these clauses when using the creed in worship is a legitimate pastoral question. Understanding what they are saying — and what they are not saying — is the prior task.

How to Read This Creed

The Athanasian Creed rewards slow reading. Its repetitions are not redundancies — they are a systematic closing of exits. If you find yourself wanting to simplify, to find the one clean formulation that makes the Trinity easy to hold, you are feeling exactly the pressure the creed is written to resist. Modalism is simpler. Tritheism is simpler. Arianism is simpler. The creed is not trying to be simple. It is trying to be true — and to name, as precisely as language allows, a reality that exceeds language.

It was designed for people who had already worked through the Apostles' and Nicene Creeds and understood the controversies behind them. For readers in that position, it offers something that the shorter creeds cannot: an extended argument, in creedal form, for why every shortcut around the Trinity fails and what the full affirmation requires.
