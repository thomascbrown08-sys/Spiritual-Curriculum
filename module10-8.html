The Celebrity Fracture

"I Am of Paul; I Am of Apollos"
← Tactic 7  |  Overview  |  Tactic 9 →

The Presenting Dispute

Two people in the same congregation are having a conversation about theology, and at some point one of them says a teacher's name — and something in the room shifts. Not the theology itself; the name. Suddenly the conversation is not about whether a position is true but about whether the teacher who holds it is trustworthy, faithful, or on the right side. The other person responds with their own teacher's name. The argument that follows is not primarily about Scripture. It is about which tribe is in the right, conducted through the proxy of which teachers they follow. They are arguing about Paul and Apollos, and neither of them knows it.

The Enemy's Actual Move

Paul identified this pattern in his first letter to Corinth with unusual directness: "What I mean is that each one of you says, 'I follow Paul,' or 'I follow Apollos,' or 'I follow Cephas,' or 'I follow Christ.' Is Christ divided? Was Paul crucified for you? Or were you baptized in the name of Paul?" (1:12–13). The question lands hard because it names the specific absurdity: the tribalism has transferred the identity-forming function from Christ to the teacher. The teacher's name is now the load-bearing element of the person's theological identity, which means challenges to the teacher are challenges to the person's self.

The contemporary amplification is significant. In Paul's Corinth, the available teachers were the few who had personally visited the community. In the contemporary church, the available teachers are effectively unlimited: podcasts, YouTube channels, books, social media accounts, conferences. A congregation member can now have a deeper relationship with a teacher they have never met — through hours of audio and video content, through a parasocial bond built over years — than with their own local pastor. And the tribal affiliations these media ecosystems create arrive at church fully formed: people show up on Sunday already sorted into Reformed Twitter, progressive Christianity, charismatic YouTube, and a dozen other camps, each with its own vocabulary, heroes, and enemies. The local church becomes the place where pre-formed tribes encounter each other, and the local pastor is outgunned before he opens his mouth.

The Scriptural Frame Being Exploited

The genuine call to "test all things; hold fast what is good" (1 Thessalonians 5:21) and to "remember your leaders, those who spoke to you the word of God. Consider the outcome of their way of life, and imitate their faith" (Hebrews 13:7) — both legitimate — are distorted into the idea that following a teacher means adopting their tribal affiliations wholesale. Hebrews 13:7 calls for imitation of faith, not adoption of faction. 1 Thessalonians 5:21 calls for testing, which implies evaluation and not simple adoption. The tribal version of teacher-following does not test: it receives, affiliates, and then defends.

Counting the Cost

The Corinthian factions. The biblical case study is fully developed in 1 Corinthians 1–4. Paul's response is instructive: he does not adjudicate between the factions by explaining why his teaching is better than Apollos's. He reorients the entire question: "For no one can lay a foundation other than that which is laid, which is Jesus Christ" (3:11). The teachers are servants, not lords. "So let no one boast in men. For all things are yours, whether Paul or Apollos or Cephas..." (3:21–22). The resolution is not to pick the right teacher. It is to recover the right foundation, relative to which all teachers are servants whose work will be tested by fire (3:13).

High-profile pastoral falls in 20th and 21st century evangelicalism. When a celebrity pastor falls morally or pivots theologically, the fracture that follows in their community of followers is entirely predictable — and its severity is a direct function of how much the followers' identity was built on the teacher rather than on Christ. Communities where the teacher's authority was primary (their interpretation of Scripture was the community's interpretation; their theological positions were the community's positions; their personality and style were the community's culture) experience a total identity crisis when the teacher falls. The question "what do we believe?" cannot be answered independently of the teacher, because the teacher was the answer. Communities where the teacher was genuinely a servant of the Word — where their teaching was a window to Christ, not a substitute for him — find their way through the crisis with their faith intact, because the foundation was never the teacher.

The Reframe

The question that cuts through the celebrity dynamic: Are we following this teacher because they help us understand Christ better — or are we following Christ because this teacher told us to?

This question is not a critique of the teacher. It is a diagnosis of the relationship. Good teachers point away from themselves. They are, in John the Baptist's language, voices calling in the wilderness — not the thing the wilderness is calling toward. A teacher who has not been tested by the question "does this help me understand Christ, or does it help me understand the teacher?" has been received as something other than a servant.

The Peacemaker's Move

When a teacher's name becomes load-bearing in a conversation: Gently redirect to the substance. "I want to set the name aside for a moment and look at the argument itself. What specifically is [teacher] saying, and what is the scriptural case for it? Because I think we can evaluate the position without evaluating whether we trust [teacher] — and if the position stands on its own, it doesn't need [teacher's] authority to back it."

When someone's faith seems more attached to a teacher than to Christ: This is a slow conversation, not an intervention. It requires the kind of relationship that makes the question receivable. If that relationship exists: "I've noticed that [teacher] is a big frame of reference for you, which makes sense — they've clearly been formative. Can I ask: what would you believe about [specific matter] if you had arrived at it without [teacher's] help? What does the text itself say when you sit with it?" You are not asking them to discard the teacher. You are asking them to check whether they have a relationship with the text independent of the teacher's mediation of it.

When a teacher falls and the community is in crisis: The pastoral priority is not to adjudicate the teacher's failure. It is to redirect the community's identity question. "What we believed about Christ when [teacher] was at their best — do we still believe it? Was it true because [teacher] said it, or was [teacher] saying it because it's true? If it was true before, it's still true. The foundation hasn't moved."

For yourself: A useful periodic examination — which teachers are genuinely helping me understand Scripture, and which ones am I reading primarily to have my existing positions confirmed and reinforced? The second kind of reading is not formation; it is tribal maintenance. Both may involve the same teacher, depending on the posture you bring to them.

⚖️ Going Deeper: Media Ecosystems, Tribal Formation, and the Local Church

The Celebrity Fracture is not new — Paul identified it in the first generation of the church. But the digital media environment has changed its scale and mechanism in ways worth understanding.

How parasocial relationships work. The term "parasocial" describes relationships in which one party invests significantly in a connection that the other party does not know exists. Listeners develop genuine feelings of loyalty, trust, and affection for podcast hosts, YouTube teachers, and social media personalities through extended exposure — the kind of relationship normally built through years of shared experience is compressed by hundreds of hours of one-directional content consumption. This is not inherently harmful; it describes the relationship most Christians have with deceased writers and theologians whose work has been deeply formative. The difference is scale and speed: contemporary media creates parasocial bonds of significant intensity in months, across audiences of millions, without the teacher having any relationship with the community in question.

The sorting machine. Each media ecosystem — Reformed Twitter, progressive Christianity podcasts, charismatic YouTube, conservative Catholic blogs — functions as a sorting and reinforcement mechanism. The algorithms that govern content delivery optimize for engagement, which means they favor content that confirms existing positions, generates strong emotional responses, and creates clear in-group / out-group distinctions. A person who begins with a moderate theological curiosity about, say, Reformed theology will find themselves, within months, having consumed content that has moved them from curiosity to identity: they now know who the in-group is, who the enemies are, what the vocabulary signals, and which positions are acceptable within the tribe. This process happens largely below conscious awareness, because it looks like learning.

What this means for the local church. A congregation that contains members who have been formed by different media ecosystems is not primarily a congregation of people with different theological positions. It is a congregation of people who are natives of different worlds — who have different heroes and enemies, different instinctive vocabularies, different emotional responses to the same theological terms, and different priors about who can be trusted. The local pastor who was not part of any of these ecosystems's formation is, from the perspective of the media-formed member, a lower-authority voice than the teacher who has been a daily presence for three years.

The response is not to abandon media consumption or to treat it as inherently corrupting. Good teaching is good teaching regardless of format. The response is cultivating the habit of evaluation — testing what is received against Scripture, bringing it to the local community for engagement rather than simply receiving it as settled, and periodically asking the foundational question: am I following this teacher to Christ, or have I started following them instead of Christ? That question, asked honestly and regularly, is a sufficient corrective for most of what the Celebrity Fracture produces.
