About This Curriculum

Seeing and Believing

What it is. How it was made. Why it exists.

What This Curriculum Is

Most introductions to Christian faith are built around a single type of evidence — historical, philosophical, experiential — and ask the reader to evaluate that evidence and decide. Seeing and Believing does something different. It takes seriously the observation in John 7:17 that understanding follows the intent to obey: that spiritual perception is not purely a pre-condition of movement, but something that often comes in the middle of it.

The curriculum is built for four kinds of reader: those who are navigating faith largely alone — without a church community, estranged from one, or not yet connected to one — and who need a resource that does not assume a gathered context; those beginning to move toward Christian faith; those who have already begun and want to understand what they are moving toward; and those who want to think more carefully about questions the church has not always handled well. It draws on historical, psychological, literary, moral, and personal evidence — because the case for Christian faith is not thin enough to rest on just one.

It is not an argument that bypasses doubt. It is an invitation to move toward the light with whatever degree of intention is honestly available, and to see what becomes visible from there.

Honest about hard questions

The curriculum does not avoid the questions that cause real people to walk away from faith. Suffering, doubt, the failures of the church, the reliability of Scripture — these are addressed directly, not managed.

Charitable across disagreements

Christians have divided sharply over questions that, looked at carefully, often don't require the division. Where genuine disagreement exists, the curriculum names it and holds it without forcing a verdict.

Accessible without being thin

The material aims to treat every reader as someone capable of thinking clearly, without explaining so much that the thinking is done for them. If it falls short of that for you, the author wants to know.

Attentive to the wounded

Every module contains material specifically addressed to those who have been hurt by the church, those in seasons of spiritual dryness, and those who wonder if the door is still open after a long absence.

Theological Commitments

This curriculum is written from within the Protestant Reformation tradition. The five convictions recovered by the Reformers from Scripture — the Solas — form its theological foundation and shape every choice of emphasis and framing throughout:

  • Sola Scriptura — Scripture alone is the supreme and final authority in all matters of faith and practice. The Bible, as the inspired and inerrant Word of God in its original giving, stands over every tradition, institution, and teacher — including this one.
  • Sola Gratia — Salvation is by grace alone. Every movement toward God is itself a gift of God; nothing we bring earns or contributes to our standing before him.
  • Sola Fide — Justification is received through faith alone — trust in the person and finished work of Christ alone as the ground of our acceptance before God.
  • Solus Christus — Christ alone is mediator, redeemer, and Lord. There is no other name under heaven by which we must be saved (Acts 4:12).
  • Soli Deo Gloria — To God alone be the glory. Every dimension of salvation — its origin, accomplishment, and application — reflects the glory of the God who planned and achieved it.

This foundation does not make the curriculum dismissive of Christians in other traditions. Where believers across the church's history have preserved genuine biblical insight — on union with Christ, on the place of honest lament, on the communal nature of faith — those insights are drawn on with gratitude. But choices of scope must be made honestly. Where traditions have located authority in structures Scripture does not sanction, or added conditions to salvation that Scripture does not require, this curriculum follows the Reformation principle and returns to the Word. That principle applies to this curriculum as much as to anything else: readers are invited to test what is written here against Scripture, and corrections are genuinely welcome.

How It Was Made

The curriculum was built by one person, working through a combination of theological reading, personal engagement with Scripture, and extended use of AI assistance — specifically Claude, developed by Anthropic — for research, drafting, and the kind of careful reasoning that benefits from a tireless conversation partner.

The use of AI in theological work is worth being honest about. The tool is useful for research, for surfacing connections across a wide body of material, and for articulating ideas that have been circling without landing. It is not a substitute for theological judgment, pastoral wisdom, or the kind of understanding that comes from lived experience. Every section of this curriculum has been authored by a human and is intended to be reviewed by theologically mature readers before broader use. The AI accelerated and clarified; it did not replace.

The curriculum draws throughout on the work of Timothy Keller — particularly his approach to finding third ways between polarized theological positions, and the tone of pastoral honesty he modeled in works like Walking With God Through Pain and Suffering and The Prodigal God. It draws on Reformed and Anabaptist traditions, on academic biblical scholarship, and on the broader history of Christian spiritual formation. Sources are noted throughout; errors in attribution or theological nuance are the author's responsibility, not the tradition's.

⚖️ A note on review: This curriculum is a living document. It has not been formally reviewed by a theological panel, though that review is intended and actively sought. Content is evaluated throughout production against the Westminster Standards, the Four Ecumenical Creeds, the Five Solas, and the broader Reformed and Puritan tradition — but this internal process is not a substitute for review by qualified human theologians and pastors. A fuller description of the standards applied is on the Theological Foundations section of the home page. If you find something that needs correcting — in theology, in pastoral judgment, or in tone — please use the contact address in the Author section below. Corrections are genuinely welcome, not merely tolerated.
What this site is not: This curriculum is not a church, not a substitute for pastoral care, and not a crisis support service. It is a set of written resources for people thinking carefully about Christian faith. If you are in acute distress or crisis, please reach out to a pastor, counselor, or crisis line — this site cannot provide the real-time human presence that genuine crises require. The 🕊️ Wounded Pilgrim page addresses spiritual wounds with care, but it is not clinical care and does not claim to be.

Why It Exists

For years there has been a patchwork of half-formed ideas — connections between Scripture and history, between theology and ordinary human experience — wanting to take shape and be shared. Christianity, honestly presented, is genuinely beautiful. The truth of God stated cleanly is one of the pleasures that survives even dry seasons. This curriculum is an attempt to state some of it clearly.

It was built for anyone ashamed, hurt, or excluded. For the person perfectly desperate for encouragement that doesn't come with strings attached or a fixing agenda. Maybe you will walk through the Dry Valley and know, because someone left a marker, that others have been here and kept walking — not because they were strong, but because they were reminded that God is faithful. Others were that reminder for the author of this curriculum. This is his attempt to pass it on.

This site is meant to preach to its author first, then to others.

A Note from the Author

The curriculum was written by Thomas C. Brown. Questions and corrections are genuinely welcome at ThomasCBrown08@gmail.com.

For those who want to understand more about where this curriculum came from — the personal circumstances that shaped it — that account is here. It is optional reading. The curriculum stands without it.

This site began as an honest attempt to sort through my own faith — not to teach, but to find my footing again after a long time away from it.

I have spent years in real distance from God and from the church. Some of that distance I caused. Some of it arose from circumstances and relationships that became painful in ways I am still learning to grieve and forgive. I am not going to pretend those two things come apart neatly, because they don't. What I can say is that I never stopped believing in Jesus — but I have felt detached and discouraged for a long time, and the warmth that faith is supposed to carry had gone very cold. To never be trusted again is a very hard sentence. I have been living under a version of that sentence, and part of building this has been slowly coming to understand that God does not pronounce it.

Perhaps this is the Prodigal coming yet again to his senses. I am not ashamed to say so.

I want to say something about how I first came to faith, because it shaped what this curriculum became. I did not arrive at Christianity through archaeology or philosophy — though I have found those things genuinely interesting in retrospect. I arrived through desperation.

I was sitting in a cold jail cell with nothing but a KJV study Bible, in a period of great shame and mourning. I knew what I was. I had known for a long time. But there is a difference between knowing abstractly that you have failed and sitting with no distractions, no escapes, and the full weight of specific things you cannot undo. The Ten Commandments had never landed with their full force before. Reading them in that context was like watching a door close. Not one door — every door. The law does not merely say "you fell short." It says that what you fell short of was infinite, and that the shortfall runs all the way through you, not just through your actions. I did not feel condemned by an external standard. I felt exposed as something that could not fix itself and, if I was honest, did not always want to.

What broke that open was not an argument. It was seeing Christ — not as a teacher or a moral example, but as the One who loved without any fleshly incentive, who gave without any calculation of return, whose life was the precise inverse of everything I had found in myself when I looked honestly. I am not describing a feeling, exactly. I am describing a recognition: this is what love actually looks like, and I have never managed it, and it is being offered to me anyway. The contrast was what reached me. It produced not optimism but worship — and then, later, something that resembled hope.

The specific verse that became an anchor was John 17:20 — Jesus's prayer on the night of his betrayal, not for the disciples in the room with him but for "those who will believe in me through their word." He was praying for me before I existed. Before I had done anything worth praying for. The assurance I found was not in my faith — my faith was too weak and too intermittent to carry any weight — but in the faithfulness of the God who had already included me in a prayer before the foundation of the world. That distinction, between the strength of my grip and the strength of his, is something I have carried ever since.

As for what convinced me that Scripture was worth trusting: it was not, primarily, the archaeological evidence — though I have since found it genuinely significant. It was the internal consistency of the document itself. A friend once helped me see it: the Old Testament foreshadows Christ's coming from beginning to end — in the sacrificial system, in the pattern of the Psalms, in the shape of the prophecies. The Gospels describe his actual life. The Epistles explain what his life and death mean and what they demand of those who follow him. The whole is an astonishing literary and theological coherence across fifteen centuries and dozens of authors. That kind of coherence does not happen by accident or by committee. I found Charles Spurgeon's instinct — his habit of finding Christ foreshadowed throughout the entirety of Scripture — more illuminating than most systematic theologies I have read, because it reflects what the document is actually doing from the inside.

The curriculum took the shape it took because I kept running into the same frustration: Christians dividing sharply over questions that, looked at more carefully, don't require the division — and doing it uncharitably, which makes everything worse. I have learned more from people like Timothy Keller — who had the patience to find the third way between errors, and the charity to hold it without condescension — than from almost anyone else. I have very little of that patience naturally. I am trying to grow some.

This work is my act of faithfulness right now. It is my attempt to draw on what God's Word has produced — in me, in the tradition, in the people who have thought carefully before me — and share it as clearly as I can. I feel the strangeness of using a lifeless instrument to handle sacred material. I also feel the strangeness of not using what was available. I am trying to be honest about which instruments I used, because this curriculum asks others to be honest, and I cannot hold that standard without holding it myself.

There are questions embedded in this curriculum I cannot yet fully answer from my own experience. I offer what I have, not what I wish I had. I am still in the middle of the story.

This curriculum was built as a bootstrap — a deliberate attempt to make use of the tools and resources available to get moving again in faith, rather than waiting for conditions that might never arrive.

In practice, that meant using Claude (an AI developed by Anthropic) not as an occasional drafting aid, but as the primary instrument of production. The direction is mine: the theological questions being asked, the traditions being weighed, the pastoral instincts being followed or resisted, the overall architecture of what this should be and for whom. The production is largely Claude's: the drafting, the research, the careful articulation of ideas I brought only in outline. That is a different kind of authorship than a reader might reasonably assume, and it deserves to be named plainly.

I am also aware of what this arrangement cannot provide on its own. An AI can reason carefully about theology; it cannot replace the judgment of someone who has spent decades in pastoral practice, or the correction that comes from a community of theologically mature readers engaging the material together. Both of those forms of oversight are still needed here, and I hold the curriculum loosely until they happen. If something needs correcting — in theology, in pastoral judgment, or in tone — that is not a courtesy to me. It is how this becomes something better than one person bootstrapping their way back toward God could produce alone.

For Reviewers and Correctors

If you are reading this curriculum with theological training or pastoral experience and find something that needs correcting, this section is addressed to you directly.

What kind of feedback is most useful

Theological errors — any claim, framing, or implication that contradicts Scripture or the Reformed confessional standards the curriculum is built on. Please cite the specific passage or WCF article where possible. Severity matters: a creedal violation is different from an imprecise phrase, and the response to each is different.

Pastoral misjudgments — material that is theologically defensible but likely to cause harm in the hands of a reader who is vulnerable, grieving, or newly coming to faith. This curriculum is read without a pastor present; the bar for pastoral caution is higher than it would be in a supervised group setting.

Tone failures — places where the language is condescending, dismissive of genuine questions, or inconsistent with the Acts 17 posture the curriculum claims to hold. This includes material about traditions other than the Reformed one.

Factual errors — incorrect historical claims, misattributed quotations, or inaccurate descriptions of other theological positions.

Contact: ThomasCBrown08@gmail.com. Please include the page name and the specific passage in question. Feedback that says "this is wrong" without locating what is wrong is hard to act on; feedback that says "page X, paragraph Y, this phrase implies Z, which contradicts WCF chapter W" is immediately actionable.

What this curriculum is trying to do — context for reviewers

The primary audience is people navigating faith without a church community — searchers, returners, and people rebuilding after church hurt. The curriculum is read alone, without pastoral mediation. This shapes several deliberate choices that might look like softness to a reviewer accustomed to confessional precision:

Where serious Christians genuinely disagree on a secondary question, the curriculum names both positions and what each is protecting, rather than adjudicating. This is intentional — the goal is not to produce Calvinist or Arminian readers, but readers who have engaged seriously with Scripture and can think through the question themselves. If you believe this approach concedes too much, that feedback is worth hearing; so is the argument for why it doesn't.

The Wounded Pilgrim material is addressed to people who have been hurt by the church and may be hostile to Christianity itself. The pastoral register there is deliberately softer than the doctrinal material elsewhere. That is not theological imprecision; it is an attempt to hold the door open for people who are almost through it.

The curriculum does not currently have a formal review panel. Until it does, the most useful external check is people like you reading it carefully and saying what they find.

A Prayer for Those Who Come Here

Lord,

This work was done in a dry season, with imperfect tools, by a person who needed it as much as anyone who might find it. We do not offer it as something finished or sufficient. We offer it as a signpost — the best one we could make — pointing toward the One who has not moved.

Bless those who find their way here in confusion or grief. Give them patience for the long road, and the particular grace of being honest with you when they cannot yet be confident in you. Remind them that the fountain is real — that the thirst they carry is the best evidence they have that something is worth thirsting for, and that you have been running toward them since before they turned.

For those who have been wounded by the church: let this place feel different from what wounded them. Let the tone here be the tone you actually take with bruised reeds and smoldering wicks. Forgive us where we have failed that standard.

For the one who built this: refresh what has grown dry. Restore what was taken or lost. Lead him, in your time, back to community — the real kind, the healing kind, the kind this curriculum describes but which he has not yet found. Hold what was entrusted to you. You have said you would. We are counting on that.

Whatever good this does, let it be yours. Whatever error it contains, give someone the wisdom to catch it and the charity to say so gently.

Amen.
