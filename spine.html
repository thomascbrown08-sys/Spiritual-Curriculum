John 7:17, Belief with Intent, and the Logic of Obedient Knowing
John 7:17

"If anyone's will is to do God's will, he will know whether the teaching is from God or whether I am speaking on my own authority."

Reading John 7:17 in Context

Before building on this verse, it is worth being precise about what it says — and what it does not say. Jesus speaks these words in the Temple courts in Jerusalem during a festival, in response to a direct challenge about the authority behind his teaching. The immediate claim is specific: a person oriented toward doing God's will is in the best position to evaluate whether Jesus' teaching comes from God or from himself.

The verse is not, in its original context, a universal epistemological principle about all spiritual understanding. But the principle it contains — that genuine willingness toward God opens perception that intellectual detachment cannot achieve — is consistently supported across Scripture. Psalm 34:8 ("taste and see"), James 1:22–25 (the doer who does not forget), and the whole logic of Paul's argument in Romans 6 all point in the same direction. The verse is used here as the sharpest single statement of a pattern that runs throughout the biblical witness.

🧭 Why the Principle Extends Beyond the Immediate Context

Jesus is not making a claim about a single disputed question. He is describing how spiritual perception works as such — which is why his listeners would have recognized it as consistent with Proverbs' insistence that the fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom, and with the Psalms' pattern of the person who delights in God's law coming to understand it. The principle is not imported from outside the text; it is the spine of a larger biblical pattern.

Belief with Intent: The Volitional Dimension of Faith

The Greek word for faith — pistis — carries a volitional weight that the English word "belief" often loses. To believe, in the New Testament sense, is not merely to acknowledge that something is factually true; it is to entrust oneself to that truth. James makes the distinction sharply: "Even the demons believe — and shudder!" (James 2:19). The demons have accurate theology. What they lack is the willingness to submit to it.

This curriculum uses the phrase Belief with Intent as a pedagogical coinage — not a translation of any single Greek word, but a phrase constructed to describe what the biblical witness consistently portrays saving faith to involve: not mere intellectual assent, but the heart's genuine orientation toward God, a turning of the will that says, in effect, I trust this, and I intend to live accordingly. The Reformers gave this a formal structure, describing faith as having three components: notitia (knowledge of the facts), assensus (agreement that they are true), and fiducia (personal trust and reliance). This three-part framework is systematic theology derived from careful reading of the biblical text — it is not a direct lexical analysis of what the Greek word pistis carries as its dictionary meaning, but a conceptual map that accurately captures distinctions the New Testament makes across multiple passages. The third element — fiducia — is what distinguishes the faith that saves from the assent that even the demons possess (James 2:19).

A note for those who want the lexical precision: there is a significant and still-active scholarly debate about Pauline phrases like pistis Christou — appearing in Romans 3:22 and Galatians 2:16 — which can be translated either as "faith in Christ" (the traditional reading) or "the faithfulness of Christ" (a reading with growing scholarly support). If the "faithfulness of Christ" reading is correct, Paul is grounding justification in Christ's own covenant faithfulness rather than in our act of believing. This is worth noting because it strengthens rather than undermines this curriculum's Christological assurance argument: we are held not by the quality of our grip but by the faithfulness of the One our trust is directed toward.

Three Things John 7:17 Teaches

Understanding has a moral dimension. Spiritual perception requires a kind of alignment. Augustine: "Our heart is restless until it rests in Thee." The will shapes what we are willing to see.

The invitation is genuinely open. "If anyone's will" — not restricted to the sufficiently prepared or the already devout. The very desire to seek is already the Spirit's work in you.

Obedience is the epistemology. The evidence invites movement, and the movement produces further evidence. Faith is not a leap in the dark. It is a step toward a light you can already partly see — taken, crucially, not from a position of strength or readiness, but from wherever you actually are. The step Module 8 calls for is not the step of someone who has conquered their weakness and is now ready to act. It is the step of someone who knows their weakness and moves anyway, in reliance on the One who said "my grace is sufficient for you."

Assurance: Christological, Not Introspective

Here is a pastoral danger that must be named plainly, before it can do harm: any framework that locates assurance in the quality of one's faith — the genuineness of one's intention, the sincerity of one's orientation, the consistency of one's obedience — will eventually produce anxiety in anyone honest enough to examine themselves carefully. Paul's Romans 7 experience is not the exception; it is the norm for sincere believers.

The Reformation's pastoral breakthrough was precisely to redirect this question. Assurance does not come from asking "was my faith genuine enough?" or "was my intention sincere enough?" It comes from asking a different question entirely: Is Christ faithful? And the answer Scripture gives is unambiguous: yes. Always. Completely. Without condition.

Luther found peace not by examining the depth of his own faith but by fixing his gaze on the faithfulness of the One his faith was directed toward. The strength of our grip on Christ is not what holds us. Christ's grip on us is what holds us. Assurance is therefore not introspective — it is Christological. We look to Christ, not to the quality of our looking.

🕊️ For Those Who Have Prayed Many Times and Wonder if It Counted

There is a specific pastoral trap that this curriculum is designed to avoid: the idea that salvation is a transaction secured by the right prayer said with sufficient sincerity. The New Testament's basis for assurance is not the quality of our prayer but the faithfulness of the God who hears it. Romans 10:9–10 describes the direction of the heart, not the precision of a formula. If you have genuinely turned toward Christ — even haltingly, even inconsistently, even in confusion — the question is not whether you said it correctly but whether God is faithful to his promise. He is.

For those wondering whether they need to pray again: the answer is almost certainly no. The call is not to repeat the transaction but to trust the Promiser. Hebrews 10:23 — "Let us hold fast the confession of our hope without wavering, for he who promised is faithful" — is addressed to people who are already doubting, already wavering. The answer given is not "pray again" but "hold fast — look at who made the promise."

🧭 For Group Discussion on Assurance

Self-examination is not wrong — Paul commends it in 2 Corinthians 13:5. But self-examination is meant to direct us back to Christ, not to produce a verdict on whether we qualify. The person who examines themselves honestly and finds inconsistency is invited to return to the vine, not to question whether they were ever truly connected. The question "is my faith real?" is answered not by analyzing the faith but by returning to its Object.

The Holy Spirit's Prior Initiative

It is vital to be clear about what makes the first movement of faith possible. Ephesians 2:8 says: "For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God." The gift is not merely salvation — the gift is the faith itself. Our turning toward God is itself a response to God's prior turning toward us.

The very desire to seek is itself evidence that the Spirit is at work in you — which means the question "but what if I'm not willing enough?" is already answered by the fact that you are asking it.

⚖️ Arminian and Reformed Traditions: Agreement and Difference

Both the Arminian tradition (prevenient grace) and the Reformed tradition (effectual calling) agree on the decisive point: God is first. We do not take a step toward God and then God responds. God moves toward us, and our step is a response to his movement already underway. This is the point the curriculum rests on, and both traditions affirm it.

Where the traditions differ significantly is in the nature and mechanism of that priority: whether grace can be resisted (Arminianism affirms it can; Reformed theology generally holds it cannot for those who are effectually called), and whether election is conditional on foreseen faith or unconditional. These are genuine and important differences within the family of orthodox Christianity — not peripheral disputes. The curriculum is designed to be usable across both traditions, but facilitators from either background should be aware that this is a live area of theological disagreement.

🧭 The Spirit Throughout the Curriculum

The Holy Spirit is not merely the one who enables the first movement of faith. He is the active agent of conviction (John 16:8), illumination (1 Corinthians 2:12–14), and ongoing transformation (2 Corinthians 3:18) throughout the entire Christian life. Every module in this curriculum is, at a deeper level, describing what the Spirit is doing in a person who is willing to cooperate with his work. Facilitators should attend to this dimension in every session — the Spirit's work is prior to and more fundamental than the curriculum's work. Crucially, the Spirit is not limited by the condition of the person he is working in — not by intellectual uncertainty, not by emotional damage, not by the protracted seasons of darkness that Module 6 names honestly. His freedom to meet people is not contingent on their readiness.

Lament: Darkness Addressed to God

One of the theological commitments running through this curriculum that deserves naming in the Spine is the status of unresolved lament. Psalm 88 — uniquely among the Psalms — ends without resolution, in pure darkness. Lamentations holds the destruction of Jerusalem before God without offering comfort. Jeremiah curses the day he was born (Jeremiah 20:14–18) and is preserved in the canon doing so. Jesus quotes the opening of Psalm 22 from the cross: "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"

This is not a collection of failures to be explained away. It is a theological statement embedded in the shape of the canon itself: honest lament spoken to God — even when it finds no resolution, even when it ends in darkness — is a legitimate and Spirit-inspired form of prayer. The God who inspired this record is not embarrassed by it. He preserved it in Scripture as normative rather than exceptional, available to every generation as permission: you may say this to God. The darkness you are in is not outside the range of what this tradition considers the life of faith.

This has practical implications for the group. Module 5 and Module 6 will almost certainly surface experiences of unresolved suffering and divine silence. The facilitator's task is not to resolve these quickly toward praise — that would contradict the very canon being studied. It is to sit with them, as the psalmist sits with his, addressed to God rather than turned away from him. The movement from lament to trust is real and attested throughout Scripture. But it is not a movement the facilitator can produce on schedule, and the pressure to produce it prematurely is itself a pastoral harm.

🧭 Holding Unresolved Lament in the Group

When someone in the group gives voice to genuine darkness — unanswered prayer, prolonged suffering, the felt absence of God — the most important facilitation move is usually the simplest: receive it without rushing to fix it. Do not immediately offer a counter-testimony or a theological resolution. Name that this is exactly what the Psalms do — speak the darkness to God honestly — and that its presence in Scripture is itself a form of validation. The Spirit can work in the silence of an unresolved psalm just as powerfully as in the triumph of a doxology. Often more so, because unresolved lament meets people where they actually are rather than where they feel they should be.

The Paradox of Sanctification: Growth Through Weakness

There is an assumption quietly embedded in most presentations of Christian growth that deserves to be examined directly before the curriculum's later modules apply it. The assumption is this: that maturity in Christ primarily looks like winning — sinning less frequently, responding better under pressure, accumulating visible evidence of transformation. Progress is the story, and the direction is upward.

Barbara Duguid, drawing on John Owen's rigorous 17th-century treatment of mortification, poses a question that reorients this entire picture: "Perhaps our greatest problem is not the reality of our sin, but our unbiblical expectations of what Christian growth should look like. What if growing in grace is more about humility, dependence, and exalting Christ than it is about defeating sin?"

This is not a counsel of passivity. It is a reframe of what the Spirit is primarily producing. Paul's testimony in 2 Corinthians 12 is instructive: he prayed three times for the thorn to be removed. It was not removed. The stated reason was so that the power of Christ might rest on him — and specifically so that it would rest on him through the weakness, not despite it. The weakness remained by design. The maturity it produced was not the maturity of someone who had overcome a limitation. It was the maturity of someone who had learned to stop trusting their own resources and to rely entirely on grace.

2 Corinthians 12:9–10

"But he said to me, 'My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness.' Therefore I will boast all the more gladly of my weaknesses, so that the power of Christ may rest upon me... For when I am weak, then I am strong."

This has a direct and practically important implication. The person in a small group who has been following Christ for twenty years and is still struggling with the same besetting sin is not necessarily a person whose faith is defective. They may be exactly where Paul was — someone whose persistent weakness has become the ongoing occasion for deeper dependence on Christ than victory would ever have produced. The question the Spirit is asking is not always "when will you finally overcome this?" It is sometimes "are you yet convinced that you cannot, and that I can?"

What this means for how spiritual growth is described — and especially for how it is discussed in a group setting — is that the signposts of genuine maturity are not always the ones we instinctively reach for. They may include: a more rapid return to Christ after failure rather than a longer interval between failures; a deeper and less defended honesty about the condition; a growing capacity to receive grace without bargaining with it; a decrease in spiritual self-congratulation; and an increase in genuine compassion for others in their failures, born of clear-eyed knowledge of one's own. None of these feel like progress from the inside. All of them are.

The Balancing Point: Mortification Is Still Real

Duguid's reframe must not be used to evacuate the genuine call to active, Spirit-enabled effort against sin. John Owen — whose work her book draws heavily on — is clear: mortification is not passive acceptance but deliberate, prayerful, Spirit-dependent effort to weaken the power of indwelling sin. The paradox is that this effort is meant to keep sin clearly before us rather than to produce the comfortable feeling that we are winning. A person who is genuinely mortifying sin will often feel more aware of it, not less, because the Spirit is opening their eyes to its depth. This is not regression. It is the very work the Spirit is doing.

It is worth noting that Dallas Willard's account of the spiritual disciplines — intentional practices that place us where God has promised to meet us — and Duguid's account of growth through weakness and dependence are not competing visions. They are approaching the same territory from different angles: Willard emphasizes training the body and will toward God; Duguid emphasizes the humbling of self-reliance that makes genuine dependence possible. Both insist on ongoing, intentional, Spirit-reliant effort. Neither describes a passive life. Module 8's integration of both threads is the point where this curriculum's call to obedience and its account of sanctification meet.

🧭 For Facilitators: How This Changes Group Discussion

The most practically important implication of this section is for how facilitators frame discussion questions about growth. A question like "how have you grown in the past year?" places every participant in the position of auditing their own progress — and will produce either performance (those who can identify growth report it) or shame (those who cannot find it stay silent). A question shaped by Duguid's reframe sounds different: "Where have you found yourself more dependent on Christ this year — even if that dependence was produced by failure?" or "Where has your own inability to change something become the occasion for grace rather than the evidence against it?" These questions open the same territory but orient it toward Christ rather than toward the self's achievement.

🕊️ For Those Who Have Been Trying and Not Winning

If you have been following Christ for years — or months — and the same struggles are still there, and the honest answer to "how have you grown?" is silence or worse: this section is written specifically for you. The New Testament does not describe a gospel that eliminates the struggle. It describes a gospel that goes into the struggle with you and produces something in the middle of it — something that looks very different from what we imagined growth would look like, and is more deeply rooted than the version we were hoping for. The thorn was not removed. The grace was sufficient. Those are not the same promise, and it matters enormously that Paul came to prefer the second.

The Church: Community as the Shape of the Gospel

A crucial dimension of this curriculum's theology — and one that becomes increasingly important as the modules progress — is that the gospel is not only an invitation for individual souls to find God. It is an invitation into a community patterned on the love of the Triune God himself.

The New Testament cannot describe salvation without describing the church. The language of adoption (Romans 8:15) places us not only in relationship with the Father but into a family of siblings. The language of the body (1 Corinthians 12) places us not only in union with Christ but in interdependence with one another. The command of John 13:34 — "Love one another as I have loved you" — is the image of Trinitarian love reproduced in human community.

This means the goal of this curriculum is not merely personal faith but belonging to and participating in the body of Christ. The fountain of living waters (Jeremiah 2:13) is not only for individual drinking. It is meant to flow outward, through those who drink, into a community that becomes itself a living sign of what the fountain is.

The Church as Healing Body

The curriculum's later modules — particularly Modules 6 and 9 — press a further and more specific claim about what community does. Christian community is not merely a context in which individuals happen to pursue their faith alongside one another. It is, in Paul's language of 1 Corinthians 12, an organic body: "If one member suffers, all suffer together." This is not a sentiment. It is a description of what the body actually is — a community in which the pain of one member is the community's pain, and the bearing of that pain is the community's calling.

This distinction — community as constitutive of healing rather than merely instrumental to it — matters more than it might initially appear. The usual assumption is that individuals come to faith and then join a community for support and fellowship. But Module 6 draws on trauma research and clinical practice to press a stronger claim: for people whose capacity for spiritual reception has been damaged — by overwhelming loss, by sustained harm, by betrayal at the hands of trusted authorities — the community itself is not a support structure added on top of the healing. It is the primary medium through which healing becomes possible. The safety of specific relationships, the patient presence of specific people who do not require recovery on a schedule, the experience of being known without being fixed: these are not preliminary to encountering God. For many people, they are the pathway to it.

The neurological dimension of this, explored in Module 6, is worth naming briefly here so facilitators understand why the posture of presence matters as much as the content of what is said. Researchers in trauma have documented that severe or sustained overwhelming experience can disrupt the brain's integration — specifically the pathways connecting propositional knowledge to felt trust. A person may hear every word of a session spoken truthfully and well and find themselves genuinely unable to receive it, not because their theology is deficient or their will is weak, but because the neurological channel has been damaged. This is not a spiritual failure. It is an injury. And it heals the way it was damaged — slowly, through genuinely safe relationships, as trust is rebuilt over time.

The pastoral and theological response to this is not despair. The Spirit of God is not limited by damaged neurology. Scripture is full of people whose capacity for trust had been devastated by experience and who were nonetheless reached by God — often in ways that bypassed their ordinary channels of reception. But the church's calling, in this light, is to be precisely the kind of community in which those alternative channels can open: unhurried, non-coercive, genuinely safe, willing to bear what the suffering person cannot yet carry alone. The healing is not the church's to produce. The conditions for healing are the church's to provide.

🕊️ For Those Hurt by the Church

The curriculum acknowledges directly what many people experience: the church — which is meant to be the visible expression of Trinitarian love — has sometimes been the source of the very wounds that make the fountain feel inaccessible. Spiritual abuse, institutional coercion, conditional acceptance dressed in theological language: these are real, they are documented across Christian history, and they are a genuine barrier to faith for many people engaging with this material.

The honest response is not to minimize this or to offer a quick defense of the institution. It is to name it, and then to hold it alongside the actual character of the God the church is meant to represent — the God who runs down the road toward the returning child, who inhabits suffering from the inside, who does not coerce. The failure of the institution does not negate the reality of the One it is meant to embody. But that argument earns no right to be heard unless the wound is first honored.

And for those whose church wound runs deep enough that the word "community" itself feels dangerous: the curriculum's vision of community as healing body is precisely not a vision of the kind of community that hurt you. It is a description of what the body is called to be — one that receives the weak rather than requiring their strength first, that sits with Psalm 88's darkness rather than insisting on premature resolution, that bears what individuals cannot bear alone. You have the right to look for that. You also have the right to take time. The invitation does not expire.

The Embodied Life: Worship That Engages the Whole Person

There is a dimension of Christian life that neither right belief nor genuine community alone can fully address: the embodied, gathered practice of worship. This curriculum has taken care to engage the mind — to give honest reasons, to name hard questions, to refuse cheap answers. Module 9 presses a further claim: the faith the curriculum points toward cannot ultimately be received only through the mind.

Scripture, sung praise, prayer spoken in common, the Lord's Supper: these are not primarily information-delivery systems. They are means of grace that engage the whole person — memory, imagination, emotion, sensation, and social belonging — not only the rational mind. What recent work in trauma and embodied formation has begun to clarify is something the church has known in practice for centuries without always being able to explain: that gathered worship creates pathways for encounter with God that purely propositional engagement cannot replicate. This is not a claim against thinking clearly about faith. It is a claim that thinking is not the whole of the person, and that God — who made bodies, who took one, who commands whole-person love in the Shema's insistence on heart, soul, mind, and strength — meets the whole person he made.

Practically, this means that a person whose intellectual channels for receiving spiritual truth have been damaged by suffering or loss may still be reached through a psalm sung in community, through the physical act of communion, through the repetition of ancient words spoken over them by people who believe them. The Spirit moves through the whole person, not only through the understanding. This is not an argument for a particular style of worship — liturgical over contemporary, formal over informal. It is an argument for the fullness of gathered practice: worship that does not reduce the encounter with God to the transmission of correct information, but that makes room for him to meet the whole person God made and is remaking.

⚖️ Formation vs. Encounter: Holding Both

Two streams of thinking about what embodied practice does can seem to be in tension. One emphasizes formation: repeated practices shape us over time, reordering our loves and desires beneath the level of conscious decision (James K.A. Smith's argument in You Are What You Love develops this from Augustine's insight that we are fundamentally creatures of desire, not merely of reason). The other emphasizes encounter: worship is not primarily self-improvement but meeting with the living God, and that encounter can be sudden, unexpected, entirely unmerited by preparation.

The Christian tradition has always held both. Dallas Willard's disciplines and Smith's liturgical formation make the same basic point from different angles: the body and its habits are not spiritually neutral — they shape what we become. And the tradition equally insists that the Spirit is not constrained by our formation — he meets people in burning bushes, in prison cells, in moments of profound darkness when no practice has prepared them. The practical wisdom is to cultivate the practices that create conditions for encounter, without treating those conditions as controls on the One who meets us in them. We prepare the ground. God sends the rain when he wills.

🧭 Implications for How Groups End

The embodied dimension of faith has a direct implication for how facilitators close Module 9 — and how they close the curriculum as a whole. A group that has spent nine sessions together on hard material has built something: trust, shared honesty, a common language. The closing session is not merely the delivery of the final content. It is, if done well, itself an act of embodied community — a gathering of specific people who have walked together and are now being sent.

Consider: reading a psalm together aloud. Praying specifically for each person by name. Acknowledging honestly what this group has been — not with performance, but with real gratitude. These acts are not supplements to the theological work. They are the theological work, completed in the body, among people who have become — however imperfectly — a small sign of the community Module 9 describes. Do not rush past them.

A seventeenth-century summary of Christian doctrine — the Westminster Shorter Catechism, drafted in 1647 — opens with a single question and answer that cuts to the heart of why this curriculum exists:

Westminster Shorter Catechism, Q. 1

Q: What is the chief end of man?
A: Man's chief end is to glorify God, and to enjoy him forever.

In plain terms: you were not made primarily to obey a set of rules, or to avoid punishment, or even to be a good person. You were made to find your deepest, most lasting satisfaction in God — and that satisfaction, fully received, turns out to be identical with his glory. The glorifying and the enjoying are not two different duties in tension with each other. They are one thing.

This is the heart of what the theologian Jonathan Edwards argued at length in his treatise The End for Which God Created the World (1765). Edwards saw that God's pursuit of his own glory and his pursuit of the joy of his creatures are not competing aims. "The happiness of the creature consists in rejoicing in God," he wrote, "by which also God is magnified and exalted." The glory of God is not diminished by our enjoyment of him — it is expressed through it. When we delight fully in God, we reflect his worth accurately. When we settle for lesser things, we implicitly claim that something else is more satisfying than he is — which is, at root, the lie that Jeremiah 2:13's broken-cistern exchange enacts.

This insight did not begin with Edwards. Augustine, writing in the fourth century, opened his Confessions with the same perception: "You made us for yourself, and our hearts are restless until they rest in you." C.S. Lewis, in Reflections on the Psalms, arrived at it through a different route: that we delight to praise what we enjoy because the praise does not merely express but completes the enjoyment — it is its natural destination. To glorify God and to enjoy him are not two acts. They are the single act of a creature who has found what it was made for.

John Piper, drawing on Edwards, has offered a crystallization of the argument: "God is most glorified in us when we are most satisfied in him." This is Piper's own distillation of Edwards's case, not a direct Edwards quotation — but it captures the logic with unusual clarity. The deepest insult to God is not irreverence. It is finding him insufficient — seeking satisfaction elsewhere, in things that cannot hold what we demand of them. Conversely: the act of finding genuine, whole-person satisfaction in God is itself worship. The two are not alternatives to be balanced. They are the same motion.

This is why the curriculum begins where it does — not with arguments for God's existence, but with the experience of the broken cistern. The thirst is the entry point. The diagnosis of Module 1 carries its full weight only when the positive vision is also named: you are thirsty because you were made for a fountain. The gospel's offer is not merely relief from pain. It is restoration to the purpose for which you were made — the "enjoy him forever" that the Catechism names as our chief end.

🧭 Using This With Groups Suspicious of Pleasure Language

Some people in the room will have been taught — explicitly or implicitly — that Christian maturity looks like wanting less, not more. That desire itself is suspect. That joy is a bonus, not a design feature. That seeking satisfaction, even in God, smells of self-centeredness.

The response is not to minimize that concern but to redirect it: the problem has never been that our desires are too strong. It is that we have aimed them at things too small. Lewis put it directly in The Weight of Glory: "We are half-hearted creatures, fooling about with drink and sex and ambition when infinite joy is offered us." The call to satisfaction in God is not a concession to self-centeredness — it is its repudiation, because self-centeredness always settles for something less than God.

For groups from ascetic traditions, where self-denial is the dominant spiritual posture, it may be worth naming that fasting, self-discipline, and sacrifice are not contradicted by this vision — they are means of disentangling desire from lesser things so that it can attach more fully to God. Willard's disciplines (Module 8) and this vision of satisfaction point in exactly the same direction.

⚖️ "Christian Hedonism" — The Label and What It Means

John Piper coined the term "Christian Hedonism" for the idea that pursuing joy in God is not only permitted but commanded — that delight in God is itself obedience. The label is deliberately provocative, meant to force the question of whether joy is peripheral or central to the Christian life.

The label can confuse, because "hedonism" in everyday usage means the pursuit of sensory pleasure as the highest good — which is precisely what this view is not. The point is not that God wants you to be comfortable. It is that the only satisfaction that fully delivers is satisfaction in him, and that settling for lesser satisfactions is a form of misalignment and ingratitude, not virtue.

The concept itself is far older than Piper and far broader than any one theological tradition. Augustine, Aquinas, Calvin, Edwards, Lewis, and Piper all arrive at versions of the same insight by different routes. It is not a distinctively Reformed or Calvinist claim. It is, arguably, the main thing the Psalter is about: "Delight yourself in the LORD, and he will give you the desires of your heart" (Psalm 37:4) is not a promise to deliver your wish list — it is a description of what happens when the desires themselves are rightly ordered toward the One who is infinitely desirable.

Before the group begins: where does each person find themselves — needing more reasons before moving, or having begun to move but wanting to understand what they are moving toward?

And: is there a difference between knowing something is true and trusting someone with your life because of it?
