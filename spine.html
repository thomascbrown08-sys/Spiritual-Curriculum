John 7:17, Belief with Intent, and the Logic of Obedient Knowing
John 7:17

"If anyone's will is to do God's will, he will know whether the teaching is from God or whether I am speaking on my own authority."

Reading John 7:17 in Context

Before building on this verse, it is worth being precise about what it says — and what it does not say. Jesus speaks these words in the Temple courts in Jerusalem during a festival, in response to a direct challenge about the authority behind his teaching. The immediate claim is specific: a person oriented toward doing God's will is in the best position to evaluate whether Jesus' teaching comes from God or from himself.

The verse is not, in its original context, a universal epistemological principle about all spiritual understanding. But the principle it contains — that genuine willingness toward God opens perception that intellectual detachment cannot achieve — is consistently supported across Scripture. Psalm 34:8 ("taste and see"), James 1:22–25 (the doer who does not forget), and the whole logic of Paul's argument in Romans 6 all point in the same direction. The verse is used here as the sharpest single statement of a pattern that runs throughout the biblical witness.

🧭 Why the Principle Extends Beyond the Immediate Context

Jesus is not making a claim about a single disputed question. He is describing how spiritual perception works as such — which is why his listeners would have recognized it as consistent with Proverbs' insistence that the fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom, and with the Psalms' pattern of the person who delights in God's law coming to understand it. The principle is not imported from outside the text; it is the spine of a larger biblical pattern.

Belief with Intent: The Volitional Dimension of Faith

The Greek word for faith — pistis — carries a volitional weight that the English word "belief" often loses. To believe, in the New Testament sense, is not merely to acknowledge that something is factually true; it is to entrust oneself to that truth. James makes the distinction sharply: "Even the demons believe — and shudder!" (James 2:19). The demons have accurate theology. What they lack is the willingness to submit to it.

This curriculum uses the phrase Belief with Intent as a pedagogical coinage — not a translation of any single Greek word, but a phrase constructed to describe what the biblical witness consistently portrays saving faith to involve: not mere intellectual assent, but the heart's genuine orientation toward God, a turning of the will that says, in effect, I trust this, and I intend to live accordingly. The Reformers gave this a formal structure, describing faith as having three components: notitia (knowledge of the facts), assensus (agreement that they are true), and fiducia (personal trust and reliance). This three-part framework is systematic theology derived from careful reading of the biblical text — it is not a direct lexical analysis of what the Greek word pistis carries as its dictionary meaning, but a conceptual map that accurately captures distinctions the New Testament makes across multiple passages. The third element — fiducia — is what distinguishes the faith that saves from the assent that even the demons possess (James 2:19).

A note for those who want the lexical precision: there is a significant and still-active scholarly debate about Pauline phrases like pistis Christou — appearing in Romans 3:22 and Galatians 2:16 — which can be translated either as "faith in Christ" (the traditional reading) or "the faithfulness of Christ" (a reading with growing scholarly support). If the "faithfulness of Christ" reading is correct, Paul is grounding justification in Christ's own covenant faithfulness rather than in our act of believing. This is worth noting because it strengthens rather than undermines this curriculum's Christological assurance argument: we are held not by the quality of our grip but by the faithfulness of the One our trust is directed toward.

Three Things John 7:17 Teaches

Understanding has a moral dimension. Spiritual perception requires a kind of alignment. Augustine: "Our heart is restless until it rests in Thee." The will shapes what we are willing to see.

The invitation is genuinely open. "If anyone's will" — not restricted to the sufficiently prepared or the already devout. The very desire to seek is already the Spirit's work in you.

Obedience is the epistemology. The evidence invites movement, and the movement produces further evidence. Faith is not a leap in the dark. It is a step toward a light you can already partly see.

Assurance: Christological, Not Introspective

Here is a pastoral danger that must be named plainly, before it can do harm: any framework that locates assurance in the quality of one's faith — the genuineness of one's intention, the sincerity of one's orientation, the consistency of one's obedience — will eventually produce anxiety in anyone honest enough to examine themselves carefully. Paul's Romans 7 experience is not the exception; it is the norm for sincere believers.

The Reformation's pastoral breakthrough was precisely to redirect this question. Assurance does not come from asking "was my faith genuine enough?" or "was my intention sincere enough?" It comes from asking a different question entirely: Is Christ faithful? And the answer Scripture gives is unambiguous: yes. Always. Completely. Without condition.

Luther found peace not by examining the depth of his own faith but by fixing his gaze on the faithfulness of the One his faith was directed toward. The strength of our grip on Christ is not what holds us. Christ's grip on us is what holds us. Assurance is therefore not introspective — it is christological. We look to Christ, not to the quality of our looking.

🕊️ For Those Who Have Prayed Many Times and Wonder if It Counted

There is a specific pastoral trap that this curriculum is designed to avoid: the idea that salvation is a transaction secured by the right prayer said with sufficient sincerity. The New Testament's basis for assurance is not the quality of our prayer but the faithfulness of the God who hears it. Romans 10:9–10 describes the direction of the heart, not the precision of a formula. If you have genuinely turned toward Christ — even haltingly, even inconsistently, even in confusion — the question is not whether you said it correctly but whether God is faithful to his promise. He is.

For those wondering whether they need to pray again: the answer is almost certainly no. The call is not to repeat the transaction but to trust the Promiser. Hebrews 10:23 — "Let us hold fast the confession of our hope without wavering, for he who promised is faithful" — is addressed to people who are already doubting, already wavering. The answer given is not "pray again" but "hold fast — look at who made the promise."

🧭 For Group Discussion on Assurance

Self-examination is not wrong — Paul commends it in 2 Corinthians 13:5. But self-examination is meant to direct us back to Christ, not to produce a verdict on whether we qualify. The person who examines themselves honestly and finds inconsistency is invited to return to the vine, not to question whether they were ever truly connected. The question "is my faith real?" is answered not by analyzing the faith but by returning to its Object.

The Holy Spirit's Prior Initiative

It is vital to be clear about what makes the first movement of faith possible. Ephesians 2:8 says: "For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God." The gift is not merely salvation — the gift is the faith itself. Our turning toward God is itself a response to God's prior turning toward us.

The very desire to seek is itself evidence that the Spirit is at work in you — which means the question "but what if I'm not willing enough?" is already answered by the fact that you are asking it.

⚖️ Arminian and Reformed Traditions: Agreement and Difference

Both the Arminian tradition (prevenient grace) and the Reformed tradition (effectual calling) agree on the decisive point: God is first. We do not take a step toward God and then God responds. God moves toward us, and our step is a response to his movement already underway. This is the point the curriculum rests on, and both traditions affirm it.

Where the traditions differ significantly is in the nature and mechanism of that priority: whether grace can be resisted (Arminianism affirms it can; Reformed theology generally holds it cannot for those who are effectually called), and whether election is conditional on foreseen faith or unconditional. These are genuine and important differences within the family of orthodox Christianity — not peripheral disputes. The curriculum is designed to be usable across both traditions, but facilitators from either background should be aware that this is a live area of theological disagreement.

🧭 The Spirit Throughout the Curriculum

The Holy Spirit is not merely the one who enables the first movement of faith. He is the active agent of conviction (John 16:8), illumination (1 Corinthians 2:12–14), and ongoing transformation (2 Corinthians 3:18) throughout the entire Christian life. Every module in this curriculum is, at a deeper level, describing what the Spirit is doing in a person who is willing to cooperate with his work. Facilitators should attend to this dimension in every session — the Spirit's work is prior to and more fundamental than the curriculum's work.

The Paradox of Sanctification: Growth Through Weakness

There is an assumption quietly embedded in most presentations of Christian growth that deserves to be examined directly before the curriculum's later modules apply it. The assumption is this: that maturity in Christ primarily looks like winning — sinning less frequently, responding better under pressure, accumulating visible evidence of transformation. Progress is the story, and the direction is upward.

Barbara Duguid, drawing on John Owen's rigorous 17th-century treatment of mortification, poses a question that reorients this entire picture: "Perhaps our greatest problem is not the reality of our sin, but our unbiblical expectations of what Christian growth should look like. What if growing in grace is more about humility, dependence, and exalting Christ than it is about defeating sin?"

This is not a counsel of passivity. It is a reframe of what the Spirit is primarily producing. Paul's testimony in 2 Corinthians 12 is instructive: he prayed three times for the thorn to be removed. It was not removed. The stated reason was so that the power of Christ might rest on him — and specifically so that it would rest on him through the weakness, not despite it. The weakness remained by design. The maturity it produced was not the maturity of someone who had overcome a limitation. It was the maturity of someone who had learned to stop trusting their own resources and to rely entirely on grace.

2 Corinthians 12:9–10

"But he said to me, 'My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness.' Therefore I will boast all the more gladly of my weaknesses, so that the power of Christ may rest upon me... For when I am weak, then I am strong."

This has a direct and practically important implication. The person in a small group who has been following Christ for twenty years and is still struggling with the same besetting sin is not necessarily a person whose faith is defective. They may be exactly where Paul was — someone whose persistent weakness has become the ongoing occasion for deeper dependence on Christ than victory would ever have produced. The question the Spirit is asking is not always "when will you finally overcome this?" It is sometimes "are you yet convinced that you cannot, and that I can?"

What this means for how spiritual growth is described — and especially for how it is discussed in a group setting — is that the signposts of genuine maturity are not always the ones we instinctively reach for. They may include: a more rapid return to Christ after failure rather than a longer interval between failures; a deeper and less defended honesty about the condition; a growing capacity to receive grace without bargaining with it; a decrease in spiritual self-congratulation; and an increase in genuine compassion for others in their failures, born of clear-eyed knowledge of one's own. None of these feel like progress from the inside. All of them are.

The Balancing Point: Mortification Is Still Real

Duguid's reframe must not be used to evacuate the genuine call to active, Spirit-enabled effort against sin. John Owen — whose work her book draws heavily on — is clear: mortification is not passive acceptance but deliberate, prayerful, Spirit-dependent effort to weaken the power of indwelling sin. The paradox is that this effort is meant to keep sin clearly before us rather than to produce the comfortable feeling that we are winning. A person who is genuinely mortifying sin will often feel more aware of it, not less, because the Spirit is opening their eyes to its depth. This is not regression. It is the very work the Spirit is doing. Module 8's material on obedience and Module 7's signposts of growth should both be read in this light.

🧭 For Facilitators: How This Changes Group Discussion

The most practically important implication of this section is for how facilitators frame discussion questions about growth. A question like "how have you grown in the past year?" places every participant in the position of auditing their own progress — and will produce either performance (those who can identify growth report it) or shame (those who cannot find it stay silent). A question shaped by Duguid's reframe sounds different: "Where have you found yourself more dependent on Christ this year — even if that dependence was produced by failure?" or "Where has your own inability to change something become the occasion for grace rather than the evidence against it?" These questions open the same territory but orient it toward Christ rather than toward the self's achievement.

🕊️ For Those Who Have Been Trying and Not Winning

If you have been following Christ for years — or months — and the same struggles are still there, and the honest answer to "how have you grown?" is silence or worse: this section is written specifically for you. The New Testament does not describe a gospel that eliminates the struggle. It describes a gospel that goes into the struggle with you and produces something in the middle of it — something that looks very different from what we imagined growth would look like, and is more deeply rooted than the version we were hoping for. The thorn was not removed. The grace was sufficient. Those are not the same promise, and it matters enormously that Paul came to prefer the second.

The Church: Community as the Shape of the Gospel

A crucial dimension of this curriculum's theology — and one that becomes increasingly important as the modules progress — is that the gospel is not only an invitation for individual souls to find God. It is an invitation into a community patterned on the love of the Triune God himself.

The New Testament cannot describe salvation without describing the church. The language of adoption (Romans 8:15) places us not only in relationship with the Father but into a family of siblings. The language of the body (1 Corinthians 12) places us not only in union with Christ but in interdependence with one another. The command of John 13:34 — "Love one another as I have loved you" — is the image of Trinitarian love reproduced in human community.

This means the goal of this curriculum is not merely personal faith but belonging to and participating in the body of Christ. The fountain of living waters (Jeremiah 2:13) is not only for individual drinking. It is meant to flow outward, through those who drink, into a community that becomes itself a living sign of what the fountain is. Module 9 develops this most fully, but the thread runs from Module 1 onward.

🕊️ For Those Hurt by the Church

The curriculum acknowledges directly what many people experience: the church — which is meant to be the visible expression of Trinitarian love — has sometimes been the source of the very wounds that make the fountain feel inaccessible. Spiritual abuse, institutional coercion, conditional acceptance dressed in theological language: these are real, they are documented across Christian history, and they are a genuine barrier to faith for many people engaging with this material.

The honest response is not to minimize this or to offer a quick defense of the institution. It is to name it, and then to hold it alongside the actual character of the God the church is meant to represent — the God who runs down the road toward the returning child, who inhabits suffering from the inside, who does not coerce. The failure of the institution does not negate the reality of the One it is meant to embody. But that argument earns no right to be heard unless the wound is first honored.
