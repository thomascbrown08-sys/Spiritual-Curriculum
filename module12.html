We Believe

The Creeds as the Church's Hard-Won Voice
Follows Module 11: Old Errors, New Labels · Advanced: The Chalcedonian Definition · The Athanasian Creed

A creed (from Latin credo, "I believe") is a formal statement of shared faith — not a magic formula, not a substitute for personal trust in God, but a voice the whole church has learned to say together. The creeds are the church's attempt — across several centuries, in multiple languages, under enormous pressure — to say the most important things about God with enough precision that they cannot be quietly twisted into something else.

Every phrase was paid for. The word homoousios ("of the same substance" — the key word of the Nicene Creed) cost decades of controversy, exile, political interference, and near-collapse of the Eastern church. Four syllables. The church fought that hard over one word because the word carried a world: if Jesus is similar to God but not the same nature, the incarnation cannot accomplish what it needs to, and we remain in our sins. The precision was not pedantry. It was pastoral urgency.

If you come from a tradition that does not use the creeds liturgically — many Baptist, nondenominational, and Pentecostal churches do not — these documents may feel like someone else's inheritance. They are not. The creeds predate every denominational division in existence. They are not Anglican or Catholic or Reformed property. They were produced before any of those distinctions existed, and they belong to the whole church — received or not.

If Module 11 asked why the creeds exist, showing the errors they were written against, this module asks what they say and why it still matters. Readers who arrive here first should spend time in Module 11 for the backstory; the two are designed as companions.

The Apostles' Creed

Despite its name, the Apostles' Creed was not written by the apostles. The legend that each of the twelve contributed one clause arose in the early medieval period and is pious legend, not history. What is almost certainly true is that it preserves, in condensed form, the baptismal confession of the Roman church from at least the 2nd century — a summary of the faith given to catechumens (those receiving instruction before baptism) as they prepared to enter the community. It is the oldest widely used creed in Christendom, and it remains the most widely recognized.

The Apostles' Creed I believe in God, the Father Almighty,
Creator of heaven and earth.

And in Jesus Christ, his only Son, our Lord:
who was conceived by the Holy Spirit,
born of the Virgin Mary,
suffered under Pontius Pilate,
was crucified, died, and was buried.
He descended to the dead.
On the third day he rose again from the dead.
He ascended into heaven
and is seated at the right hand of God the Father Almighty.
From there he will come to judge the living and the dead.

I believe in the Holy Spirit,
the holy catholic church,
the communion of saints,
the forgiveness of sins,
the resurrection of the body,
and the life everlasting. Amen.

A Clause-by-Clause Reading

The creed is not decorative. Every line carries weight that centuries of controversy pressed into it. What follows tries to hear what each clause is doing.

"I believe in God, the Father Almighty, Creator of heaven and earth"

Almighty translates the Greek pantokrator — not merely "very powerful" but "sovereign over all things." Creator of heaven and earth is a direct anti-Gnostic declaration: matter is not evil, the physical world is not a prison, and the God who made it is the same God who redeems it. The Marcionite move — splitting Creator from Redeemer — is excluded before the creed has finished its first sentence.

"And in Jesus Christ, his only Son, our Lord"

Only Son — not one of many divine beings, not a high-ranking creature. Anti-Arian before Arius was born. Lord is the Greek Kyrios — the word the Septuagint (the Greek Old Testament) used to translate the divine name YHWH. Calling Jesus Lord in the 1st century was not a courtesy title. It was a claim about who he is in relation to the God of Israel.

Anti-Arian → Arianism in Module 11
"Who was conceived by the Holy Spirit, born of the Virgin Mary"

He was born — a real birth from a real woman. This is anti-Docetist: the humanity is genuine, not a costume. God did not descend into a human body like a hand into a glove; he took on human existence from the inside. The Spirit's role also connects the incarnation to the entire Trinity from its first moment.

A note worth knowing: the Council of Ephesus (431 AD) declared Mary to be Theotokos — literally "God-bearer" or "the one who gave birth to God." The council's intent was not primarily to honor Mary but to protect the doctrine of Christ: if the one born of Mary was truly God incarnate from the first moment of conception, then Mary genuinely bore God in the flesh. The title is a Christological statement before it is a Marian one, and it was affirmed precisely to exclude the view that Mary bore only the human Jesus, into whom the divine Son later descended. Protestant traditions have generally not used the title liturgically, while recognizing the Christological truth it was coined to protect. Whatever weight a reader's tradition places on Mary herself, the creed's mention of her name is doing the same work Ephesus did: nailing the incarnation to a real woman, a real birth, a real entry into human flesh.

Anti-Docetist → Docetism in Module 11
"Suffered under Pontius Pilate, was crucified, died, and was buried"

Pontius Pilate is here not for historical precision's sake, but because his name nails the events to actual history. This is not myth or inner spiritual experience — a dateable Roman official, whose records survive, was involved. The suffering and death were real. This is also one of the creed's most pastorally profound moments: the one we call Lord genuinely suffered. Hebrews 4:15 does not say we have a high priest who understands our weakness academically. He was "tempted in every way, just as we are." That only holds if what the creed says here is true.

"He descended to the dead"

The most debated clause in the creed. The Latin is ad inferna. The range of serious interpretation: (1) he truly died — entered the state of death completely; (2) he proclaimed victory to imprisoned spirits (1 Peter 3:18–19); (3) he liberated the righteous dead of the Old Testament era. None of these requires the popular image of Christ suffering in hell. Calvin's interpretation — that Christ experienced the full weight of divine judgment in the depths of his dying — is a serious theological option. What all share: his death was complete and real. He did not exit through a back door.

"On the third day he rose again from the dead"

Bodily — rose, not merely appeared spiritually. Paul's argument in 1 Corinthians 15 depends entirely on this: "if Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile and you are still in your sins" (v.17). The resurrection is not a coda; it is the hinge of everything.

"He ascended into heaven and is seated at the right hand of God the Father Almighty"

The ascension is underpreached. It is not "Jesus left" — it is the exaltation of humanity, in the person of the risen Christ, into the presence of God. Something that was not there before is now there: a human being, in a glorified body, at the center of divine reality. Seated at the right hand means enthroned, reigning, and actively interceding. Romans 8:34 and Hebrews 7:25 both describe the ascended Christ as making intercession for his people. He did not finish his priestly work at the cross.

"From there he will come to judge the living and the dead"

Eschatology in one clause. The creed does not specify when or how — those questions have occupied Christians since the first century. What it affirms is the shape: history is moving toward judgment and consummation. The one executed under Pontius Pilate will return as judge. This is not triumphalism; it is hope with weight.

"The holy catholic church"

Catholic here means universal, not Roman Catholic — a distinction worth stating directly because the confusion is nearly universal. The word derives from the Greek kata holon, "according to the whole." The holy catholic church is the whole church across all times and places. Roman Catholics did not appropriate this word; they retained it while others walked away. It predates every denominational division and belongs to the whole tradition.

"The communion of saints"

The church is not only the living. We are joined to those who have gone before — the body of Christ is one body across death. When we receive the Lord's Supper, we do so in the company of Augustine and Athanasius and the martyrs and all who have called on the name of the Lord across twenty centuries. The tradition is family.

"The resurrection of the body, and the life everlasting"

Resurrection of the body — not the immortality of the soul, which is a Greek philosophical concept the New Testament does not teach as its central hope. The creed insists on bodily resurrection: redeemed matter, not escaped matter. The new creation will be material. This is the final anti-Gnostic move: even at the end of all things, God redeems the physical rather than abandoning it.

The Nicene Creed

The Nicene Creed is technically the Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed — produced in first form at Nicaea (325 AD) and expanded at Constantinople (381 AD). It is the most widely used creed in Christendom: Roman Catholic, Eastern Orthodox, Anglican, Lutheran, Methodist, and many Reformed traditions all use it. It is the ecumenical (from Greek oikoumene, "the whole inhabited world" — representing the whole church, not a regional faction) creed par excellence.

The Nicene Creed We believe in one God,
the Father Almighty,
maker of heaven and earth,
of all things visible and invisible.

We believe in one Lord, Jesus Christ,
the only-begotten Son of God,
born of the Father before all ages,
God from God, Light from Light,
true God from true God,
begotten, not made,
of one Being with the Father. [homoousios]
Through him all things were made.
For us and for our salvation he came down from heaven;
he became incarnate by the Holy Spirit and the virgin Mary,
and was made man.
For our sake he was crucified under Pontius Pilate;
he suffered death and was buried.
On the third day he rose again in accordance with the Scriptures.
He ascended into heaven
and is seated at the right hand of the Father.
He will come again with glory
to judge the living and the dead,
and his kingdom will have no end.

We believe in the Holy Spirit, the Lord, the giver of life,
who proceeds from the Father [and the Son — Western addition; see below],
who with the Father and the Son is worshiped and glorified,
who has spoken through the prophets.

We believe in one holy catholic and apostolic church.
We acknowledge one baptism for the forgiveness of sins.
We look for the resurrection of the dead,
and the life of the world to come. Amen.

Note on the text: The phrase [and the Son] reflects the Western filioque addition, adopted by Rome in 1014. Eastern Orthodox texts read simply "who proceeds from the Father." The full story is below.

The Story Behind the Words

Athanasius of Alexandria c. 296–373 AD He attended Nicaea as a young deacon, assistant to the bishop of Alexandria, and became the intellectual center of the anti-Arian cause. When the political winds shifted, Arian emperors exiled him five times — 17 years total away from his congregation. Each time he returned. The phrase coined about him, Athanasius contra mundum — "Athanasius against the world" — captures the reality: at several points he was among the only major bishops still holding the Nicene position while the majority accommodated the empire. His On the Incarnation, written while still a young man, is among the most accessible early theological works. C.S. Lewis wrote the introduction to the modern edition.

Nicaea, 325 AD. Constantine, newly sole emperor, called approximately 300 bishops to a resort town in Asia Minor to settle the Arian controversy. Arius and his supporters were present. Athanasius, not yet a bishop, attended as assistant to the bishop of Alexandria and became the intellectual spine of the opposition. After weeks of debate, the council produced the word that would define orthodoxy: homoousios — "of the same substance" as the Father. Not homoiousios — "of similar substance," the Arian compromise. The difference is a single letter: iota, the smallest in the Greek alphabet. It carried a world of consequence. Arius was condemned and exiled.

What happened next is the story most people do not know. The decades after Nicaea were very nearly a catastrophe for the Nicene position. Arian emperors succeeded Constantine. Athanasius was exiled five times. At several points, the Arian position commanded the majority of Eastern bishops, backed by imperial favor. Jerome, writing later, put it memorably: the world had groaned to find itself Arian. The creed we recite today was held, for decades, by a minority who paid for it.

Constantinople, 381 AD. Under the emperor Theodosius, the Nicene position was finally consolidated. The creed was expanded — especially its section on the Holy Spirit, whose full divinity had come under sustained challenge from the Pneumatomachians (literally "Spirit-fighters" — those who accepted the Nicene position on the Son but denied the full divinity of the Spirit). The expanded creed affirmed the Spirit as "the Lord, the giver of life, who with the Father and the Son is worshiped and glorified." What we call the Nicene Creed today is technically this Niceno-Constantinopolitan text.

The Cappadocian Fathers 4th century Three figures who did the theological heavy lifting between Nicaea and Constantinople: Basil the Great (bishop of Caesarea), his brother Gregory of Nyssa, and their friend Gregory of Nazianzus. They developed the precise Trinitarian vocabulary that made the Nicene position intellectually defensible: one ousia, three hypostases — one substance or being, three persons or subsistences. This framework clarified that the church was not teaching three Gods (tritheism) or one God wearing three masks (Modalism), but one divine being eternally existing as three distinct persons. Their contribution was finding language precise enough to carry what Scripture teaches.

Key Phrases

"Born of the Father before all ages — begotten, not made"

"Before all ages" directly answers Arius: there was not a time when the Son was not. His generation from the Father is eternal, not a moment in time. Begotten, not made draws the critical distinction: the Son's relationship to the Father is not creator-to-creature but Father-to-Son — a relationship within the divine being itself.

"God from God, Light from Light, true God from true God"

The repetition is deliberate hammering — every exit closed. The Son is not derived divinity or reduced divinity. The light metaphor: when a candle lights another candle, the new flame is not a lesser flame. It is genuinely fire, fully light, the same thing. Genuine derivation without diminishment.

"Of one Being with the Father" [homoousios]

The word that cost more than any other in the history of Christian doctrine. Everything depends on it. If Jesus is merely similar to God, the incarnation cannot accomplish what the New Testament says it accomplishes. A mediator who is almost God on one side and almost human on the other touches neither shore. The church fought for this word because they understood, correctly, that everything was at stake in it.

"For us and for our salvation he came down from heaven"

The creed pauses here to name the purpose. Not an abstract cosmic event — for us. The incarnation is aimed. The one who is "true God from true God" chose to enter, specifically and personally, what we call history.

"The Holy Spirit, the Lord, the giver of life… who with the Father and the Son is worshiped and glorified"

Constantinople's contribution — the expansion of what Nicaea left underdeveloped. The Spirit is named Lord (the divine title given also to Christ) and giver of life. Most precisely: the Spirit is worshiped and glorified together with Father and Son. This is a statement of co-equal divinity as clear as language can make it. The Pneumatomachians who wanted to honor the Son but subordinate the Spirit were answered directly: worship names the nature.

The Filioque — One Word That Divided a Billion Christians

The Latin word filioque means "and the Son." The Western church added it progressively — first appearing in Spain (Toledo, 589), spreading across the Frankish church, and formally adopted by Rome in 1014 — so that the line read: "the Holy Spirit, who proceeds from the Father and the Son" — rather than simply "from the Father." The Eastern church never agreed and considers it an unauthorized alteration of an ecumenically agreed text.

The theological question is whether the Spirit's eternal origin is from the Father alone (the Eastern position) or from the Father and Son together (the Western). This is not merely wordsmithing — the two positions carry different implications for how we understand the Trinity's internal relations and the nature of spiritual authority. Most serious theologians on both sides now acknowledge the underlying Trinitarian conviction is likely shared, even if the formulation differs.

The historical weight is enormous. The filioque is one of the contributing causes of the Great Schism of 1054 — the split between Eastern Orthodoxy and Roman Catholicism that has never been formally healed. Over a billion Christians remain in separated communions partly because of this word. This is the Vocabulary Trap from Module 10 operating at civilizational scale: a real theological disagreement, amplified by institutional pride and circumstance, calcified into permanent division.

Going Further: Two More Creeds

Augustine of Hippo 354–430 AD Not a creed writer, but the West's greatest interpreter of the Nicene settlement. His Sermons on the Creed — delivered to catechumens preparing for baptism — remain among the most vivid expositions of the Apostles' Creed ever written. His personal story is among the most compelling in Christian intellectual history: a restless North African academic who spent years resisting faith while his mother Monica prayed, converted at 32, and spent the rest of his life thinking harder about God than almost anyone before or since. His Confessions opens with the sentence that became the most quoted line in Christian literature: "Our heart is restless until it rests in thee."

Two more documents press deeper into the Christological and Trinitarian questions the Apostles' and Nicene Creeds raised. They are available as linked reading for those who want to go further.

The Chalcedonian Definition (451 AD) is the church's most precise formulation of the incarnation — the answer to centuries of pressing the question of how Jesus can be fully human and fully divine in one person. The "four fences" of Chalcedon — without mixture, without confusion, without separation, without division — define orthodox Christology by excluding what it is not. This document settled the Docetism and Nestorianism controversies and established the grammar of Christology that the vast majority of Christians still use.

The Athanasian Creed is the most philosophically dense of the creeds — a sustained exposition of Trinitarian doctrine at maximum precision. Best approached as advanced material. The linked page treats it as such, including honest engagement with its famous "damnatory clauses."

Receiving the Gift

The creeds were not dropped from heaven. They were produced by people who argued across decades, were exiled, lost institutional support, watched friends capitulate, and kept going because they believed the truth about God was worth that much. Athanasius spent seventeen years away from his congregation rather than sign a compromise he believed was false. The Cappadocian Fathers developed their precise Trinitarian vocabulary not in seminar rooms but in the middle of live controversy, under real pressure to simplify or accommodate. These are not abstract documents. They are the residue of people who cared.

Receiving the creeds is receiving a gift from those people. Every time the church says homoousios — or its translation, "of one Being with the Father" — it is reaching back across seventeen centuries and accepting something that was paid for. The least we can do is understand what was at stake.

Some readers will feel a specific hesitation: I cannot honestly recite something I don't fully understand. This is a scruple worth taking seriously. But the creed was not designed for people who had completed their theological education. It was designed for catechumens — people preparing for baptism, often just beginning their understanding of the faith. They were invited to say the creed not as a declaration of complete theological clarity but as an act of joining — adding their voice to the centuries-long act of worship and witness that the church has been engaged in from the beginning. The understanding can grow inside the saying. Many Christians report that the creed has meant more to them as they have aged precisely because they began saying it before they fully understood it, and found that understanding arrived gradually through the practice.

Finally: in a culture that treats all truth claims as equally valid, all religious opinions as purely personal, and all theology as ultimately a matter of taste — saying we believe, specifically and pointedly, in the words the church has used for over a thousand years, is a countercultural act. Not triumphalism. Not gatekeeping. Witness. The creed says: we are not making this up as we go. We received something. We are passing it on. The God who is "true God from true God" is not a projection of our preferences — he is the one who actually is, and these are the words we have found, at great cost, to say so. For anyone who has traveled the arc of this curriculum from a place of distance or wounding, this may be the most important thing the creeds offer: not a wall to climb before you are welcome, but a voice to join when you are ready — the church saying, with you and for you, what it has always believed about the One who has been searching for you far longer than you have been looking for him.
