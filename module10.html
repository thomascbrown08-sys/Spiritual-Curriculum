Divided We Fall

Recognizing the Enemy's Playbook in the Family of God
Module 9 described what the church is meant to be. This module addresses why it so often isn't — and why the gap between that vision and reality is not mainly a failure of effort or sincerity. There is an intelligent adversary at work in church conflict, and his methods are precise enough to name. Naming them is not defeatism; it is the first move of a peacemaker who has decided to see clearly. · Companion: Module 11 addresses how the same adversary works through doctrinal confusion rather than relational tactics.

Meet the Strategist

There is a reason Jesus prayed for unity the night before the cross. "May they be one, as we are one" — words from John 17, addressed to the Father, about the people who would carry the gospel into the world. He was not praying for uniformity of opinion. He was praying for the kind of coherent love that would make the watching world stop and ask what was different about these people. "By this all people will know that you are my disciples," he said elsewhere, "if you have love for one another."

The Enemy heard that prayer. He has been working against it ever since.

This is not metaphor. The New Testament describes a real adversary with real intentions: a deceiver (John 8:44 — "the father of lies"), an accuser (Revelation 12:10 — "the accuser of our brothers"), and an adversary (1 Peter 5:8 — "your adversary the devil prowls around like a roaring lion"). But his most effective work in the church does not look like a lion. It looks like a misunderstanding that festers. It looks like a reasonable concern that somehow keeps escalating. It looks like two people who love Scripture arriving at the same passage and concluding that the other person must not really love God.

This module takes the Enemy seriously as a strategist — not a bogeyman, not a vague spiritual force, but an intelligent adversary who has had two thousand years to study what fractures the church, what escalates disagreement, what makes reconciliation feel like betrayal. His methods are identifiable. They follow patterns. And the first step toward peacemaking in a church conflict is often simply being able to name what is actually happening, because named tactics lose much of their power.

📖 A WORD ABOUT WHAT THIS MODULE IS NOT

This module does not explain away real wrongs. Church conflicts often involve genuine failures, genuine harm, and genuine theological disagreement that deserves honest engagement. The Enemy's playbook does not create conflict from nothing — it works with real material. Recognizing his tactics does not mean the concerns you or others have raised are invalid. It means those concerns deserve to be addressed without the Enemy's amplification — on their actual merits, at the actual level of seriousness they warrant, by people who can distinguish their legitimate conviction from the manipulation that has been layered on top of it.

This module is also not a tool for winning arguments. If you find yourself using it primarily to explain why your opponents are being manipulated rather than why you might be — it is not working as intended.

The Family Frame

The New Testament does not primarily describe the church as an institution, a corporation, a movement, or a club. It describes it as a family. "Household of God" (Ephesians 2:19). "Brothers and sisters" — used more than two hundred times. "Father" for God in a way that makes every believer a sibling to every other. The family language is not decorative. It shapes how everything else is supposed to work: conflict resolution, accountability, care for the weak, the handling of wounds.

Families fight. That is not evidence that the family model has failed — it is evidence that something worth protecting is under pressure. The Enemy's goal in any given church conflict is not just to produce a disagreement. It is to make the people involved experience each other as threats rather than family — to reframe brothers and sisters as opponents, to make backing down feel like betraying God rather than protecting a relationship. Once that reframe is complete, the conflict becomes self-sustaining. The original issue almost ceases to matter.

The peacemaker in a family dispute is not the person who avoids the conflict. It is the person who can hold onto the family frame when everyone around them has forgotten it — who can say, to both parties, "I know what this feels like. I also know who you both are to each other, and to God, and that matters more than who is right about this." That person is not a compromiser. They are, in the New Testament's precise language, a peacemaker — and Jesus called them blessed, and called them children of God (Matthew 5:9).

How to Use This Module

The nine entries below cover the most common tactical patterns the Enemy deploys in church conflict. They are organized from most personal and immediate to most structural and cultural. You do not need to read them in order.

If you are reading this because you are currently hurting — because you have been in a church conflict that damaged you, cost you community, or left you questioning whether belonging to a church is worth the risk — start with Sub-Module 3: The Wounded Proxy War or Sub-Module 7: The Social Hostage. Both were written with you specifically in mind.

If you are in an active conflict that has not yet become a wound — a dispute in your congregation that feels stuck, that keeps escalating, that you cannot see a way through — start with Sub-Module 1: The Escalation Trap or Sub-Module 2: The Vocabulary Trap. These are the most common presenting patterns.

If you are a lay leader or facilitator looking for tools to hand people in your community, the Peacemaker's Move section in each entry gives specific language — what to say, not just what to think.

If you want the fuller historical and theological treatment, each entry has a Going Deeper branch. These are not required for the practical content, but they provide the evidence that the stakes are real and the patterns are old.

The Tactics at a Glance

One-paragraph summaries of each entry. Follow the links for the full treatment.

1 · The Escalation Trap — "Making Every Hill a Dying Hill"

The Enemy takes a real disagreement — which may be entirely legitimate — and quietly elevates it from preference to conviction, conviction to essential doctrine, essential doctrine to salvation-level. By the time anyone notices, backing down feels like apostasy. Nobody consciously chose the escalation; it happened to both parties simultaneously. The remedy is forcing the question of actual doctrinal level into the room before advancing the argument.

2 · The Vocabulary Trap — "Speaking Different Languages in the Same Room"

The Enemy needs no false teaching. He only needs two people to use the same word — grace, authority, accountability, submission — to mean substantially different things long enough to conclude the other person must be dangerous. Words carry different freight depending on the tradition, the reading, and the painful histories people bring to them. The remedy is defining terms together before arguing about them.

3 · The Wounded Proxy War — "Fighting About Theology When You're Really Fighting About Pain"

Wounded people often cannot say "I was hurt and I don't trust authority structures anymore." But they can argue about elder governance, church discipline, or women's roles — topics where their real wound has a legitimate theological address. The argument is real and deserves engagement. But it is also carrying freight it was never built to carry, making resolution structurally impossible unless someone is skilled enough to hold both the theological validity and the wound simultaneously.

4 · The Uniformity Substitution — "Confusing Agreement with Unity"

Once a church mistakes uniformity (same practices, same conclusions, same style) for unity (coherence of purpose and love amid diversity), the Enemy doesn't need to introduce conflict. He just waits. Any difference becomes a threat to unity by definition — and Paul's entire body metaphor, which insists on diversity of function as necessary to the body's health, has been quietly disabled.

5 · The Certainty Weapon — "When Confidence Becomes a Cudgel"

The appropriate virtue of conviction — holding one's beliefs with confidence because they are true — gets converted into a tool for silencing rather than persuading. The signal is that certainty is deployed not to illuminate but to end conversations: to make questioning feel like faithlessness, and to position doubt as spiritual weakness. This is most dangerous in communities that have rightly learned to resist relativism, because their strength becomes the mechanism of harm.

6 · The Tradition Trap — "Thus Saith the Committee of 1987"

The weight of Scripture gets quietly attached to what is actually the weight of institutional habit or generational preference. Once tradition is treated as load-bearing in the same way Scripture is, every reforming impulse looks like an attack on the Bible itself. The Pharisees' korban controversy — Jesus' clearest confrontation of this mechanism — demonstrates how a tradition can void a commandment while everyone involved believes they are being faithful.

7 · The Social Hostage — "Your Friends or Your Convictions"

The Enemy quietly attaches belonging and community ties to theological conformity, so that asking honest questions — or simply changing one's mind — feels like losing one's people. This exploits the God-given need for community and turns it into leverage. The person is not threatened with punishment; they are threatened with loneliness. This tactic is most effective, and most cruel, on people who have already been isolated or hurt.

8 · The Celebrity Fracture — "I Am of Paul; I Am of Apollos"

Paul named this pattern in 1 Corinthians 1, and it has never gone away — but digital media has amplified it dramatically. Tribes form around teachers rather than around Christ, so that when a teacher falls, pivots, or is challenged, the tribe experiences it as an attack on the gospel. In the contemporary version, congregations fracture not over their own pastor's theology but over which outside teachers different members are listening to.

9 · The Purity Spiral — "More Faithful Than Thou"

An escalating competition for doctrinal or behavioral purity in which the goalposts keep moving, fellowship becomes impossible with anyone outside the innermost circle, and the community increasingly resembles a closed system. It is driven by people who genuinely love Scripture and are trying to honor God — which is what makes it so difficult to name. Within a generation, positions that would have been considered extreme become the assumed baseline.
