For Those Ready to Respond

Responding to the Gospel

What It Means, What It Involves, and What Comes Next

This page exists for anyone who has encountered the material on this site — or the God this site describes — and wants to understand what it means to respond. It is not an assumption that every reader needs this. Many who visit this site already know the gospel well and are here for its deeper explorations. This page is for those who are ready to take the next step and want that step to be clear.

It is also for those who have taken the step — perhaps more than once — and wonder whether it counted. If that is you, this page is written with you specifically in mind. Stay with it.

The Situation: What Has Gone Wrong

The curriculum's first module describes the human condition in three registers, and all three matter here because different people experience the need differently.

We are enslaved. Not merely people who make bad choices, but people caught in patterns we cannot break by willpower alone. Paul describes this in Romans 7 — wanting to do good and finding ourselves doing the opposite. If you have ever tried to change something about yourself and found, sincerely, that the change would not hold — you know what this feels like from the inside. The gospel calls this bondage, and it addresses it with liberation.

We are guilty. Not merely feeling guilty — actually guilty. There is a moral weight to our actions that cannot be resolved by simply deciding to feel better about them. We have done real harm, to others and to our relationship with the God who made us. The gospel calls this condemnation, and it addresses it with justification — a real verdict, a genuinely cleared record, based not on our performance but on what Christ has done.

We are ashamed. Deeper than guilt: the sense not just of having done wrong things, but of being a wrong kind of person. Defective at the core. Unworthy of love not because of any particular act but because of who we are. The gospel calls this shame, and it addresses it with honor restoration — the father running toward the returning child, publicly reinstating what shame had destroyed.

You may feel all three of these, or primarily one. The gospel meets you in whichever register is truest to your experience.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

The three registers above are not three separate problems requiring three separate solutions. They are three angles on one condition: we are separated from the God who made us, and we cannot bridge that separation ourselves. The separation is real — it is not just a feeling, and it is not something we can fix by trying harder, being better, or thinking more clearly. The Bible's word for this whole condition is sin — a word that has been so overused and weaponized that it may have lost its meaning. What it actually points to is this: you were made for a relationship with the living God, and something has gone wrong with that relationship at a level deeper than behavior. The gospel's claim is that what has gone wrong is not something you can repair from your side. It requires God to act. And the extraordinary news is: he has.

The Person: Who Jesus Is

The Christian faith is not primarily a set of principles or a moral code. It is about a person — and what that person did.

Jesus of Nazareth, born in the first century in Roman-occupied Palestine, was not merely a teacher of wisdom, though he was that. He was not merely a moral example, though he was that. The claim the New Testament makes — the claim his earliest followers were willing to die for — is that Jesus is the eternal Son of God, the second Person of the Trinity, who entered human existence as a human being.

This is a staggering claim. It means God did not remain at a distance from the mess we had made. He entered it. He took on a body, experienced hunger and exhaustion and grief and betrayal, lived a fully human life — and did so without the fracture that marks the rest of us. He was, in the language of Hebrews, "tempted in every way as we are, yet without sin" (Hebrews 4:15). He is not a God who judges from the outside. He is a God who has been here.

The Act: What Jesus Did

The heart of the gospel is not what Jesus taught. It is what Jesus did.

On the cross, something happened that addresses all three registers of our condition simultaneously. The New Testament uses multiple images to describe it because no single image captures the whole:

For bondage: the cross is a liberation. "He has delivered us from the domain of darkness and transferred us to the kingdom of his beloved Son" (Colossians 1:13). The power that held us has been broken by a greater power.

For guilt: the cross is a substitution. "He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree" (1 Peter 2:24). The penalty was real; it was paid by someone else. Not because God is vindictive, but because guilt is real and must be genuinely addressed, not merely overlooked. The cross is God's way of taking the weight of what we have done onto himself rather than leaving it on us.

For shame: the cross is a welcome. The Son of God endured the most shameful death the Roman world could devise — public, naked, criminal — and transformed it into the means of restoration. He entered our shame so that we could enter his standing.

And then, three days later, the resurrection. Module 4 examines this in detail, but its significance for responding is direct: the resurrection is the Father's vindication that the cross accomplished what it claimed. The debt is genuinely paid. The liberation is real. The new creation has begun. The risen Christ is not a historical figure to study but a living person to encounter.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

The paragraph above uses the word substitution — the idea that Christ took what we deserved so that we could receive what he deserved. This is sometimes called "penal substitutionary atonement" in theological language, and it is one of the central ways the New Testament describes what happened on the cross. But it is not the only way. The New Testament also describes the cross as a victory over evil powers (Colossians 2:15), as a reconciliation between God and humanity (2 Corinthians 5:18-19), and as a demonstration of God's love (Romans 5:8). These are not competing theories — they are different angles on an event so large that no single image exhausts it. Together they say: whatever has gone wrong between you and God, the cross is God's answer to it — comprehensive, costly, and real.

The Response: What It Means to Believe

The gospel is an announcement — good news about what God has done. The question is not "what must I do to earn this?" but "how do I receive it?"

The New Testament's answer is faith. But "faith" in the New Testament sense is richer than the English word often suggests. It is not merely intellectual agreement that certain facts are true. Even the demons believe that, James says, and shudder (James 2:19). Faith is trust — the kind of trust that stakes something on what it believes. The Reformers described it with three Latin words that are worth knowing because they clarify something important:

Notitia — knowing the facts. You understand what the gospel claims: that you are separated from God, that Christ died and rose to bridge that separation, that the gift is offered freely.

Assensus — agreeing that these facts are true. You are not merely aware of the claims; you are persuaded by them.

Fiducia — personal trust and reliance. This is the step that makes faith faith. It is the difference between believing a chair will hold your weight and actually sitting down in it. It is entrusting yourself to the person the facts point to — saying, in effect: I trust you with this. I trust you with me.

This curriculum uses the phrase Belief with Intent to describe what that third element looks like in practice: not merely agreeing that the gospel is true but orienting your life toward the God it reveals. It is the heart's genuine turning toward Christ — what the Bible sometimes calls repentance.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

Repentance is another word that needs rescuing from its popular image. It does not primarily mean "feeling sorry enough" or "promising to be better." The Greek word — metanoia — means a change of mind, a turning around, a fundamental reorientation. Think of it less like groveling and more like the prodigal son in Luke 15: coming to his senses and turning back toward home. Repentance is the turning. The father's running to meet him is grace. You do not need to have your life cleaned up before you turn. You turn as you are, with whatever mess you are carrying, and you find the father already moving toward you. The turning and the being-met are not two separate events. They are one — because, as the Spine of this curriculum explains, even the desire to turn is itself the Spirit's work in you. If you are reading this page and something in you is responding, that response is already evidence that God is at work. You are not generating faith from scratch. You are responding to someone who has been calling you.

Receiving: What Happens When You Believe

Romans 10:9–10

"If you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved. For with the heart one believes and is justified, and with the mouth one confesses and is saved."

What God does, in response to genuine faith, is comprehensive. The New Testament describes it in several overlapping ways:

Justification: You are declared righteous — not because you are righteous, but because Christ's righteousness is credited to you. The record is cleared. The legal standing before God is settled. This is a finished verdict, not a provisional one (Romans 8:1).

Adoption: You are brought into God's family — not as a guest or a servant but as a child. "To all who did receive him, who believed in his name, he gave the right to become children of God" (John 1:12). The language of family runs throughout the New Testament because what God offers is not merely forgiveness but belonging.

Union with Christ: The Christian life is not a solo project of moral improvement with occasional divine assistance. It is being joined to a living person — grafted into the vine, as Jesus says in John 15. His life becomes yours. His standing becomes yours. His Spirit takes up residence in you — not as an occasional visitor but as a permanent, interior presence.

The beginning of transformation: The Spirit who now lives in you begins the slow, real, sometimes painful work of making you into the person you were created to be. This is not instant. Module 7 is honest about what it actually looks like — and it looks more like growing dependence on Christ than like conquering your problems. But it is real, and it is the Spirit's work, not yours to produce by effort.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

The four items above may sound like a checklist of benefits — but they are better understood as four descriptions of one thing: being brought into relationship with the living God through Christ. Justification means your legal problem is solved — the record is clear. Adoption means your relational problem is solved — you belong. Union with Christ means your existential problem is solved — you are connected to the source of life itself. Transformation means the practical outworking has begun — the Spirit starts to make real in your daily experience what is already true of your standing. None of these depend on the quality of your prayer or the intensity of your emotion at the moment of believing. They depend on the faithfulness of the One you are believing in. This is the Christological assurance the Spine of this curriculum describes: we are held not by the strength of our grip on Christ but by the strength of his grip on us.

Speaking to God: What This Looks Like

There is no magic formula. The New Testament does not prescribe a specific prayer. What it describes is a posture of the heart: turning toward God, acknowledging the truth about your need, and trusting Christ with the weight of it.

If it would help to have words, here is a way to begin — not as a script to recite but as an honest expression of what turning toward God sounds like:

God, I am not coming to you because I have my life together. I am coming because I don't, and I cannot fix that on my own. I believe that Jesus is who the gospel says he is — your Son, who lived and died and rose for me. I trust him with my guilt, my shame, my inability to change myself. I am turning toward you as I am, not as I wish I were. Receive me — not because I deserve it, but because of what Christ has done. I want to follow him. Help me, by your Spirit, to begin.

If you have spoken to God honestly in this way — or in any way that expressed genuine trust in Christ — something has happened. Not because of the quality of your prayer but because of the faithfulness of the One who heard it.

🕊️ For Those Who Have Done This Before — Perhaps Many Times

If you have prayed "the prayer" before — sincerely, perhaps repeatedly — and you are reading this wondering whether it counted: the answer is almost certainly yes, and the repetition is almost certainly not required.

The pastoral trap this curriculum is built to avoid is the idea that salvation is a transaction secured by the right prayer said with sufficient sincerity. The New Testament's basis for assurance is not the quality of your prayer but the faithfulness of the God who hears it. Romans 10:9–10 describes the direction of the heart, not the precision of a formula.

The question is not "did I say it right?" but "is God faithful to his promise?" And the answer Scripture gives is unambiguous: he is. Always. Completely. Without condition.

"He who calls you is faithful; he will surely do it" (1 Thessalonians 5:24).

The call now is not to pray again. It is to trust the Promiser. If you have genuinely turned toward Christ — even haltingly, even in confusion, even in the middle of significant doubt — you are not outside his grip. You are in it. The work now is not to secure the transaction but to grow in the relationship that is already real.

⚖️ Assurance: A Genuine Theological Landscape

Christians in different traditions experience and think about assurance differently, and it is worth being honest about the landscape.

The Reformed tradition generally teaches that genuine believers can have confident assurance of their salvation, grounded in God's electing love and Christ's finished work. The Westminster Confession states that this assurance is "an infallible assurance of faith" (WCF 18.2), though it acknowledges that believers may wait long and contend with many difficulties before obtaining it.

The Arminian tradition affirms that genuine assurance is available through the witness of the Spirit (Romans 8:16) and the evidence of a transformed life, while holding that the possibility of apostasy — genuinely falling away from faith — is real. For Arminians, assurance comes not from the impossibility of losing salvation but from the present reality of the Spirit's witness and the ongoing faithfulness of God to those who persevere.

What both traditions agree on — and this is the ground this curriculum stands on — is that assurance is ultimately Christological: it rests on who Christ is and what he has done, not on the quality of our faith or the consistency of our obedience. The Reformed and Arminian traditions differ genuinely on the mechanism of perseverance, but both point away from introspection as the primary source of confidence and toward Christ as its proper object.

If you are uncertain which tradition you belong to, that is normal and not a barrier to anything. Trust Christ. The theological questions can be explored over time — and this curriculum's Spine engages them with care.

What Comes Next: First Steps

If you have responded to the gospel — whether just now or years ago — there are concrete next steps that matter. Not because they earn or secure what you have received, but because they are the natural shape of a life that has been met by God.

Tell someone. Faith is not a private transaction. If you have turned toward Christ, telling someone — a trusted friend, a pastor, the person who gave you this link — does something important: it moves the decision from the interior to the relational. Romans 10:9 links confession with the mouth to belief in the heart. Speaking it out loud is not a separate condition of salvation — it is the natural expression of a heart that has genuinely turned.

Begin reading Scripture. Start with one of the Gospels — Mark is the shortest and most direct; John is the most theologically rich. Read slowly. You are not studying a textbook; you are listening to the voice of the person you have just entrusted yourself to. If the Bible feels overwhelming or foreign, that is normal. It will become less so over time.

Pray. Not with performance or polish. Pray the way you would talk to someone you trust — honestly, specifically, without pretending to be further along than you are. The Holy Spirit is already interceding for you (Romans 8:26–27) — which means even your most inarticulate prayers are being received by the Father in exactly the right way.

Find a community. The Christian life is not designed to be lived alone. Module 9 describes what the church is meant to be — and the Wounded Pilgrim section there offers honest guidance for those who have been hurt by the church and are cautious about returning. Look for a community where the Bible is taken seriously, where doubt is tolerated rather than policed, where leadership is accountable, and where the weak are genuinely welcomed rather than managed. You have the right to look carefully, and you have the right to take time.

Be patient with yourself. Growth is real but slow. Module 7 describes what it actually looks like — and it looks less like a victory march and more like a deepening dependence on grace. You will not be a different person tomorrow. You will be a person who has begun a journey. The journey itself is the life. And the One who called you into it has promised to finish what he started (Philippians 1:6).

🧭 For Facilitators: Using This Page

This page is designed to be linked from strategic points throughout the curriculum — particularly from Module 1 (after the diagnosis), Module 2 (after the announcement), Module 4 (after the resurrection), and the Spine's assurance material. It can also be shared as a standalone resource.

Two pastoral cautions. First: resist the impulse to push people toward this page prematurely. The curriculum is designed to build honestly toward the point where the question "how do I respond?" arises naturally. Sending someone here before they have felt the need or encountered the story can make the gospel feel like a sales pitch rather than an answer to a question they are actually asking. Second: if someone in the group responds to the gospel during or after a session, receive it with genuine warmth and avoid over-processing it in the group. A brief, honest acknowledgment — and a private conversation afterward — is usually more pastorally appropriate than a public moment that can feel performative. The Spirit's work in a person does not require an audience to be real.

🌍 Entry Points Across Cultures

The three registers of the human condition — power, guilt, and shame — land with different weight in different cultural contexts. In Western settings shaped by legal and individualist frameworks, guilt is often the primary register: "I have done wrong things and stand condemned." In honor-shame cultures — including much of the Middle East, East Asia, parts of Africa and Latin America — shame is often the deeper felt reality: "I am not merely guilty but defective, unworthy of belonging." In contexts marked by oppression, addiction, or systemic powerlessness, the bondage register often resonates most: "I cannot change, no matter how hard I try."

All three are real. All three are addressed by the gospel simultaneously. Facilitators and those sharing this page should listen carefully for which register a person is speaking from and meet them there rather than defaulting to a single framing. The father's running in Luke 15 — which addresses shame — may open a door that the courtroom language of justification cannot, and vice versa. The gospel is large enough to meet all three. The question is which door is the right door for the person in front of you.

For Reflection

Where do you find yourself? Not where you think you should be, but where you actually are — right now, after reading this?

And: is there something you want to say to God that you have not yet said?
