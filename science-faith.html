Science and Faith

Thinking Clearly About the Relationship Between Two Ways of Knowing
When the apostle Paul stood at the Areopagus in Athens, he did not open by attacking his audience. He opened by noticing what they had already understood and built on it from there (Acts 17:22–28). That posture — genuinely curious, intellectually honest, willing to acknowledge what others have gotten right — is the one this page tries to model. The science-faith question deserves better than the shouting match it usually gets.

The Real Question

The science-faith debate is often framed as a competition: science advances, faith retreats; science explains, faith becomes unnecessary; science is for adults, faith is for people who haven't grown up yet. The frame itself is worth examining before the argument begins — because it contains an assumption that most careful thinkers, including many scientists, do not share.

Science, rigorously understood, is a method: a way of investigating the natural world through observation, hypothesis, and testing. It is extraordinarily good at what it does. It is also, by design, silent on a range of questions that are not reducible to empirical observation: Why is there something rather than nothing? What grounds the reliability of reason itself? What makes anything morally obligatory? Is there a personal God? These are not questions science refuses to answer out of timidity. They are questions that lie outside its jurisdiction by method — the way a tape measure is not the right instrument for assessing a painting. Using the right tool for the right job is not anti-science. It is good epistemology.

The question Christianity addresses is not "how did the universe begin?" but "why does anything exist at all, and does it have a purpose?" These are not competing answers to the same question. They are different questions. A complete account of the Big Bang's mechanism does not in principle exclude the claim that a personal God initiated and sustains it. Most physicists would agree that the question of mechanism and the question of ultimate origin are different in kind, even if they disagree about the second question's answer.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

Imagine a kettle boiling. You can explain the boiling completely through physics and chemistry — water molecules absorbing heat energy, reaching a phase transition, converting to steam. That explanation is accurate and complete as far as it goes. But it does not answer the question "why is the kettle on?" — which requires knowing that someone put it on to make tea. Both answers are true. Neither cancels the other out. The physical explanation and the purposive explanation operate at different levels. This is roughly the relationship between scientific explanation and theological explanation. Science tells you the mechanism; theology addresses the question of the agent and purpose behind the mechanism. You need both, and neither answer makes the other false.

The Conflict Myth: Science and Christianity Are Not at War

The popular narrative — science versus religion, reason versus faith, progress versus superstition — has a specific historical origin, and it is not the history itself. It is two books: John William Draper's History of the Conflict Between Religion and Science (1874) and Andrew Dickson White's A History of the Warfare of Science with Theology (1896). These books established the "warfare thesis" — the idea that Christianity and science have always been in fundamental conflict. The thesis became textbook orthodoxy. It has also been comprehensively discredited by subsequent historical scholarship.

The actual history of science is inconveniently theological. Modern science did not grow up despite Christianity — it grew largely from it. The conviction that the universe is orderly, rational, and comprehensible — without which empirical science would be impossible — is not an obvious assumption. Many ancient cultures did not hold it. It is, however, exactly what you would expect if a rational God made a rational creation and gave rational creatures the capacity to understand it. Historians of science have extensively documented the Christian convictions of many of the founders of modern science:

Nicolaus Copernicus was a church canon and dedicated his heliocentric theory to the Pope. Johannes Kepler wrote in his notebooks that he was "thinking God's thoughts after him." Blaise Pascal — mathematician, physicist, inventor of the mechanical calculator, contributor to probability theory — was also one of the seventeenth century's most searching Christian apologists. Isaac Newton wrote more theology than physics and considered his scientific work a reading of God's creation. Michael Faraday, whose electromagnetic discoveries undergird every modern electrical device, was a devout Christian who led his small congregation for decades. Gregor Mendel, founder of genetics, was an Augustinian monk. Georges Lemaître, the Belgian priest who first proposed what became the Big Bang theory, was told by Einstein that his physics was excellent. The list extends through every century of modern science.

The Galileo affair — most commonly cited as the definitive proof of science-religion conflict — is, on careful examination, considerably more complicated than the popular version suggests. Galileo had the support of many senior Church figures, including cardinals who found his work persuasive. His conflict was as much about personality, politics, and his public mockery of Pope Urban VIII (a former supporter) as it was about heliocentrism. The Catholic Church had accepted heliocentric cosmology within decades of his death. The story has been significantly mythologized, and the mythology serves the warfare thesis more than the evidence does.

⚖️ On the Galileo Affair — A Note

None of this makes the Church's treatment of Galileo admirable — it was not. He was threatened, humiliated, and spent his final years under house arrest for being right about the solar system. That is a genuine failure and worth acknowledging plainly. The point is narrower: the affair does not establish the warfare thesis as a historical principle. It establishes that a specific conflict occurred in a specific political moment involving a specific set of personalities — which is very different from science and Christianity being constitutionally incompatible. The Catholic Church eventually formally apologized to Galileo in 1992. The delay was three and a half centuries too long.

Questions as Weight: A Rhetorical Pattern Worth Naming

There is a specific rhetorical pattern that appears frequently in popular anti-religious argument, and it is worth naming clearly — not to score points, but because recognizing it is genuinely useful for thinking clearly. The pattern: accumulate a large number of questions that the Christian cannot immediately answer, and treat the accumulation as evidence that Christianity is false.

"How do you explain the age of the universe? How do you account for evolution? What about the problem of suffering? What about all the other religions? What about the Crusades? What about biblical contradictions? What about miracles violating the laws of nature?" Each question, fired rapidly, before the previous one can be addressed, creates the impression of a crumbling edifice. The implicit argument is: a position that generates this many questions must be indefensible.

The logical error here has a name in formal debate: the Gish Gallop, after the creationist debater Duane Gish who perfected it. The volume of challenges is not evidence of anything except the volume of challenges. Any worldview — including atheism — can be subjected to the same treatment. "If there is no God, why does the universe have the rational structure that makes science possible? What grounds the reliability of human reason in a purely material world? How do you account for consciousness? What is the basis of moral obligation? Why does anything exist rather than nothing?" These questions do not prove theism. They do show that the strategy of multiplying unanswered questions is not argument — it is ammunition.

The point is not that the questions are unimportant. They are important. This curriculum takes them seriously, and deals with several of them across its modules. The point is that the appropriate response to each question is engagement — careful, honest, patient engagement — not the rhetorical move of treating a list of open questions as a verdict. Every worldview has open questions. The measure is not whether questions remain but whether the framework has the resources to address them honestly and has the better overall account of what we actually know.

⚖️ On Tone: A Word About How This Debate Often Goes

Some of the most prominent voices in popular atheism have treated religious believers with contempt — a contempt that is, frankly, sometimes returned in kind by religious apologists. Neither approach is intellectually honest or particularly useful. Contempt does not produce argument. It produces theater.

The Acts 17 approach this curriculum models — Paul at the Areopagus, genuinely engaging, acknowledging what his audience had gotten right, finding the point of contact before the point of departure — is not diplomatic cowardice. It is intellectual honesty. You cannot argue with someone you have already dismissed. And the person who finds you willing to take their actual argument seriously, rather than a caricature of it, is far more likely to take yours seriously in return.

The contempt sometimes directed at believers is worth naming — not to feel aggrieved, but because it is itself a failure of the intellectual standards science claims to embody. Science at its best is characterized by rigor, curiosity, openness to evidence, and willingness to follow an argument wherever it leads. Applied to the question of God, those virtues produce not contempt but genuine inquiry. The person who has already decided the question is not doing science. They are doing rhetoric.

A Universe Fine-Tuned: The Argument Worth Knowing

One of the most significant developments in twentieth-century physics was the discovery of what cosmologists call the fine-tuning of the universe — the observation that the fundamental constants of physics are calibrated with extraordinary precision for the emergence of complex, carbon-based life. The cosmological constant, the ratio of electromagnetic force to gravitational force, the mass of the electron, the strong nuclear force — these and dozens of other constants are set within extraordinarily narrow ranges. Change any of them even slightly, and the universe produces either no structure at all or no chemistry capable of supporting life.

The physicist Paul Davies — not a Christian — wrote that "the impression of design is overwhelming." Stephen Hawking acknowledged in A Brief History of Time that the precision of the initial conditions of the universe is remarkable. This is not an argument that science itself makes or can make — the question of why the constants are what they are lies outside the domain of physics. It is an observation from physics that demands a philosophical response.

The responses are essentially three. One: the constants are what they are by chance, which requires accepting an extraordinarily improbable state of affairs as brute fact. Two: a multiverse — an infinity of universes with varying constants — means that ours, hospitable to life, is a selection effect; we are here to notice it because only universes like ours produce noticers. Three: the universe was designed with these constants by an intelligence capable of setting them. The multiverse hypothesis is scientifically intriguing but currently unfalsifiable and comes with its own philosophical baggage. The design hypothesis is what most people throughout history — including many distinguished scientists — have found most compelling. None of this proves theism. It does suggest that the dismissal of design arguments as obviously primitive requires more philosophical work than is usually acknowledged.

📚 For Further Study — Science and Christian Faith
  • John Lennox, God's Undertaker: Has Science Buried God? (Lion, 2007) — An Oxford mathematician and philosopher of science argues that science, far from undermining theism, points toward it. Rigorous, readable, and unflappable.
  • Alister McGrath, The Dawkins Delusion? (IVP, 2007) — A response to Richard Dawkins by a man who holds doctorates in both molecular biology and Christian theology. McGrath is not defensive — he is simply more rigorous than his target.
  • Francis Collins, The Language of God (Free Press, 2006) — The director of the Human Genome Project describes his journey from atheism to faith. Particularly valuable as a scientist's first-person account of finding the two not in conflict but complementary.
  • Timothy Keller, The Reason for God (Dutton, 2008), Chapters 6–7 — "Science Has Disproved Christianity" and "You Can't Take the Bible Literally" — clear, careful, and aimed directly at the objections most commonly heard in educated secular settings.
  • C.S. Lewis, Miracles (HarperOne, 1947) — Lewis's philosophical argument that naturalism — the view that nature is all there is — is self-refuting, because it cannot account for the rational faculties we use to evaluate it. Still one of the most original arguments in the literature.
  • BioLogos.org — An organization founded by Francis Collins exploring the relationship between Christian faith and the biological sciences. Particularly useful for questions about evolution and faith.
  • Reasons to Believe (reasons.org) — An old-earth creationist ministry founded by astrophysicist Hugh Ross. A good representative of the position that mainstream cosmology and a literal Genesis are compatible.

Origins: An Honest Map of the Positions

The question of biological origins — specifically evolution — is where the science-faith debate most often heats up. There are genuine disagreements here among serious Christians, and the curriculum names them honestly rather than implying one is obvious.

Young Earth Creationism (YEC) holds that the universe and earth were created within the past ten thousand years, broadly in accordance with a literal seven-day reading of Genesis 1. It maintains that the scientific consensus on the age of the earth and the universe is mistaken. What it protects: the authority and plain-sense reading of Scripture; a commitment to taking Genesis as historical rather than accommodating it to contemporary scientific consensus. The scientific community — including many Christian scientists — finds the empirical case for a young earth unconvincing. What YEC advocates find unacceptable is treating the scientific consensus as automatically the final word on questions where Scripture appears to speak.

Old Earth Creationism (OEC) accepts the standard scientific age of the universe (approximately 13.8 billion years) and earth (approximately 4.5 billion years) but holds that species were separately created rather than evolving from common ancestors. It reads the Genesis "days" as ages or epochs rather than twenty-four-hour periods, a reading with a long history in Christian interpretation (Augustine argued for it in the fifth century). What it protects: both the integrity of mainstream cosmology and a meaningful account of special creation as distinct from purely naturalistic evolution.

Theistic Evolution (TE) — also called Evolutionary Creationism — accepts the mainstream scientific account of biological evolution as the mechanism God used to bring about the diversity of life, including humans. It holds that this is fully compatible with Christian theology: God is the author and sustainer of the evolutionary process, and the image of God in humanity is a theological claim about human dignity and vocation, not a claim about the specific mechanism by which humanity came to be. Francis Collins and BioLogos represent this position prominently. What it protects: full engagement with mainstream science without apology, alongside full confidence in Christian faith. The challenge it faces: how to integrate the historical Adam and Eve with evolutionary timelines is a genuine exegetical and theological question that TE scholars continue to work through.

Each of these positions is held by serious, Scripture-honoring Christians. Each is protecting something real. The curriculum's instinct — consistent with how it handles other theological disagreements — is to name what each position protects rather than adjudicating between them. The first-order conviction shared by all three: God created. The mechanism and timeline are genuinely contested, and the question of which interpretation of Genesis best accounts for both the text and the natural world is one where Christians continue to reason together.

Scientists Who Believe: A Non-Exhaustive List

The popular impression that science and Christian faith are incompatible is not supported by the actual distribution of beliefs among scientists — which is more varied than the loudest voices on either side suggest. A few names worth knowing:

Francis Collins — director of the Human Genome Project and former director of the National Institutes of Health. Became a Christian in his late twenties after reading C.S. Lewis. Author of The Language of God and founder of BioLogos. One of the most distinguished biologists of the twentieth century.

John Polkinghorne — theoretical physicist at Cambridge who contributed to the discovery of the quark, then became an Anglican priest. Wrote extensively on the relationship between science and theology. Recipient of the Templeton Prize.

John Lennox — emeritus professor of mathematics at Oxford. Has debated Richard Dawkins, Christopher Hitchens, and Peter Singer publicly. A rigorous philosopher of science who finds that science points toward rather than away from God.

Alister McGrath — holds doctorates in molecular biology (Oxford) and theology (Oxford). Professor of Science and Religion at Oxford. Formerly an atheist, became a Christian in his late teens. Has written more carefully about the science-faith interface than almost anyone alive.

William Phillips — Nobel Prize in Physics (1997). A Christian who has spoken publicly about his faith and his science as complementary rather than competing.

Jennifer Wiseman — senior astrophysicist at NASA. Director of the American Association for the Advancement of Science's Dialogue on Science, Ethics, and Religion. A Christian who has written and spoken about science and faith throughout her career.

The point is not that the existence of believing scientists proves Christianity true. It demonstrates — against the popular narrative — that the perceived conflict between rigorous science and Christian faith is not as obvious to the people doing the science as it appears to those who study it from the outside.

🧭 For Facilitators — Using This Page

This page works best with groups that contain people who have been told — directly or by cultural atmosphere — that science has rendered Christianity intellectually untenable. The most useful move is usually to start with the conflict myth section: the history of science growing largely from Christian soil is genuinely surprising to most people and shifts the framing before the more contested questions are reached.

On origins: if your group contains both young-earth and old-earth members, be prepared for this to be the most contested section. The honest pastoral move is to affirm what all the positions share — God created; the universe is not an accident; human beings are made in the image of God — and to hold the mechanism question with genuine openness rather than forcing premature resolution. The origins debate, while real, is not the load-bearing structure of the gospel, and treating it as such fractures communities over questions the church has never achieved uniform agreement on.

The tone section — on not mirroring contempt — is worth reading aloud in a group that contains someone carrying genuine anger at how they or their faith have been dismissed. Naming the pattern gives people language for what they have experienced without requiring them to escalate it.
