The Vocabulary Trap

"Speaking Different Languages in the Same Room"
← Tactic 1  |  Overview  |  Tactic 3 →

The Presenting Dispute

Two people are having a conversation about something important — grace, perhaps, or authority, or accountability. Both of them take the topic seriously. Both of them have thought about it. Both of them believe they are being faithful to Scripture. And yet somehow, the more they talk, the less they seem to agree — and more troublingly, the more each of them suspects the other person may hold a dangerously wrong position.

The conversation eventually ends in frustration or rupture. Both parties leave believing the other is mistaken in a serious way. Neither party has noticed that they were using the same words to mean substantially different things — that they were, in effect, arguing past each other in two different theological dialects, each one convinced the other was speaking badly about something real.

This is the Vocabulary Trap. The Enemy requires no false doctrine to deploy it. He only needs two people, two histories, and one word they both think they understand.

The Enemy's Actual Move

Words in theological conversation do not arrive as neutral containers waiting to be filled. They arrive loaded — freighted with the tradition that formed the speaker, the books they have read, the community that shaped them, and sometimes with painful history that has permanently altered how certain terms land. When someone who was formed in a Reformed tradition says grace, they mean something precise: the sovereign, unmerited favor of God that precedes and enables every human response. When someone formed in a Wesleyan tradition says grace, they also mean something precise — prevenient grace, the grace that goes before, that enables free response but does not compel it. These are not the same definition. Both traditions believe they are being faithful to Scripture. A conversation that uses the word without establishing which definition is operating is almost guaranteed to produce a misdiagnosis — each person concluding the other has a deficient view of grace, when in fact they have a different doctrine of grace.

The particular cruelty of the Vocabulary Trap is its effect on people whose theological vocabulary was formed in contexts of abuse. When someone who was harmed in a high-control religious environment hears the words submission, authority, or accountability, they are not hearing neutral theological terms. They are hearing the specific language that was used to harm them — words that arrived attached to demands that were not biblical, enforced by people who were not safe, and dressed in the language of faithfulness. When someone else uses those words in genuine good faith — meaning something entirely different by them — the person with the damaged history cannot hear the intended meaning. They hear the memory. The conversation breaks down, and neither party understands why.

The Scriptural Frame Being Exploited

The genuine call to "sound doctrine" and "right teaching" (2 Timothy 4:3, Titus 2:1) is exploited here by removing a key distinction: the difference between holding wrong doctrine and using a shared word differently. Someone who uses grace in a Wesleyan sense is not necessarily teaching false doctrine. Someone who uses authority in an institutional sense is not necessarily teaching abuse. But once the assumption takes hold that using a word differently means holding a different (dangerous) theology, the call to guard sound doctrine becomes the mechanism for treating vocabulary differences as heresy.

James 3:1–5 names the tongue as a small thing with enormous consequences — "a small fire can set a great forest ablaze." The Vocabulary Trap is one of the most common ways this plays out in practice: not through outright falsehood but through the friction of mismatched meaning, which generates heat without light and produces the perception of dangerous error where only semantic difference exists.

Counting the Cost

The Filioque Controversy and the Great Schism of 1054. For several centuries, a single Latin word — filioque ("and from the Son") — sat at the center of what eventually became the permanent division between Eastern and Western Christianity. The Western church had added it to the Nicene Creed without an ecumenical council: "the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father and from the Son." The Eastern church rejected the addition on two grounds: the procedural ground (no council had authorized the change) and the theological ground (the Spirit proceeds from the Father alone).

Both sides believed they were defending the same Trinitarian faith with different vocabulary. The Eastern tradition's concern was to protect the monarchy of the Father as the single source of divine life; the Western tradition's concern was to protect the full equality of the Son. Contemporary ecumenical scholarship has concluded that the two positions, properly understood, are not contradictory — they are two different ways of describing the same reality within the same shared Trinitarian commitment. The split was not, at its root, a split about the Trinity. It was a split about a vocabulary choice, escalated through procedural grievance and centuries of accumulated cultural distance. Over a billion Christians remain separated. The wound has never closed.

Intergenerational church conflicts. In nearly every contemporary Western congregation, the words community, accountability, authenticity, and authority mean substantially different things to different generations — shaped by different cultural formations, different experiences of institutional life, and in many cases different painful histories with the church itself. When an older member says "accountability," they often mean the mutual pastoral care of a covenant community. When a younger member hears "accountability," they may hear the surveillance and shame they experienced in a youth group. The conversation about whether the church should practice accountability is not being conducted about the same thing by both parties — and until someone notices that, it cannot go anywhere useful.

The Reframe

The question that cuts through the Vocabulary Trap is simple enough to seem obvious, and rare enough in practice to be genuinely disruptive to the pattern: When you say that word, what do you mean by it?

Not in a gotcha spirit — as a genuine inquiry. The move is to define together before arguing about. Two people who discover they mean the same thing by a word they were apparently fighting about will often find that their actual disagreement is smaller than they thought, or located somewhere different than expected. Two people who discover they mean genuinely different things can then have the more honest conversation: "Here is what I mean, and here is why I think Scripture supports it. What do you mean, and what does Scripture say about that?"

The reframe does not assume that all vocabulary differences hide identical theology. Sometimes the definitions really do differ, and the disagreement is real. But it establishes the actual ground of disagreement — which is the only ground on which genuine resolution is possible.

The Peacemaker's Move

When a conversation is going in circles and you can feel the frustration building: "Can I try something? I want to make sure we're talking about the same thing. When you say [word], can you help me understand what you mean by it — specifically? Not what it's supposed to mean in general, but what you mean by it in this context?" Then listen, and don't assume you know what they'll say.

When someone seems to be reacting to a word more strongly than the situation seems to warrant: Slow down before advancing the argument. "I notice this word seems to be landing hard. I want to use it carefully — is there history with that term for you? I'm not going anywhere; I want to understand what it means when I use it in this context." You may not be entitled to that information, and they may not be ready to give it. But asking opens a door the Vocabulary Trap has slammed shut.

When you realize you and someone else have been using a word differently: Name it without blame. "I think we may have been talking past each other. I've been using [word] to mean [X] — I think you've been hearing it as [Y]. Does that sound right? If so, let me try to say what I actually mean without using that word, and then you can tell me if you still disagree with the thing itself." This is not a concession; it is a precision move that brings the real conversation into focus.

When someone accuses you of teaching false doctrine based on vocabulary: "I want to take that seriously — if I'm teaching something wrong, I want to know. Can you tell me specifically what you heard me saying, in your own words? I want to make sure we're disagreeing about the actual thing and not about the word I used to describe it."

⚖️ Going Deeper: How Traditions Develop Theological Dialects

One of the most illuminating — and humbling — facts about Christian history is that the major theological traditions have each developed their own vocabulary not primarily through error but through emphasis. Each tradition formed around a genuine concern of Scripture, and its vocabulary reflects that concern. Understanding why the dialects differ makes vocabulary disputes less threatening and more tractable.

The Reformed tradition developed its vocabulary in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries in response to what its founders saw as the Roman church's confusion of divine grace and human merit. The result is a theological dialect intensely precise about divine sovereignty and human incapacity: words like election, effectual calling, irresistible grace, perseverance of the saints — all designed to protect the absolute priority of God's initiative. When a Reformed speaker uses grace, they are protecting against any suggestion that the human being contributes anything to salvation. That is a genuine biblical concern (Ephesians 2:8–9). The precision of the vocabulary reflects the intensity of the concern.

The Wesleyan/Arminian tradition developed its vocabulary in response to what it saw as the Reformed tradition's collapse of genuine human freedom and moral responsibility. Its dialect emphasizes prevenient grace (grace that goes before and enables free response), entire sanctification (genuine moral transformation possible in this life), and conditional security (assurance grounded in continued faith rather than unconditional election). These too are genuine biblical concerns — the New Testament's many warnings and conditional promises mean something, and a theology that reads them purely as means of assurance rather than genuine conditions deserves to be pressed on that question.

The Anabaptist tradition developed its vocabulary in response to what it saw as the Magisterial Reformation's incomplete break from Christendom — the continued entanglement of church and state power. Its dialect centers on discipleship, community discernment, costly obedience, and nonresistance. When an Anabaptist speaker uses community, they mean something with stronger ecclesiological weight than many other traditions: the gathered community is not primarily a resource for individual growth but the locus of discernment, accountability, and the visible witness of the kingdom. This too is a genuine biblical concern (Matthew 18:15–20, 1 Corinthians 5).

The Pentecostal/charismatic tradition developed its vocabulary around direct experience of the Spirit's work: baptism in the Spirit, tongues as initial evidence, word of knowledge, healing. Its dialect is experiential and immediate where other traditions are doctrinal and mediated. When a Pentecostal speaker uses faith, they may be describing something more affective and expectant than what a Reformed speaker means by the same word. Again — genuine biblical concern, genuine dialect difference.

The practical implication: when you discover that someone uses a word differently from you, the most useful question is not "which of us is wrong?" but "which biblical concern is this dialect protecting?" Almost always you will find that their emphasis and yours are both present in Scripture, and that the real question is one of balance and emphasis rather than truth and error. That realization does not end the theological conversation. It makes it possible to have one.
