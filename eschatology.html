The Life to Come

What Christianity Claims About Death, Resurrection, and the World Made New
C.S. Lewis observed that if you read history, the Christians who did most for the present world were precisely those who thought most about the next one. The abolitionists, the hospital founders, the orphanage builders, the prison reformers — they were, with striking consistency, people who took eternity seriously. This page is about what they believed, and why it produced the lives it did.

The Moment of Death: What Happens Next?

Philippians 1:23

"I am hard pressed between the two. My desire is to depart and be with Christ, for that is far better."

The Christian account of death begins with a distinction almost never made in popular discussion: the difference between the intermediate state (what happens immediately after death, before the resurrection) and the final state (the resurrection life that follows Christ's return). Confusing the two — or treating "heaven" as a permanent disembodied existence — produces a picture that is less than what Scripture actually describes, and one that fuels the sneering "clouds and harps" caricature that serious Christianity has never actually taught.

The intermediate state, according to the New Testament, is personal, conscious, and with Christ. Paul's language in Philippians 1:23 is not metaphor or wishful thinking — it is a specific theological claim: to depart is to be with Christ, and that is "far better" than remaining. In 2 Corinthians 5:8 he says he would "prefer to be away from the body and at home with the Lord." Jesus tells the thief on the cross: "Today you will be with me in paradise" — today, not after a long sleep (Luke 23:43). Whatever form this intermediate existence takes, it is neither annihilation nor unconscious waiting. It is presence with Christ.

This matters because a significant alternative — soul sleep, the doctrine that the soul is unconscious between death and resurrection — is popular in some traditions but directly contradicted by these passages. The Westminster Confession is explicit: the souls of the righteous, "being then made perfect in holiness, are received into the highest heavens, where they behold the face of God in light and glory, waiting for the full redemption of their bodies" (WCF XXXII.1). They are waiting. They are not asleep.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

When a Christian dies, Scripture's consistent testimony is that they go immediately into the presence of Christ. This is real and genuine — "far better" than this life, in Paul's own words. But it is not the final destination. Think of it as being with the host at a party before the feast officially begins. The relationship is real and the presence is real — but something even larger is still coming. The resurrection, the new creation, the fullness of the kingdom — those await the day Christ returns. What Scripture describes is not a ghost story (disembodied souls floating in eternity) but a staging: presence with Christ now, bodily resurrection and new creation at the end.

The Resurrection Body: Physical, Not Ghostly

1 Corinthians 15:42–44

"What is sown is perishable; what is raised is imperishable. It is sown in dishonor; it is raised in glory. It is sown in weakness; it is raised in power. It is sown a natural body; it is raised a spiritual body."

The resurrection of the body is one of the most misunderstood doctrines in popular Christianity — and one of the most important. Paul's language of "spiritual body" is routinely read as "immaterial body," which is the opposite of what he means. The Greek sōma pneumatikon does not mean a body made of spirit (as opposed to matter), but a body animated and governed by the Spirit — as opposed to the present body, which is animated by natural life alone. It is more, not less, than what we have now.

The evidence for the physicality of the resurrection is in the Gospels themselves. The risen Jesus ate fish (Luke 24:42–43). He invited Thomas to touch his wounds (John 20:27). He was recognizable as himself. He was also transformed — capable of appearing in a locked room, of vanishing from sight — in ways that suggest the resurrection body operates with freedoms the present body does not have. But it was emphatically a body. The disciples were not hallucinating a ghost. They were encountering a person.

This is not a small point. The Greek-influenced instinct in Western culture is to regard the physical as inferior to the spiritual — to think that the goal of religion is to escape the body. Christianity has always resisted this. The body is God's creation, called good in Genesis 1, taken on permanently by the Son of God in the incarnation, and destined for redemption rather than disposal. The resurrection is not an escape from physicality. It is its transformation. Randy Alcorn's phrase is memorable: we are not headed for a bodiless existence in a placeless realm but for "the earth made new" — a physical world fully liberated from sin's corruption.

⚖️ What Paul Means by "Spiritual Body"

The confusion here has a long history. The Platonic tradition divided reality into the spiritual (good, eternal, real) and the material (inferior, temporary, to be escaped). This picture entered popular Christianity early and has never fully left. But Paul's contrast in 1 Corinthians 15 is not spirit vs. matter — it is two kinds of body, one animated by psychē (natural life) and one animated by pneuma (Spirit). The difference is in source and power, not substance. Paul uses the analogy of a seed: the seed and the plant are genuinely continuous, genuinely different, and the plant is more than the seed — not less. The resurrection body is continuous with the present body (it is the same person), genuinely transformed (imperishable, glorious, powerful), and more than what we currently have, not less.

The New Creation: Renewal, Not Escape

Romans 8:19–21

"For the creation waits with eager longing for the revealing of the sons of God... in hope that the creation itself will be set free from its bondage to corruption and obtain the freedom of the glory of the children of God."

The final vision Scripture offers is not the destruction of the physical world but its liberation. The creation itself — trees, rivers, soil, animals, the whole material order — is described in Romans 8 as groaning in labor pains, waiting for redemption. John's Revelation describes not human souls escaping to a distant spiritual realm but the New Jerusalem coming down from heaven to earth — God's dwelling established among his people in a renewed physical world (Revelation 21:1–4). The direction of travel in Scripture is not upward and away but downward and new.

This has enormous practical implications. If the final destination is an ethereal non-world of disembodied worship, then this physical creation is ultimately disposable — to be abandoned rather than redeemed. But if the new creation is the fulfillment and renewal of this one, then everything done in this world in Christ's name — care for the poor, cultivation of beauty, the relief of suffering, the building of just institutions — has permanent significance. It is not practice for the real thing. It is, in some sense, the real thing in seed form.

N.T. Wright makes this argument with characteristic force in Surprised by Hope: the resurrection of Jesus is the beginning of the new creation, not merely a one-off miracle. When God raised Jesus bodily, he was announcing the first act of the world's final renewal. Everything that follows — every act of beauty, justice, and love done in the Spirit — is participation in that new creation breaking into the old. Nothing done in the Lord is wasted (1 Corinthians 15:58).

📚 For Further Study — The New Creation
  • Randy Alcorn, Heaven (Tyndale, 2004) — The most comprehensive popular treatment of the new creation's physical concreteness. Alcorn demolishes the clouds-and-harps caricature with thoroughness and warmth. If this topic is new to you, start here.
  • N.T. Wright, Surprised by Hope (HarperOne, 2008) — The most intellectually rigorous popular account of resurrection and new creation, and why they matter for how we live now. Essential reading.
  • Paul Marshall & Lela Gilbert, Heaven Is Not My Home (Thomas Nelson, 1998) — A Reformed account of the goodness of earthly life and the new creation as its fulfillment.

The Final Judgment: Why Jesus Talked About It More Than Anyone

Matthew 25:31–32

"When the Son of Man comes in his glory, and all the angels with him, then he will sit on his glorious throne. Before him will be gathered all the nations, and he will separate people one from another as a shepherd separates the sheep from the goats."

It is a striking historical fact that Jesus — who spoke more tenderly about the kingdom of God than anyone in Scripture — also spoke more plainly and more frequently about final judgment than any other figure in the New Testament. More than Paul. More than John. The Sermon on the Mount, which contains the Beatitudes, also contains warnings about hell. The parables of grace — the Prodigal Son, the Lost Sheep, the Great Banquet — are in the same Gospel as the parables of judgment — the Sheep and Goats, the Ten Virgins, the Talents. The same voice that says "Come to me, all who are weary" also says "Depart from me, I never knew you." These are not in tension in Jesus's teaching. They are two dimensions of the same reality.

The WCF's statement on the last judgment is precise: "God has appointed a day wherein he will judge the world in righteousness, by Jesus Christ" — and its outcome will be either "everlasting life and joy in the Lord" or "everlasting torments" (WCF XXXIII.2). This curriculum does not soften that claim. What it does insist on is the context: the final judgment is not the primary frame through which the gospel is offered. It is the backdrop that gives the invitation its full weight. A gift that costs the giver nothing is easy to decline. A gift that was bought at infinite cost, offered by a God who will one day settle all accounts, has a different gravity. The urgency of the gospel is not manufactured. It is real — because the stakes named by Jesus are real.

🕊️ For Those Who Find Judgment Language Frightening

If the language of final judgment produces fear rather than solemnity — if it awakens dread rather than the sober weight of things that genuinely matter — there is something worth naming here. The judgment described in Romans 8:1 has already been settled for those who are in Christ: "There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus." The believer stands before the judgment seat not as someone whose fate is unknown but as someone whose verdict has already been declared in the resurrection of their representative. The Judge and the Advocate are the same person.

Fear of judgment — a particular kind of anxious dread about one's standing — is often a signal to return to the gospel's indicative rather than its imperative. The question is not "have I been good enough?" but "is Christ's work sufficient?" The answer Scripture gives is unambiguous: it is. The assurance question is addressed more fully in The Spine and in the Responding page.

The Millennium: A Genuine Disagreement

On the question of the millennium — the thousand-year reign described in Revelation 20 — this curriculum names a genuine disagreement among serious, Bible-believing Christians rather than pretending one position is obvious.

Amillennialism holds that the thousand years is not a future literal period but a symbolic description of Christ's current reign from heaven, which runs from the resurrection to his return. The binding of Satan in Revelation 20 is understood as the restriction of his power accomplished through the cross and the spread of the gospel. This has been the majority Reformed and Anglican position historically, associated with Augustine, Calvin, Louis Berkhof, and more recently Anthony Hoekema. What it protects: the kingdom is already present and active; Christ is already reigning; the church is not waiting passively for history to take a different turn.

Historic Premillennialism holds that Christ will return before a literal thousand-year earthly reign, during which Satan is bound and the saints reign with Christ, followed by a final judgment. This view is associated with several early church fathers and with contemporary scholars like George Eldon Ladd and Wayne Grudem. What it protects: a genuine future chapter of visible, embodied, earthly flourishing under Christ's direct rule — the physical creation's full redemption made visible.

Dispensational Premillennialism adds a pretribulation rapture and a specific structure of the end times drawn largely from a particular reading of Daniel and Revelation developed in the nineteenth century. This is the view popularized by the Left Behind series. It is held sincerely by many; its interpretive method is the most contested among Reformed and evangelical scholars.

Postmillennialism holds that the gospel will progressively transform the world until the majority of humanity is Christian, after which Christ returns to a world already largely under his influence. Associated historically with the Puritans and more recently with figures like R.J. Rushdoony and Douglas Wilson. What it protects: the power of the gospel to genuinely transform human culture and society, not merely rescue individuals out of it.

The honest note is this: all four positions are held by serious Christians who have read the same Bible carefully. The first-order convictions — Christ will return, the dead will be raised, the new creation will come, all will be judged, those in Christ will be with him forever — are not in dispute. The sequencing and structure of how it unfolds is where the disagreement lives, and those questions, while interesting and worth studying, are not the load-bearing structure of the gospel.

📚 For Further Study — Eschatology and the Millennium
  • Anthony Hoekema, The Bible and the Future (Eerdmans, 1979) — The most thorough and accessible Reformed (amillennial) treatment of eschatology. A benchmark work.
  • George Eldon Ladd, The Blessed Hope (Eerdmans, 1956) — Historic premillennialism presented by its finest twentieth-century advocate.
  • John Piper, Future Grace (Multnomah, 1995) — Less a technical eschatology and more a theology of how the certainty of God's future promises shapes present obedience and joy. Essential reading for anyone who wants to understand how eschatology produces motivation rather than passivity.
  • N.T. Wright, Surprised by Hope (HarperOne, 2008) — The most widely read contemporary account of resurrection, new creation, and their implications for present Christian living.

Heavenly-Minded, Earthly Good

C.S. Lewis identified a paradox that the evidence of history keeps confirming: "If you read history you will find that the Christians who did most for the present world were just those who thought most of the next one. It is since Christians have largely ceased to think of the other world that they have become so ineffective in this." The accusation that heavenly-mindedness produces uselessness has the history exactly backwards.

George Müller (1805–1898) is perhaps the clearest example. He founded five orphan homes in Bristol, England that housed over ten thousand children during his lifetime. He never asked anyone for money. He never told anyone — not even his closest associates — when funds ran out. He prayed. Over his lifetime, more than seven and a half million dollars passed through his hands for orphan care, entirely through unsolicited gifts. When asked why he did it, Müller said his purpose was to demonstrate that the God of the Bible is real and answers prayer — so that believers would be encouraged and skeptics would have reason to look again. His eschatological certainty — his settled confidence that God would provide, because God had made specific promises and could be trusted — was the engine of his entire ministry. A man who believed less firmly in God's faithfulness to his people could not have done what Müller did.

William Wilberforce (1759–1833) spent forty-six years in the British Parliament fighting to end the slave trade and then slavery itself throughout the British Empire. He was shaped decisively by his evangelical faith and by a clear sense of accountability before God — that he would one day answer for what he had done with the influence God had given him. His journal records the conviction that drove him through decades of political failure and personal illness: he was doing this for an audience of one, and that audience would still be there when Parliament had long adjourned. The abolition bill passed three days before he died. He heard the news from his bed and died having done what he was put here to do.

Amy Carmichael (1867–1951) — already mentioned in the church history section — went to India at twenty-eight and never came home. She founded the Dohnavur Fellowship, rescuing children from temple prostitution across fifty-five years of uninterrupted service in a foreign country, with no furloughs, on a faith mission with no guaranteed income. From a sickbed, she wrote some of the most profound devotional literature in the English language. She was not a person for whom eternity was an abstract comfort. It was the ground she stood on while she worked.

John Wesley (1703–1791) gave away all but ten pounds a year of his income for most of his adult life, regardless of how much he earned. When he earned more, he gave more rather than spend more. The logic was eschatological: he could take nothing with him, and everything left behind would be accounted for. He organized the poor, founded clinics, visited prisons, and preached to miners in fields because those were the people in front of him, and they were made in the image of God, and that image was heading somewhere.

The pattern is consistent across traditions, centuries, and personalities. The people who changed the most in this world were, with remarkable regularity, people for whom this world was not the final word. The certainty that God's purposes do not end at death — that every act of justice and mercy and beauty matters permanently, that nothing done in the Lord is wasted — produced a quality of perseverance and a freedom from self-preservation that purely this-worldly motivations cannot reliably generate.

Randy Alcorn makes the application concrete: when you understand that the new creation is a physical, relational, culturally rich world that is the fulfillment rather than the disposal of this one — when you understand that what you do in your body, for other bodies, in this physical world has permanent resonance — the question "why bother?" dissolves. You bother because it matters. Not just now. Always.

🧭 For Facilitators — Using This Page

This page works best as optional reading assigned alongside or after Module 6 (suffering) or Module 9 (living it out). The eschatological framework gives suffering its fullest context — the present darkness is real and the new creation is its answer — and gives the lived Christian life its fullest motivation.

The Müller, Wilberforce, Carmichael, and Wesley examples are particularly useful for groups that contain people who are uncertain whether Christian faith produces anything useful in the world — the "what does it matter?" question. The answer the history gives is striking and often surprising. Very few people know that the driving force behind the abolition of the British slave trade was an evangelical who prayed every morning for an hour because he believed he was accountable to a God who was watching and would one day judge.
