Learning to Pray

Conversation with a God Who Hears
Prayer is where the Christian life either comes alive or quietly dies. It is also where most new believers — and many experienced ones — feel most confused, most inadequate, and most alone. This page does not offer a technique. It tries to describe an encounter, and to be honest about the times that encounter seems to go dark.

What Prayer Actually Is

Westminster Shorter Catechism, Q. 98

"Prayer is an offering up of our desires unto God, for things agreeable to his will, in the name of the Son, by the help of his Spirit, with confession of our sins, and thankful acknowledgment of his mercies."

That definition repays attention. Prayer is not a spiritual technique for adjusting circumstances. It is not a transaction in which sufficient sincerity produces results. It is not a discipline that, if practiced correctly, puts you in a state of inner calm. It is an address — a speaking to a specific Someone. The catechism's definition locates prayer within the whole shape of the gospel: it is offered through the Son, made possible by the Spirit, and directed toward the Father. Every act of Christian prayer is implicitly Trinitarian, even when the person praying has never heard that word.

That Trinitarian structure matters practically. We come to the Father not on the basis of the quality of our prayers but on the basis of Christ's mediation. The writer of Hebrews puts it plainly: because we have a high priest who has passed through the heavens, who was tempted in every way as we are, we can approach the throne of grace with confidence — not performance, not sufficient spiritual preparation, but confidence (Hebrews 4:14–16). The throne of grace is accessible precisely because of what Christ has done, not because of what we feel when we approach it.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

If prayer feels like shouting into an empty room, there is a prior question worth asking: who do you think you are speaking to, and on what basis do you think you have access? The Christian answer is not "you have access because you prayed sincerely enough" or "because you put in the time." It is "you have access because Jesus Christ, who is seated at the right hand of the Father and is currently interceding for you, has opened the way." That is either true or it isn't. If it is true, it completely changes the terms of the conversation — including the times when you feel nothing in return.

The Psalms: The Bible's Own School of Prayer

The most important thing to know about prayer is that God gave us a prayer book. The 150 Psalms are not merely poems about God — they are prayers, meant to be prayed. They were the prayer book of Israel, the prayer book Jesus used (he quoted them from the cross), and the prayer book the early church inherited. If you want to learn to pray, the Psalms are the place to start — not because they are a model to imitate but because they are a permission structure: they show you the full range of what can be said to God.

That range is startling. The Psalms contain adoration, confession, thanksgiving, and supplication. They also contain complaint, accusation, disorientation, rage at enemies, and raw grief. Psalm 22 opens with the cry Jesus made from the cross: "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" Psalm 44 accuses God of sleeping while his people suffer. Psalm 88 — the darkest psalm, the one that ends without resolution — closes with the word darkness and nothing else. None of these are censored from the canon. God put them there. The implication is that this, too, is prayer — not the absence of faith but its honest expression.

The Psalms also move. They rarely stay where they start. A psalm that begins in anguish often ends in trust — not because the circumstances resolved but because the act of bringing the anguish honestly to God changed something in the one praying. Walter Brueggemann's framework is helpful here: the Psalms cycle through orientation (life as it should be), disorientation (the honest cry from the dark), and new orientation (not a return to the old normal but a harder-won trust from the other side of the dark). That three-part movement is the shape of many faithful lives.

📚 For Further Study — The Psalms as Prayer
  • Dietrich Bonhoeffer, Psalms: The Prayer Book of the Bible (Augsburg, 1970) — Brief, essential, written from a prison cell. The best short introduction to praying the Psalms.
  • Walter Brueggemann, Praying the Psalms (Cascade, 2007) — The orientation/disorientation/new orientation framework applied pastorally. Not written from a Reformed perspective but theologically careful and practically rich.
  • Timothy Keller, The Songs of Jesus (Viking, 2015) — Daily readings through all 150 Psalms with brief, searching reflections. A practical companion for anyone wanting to pray through the Psalter.

When Prayer Goes Dark

Psalm 22:2

"O my God, I cry by day, but you do not answer, and by night, but I find no rest."

There is an experience that almost every person who prays with any seriousness will encounter: prayer that feels like nothing. You speak and hear nothing back. The words leave your mouth or your mind and seem to dissolve before they reach anywhere. The sense of God's presence — which may have been vivid at some earlier point — has gone quiet or disappeared entirely. You continue praying because you have been told to, or because you can't think of what else to do, but it feels like talking to a wall.

This experience has a long name in the tradition — the dark night of the soul, a phrase borrowed from the sixteenth-century Spanish mystic John of the Cross — and an even longer history. Augustine described it. Luther went through extended periods of what he called Anfechtung — a German word for a kind of spiritual assault that combines anxiety, despair, and the felt absence of God. The Puritans wrote about it at length; Thomas Goodwin called it "the withdrawing of the light of God's countenance." Charles Spurgeon, possibly the greatest English preacher of the nineteenth century, suffered from depression so severe that he sometimes could not get out of bed, and wrote about it with painful honesty. Even in the twentieth century, Mother Teresa's private letters — published decades after her death — revealed that she had experienced almost no felt sense of God's presence for the last fifty years of her life while continuing to serve the dying in Calcutta. She wrote to her confessor: "The silence and the emptiness is so great that I look and do not see, listen and do not hear."

Two things matter here. First: the felt absence of God is not evidence of his actual absence. The distinction between what we feel and what is true is one of the most important in the Christian life, and nowhere more so than in prayer. The Westminster Confession is careful on this: assurance is real and possible, but it acknowledges that believers can go through seasons in which that assurance is temporarily lost — through sin, through trial, through what the Confession calls "God's withdrawing the light of his countenance" — without their union with Christ being broken (WCF XVIII.4). The relationship is not terminated by the feeling of distance.

Second: the dark night, in the tradition's honest account, is not always a problem to be fixed. Sometimes it is what is being done to you rather than something that is going wrong. The writers who went through it most severely — John of the Cross, Luther, Bunyan, Spurgeon — emerged on the other side with a faith that was less dependent on felt experience and more anchored in the objective facts of the gospel: Christ died, rose, intercedes, holds. A faith tested in the dark is not the same faith it was before the dark. It tends to be more useful to others who are also in the dark.

🕊️ If You Are in the Dark Right Now

If prayer has felt like nothing for a long time — weeks, months, longer — the first thing to say is: you are not alone, and this does not mean what you fear it means. It does not mean God is absent. It does not mean your faith was never real. It does not mean you have done something to deserve it, though honest self-examination is always worth doing. It may simply mean you are in a season that the entire tradition, across every century, has recognized and named.

The counsel of the tradition is consistent: keep praying. Not because the feeling will return if you try hard enough — it may not return quickly — but because prayer in the dark is still prayer, still heard, still somehow the right place to be. Bring the darkness itself. "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" is the opening of a psalm, and Jesus prayed it. If that is the most honest prayer you can manage today, it is enough. The darkness does not have the last word about who is listening.

📚 For Further Study — Spiritual Dryness and the Dark Night

  • Thomas Goodwin, A Child of Light Walking in Darkness (Works, Vol. 3) — The Puritan account of spiritual desertion. Dense but uniquely searching.
  • Barbara Duguid, Extravagant Grace (P&R, 2013) — On John Owen's account of how God uses our ongoing weakness and failure in sanctification. The most accessible contemporary bridge to the Puritan material on spiritual struggle.

Lament as Prayer

Lament is prayer that does not pretend. It is the voice of someone who is bringing to God not a tidy spiritual experience but the actual contents of their life — grief, anger, confusion, a sense of betrayal, the specific weight of a specific loss. The Psalms of lament (roughly a third of the entire Psalter) model what this sounds like: direct address to God, an honest description of the situation, a complaint or petition, and — not always, not instantly — a turn toward trust that is never cheap because of where it came from.

The church has often been better at encouraging thanksgiving and praise than at making room for lament. The result is that people in genuine pain frequently feel they cannot bring their real experience into prayer — that they have to perform a kind of spiritual okayness they do not feel. This is a pastoral failure, not a biblical one. The Bible is full of complaints brought directly to God. Job argues with God for thirty-seven chapters. Jeremiah curses the day he was born (Jeremiah 20:14–18) and God does not rebuke him for it. The psalms of lament — Psalms 13, 22, 44, 88, 130 among them — were written for and preserved in the worship life of Israel precisely because this kind of prayer is real and legitimate.

Lament is not the same as despair. Despair turns away from God; lament turns toward him with the full weight of the situation. The difference is the address. When Psalm 88 cries into the darkness, it is still crying to God. The darkness has not become the destination — it has become the content of a prayer that still knows who is listening, even when that listening feels absent.

Unanswered Prayer

2 Corinthians 12:8–9

"Three times I pleaded with the Lord about this, that it should leave me. But he said to me, 'My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness.'"

The hardest question about prayer is not how to do it. It is why it so often seems to produce nothing. Prayers offered sincerely, consistently, urgently — for a healing that did not come, a marriage that did not survive, a person who did not return, a door that did not open — leave real wounds. The wound is not abstract. It is the specific gap between what was asked and what happened, and it sits in the body long after the mind has found a theological framework for it.

The tradition offers several frameworks for unanswered prayer, and they are not mutually exclusive:

The "not yet" answer. Some prayers are answered later, in ways not initially recognizable. Abraham waited decades for the son he was promised. Joseph's brothers sold him into slavery — the very event that would eventually be the means of his family's rescue. The New Testament is full of people whose situation got dramatically worse before God's purpose became visible in it. This is not a comfortable framework to offer someone in acute pain. It becomes true in retrospect more often than it can be believed in advance.

The "no" answer — and what it means. Paul asked three times for his "thorn in the flesh" to be removed. The answer was no — not because God was absent or indifferent, but because God's purpose was to display his power through Paul's weakness rather than by removing it. The answer Paul received was not the removal of the difficulty but the presence of grace inside it: "My grace is sufficient for you." That is not nothing. But it is not what Paul asked for.

Tim Keller's framework for the "no" answer is among the most pastorally useful in the contemporary Reformed tradition. His argument runs roughly like this: a loving and wise parent does not give a child everything the child asks for, precisely because the parent can see what the child cannot. The child who asks for a knife, or for candy before dinner, or to skip school, is not being neglected when the parent says no. The parent's refusal is an expression of love operating with greater knowledge. God's refusals, Keller argues, operate the same way — except that the gap in knowledge and wisdom between us and God is incomparably larger than the gap between a child and a parent. We are asking from inside a single moment of a single life. God answers from the perspective of eternity, the full weight of what he is doing in us and through us, and a love that is not merely fond but redemptive.

This framework is true, and it is also hard. Keller does not pretend otherwise. The difficulty is that it can too easily become a way of closing the conversation before someone has actually been allowed to grieve the gap between what they asked and what happened. The right order matters: lament first, framework second. A theology of unanswered prayer offered too quickly sounds like a defense of God rather than a presence with the person who is hurting.

⚖️ What Unanswered Prayer Does Not Mean

Two errors are worth naming explicitly because they cause disproportionate damage.

It does not mean your faith was insufficient. The prosperity gospel framework — in which answered prayer is the reward of sufficient faith, and unanswered prayer therefore implies a faith deficit — is directly contradicted by the New Testament. Paul's thorn was not removed despite his three earnest petitions. Jesus prayed in the garden of Gethsemane that the cup might pass from him, and it did not. The writer of Hebrews lists heroes of faith who "died without receiving the things promised" (Hebrews 11:13). Unanswered prayer does not diagnose a faith problem. It diagnoses a world that is not yet fully redeemed.

It does not mean God is indifferent. James 5:16 says the prayer of a righteous person has great power in its effects. Matthew 7:11 says God gives good gifts to those who ask. The God of Scripture is not an absentee landlord who has handed the world to secondary causes and walked away. Unanswered prayer is a mystery, not evidence of abandonment. The mystery lives inside a relationship that Scripture consistently characterizes as attentive, involved, and moved by what his people bring to him.

🕊️ If a Specific Prayer Was Not Answered

If you are carrying the specific weight of a prayer that was not answered — a healing, a person, a situation — this page does not want to rush you to a theological resolution. The frameworks above are real. They may help over time. They are not meant to be deployed against grief before grief has been honored.

What is true, and what may be worth sitting with, is this: Paul's request was denied and he wrote the most joyful letters in the New Testament from prison. He said he had learned contentment "in whatever state I am" (Philippians 4:11) — learned, not arrived at naturally. The word implies a process, a hard-won education. That process is real and costs something. The God who said "my grace is sufficient" was not offering a platitude. He was offering himself in place of what had been withheld. Whether that exchange feels like enough is something each person has to work out, honestly, over time.

📚 For Further Study — Unanswered Prayer
  • Timothy Keller, Prayer: Experiencing Awe and Intimacy with God (Dutton, 2014) — The most comprehensive treatment of prayer from a contemporary Reformed perspective. Chapters 16–18 address unanswered prayer directly with the framework described above.
  • Philip Yancey, Prayer: Does It Make Any Difference? (Zondervan, 2006) — Yancey's most honest and probing book. Not written from a Reformed standpoint but theologically careful, and the best popular treatment of why prayer is harder than it sounds.
  • D.A. Carson, A Call to Spiritual Reformation: Priorities from Paul and His Prayers (Baker, 1992) — Rigorous study of Paul's actual prayers in the epistles; reorients the question of what we should be asking for.

The Spirit Who Intercedes

Romans 8:26–27

"Likewise the Spirit helps us in our weakness. For we do not know what to pray for as we ought, but the Spirit himself intercedes for us with groanings too deep for words. And he who searches hearts knows what is the mind of the Spirit, because the Spirit intercedes for the saints according to the will of God."

This passage does something important: it moves the ground of prayer from our competence to God's. The problem Paul describes — we do not know what to pray for as we ought — is not a problem with beginners who haven't learned the right techniques yet. It is the permanent condition of every believer, including Paul, including the saints across twenty centuries who have prayed with far more depth and consistency than most of us will manage. We don't know what to ask for. Our vision is too short, our self-knowledge too limited, our understanding of what we actually need too compromised by what we merely want.

Into that gap, the Spirit enters. He intercedes — takes up the prayer, carries it, presents it — with groanings too deep for words. Whatever that phrase means in its full depth (and scholars have discussed it at length), the pastoral point is clear: when we cannot find the words, when the prayer is inarticulate, when all we have is the ache and not the language for it, something is still happening. The Spirit is not waiting for us to produce a well-formed petition. He is present in the wordless place and is himself the intercession.

This connects to the dark night. One of the reasons extended seasons of prayerlessness are not the same as abandonment is precisely Romans 8:26–27: the Spirit's intercession does not depend on the felt quality of our prayer. The One who searches hearts knows what the Spirit is doing on our behalf, even when we can neither feel it nor articulate it. The traffic between us and the Father does not stop when our words do.

For a fuller treatment of the Holy Spirit — who he is, what he does, and the contested question of spiritual gifts — see The Holy Spirit.

The Shape of Prayer

There is no single prescribed form for Christian prayer. The New Testament contains prayers of a line (Peter sinking: "Lord, save me!"), prayers of extended intercession (John 17), prayers of thanksgiving, prayers of lament, prayers in corporate worship, prayers alone in the dark. The Lord's Prayer — Jesus's answer when the disciples asked "teach us to pray" — is a model of remarkable compression: address, kingdom orientation, daily provision, forgiveness and the posture of extending it, protection, and doxology, in six petitions. Jesus said to pray "in this way" — not to recite this exact text as a formula, but to let its shape form the structure of prayer.

The ACTS framework (Adoration, Confession, Thanksgiving, Supplication) that many traditions teach is a useful guide for beginners because it pushes against the natural human gravity toward making prayer entirely about requests. Starting with adoration — with who God is rather than what you need — reorients the entire conversation. Confession before supplication is the posture of someone who knows they are coming to the throne of grace rather than a vending machine.

But the framework should remain a scaffold, not a cage. The goal is not to complete the categories. The goal is honest conversation with a God who is listening — who, in the words of the Westminster Shorter Catechism, is "more ready to hear than we are to ask." What that conversation looks like will change across seasons of life. It will sometimes be long and sometimes two words. It will sometimes be articulate and sometimes inarticulate. It will sometimes feel like communion and sometimes like shouting into silence. All of that is prayer. None of it is wasted.

🧭 For Facilitators — Using This Page in a Group

This page is best introduced after Module 8 (Obedience as the Door) rather than earlier, because Module 8 establishes spiritual disciplines as practices of placing ourselves where God has promised to meet us — prayer lives naturally in that framework. It can also be assigned between sessions as individual reading for groups that are ready for more personal engagement.

The dark night and unanswered prayer sections deserve particular care. If you open these topics in a group, be prepared for the conversation to go personal quickly. People carry specific unanswered prayers — often for years — and rarely have a safe place to name them. Do not rush toward resolution. The goal is not to get the group to a theological conclusion about unanswered prayer. It is to make it safe to bring the actual question, which is usually more specific and more painful than any framework can immediately address.
