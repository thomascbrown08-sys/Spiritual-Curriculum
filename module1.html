The Honest Mirror

What We Were Made For, and What We Have Turned Away From
We begin here because it requires no prior commitment to Scripture's authority — only honesty about one's own experience. The module begins not with what is wrong with us but with the God who is genuinely worth wanting, and then names the tragedy of turning away from him. The diagnosis lands hardest when we have already seen what is being forsaken. One note before beginning: if you are engaging this material with genuine openness — if these questions feel live rather than merely academic — that is not a neutral human achievement. The desire to look honestly is itself a gift. Something has already begun.
📖 A NOTE BEFORE YOU BEGIN

Jesus told a story about a man with a plank of wood in his eye who kept trying to remove the splinter from his neighbor's eye (Matthew 7:3–5). The comedy is the point: we are always the surgeon when we should be the patient. Read what follows as the patient — not as someone who has finally found the right diagnosis for someone else. The beam-and-speck problem is not that we care about others' faults. It is that we notice them first, longest, and most clearly. Reverse that order, and something changes — both in you and in how you carry what you've learned to anyone else.

Part A: What We Were Made For

Jeremiah 2:13

"My people have committed two evils: they have forsaken me, the fountain of living waters, and hewed out cisterns for themselves, broken cisterns that can hold no water."

The indictment of Jeremiah 2:13 has two parts — but it is the first part that gives the second its weight. The forsaking is a tragedy because of what is being forsaken. A fountain of living waters. God himself. We begin with the fountain, not the broken cisterns. The diagnosis is sharpest when we have already seen what is being abandoned.

The God this curriculum describes is not a cosmic rule-enforcer. He is the God who plants gardens and walks in them in the cool of the day. He is the God whose eternal nature — Father, Son, and Spirit — is self-giving love overflowing between three Persons from before time began. Creation was not a necessity for him. It was an act of extravagant generosity — and it was, at the same time, an act for his own glory: Isaiah 43:7 says he created us "for my glory," and Revelation 4:11 speaks of all things existing "for his pleasure." Love and glory are not in tension here; the God who is love creates a world that glorifies him precisely by flourishing in the love he designed it to display.

📐 ON CREATION FOR GLORY AND FOR LOVE — WHY THESE ARE ONE THING

Scripture says all things were created for Christ (Colossians 1:16) — not merely by him, but for him, as their goal and destination. The gospel's own shape then declares that the end for which we were made is union with Christ: to be brought into the eternal fellowship of Father, Son, and Spirit, to know and be known by the God who is love. What this means is that God's glory and our deepest good are not competing ends pulling in different directions. They are the same end seen from two sides of the same gift.

Jonathan Edwards argued this with characteristic precision: God glorifies himself most fully by communicating himself to creatures who enjoy him. The glory is not diminished by being shared — it is expressed through the sharing. When we flourish in union with Christ, when we enjoy him and are enjoyed by him, we are not distracting from his glory; we are the form his glory takes in creatures made for exactly this. Isaiah 43:7 says he created us for his glory; Ephesians 1:9–10 says his purpose is to unite all things in Christ; these are not in tension — the glory is the union.

Edwards expressed this with an image that is worth sitting with: "The refulgence shines upon and into the creature, and is reflected back to the luminary. The beams of glory come from God, are something of God, and are refunded back again to their original. So that the whole is of God, and in God, and to God; and he is the beginning, and the middle, and the end." The creature's joy in God does not divert the glory — it completes the circuit. Giving is the nature of the fountain; returning praise is the nature of the creature who has drunk from it. They are one motion.

The apparent dilemma — love-overflow versus glory — dissolves when the telos is named: we were made for Christ, which means our good and his glory are not two things. They are one thing. The fountain of living waters does not share himself at cost to himself. Giving is the nature of the fountain.

John 17:24

"Father, I desire that they also, whom you have given me, may be with me where I am, to see my glory that you have given me because you loved me before the foundation of the world."

This prayer of Jesus points toward something remarkable: the love between Father and Son pre-dates creation. The gospel is an invitation into that eternal communion — a claim that rests on the doctrines of union with Christ, the Spirit's indwelling, and the participation of the redeemed in divine life. These are large theological movements that later modules develop further. What is established here is the direction of travel: the gospel is not only rescue from danger — it is invitation into the relationship that has always been. The rescue is real and necessary; it is the door through which the invitation is extended. What the rescue saves us for is what gives the rescue its full weight.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

Three phrases in the paragraph above carry a lot of weight. Here is what they mean in practice. Union with Christ means that the Christian's relationship with God is not just legal — a pardon from a judge — but relational and alive, like being grafted into a living vine (Jesus uses exactly that image in John 15). The Spirit's indwelling means that the Holy Spirit actually takes up residence in a person who trusts Christ — not as an external force that occasionally shows up, but as a permanent, interior presence. Participation in divine life is the largest claim: that the goal of the gospel is not merely to get us to heaven but to draw us into the kind of love and life that God himself has always had — the self-giving, other-oriented love that has always flowed between Father, Son, and Spirit. The gospel is not a rescue operation that ends with safety. It is the beginning of belonging to something you were made for.

This phrase — participation in divine life — is grounded directly in 2 Peter 1:4, which describes believers becoming θείας κοινωνοὶ φύσεως (theias koinōnoi physeōs): "partakers of the divine nature." This is the scriptural anchor for what the Reformed tradition describes as Calvin's unio mystica — mystical union with Christ, the source from which both justification and sanctification flow, not merely their legal consequence. Union with Christ in Reformed theology is not an optional add-on to forensic justification; it is the root from which the whole Christian life grows. The Eastern Christian tradition has developed the concept of theosis as a parallel account — transformation by genuine union with God: not a merging that erases creaturely identity, but a participation in his life that restores the image we were made to bear. Both traditions are drawing on the same scriptural wellspring of 2 Peter 1:4, though they develop it through different theological frameworks and with different governing concerns. What Scripture makes plain, and what every serious stream of Protestant theology affirms, is that the gospel is simultaneously declaration and transformation — what is declared over us and what is being done within us are two dimensions of the same gift, and neither is complete without the other.

A seventeenth-century catechism asks: "What is the chief end of man?" — and answers: "Man's chief end is to glorify God, and to enjoy him forever." A more contemporary way to say the same thing: you were made to find your deepest and most lasting satisfaction in God — and that satisfaction, fully received, turns out to be the very thing that glorifies him. The glorifying and the enjoying are not two separate duties. They are one act. Jonathan Edwards, the eighteenth-century theologian, put it this way: "The happiness of the creature consists in rejoicing in God, by which also God is magnified and exalted." Augustine said it earlier still: "You made us for yourself, and our hearts are restless until they rest in you." The thirst Module 1 names is not accidental — it is the signature of a design. You are thirsty for a reason. And the reason is a fountain.

⚖️ What God's Love Is — and What It Isn't

There is a version of the gospel circulating widely in popular Christianity that defines God's love as his making much of us — affirming our worth, boosting our sense of value, meeting our felt emotional needs. It is easy to understand why this version is appealing. It sounds kind, and it speaks directly into a culture where people routinely feel dismissed and unvalued.

But Edwards observes, with unusual precision, that this gets the direction of the love exactly backwards. The gospel's good news is not that God makes much of us. It is that he graciously frees us and empowers us to make much of him — which, because of who he is, turns out to be the deepest possible source of human joy. As Edwards says: "True saints have their minds, in the first place, inexpressibly pleased and delighted with the things of God. But the dependence of the affections of hypocrites is in a contrary order: they first rejoice that they are made so much of by God; and then on that ground, he seems in a sort, lovely to them." The hypocrite's gospel has God orbiting human need. The true gospel has human beings finding their orbit in God — and discovering, in that reorientation, the satisfaction they were made for.

This distinction is not a minor refinement. The gospel of self-esteem, however kindly intended, offers a stone in place of bread. It gives people an affirmed version of the same self that was already in the grip of the broken cisterns. The real gospel offers something far more unsettling and far more satisfying: not a better opinion of yourself, but a new object for your wanting — One who is actually capable of filling what is actually empty.

🌍 The Triune God and Honor-Shame Cultures

Western presentations of the gospel often focus primarily on legal guilt — the broken law, the just penalty, the acquittal. In many cultures shaped by honor-shame frameworks (much of the Middle East, East Asia, parts of Africa and Latin America), this framing lands differently. The Trinitarian picture of a God who is inherently relational and self-giving — and whose gospel is an invitation into belonging — can be more immediately compelling than the legal register. The image of the father running to restore honor to the returning son (Luke 15) speaks directly into shame-based experience in ways the courtroom metaphor does not.

Facilitators working with groups whose primary framework is honor-shame rather than legal guilt should foreground Part A's community and belonging language, and draw on Part C's shame register rather than leading with guilt.

Part B: The Honest Diagnosis

Now the indictment lands with its full weight. The exchange Jeremiah names is not primarily about law-breaking — it is about a trade that makes no sense: inexhaustible life for things that leak. C.S. Lewis's description in The Weight of Glory captures it precisely: we are half-hearted creatures settling for too little, not creatures whose desires are excessive. The problem is not what we want but what we have attached our wanting to.

There is a precision here that is worth pausing on, because it changes the emotional register of the diagnosis entirely. The Greek word translated "fall short" in Romans 3:23 — husterountai — does not mean an arrow that was aimed at the right target and fell short of the mark. It means lack: you could have had this as treasure, and you do not have it, because you chose something else instead. Romans 1:23 confirms the direction: people "exchanged the glory of the incorruptible God for an image." This is not a failure of effort. It is a deliberate transaction — the selection of an inferior good when a superior one was on offer. Which means the weight of the diagnosis is not shame at how far we fell, but grief at what we traded away. The broken cisterns are not evidence of inadequacy. They are evidence of an astonishing exchange.

Romans 7:24–25

"Wretched man that I am! Who will deliver me from this body of death? Thanks be to God through Jesus Christ our Lord!"

Paul's anatomy in Romans 6–7 is the most precise description of the inner experience of sin in the New Testament. It describes not a series of bad choices but a power — a dominion that exercises a pull we cannot simply decide to resist. The person who has sincerely tried to change and found themselves unable to sustain it is not describing a character flaw. They are describing a condition Paul names and the gospel directly addresses.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

Paul is not just saying people make bad choices. He is saying that sin operates more like an addiction or a compulsion than like a simple mistake — something that has its hooks in us at a level that willpower and good intentions cannot reliably reach. Most people know this from experience: there are patterns in their lives they have genuinely tried to change, more than once, and the change didn't hold. Paul's point is that this experience is not a sign that you haven't tried hard enough. It is an accurate diagnosis of a condition that requires outside help — specifically, the kind of liberation that only the gospel offers. This is why the gospel has to be news about what God has done, not advice about what you should try harder to do.

🧭 The Romans 7 Debate — A Facilitator Note

Whether Romans 7:14–25 describes Paul's pre-Christian experience, his ongoing Christian experience, or Israel's corporate experience under the Law is one of the most genuinely contested questions in Pauline scholarship. Augustine initially read it as pre-Christian and later changed his view. Luther, Calvin, and the Reformed tradition have read it as the ongoing struggle of a genuine believer. N.T. Wright and New Perspective scholars read it as Israel's corporate experience under the Mosaic Law. Each position is held by serious students of Scripture who have read the text carefully.

Facilitators should not feel pressure to resolve the debate — and the module does not require them to. What matters for this session is what every interpretive tradition draws from the passage, because on the points that matter most they converge:

The condition described is real. Whether the "I" is pre-Christian, Christian, or corporate Israel, what Paul depicts — wanting to do good and finding oneself unable to sustain it, moral earnestness that cannot save itself — resonates with honest human experience across every tradition, century, and culture. People recognize it from the inside.

The answer must come from outside. Every reading arrives at the same place: verse 25's cry — "Thanks be to God through Jesus Christ our Lord!" — is the only resolution the passage itself offers. No tradition reads verse 24 as an invitation to try harder. The direction of rescue is always outward, always toward Christ.

The gospel addresses a condition, not merely a pattern of bad choices. Whatever one concludes about the speaker's identity, the passage describes something that requires liberation, not self-improvement. That is the diagnostic use this module makes of it — and that use holds across all three readings.

If the debate arises in discussion, facilitators can honestly name the different views, note that serious scholars hold each, and redirect toward the convergence: the cry is real, and its answer is Christ. That convergence is the ground the module stands on.

🧭 Preaching to Yourself First — A Facilitator Note

As you prepare to lead others through this material, there is one discipline worth naming: apply what you find here to yourself before you bring it to anyone else. Not because your group doesn't need it, but because a truth you have not honestly applied to your own life tends to be delivered with an edge the message itself does not require — and the edge is often all the other person hears.

Martyn Lloyd-Jones called self-examination the first task of the Christian mind. The Puritans built it into their devotional practice so deliberately that their sermon preparation often looked more like confession than composition — they were preaching to themselves first, and only then to their congregations. John Owen, perhaps the most rigorous theologian of that era, wrote that a minister "may fill his pews, and empty hell" without having first applied the gospel to his own soul. The warning was aimed at himself as much as anyone.

The practical shape of this for small group leaders: before each session, sit with the diagnostic question the module raises and answer it honestly for yourself. Where do you recognize the condition being named? Where do you feel the pull of the broken cisterns? The group will sense whether their facilitator is standing above the material or inside it — and it makes all the difference to how the room receives it.

Part C: Three Faces of the Same Condition

Different people in the room will find their primary experience of the human problem in different registers. The three faces are not three separate problems — they are three angles on the same condition, each true simultaneously, each pointing toward the same need.

Before naming those three faces, one thing deserves to be stated plainly — because the rest of the curriculum depends on it, and because it is the thing most commonly missed when Christianity is reduced to a system of moral improvement:

The answer to sin is not a better no. It is a better yes.

The heart does not release what it loves by being told not to love it. It releases what it loves when it encounters something it loves more. This is not a therapeutic observation — it is a theological one, grounded in the nature of the human will and expressed clearly in Scripture. Jesus does not say "stop wanting" — he says "if you want to be satisfied, come to me" (John 6:35; 7:37–38). The Psalmist does not say "suppress your desires for inferior things" — he says "delight yourself in the LORD, and he will give you the desires of your heart" (Psalm 37:4). The logic of the gospel is not suppression but displacement: the affection that held you is crowded out by an affection that is larger.

Thomas Chalmers, a nineteenth-century Scottish preacher, named this mechanism in what became one of the most influential short sermons in the English Reformed tradition: The Expulsive Power of a New Affection. His argument, developed at length from 1 John 2:15 ("Do not love the world or the things in the world"), runs as follows. You cannot command a person to stop loving what they love and expect the command to succeed. The love will remain because the will is shaped by affection, not by instruction alone. The only power capable of displacing an entrenched love is a superior love — one that makes the inferior object lose its appeal not because it was forbidden but because it was outshone. The world is not expelled from the heart by a moral campaign against it. It is expelled when God — the fountain of living waters — becomes more real, more beautiful, more satisfying to the person than the broken cisterns ever managed to be. At that point, the cisterns are not abandoned under duress. They are abandoned because something better has arrived.

The word that names what Chalmers is describing is beauty. Not beauty as a decorative quality — something pleasant to look at — but beauty in the older, fuller sense that the Christian tradition inherited from Scripture: the perfection of a thing, the quality that makes it fully and genuinely itself, the kind of excellence that produces not merely appreciation but desire. This is what the Psalmist is reaching for in one of the most concentrated verses in the Hebrew Psalter:

Psalm 27:4

"One thing I have asked from the LORD, that I shall seek: that I may dwell in the house of the LORD all the days of my life, to behold the beauty of the LORD and to meditate in his temple."

Notice what David names as the single thing worth asking for. Not comfort, not safety, not answered prayer, not vindication from his enemies — all of which he asks for elsewhere in the same psalm. The one thing: to behold the beauty of the LORD. The Hebrew word translated beauty here — nōʿam — carries connotations of pleasantness, loveliness, and delight. It is used elsewhere of the pleasantness of wisdom, the loveliness of friends, the graciousness of speech. David is not speaking metaphorically or abstractly. He is saying that God himself is genuinely, objectively, and in the most fundamental sense beautiful — and that seeing this is the organizing desire of his entire life.

Jonathan Edwards grounded his entire theology in exactly this claim. For Edwards, beauty is not merely a pleasant attribute of God — it is a description of what the Trinity fundamentally is. The Father, Son, and Spirit in their eternal self-giving love are the original beauty from which all other beauty derives its quality and its pull. He wrote: "All the beauty to be found throughout the whole creation is but the reflection of the diffused beams of that Being who hath an infinite fullness of brightness and glory." The sunset that stops you, the music that reaches what words cannot, the moment of self-forgetfulness before something vast and lovely — for Edwards, all of it is shadow, echo, and reflection of the primary beauty that is God himself. Creation's beauty is not an end; it is an arrow. Every beautiful thing in the world is pointing somewhere.

This beauty can be perceived in many registers, and different people find the arrow at different points. For some it is the natural world — the kind of experience that prompted C.S. Lewis to describe an inconsolable longing, a desire that no earthly thing fully satisfies, that points beyond itself to "something which cannot be put into words." For others it is music — Augustine described how he wept when he first heard the church in Milan singing, moved by something he could not yet name. For others it is the moral beauty of a life genuinely given for others — the kind of beauty Dostoyevsky was reaching for when he wrote that beauty will save the world; he meant specifically the beauty of sacrificial love, of which the cross is the supreme expression. For still others it is the intellectual beauty of Scripture's coherence — the experience described in Module 3 of finding that forty authors over fifteen centuries tell one story with an integrity that human conspiracy cannot explain. And for some it arrives in the deep interior of suffering, in the unexpected discovery that God is present in darkness — the kind of beauty that the Russian tradition calls the beauty that comes in the night. These are not competing paths. They are different facets of the same light, different angles on the same face.

This is why the gospel is not fundamentally a demand or even primarily an argument — though it makes demands and it has arguments. It is fundamentally a revelation: God, showing himself as he actually is. The response it calls for is not primarily compliance or even intellectual assent, but the response a person makes when they encounter something genuinely beautiful — recognition, desire, the sense of having found what they were looking for without knowing they were looking for it. Augustine described it as his heart being restless until it rested in God. Lewis described it as Joy — the shooting arrow of desire that wounds with its pleasure. David described it as the one thing worth asking for. The curriculum's task is not to manufacture this response. It is to remove obstructions and point clearly at what is there to be seen.

This shapes everything about how this curriculum approaches the Christian life. The goal is not to produce people who are better at avoiding what is wrong. The goal is to produce people who are increasingly captivated by what is right — specifically, by the God who is right and good and beautiful and who is, as Augustine said, the rest for which every restless heart was made. The avoidance of sin is a consequence of this captivation, not a substitute for it. A person who is genuinely growing in love for God will find, over time, that the competing loves lose their grip — not because they attacked them but because something better displaced them. The diagnosis in Part B (the exchange) implies this answer: if the problem is that we chose a lesser good over a greater one, the answer is encountering the greater good so fully that the comparison becomes ridiculous.

⚖️ Does This Let Us Off the Effort Hook?

Not for a moment. The New Testament does not say "wait until you love God enough and then obedience will flow effortlessly." It says "reckon yourselves dead to sin" (Romans 6:11) — a command, addressed to people for whom this is not yet their felt experience. And it says "put to death what is earthly in you" (Colossians 3:5) — also a command. The disciplines, the mortification, the active effort that Module 7 and Module 8 describe — none of that is eliminated by Chalmers' insight. What his insight does is correct the motive and the mechanism. The effort is not aimed at generating holiness through willpower. It is aimed at increasing exposure to the One whose beauty displaces the competition. You pray, read Scripture, fast, serve, and gather — not to earn points but to put yourself where God has promised to meet people and to cultivate the attention to him that makes the affection grow. The effort creates conditions. God produces the captivation. Both are necessary.

Sin as Power: I Cannot Change

The Romans 6–7 register — the experience of bondage, of compulsion, of genuinely wanting to be different and finding oneself unable to sustain it. The gospel's answer: liberation (Luke 4:18).

Sin as Guilt: I Stand Condemned

The Romans 1–3 register — objective moral fault that cannot simply be overlooked. The Greek verb dikaioō (to justify) carries two inseparable dimensions: a forensic one (a legal declaration that the record has been genuinely addressed — not overlooked but dealt with at the cross) and a covenantal one (a declaration of full membership in the people of God — belonging, acceptance, standing in the family). The gospel's answer to guilt is not merely an acquittal but a welcome.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

The word "justify" in the New Testament (from the Greek dikaioō) means something more than "make me feel okay about myself." It has two distinct layers worth separating. The first is like a courtroom: a judge looks at the record and declares the case settled — not because nothing happened, but because the penalty has been genuinely paid. Guilt is real; the cross is the real answer to it. The second layer is like a family: you are not just cleared of charges, you are welcomed in, given full standing, treated as a full member. Not on probation. Not in the waiting room. The gospel's answer to guilt is both: the charges are dropped and you are brought home.

Sin as Shame: I Am Defective

Deeper than guilt: not "I have done wrong things" but "I am a wrong kind of person." The Prodigal Son story addresses this most directly. The father's response — running, robe, ring, sandals, feast — is specifically an honor restoration. He is not only forgiven; he is reinstated.

🕊️ The Person Who Has Already Tried the Fountain

Some people in the room will not be strangers to Christianity. They prayed. They attended. They tried — sincerely — and came back thirsty. Their broken cisterns are not alternatives they chose over God; they are what they turned to after the God-option seemed to fail them.

Read Psalm 42 in full: this is not the voice of someone who has never tasted the fountain. "These things I remember as I pour out my soul: how I used to go to the house of God." The past tense matters. The panting of verse 1 is not longing from a distance — it is the anguish of someone who knew the water and now cannot find their way back.

Isaiah 45:15 names God's hiddenness as a revealed theological category, not an anomaly: "Truly you are a God who hides himself, O God of Israel, the Savior." And most significantly: on the cross, the Son of God cited Psalm 22 — "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" The one who is the fountain experienced, from the inside, what it is to seek the Father and find silence. That experience has been entered by God himself. It is not the last word — but it is a real word, honored by inclusion in the canon.

Part D: Good News vs. Good Advice

The three conditions — enslaved, condemned, ashamed — share one feature: they are conditions, not merely performances. A slave cannot advise their way to freedom. A condemned prisoner cannot morally improve their way to acquittal. A person drowning in shame cannot decide to stop feeling defective. Each requires an outside intervention — liberation, a verdict, a welcome — that comes from beyond the person's own resources.

Romans 8:1–3

"There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus. For the law of the Spirit of life has set you free in Christ Jesus from the law of sin and death. For God has done what the law, weakened by the flesh, could not do."

The resurrection seals this announcement. The liberation, justification, and honor-restoration proclaimed in the gospel are not only past declarations — forensic and covenantal — they are present realities made available through the Spirit of the risen Christ. Romans 8:11 ties it together: the same power that raised Jesus from the dead is the power that now works in those who belong to him. The good news is not a promise about the distant future. It is an announcement about what God has already done — and what the Spirit is actively doing now.

The Antinomianism Guard

The good news vs. good advice distinction can be misread as: "since the gospel is an announcement, there are no commands." Paul anticipates and rejects this immediately in Romans 6:1. "Reckon yourselves dead to sin and alive to God" (6:11) is a command. "Present your members to God as instruments for righteousness" (6:13) is a command. The imperatives do not earn the grace; they are the response grace naturally calls forth — and also the explicit command of the God who gives grace. Both are true without tension.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

"Antinomianism" is a technical word for the idea that grace means there are no rules — that since God forgives everything, it doesn't matter how you live. Paul raises this objection himself in Romans 6 and is blunt in rejecting it: "By no means!" The point is not that obedience doesn't matter — it does, and there are real commands in the New Testament. The point is that obedience is the response to grace, not the way to earn it. Think of it this way: a child who has just been rescued from drowning doesn't obey their rescuer to earn the rescue. The rescue already happened. But love and gratitude toward the person who saved them is the natural response — and the rescuer has every right to ask things of them going forward. The commands are real. They just come after the grace, not before it.

There is something worth planting here, even though it belongs more fully to Module 8: both the person who tries to earn God's favor through religious performance and the person who has quietly stopped caring about obedience tend to assume the same thing about God — that he doesn't actually love them freely. The rule-follower performs because they are afraid that his approval might be withheld. The person who has stopped trying has concluded that his love isn't available anyway, so why bother? Both are responding to a distorted picture of who God is. The remedy for both is not a better balance of law and grace — it is a truer vision of the Father himself. Module 8 traces this to its root.

Discussion Questions
  • Is there an experience, a moment of beauty or love, that gave you a sense of something larger than yourself — something you couldn't name but couldn't dismiss?
  • Where do you most recognize the Romans 7 experience — wanting to be different and finding yourself unable to sustain it?
  • Which of the three registers — power, guilt, or shame — feels most accurate to your primary experience?
  • What would it mean for the gospel to be news rather than advice — an announcement of what has been done rather than guidance on what to do?
