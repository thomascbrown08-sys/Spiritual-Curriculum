Module 12 · Extended Reading · Council of Chalcedon, 451 AD

The Chalcedonian Definition

How the Church Said What Nicaea Left Unfinished
← Return to Module 12: We Believe · Also available: The Athanasian Creed

The Nicene Creed established, at great cost, that Jesus is fully God — "of one Being with the Father." But it left a question that would occupy the church for another century: if Jesus is fully God, how can he also be genuinely human? And if he is both, how do those two natures relate to each other in one person?

These questions were not abstract. They touched the pastoral center of Christianity: does God, in Jesus, genuinely know what it is to be us? Has God truly entered human suffering, limitation, and death from the inside? Or is the incarnation something less — a divine being wearing a human costume, or a human being elevated to near-divine status, or two beings somehow sharing a body? The Council of Chalcedon (451 AD) produced the church's most precise answer: the four fences.

The Story Behind the Definition

Cyril of Alexandria c. 376–444 AD The central figure in the controversy leading to Chalcedon — and a morally complex one. His Christology is essentially vindicated by the council: he insisted that the divine and human in Christ constitute a genuine unity, not a loose partnership. But his methods were often harsh; his treatment of opponents (including Nestorius, whom he hounded into exile) was aggressive even by the standards of late-antique church politics. He is included here deliberately: the early church fathers were not uniformly noble figures. They were people under enormous pressure, working out enormously consequential questions, and they were sometimes wrong about how to treat their enemies even when they were right about the theology. The truth they found is not diminished by the cost at which they found it.

After Nicaea, a new controversy emerged around Nestorius, bishop of Constantinople (d. c. 451), who taught a position that effectively produced two persons in Christ — a divine person and a human person so sharply distinguished that they barely touched. For Nestorius, Mary should not be called Theotokos ("God-bearer" or "Mother of God") because she bore only the human Christ, not God himself. His opponents, led by Cyril of Alexandria, recognized that this position effectively divided Christ into two subjects and collapsed the incarnation's significance: if the divine and human in Jesus are merely associated rather than genuinely united in one person, then it is not God who is born, who suffers, who dies and rises. It is a human being with divine backup.

The Council of Ephesus (431 AD) condemned Nestorius and affirmed Theotokos — a Christological statement, not a statement about Mary's nature. The point was this: if Jesus is God, then the woman who bore him bore God. Mary is called God-bearer because of who her son is, not because of anything about her own nature.

But the pendulum then swung in the opposite direction. A monk named Eutyches taught that after the incarnation, Christ has only one nature — the divine absorbing the human so completely that his humanity was essentially dissolved. The church recognized this as a different but equally serious error: if Christ's humanity is absorbed into his divinity, then he does not genuinely share the human condition, and the incarnation loses its pastoral weight. Hebrews 4:15 — "he was tempted in every way, just as we are" — becomes meaningless if there was no genuine human nature to be tempted.

Chalcedon (451 AD) was convened under the emperor Marcian to settle both the Nestorian and the Eutychian errors. Approximately 500 bishops gathered — roughly twice the attendance of Nicaea, and the largest of the early councils, reflecting how much the empire itself had grown. What they produced is one of the most precisely crafted theological documents in Christian history.

The Four Fences

The Chalcedonian Definition works primarily by exclusion. Rather than explaining how the divine and human natures are united (which would require more knowledge than the church claimed to have), it defines the boundary conditions — what the incarnation is not. Four negatives, each closing off a specific error:

Without mixture The two natures are not blended into something new. There is no "Christ-nature" that is neither fully divine nor fully human. The divine remains fully divine; the human remains fully human.
Without confusion The natures do not lose their distinct properties by being joined. The divine nature does not become mortal; the human nature does not become omniscient. Each nature retains what belongs to it.
Without separation The natures are not held together loosely, as if Christ could be "split" into his divine and human aspects. They constitute one person — genuinely, inseparably.
Without division There is no Nestorian division into two persons — a divine person and a human person awkwardly sharing a body. One person. One subject. "The same Lord Jesus Christ."

The formula produced: one person in two natures. Fully human, fully divine, united without mixture or confusion, inseparable without separation or division.

The Chalcedonian Definition (451 AD) — Condensed Following the holy fathers, we all with one voice confess our Lord Jesus Christ to be one and the same Son, the same perfect in Godhead and the same perfect in manhood, truly God and truly man… of one substance with the Father as regards his Godhead, and the same of one substance with us as regards his manhood… acknowledged in two natures, without confusion, without change, without division, without separation; the difference of the natures being in no way abolished because of the union, but rather the characteristic property of each nature being preserved, and concurring into one Person and one hypostasis.

Why This Matters Pastorally

Chalcedon is sometimes dismissed as theological hairsplitting. It is the opposite. Every clause addresses a question with pastoral consequences.

Fully human: If Christ is not genuinely human — if his suffering in Gethsemane is theatrical, if his cry of dereliction is performed — then we do not have a God who has entered our condition. We have a God who visited it from a safe distance. Hebrews 2:17–18 says Christ "had to be made like his brothers and sisters in every way" so that "he is able to help those who are being tempted." The ability to help depends on the reality of the sharing. Chalcedon protects that.

Fully divine: If Christ is not genuinely God, then his death is the death of a creature — however noble — and cannot bear the weight the New Testament places on it. Only a mediator who is genuinely both God and human can bridge both sides of the relationship. Chalcedon protects that too.

One person: If the divine and human are two persons merely associated in one body, there is no genuine incarnation — no moment where God truly became what we are. It is God and a human being, not God-become-human. Chalcedon insists: one person, one subject, genuinely both.

Where Chalcedon Is Still Contested

Chalcedon did not heal all divisions. Several ancient churches — the Oriental Orthodox communion, including the Coptic Church (Egypt), Ethiopian Orthodox, Armenian Apostolic, and Syriac Orthodox — did not accept the Chalcedonian formulation and are technically classified as Miaphysite: holding that Christ has one nature (divine and human united), not two natures in one person. These are living communities with hundreds of millions of members, maintaining a position their own tradition calls Miaphysite — one composite nature of divinity and humanity, not two. The Chalcedonian churches have historically called this position Monophysitism (one nature), a label the Oriental Orthodox generally reject as a mischaracterization: they argue their position is not the same as Eutyches’ (the divine absorbing the human), but a different account of unity. The naming dispute itself is part of why modern ecumenical dialogue has made progress: when both sides describe what they actually mean, the underlying convictions are often closer than the historical labels suggest.

This is an open question, not a settled one — and a reminder that even 1,500 years later, the questions Chalcedon addressed have living communities still working through them.
