The Escalation Trap

"Making Every Hill a Dying Hill"
← Back to Overview  |  Next: Tactic 2: The Vocabulary Trap →

The Presenting Dispute

It starts with something real. Two people — or two factions — disagree about something that matters to them: a change to the worship service, a decision by the elders, a doctrinal emphasis, a speaker someone invited. The disagreement is expressed. Others join in. Positions harden. Before long, people who began by saying "I don't think this is wise" are saying "this is a faithfulness issue," and people who were initially open to discussion are saying "if you push this, you're questioning the authority of Scripture."

Nobody planned this. Neither party woke up that morning intending to make the conversation nuclear. But somehow, by a series of small moves that each felt reasonable at the time, a dispute about a preference has become a dispute about salvation — and now nobody can step back without feeling like they are abandoning their convictions.

This is the Escalation Trap. And it is one of the Enemy's most reliable tools precisely because it requires no deception about facts. It only requires a gradual, nearly invisible elevation of stakes.

The Enemy's Actual Move

The mechanism is simple: the Enemy exploits the genuine virtue of seriousness. Christians who take God's Word seriously do not want to be the person who shrugged at something that mattered. So when a disagreement is framed as a faithfulness question, the instinct of a serious believer is to take it seriously. When someone implies that backing down is backing away from the truth, a person who loves the truth will dig in.

The escalation works by moving a dispute through levels of seriousness without anyone calling the move explicitly. There is a rough ladder it tends to follow:

  • Level 1: Preference. "I like it this way; you like it that way. Both are fine."
  • Level 2: Conviction. "I think this way is better, and here's why."
  • Level 3: Principle. "This isn't just preference — it reflects something important about how we understand Scripture."
  • Level 4: Essential doctrine. "This is a matter of faithfulness. I cannot compromise on it."
  • Level 5: Salvation-adjacent. "If this goes through, I don't know what this church believes anymore."

Each step up the ladder is small enough to feel justified. By Level 5, the original question — which may have been a genuinely Level 1 or Level 2 matter — is being handled with the gravity of a Level 5 dispute. And Level 5 disputes cannot be resolved by reasonable conversation. They can only be won or lost.

The Enemy's move is to make the escalation happen without it being named. Once someone names it — "I notice this has gone from a preference to a salvation question; I don't think it belongs at that level" — the mechanism stops. The naming is almost always sufficient, because most people engaged in an escalated dispute did not choose to escalate it. They were carried there by the current of the argument.

The Scriptural Frame Being Exploited

Jude 3 calls believers to "contend earnestly for the faith that was once for all delivered to the saints." This is a real command. There are things worth contending for, things that cannot be yielded without yielding Christ himself. The Enemy takes this genuine call and removes the one thing that makes it navigable: the recognition that not everything is at that level.

Paul makes this distinction explicitly in 1 Corinthians 15:3: "For I delivered to you as of first importance what I also received." The phrase "first importance" (Greek: ἐν πρώτοις, en prōtois) implies, by its existence, that other things are of secondary or lesser importance. Paul has a category for matters that are important but not of first importance. The Escalation Trap works by collapsing that category — by treating everything as if it belongs to the list in 1 Corinthians 15.

The classical Christian tradition developed vocabulary for this distinction that remains enormously useful: dogma (what must be believed for salvation), doctrine (important theological positions on which serious Christians disagree), and adiaphora (matters of indifference — neither commanded nor forbidden by Scripture, where Christians have genuine freedom). The Going Deeper section below develops this further. But the practical point is simply that the question "which level is this?" is always legitimate, always necessary, and almost never asked early enough in a church dispute.

Counting the Cost

Two examples — one ancient, one recent — illustrate what happens when escalation is left unchecked.

The Donatist Controversy (4th–5th century North Africa). In the years following the Diocletianic persecution, a group of North African Christians refused to accept the ministry of clergy who had surrendered their Scriptures to avoid martyrdom — the so-called traditores, the "handers-over" (from which we get the word traitor). This was a real and serious question: could the sacraments administered by a cowardly bishop be valid? It deserved careful thought.

What it got instead was escalation. The question moved from a pastoral concern about the integrity of the clergy to a full ecclesiological position: the church's holiness depended on the holiness of its ministers; a compromised minister invalidated the sacraments; any church that accepted compromised ministers was not the true church. By the time Augustine engaged the controversy, it had produced a separate schismatic church with its own bishops, its own martyrs (the Circumcellions, who committed acts of violence in the name of the pure church), and a century of institutional damage. North African Christianity never recovered its pre-persecution strength. It was already fractured when the Arab conquests arrived in the 7th century.

The original question was not trivial. But its escalation to a salvation-level ecclesiological position destroyed what it was trying to protect.

The 20th-century worship wars. Across nearly every Western Protestant denomination from the 1960s onward, the introduction of contemporary worship music produced conflict that in many cases split congregations and ended careers. What was, at its core, a preference dispute — different generations finding different musical forms more or less fitting for worship — was systematically escalated into a faithfulness question by voices on both sides. "If we let this in, we're abandoning reverence." "If we keep this out, we're abandoning the next generation." Neither of those claims is a biblical argument. Both were believed with the intensity of one.

The human cost was real: pastors forced out, families who left and never returned, communities that spent decades managing a wound that never fully healed — all over a question that could have been held at the level it actually occupied.

The Reframe

The single question that does the most work in an escalating dispute is this: What level of doctrine is actually at stake here?

This question is not a retreat from seriousness. It is an act of precision. It asks the people in the dispute to place their concern on an actual map — to say specifically whether they believe the matter in question is salvation-level, fellowship-level, or preference-level, and to give a reason drawn from Scripture for that placement. Most people, when asked to do this work explicitly, discover that they have been operating at a level higher than their actual conviction warrants.

The reframe does not resolve the underlying disagreement. It returns the disagreement to the level at which it can actually be discussed by reasonable people of good faith. Level 1 and Level 2 disagreements can coexist in a healthy community. Level 5 disagreements cannot — and if a Level 2 disagreement has been elevated to Level 5, it cannot come down again without someone doing the explicit naming work.

The Peacemaker's Move

What does the person who has seen through this tactic actually do? Here is specific language for specific situations.

In a conversation that is escalating: Interrupt gently and name the level directly. "Can I ask a question before we go further? I want to understand where you see this landing theologically. Is this a salvation issue for you — something that changes whether we're preaching the same gospel? Or is it more of a faithfulness issue — something really important, but where you can imagine serious Christians landing differently? I'm asking because I think the conversation needs to go differently depending on the answer."

In a meeting where the language is hardening: "I've noticed we've moved from 'I think this is a better approach' to 'I can't stay if this happens.' I don't think that movement was intentional. Can we back up and look at what level of doctrine we're actually dealing with? I want to take this seriously — and I think taking it seriously means being precise about how serious it actually is."

When someone accuses you of not taking it seriously enough: "I take it seriously enough to want to place it accurately. If this is a salvation issue, I want to know that — and I'll treat it accordingly. If it's a conviction I hold strongly but where I acknowledge that faithful people disagree, I want to know that too, because those two situations require different responses from me. Which do you believe it is?"

When you are the one who has escalated: "I realize I've been treating this like a hill I'm prepared to die on, and I'm not sure it deserves that. Let me try to say what I actually believe: I think [position X] is right, I care about it, and I'd argue for it. But I don't think [position Y] puts you outside the faith. I escalated this further than I should have, and I want to walk that back."

⚖️ Going Deeper: Dogma, Doctrine, and Adiaphora

The classical Christian tradition developed a three-level framework for theological disagreement that does enormous practical work once understood. Here is an accessible treatment of each level and why the distinctions matter.

Dogma — the innermost circle. These are the convictions that define the Christian faith itself; without them, there is no Christianity to speak of. The canon of ecumenical creeds (Nicene, Apostles', Athanasian) defines this territory with reasonable precision: the Trinity, the full divinity and full humanity of Christ, the bodily resurrection, salvation through Christ alone. These are not negotiable, not because the church says so, but because they are the load-bearing structure of the gospel. Remove them and the building collapses. The Escalation Trap almost never involves actual dogma — but it routinely treats second-level matters as if they were dogma.

Doctrine — the middle ring. These are significant theological positions on which serious, Scripture-honoring Christians have disagreed throughout church history and continue to disagree today. Predestination vs. free will. The mode and subjects of baptism. The nature of Christ's presence in the Lord's Supper. Church governance models. Eschatological timelines. These matters deserve careful, serious engagement. They have real pastoral consequences. But their disagreement does not place anyone outside the family of God — and the historical evidence is that the church has never achieved uniform agreement on them, nor has the absence of agreement prevented genuine, vital Christian community.

Adiaphora — the outer ring. This Greek word (literally "things that make no difference") refers to matters neither commanded nor forbidden by Scripture: worship style, service format, building design, program choices, organizational structures. Christians have enormous freedom here. The danger is not in having preferences about these things — it is in forgetting they are preferences, and treating them as if they belong to the first or second ring.

A practical test for placing an issue: Can you cite a specific biblical command or prohibition that directly addresses it? If yes, what ring does that command occupy — core gospel, secondary doctrine, or cultural application? If no direct command exists, the issue almost certainly belongs in the third ring, however strongly you feel about it. Strong feeling is not the same as doctrinal weight.

The Reformers developed the related concept of status confessionis — a "state of confession" in which a matter has become so urgent that division is justified. The Lutheran Confessions were originally produced in a status confessionis over justification. The Barmen Declaration was a status confessionis over Nazi infiltration of the German church. The category is real and the history validates it. But its invocation requires a level of theological gravity and historical self-awareness that most contemporary church disputes simply do not warrant — and the Enemy exploits the urgency language of status confessionis for disputes that are nowhere near that threshold.
