The Living Story

A Tour of Church History
The church is not a museum housing relics of a past faith. It is a living community with a living story — one that stretches from a borrowed upper room in Jerusalem to every continent on earth. Knowing that story will not save you. But it will almost certainly make you more grateful, more humble, and more astonished that the thing has survived at all.

The Apostolic Age

c. 30–100 AD

On Good Friday, the disciples scattered. Peter denied knowing Jesus three times before the rooster crowed. By Sunday, something had changed — and within weeks, those same frightened people were standing in the public square of the city where the crucifixion had happened, proclaiming the resurrection to the people who had been there. They did not soften the message to make it safer. They were not running a careful PR campaign. They were reporting what they said they had seen.

Within thirty years of the resurrection, communities bearing the name of Christ existed in Jerusalem, Antioch, Corinth, Ephesus, Rome, and across the Mediterranean world. The letters that became the New Testament were written into those specific communities, addressing their specific crises — which is why they feel so alive to read. They were not composed as abstract theology. They were pastoral correspondence to real people in real trouble.

By the end of the first century, most of the apostles were dead — the majority executed for refusing to stop talking. Peter, by tradition, crucified upside down in Rome because he felt unworthy to die the same way as his Lord. Paul beheaded. James stoned. The movement they gave their lives to was, by any reasonable human calculation, too small and too persecuted to survive. It had no buildings, no state patronage, no army, and a founder who had died on a Roman criminal's cross. What it had instead was an unshakeable conviction that the founder was not, in fact, still dead.

The Church Fathers and the Roman Empire

c. 100–500 AD

Imagine you are Polycarp, Bishop of Smyrna, standing before the Roman proconsul in roughly 155 AD. You are in your mid-eighties. The arena is full. The crowd is calling for your death. The proconsul offers you a way out: just curse the name of Christ. Polycarp's answer has survived the centuries: "Eighty-six years I have served him, and he has never done me wrong. How can I blaspheme my King who saved me?" He was burned alive. The fire, according to eyewitnesses, curled away from him in an arch before a soldier stepped in with a sword. Whether you take that detail literally or not, the man's composure in the face of it is a historical fact.

The church fathers were not pale, otherworldly academics. They were pastors, bishops, and thinkers who did their theology in the middle of persecution, heresy, and political pressure — and who, in the process, gave the church the vocabulary it still uses today. Justin Martyr brought Greek philosophy into conversation with the gospel and was executed for it around 165 AD. Tertullian, the brilliant and irascible North African lawyer, gave us the word Trinity itself. Origen — wildly creative, sometimes problematic, endlessly fascinating — wrote more than any other ancient Christian thinker and spent his later years in prison for his faith.

Then there is Athanasius — the man who would not bend. In the fourth century, the entire Eastern Roman Empire, with imperial backing, went Arian (see Module 11). The emperor pressured Athanasius to compromise. Bishops capitulated one by one. Athanasius was exiled five times. His critics taunted him: Athanasius contra mundum — Athanasius against the world. His reply, essentially: if the world is against the truth, then I am against the world. The Nicene Creed, which the church still confesses, exists in no small part because of that stubbornness.

And Augustine. Born 354 AD in North Africa to a praying mother (Monica) and a pagan father, brilliant and restless, Augustine tried everything — Manichaeism, Neoplatonism, a career in rhetoric, an affair that lasted years — before being ambushed by grace in a garden in Milan in 386 AD while reading a passage from Paul. His Confessions remains one of the most extraordinary books ever written: a prayer, an autobiography, and a piece of theology all at once, full of sentences that still land like a fist. "Our hearts are restless until they rest in Thee." He became the most influential theologian in Western Christian history, and his thought shaped both the Reformation and the Catholic Counter-Reformation — each side claiming him.

🕊️ When the Church Gained Power — and What It Did with It

There is a quiet tragedy buried in the church's fourth-century triumph. Before Constantine's conversion (c. 312 AD) and the Edict of Milan, Christians were the people who got thrown to the lions. After it, they were the people who ran the empire. The transition was dizzying — and not entirely healthy.

The story begins a generation before Augustine, in the crucible of the Diocletian persecution (303–311 AD). Under pressure, a significant number of clergy did the unthinkable: they handed over the Scriptures — and sometimes fellow believers — to the Roman authorities to save their own lives. The Latin word for this act of betrayal became their permanent nickname: traditores. Traitors. Our English word "traitor" comes directly from it. When the persecution ended, these clergy wanted to return to their positions. A group called the Novatianists — and later, more forcefully, the Donatists — refused to accept them back. Their argument was theological: clergy who had betrayed Christ during persecution were spiritually disqualified, and any sacraments they administered — including ordinations — were invalid. The rest of the church disagreed and extended mercy. Neither side budged. The schism was never resolved — it was simply tolerated — and it set the stage for what came next.

Augustine himself is the sharpest illustration of what power does to even the best theology. The man who wrote "our hearts are restless until they rest in Thee" — who understood grace more deeply than almost anyone before the Reformation — did not arrive at compulsion quickly or eagerly. He spent years arguing with the Donatists through letters and public debates, insisting that only spiritual means should be used to reunite the church. He initially opposed the state measures already being taken against them. But when argument failed and the schism hardened, something in Augustine shifted. Around 408 AD — documented in his Letter 93 to Vincentius — he crossed a line he had previously refused to cross: he endorsed the use of imperial force to compel the Donatists into compliance, and reached for a biblical text to justify it. Luke 14:23 — coge intrare, "compel them to come in" — describes a feast-master urging his servants to beg the poor of the city to attend his banquet. Augustine re-read it as a license for state coercion. He convinced himself that a coerced Christian was still a Christian, and that forcing people into the church was, in some sense, an act of love. Donatist churches were seized. Their bishops were banished. By 415 AD, Donatists were forbidden to meet for worship on pain of death. The group that had refused to compromise with Roman power during persecution was now being crushed by Roman power — wielded by the church.

He was wrong. And once that doctrine was minted, it could not be unminted. It did not stay in Augustine's hands or in North Africa. The fifth-century Christological councils — the very councils that produced the creeds the church still confesses — show the same pattern spreading. At the Council of Ephesus in 431 AD, Cyril of Alexandria opened the proceedings before the Syrian delegation supporting Nestorius had even arrived, secured Nestorius's condemnation, and had him deposed and exiled before the other side could present its case. Whether Cyril was right about the theology is beside the point: a man certain he was right had reached for the weapon Augustine had forged. The expelled Nestorians — the Church of the East — fled into Persia, then Arabia, then Central Asia, and eventually China, planting Christianity across the Silk Road in one of history's most remarkable missionary expansions. The compelled-out became missionaries. The compellers stayed home and argued. The Council of Chalcedon in 451 AD produced the Hypostatic Union — the definition of Christ as one person in two natures, fully divine and fully human — which remains orthodox Christology to this day. But the Egyptian and Syrian churches, the Copts and the Ethiopians and the Syriac Christians, read Chalcedon as a backdoor division of the Christ they had bled to defend. They were not heretics by intent. They were survivors of Roman persecution who now found themselves on the wrong side of an imperial formula. Constantinople responded with compulsion: deposing their bishops, installing imperial appointees, using state machinery to enforce theological uniformity. It didn't work. It never works. Those churches, exhausted by generations of imperial pressure and increasingly alienated from Constantinople, offered little resistance when the Arab armies arrived in the seventh century. Some quietly welcomed the change of administration. Augustine had imagined compulsion as a tool for unity. What it actually produced, across council after council, was fracture dressed up as victory.

The downstream damage is well documented. The same doctrine provided the theological framework for the Albigensian Crusade, the Spanish Inquisition, the forced conversions of Jews, and the persecution of the Waldensians and Anabaptists. Whole rivers of blood flow from that footnote in Augustine's correspondence. None of the men who wielded the doctrine were, for the most part, monsters. The tragedy is sharper than that: a bad doctrine, once legitimized, does not require a monster to operate it. It requires only a person who is certain they are right — certain enough to stop persuading and start compelling.

The lesson is not that Augustine was a fraud or that his theology is unreliable. It is something more unsettling: that even the most gifted theologians carry the blind spots of their moment, their culture, and their fear. The same mind that saw God so clearly could also rationalize what it wanted to do. We are all capable of exactly this. The church's history of persecution should make every generation of Christians more humble, more suspicious of their own certainties, and more committed to the principle that faith compelled is not faith at all.

How We Got the Bible: The Story of the Canon

c. 50–400 AD

One of the most common misconceptions about the Bible is that a single council — usually invoked as a shadowy conclave of power-hungry bishops — voted to decide which books were "in" and which were "out." This is not what happened, and understanding what actually happened is both more interesting and more reassuring.

The canon of Scripture was not so much decided as recognized. The books that make up the New Testament were already being read, copied, circulated, and used to settle doctrinal disputes before anyone formally listed them. The process by which the church identified which texts carried the weight of apostolic authority was organic, sometimes messy, and took several centuries — but it converged. Letters from churches in Rome, Alexandria, Antioch, and Carthage, written independently, list substantially the same books. The outliers — the handful of books whose status was debated (Hebrews, James, 2 Peter, Revelation) — were not suppressed alternative gospels. They were texts with genuine questions about apostolic authorship.

The councils that eventually produced formal canonical lists — Laodicea (363 AD), Hippo (393 AD), Carthage (397 AD) — were not inventing the canon. They were codifying what the church had already been practicing. The criteria they applied were: Was it written by an apostle or an apostolic associate? Was it consistent with the rule of faith the church had always held? Was it being received and used by the church broadly? The 27 books of the New Testament passed these tests. The Gnostic gospels — often invoked as the great suppressed alternatives — were not serious contenders. They appeared much later, often claimed false authorship, and taught doctrines the church had been rebutting for generations.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS: Why Did the Canon Close?

The canon "closed" not because some authority decided to stop listening to God, but because the apostolic generation — the direct eyewitnesses of Jesus — had died. Once that generation was gone, any new text claiming apostolic authority was by definition suspicious. The church's instinct was: we have what the eyewitnesses left us; we hold it carefully; we don't add to it. That instinct is consistent with the way Scripture speaks about itself: complete, sufficient, and authoritative (2 Timothy 3:16-17; Revelation 22:18-19). Closing the canon was an act of fidelity, not suppression.

The Medieval Church

c. 500–1500

The Middle Ages get a bad reputation — "the Dark Ages," the period of superstition between the glories of Rome and the light of the Renaissance. The reality is considerably more interesting, and considerably more complicated.

After the fall of Rome in 476 AD, the church became the primary bearer of civilization in the West. It was monks in Ireland and Britain who, huddled over their manuscripts, painstakingly copied and preserved the classical texts that would have otherwise been lost forever. Thomas Cahill's description in How the Irish Saved Civilization is not hyperbole: without those monks in their beehive huts on the Atlantic cliff-edges, the intellectual heritage of Greece and Rome would simply not exist today. The church was not the enemy of learning in this period. It was often its sole patron.

Bernard of Clairvaux (1090–1153) was the most influential churchman of his century — mystic, preacher, theologian, reluctant kingmaker — a man who could fill a city square with his preaching and then spend his days in contemplative prayer. He was deeply Christocentric in a way that was unusual for his era, and his devotional writings (including the hymn O Sacred Head Now Wounded and the poem behind Jesus, the Very Thought of Thee) sit comfortably on a Reformed bookshelf. Anselm of Canterbury (1033–1109) gave the Western church its most precise account of the atonement as satisfaction of divine justice — the conceptual foundation on which penal substitution is built.

And then Thomas Aquinas — vast, patient, brilliant, apparently so large that a semicircle had to be cut into his dining table. His Summa Theologiae is one of the most ambitious intellectual projects in history: a systematic integration of Aristotelian philosophy and Christian theology. The Reformers would push back hard against parts of it, particularly his account of grace and merit, but they were working in the categories he helped define.

The medieval period also gave us genuine spiritual decay — the corruption of the papacy, the abuse of indulgences, the entanglement of church and political power, the Crusades, and a growing gap between the Latin church and ordinary laypeople who could neither read nor understand the Mass. These were not peripheral problems. They set the stage for everything that followed.

🕊️ The Crusades, the Inquisition, and the Church That Forgot What It Was

It is not possible to tell the history of the medieval church honestly without sitting with two of its most lasting wounds.

The Crusades (1095–1291) began with a genuine religious impulse: the desire to reclaim the Holy Land from Muslim rule and protect Christian pilgrims. Pope Urban II's sermon at Clermont in 1095 promised spiritual merit to those who took the cross, and tens of thousands responded with what they understood as devotion. What followed was a series of campaigns marked by extraordinary courage and extraordinary atrocity in roughly equal measure. The sack of Jerusalem in 1099 — in which the crusaders massacred the city's Muslim and Jewish inhabitants — was recorded with horror even by some contemporaries. The Fourth Crusade (1204) notoriously ended with crusaders sacking Constantinople, the capital of Eastern Christendom, widening the schism between Eastern and Western Christianity in ways that have never fully healed. The legacy of the Crusades is still present in Muslim-Christian relations today, eight centuries later. The church preached them in Christ's name. That fact cannot be softened.

The Inquisition — in its various forms across several centuries — was the institutionalization of Augustine's logic of compulsion taken to its worst conclusion. Torture was used to extract confessions. Heretics were burned. The Spanish Inquisition (established 1478) operated with particular severity. Jews and Muslims who converted under duress and were then suspected of secretly practicing their original faith were its primary targets. Torquemada, its first Grand Inquisitor, supervised the execution of somewhere between two thousand and four thousand people. The numbers are disputed; the fact is not.

These are not ancient embarrassments to be explained away. They are the fruit of what happens when the church forgets that it has no coercive mandate — that Jesus said "my kingdom is not of this world," that Paul wrote with ink not iron, and that faith wielded by the sword is not faith at all but its counterfeit. Every generation of Christians needs to remember what the church became when it acquired the state's power to compel — and to ask, honestly, whether something of that same tendency lives in us.

⚖️ Pre-Reformers: Wycliffe and Hus

The Reformation did not arrive from nowhere. John Wycliffe (c. 1330–1384), an Oxford scholar, argued a century and a half before Luther that Scripture alone — not the pope or church councils — was the supreme authority for Christians. He produced the first complete English translation of the Bible. His followers (the Lollards) were persecuted, and his bones were posthumously exhumed and burned as a heretic. Jan Hus (1369–1415) took up Wycliffe's ideas in Bohemia, was promised safe conduct to a council to defend them, was burned at the stake anyway, and reportedly died singing. His last words were said to be: You are roasting a goose now, but in a hundred years you will hear a swan singing, whom you will not be able to silence. One hundred years later: Martin Luther. The Reformers knew they were not inventing something new — they were recovering something old.

The Renaissance and the Road to Reformation

c. 1300–1517

The Renaissance — the great rebirth of classical learning that swept through Italy from roughly the fourteenth century onward — seems at first glance like a story about painting and sculpture and Medici patronage, not a chapter in church history. But it prepared the ground for the Reformation in ways that cannot be overstated.

The humanists of the Renaissance did not set out to reform the church. What they did was recover the tools for doing so. The movement to return ad fontes — "to the sources," the original texts — produced scholars who could read the New Testament in Greek rather than through the centuries-thick lens of the Latin Vulgate. And when they read it in Greek, things looked different. Erasmus of Rotterdam (1466–1536), the greatest humanist scholar of his age, produced a critical edition of the Greek New Testament in 1516. He did not intend a revolution. He intended better scholarship. What he got was both.

Then there was the printing press. Johannes Gutenberg's invention (c. 1440) was the most consequential technological development in the history of Christianity since the codex replaced the scroll. Before the press, a Bible cost the equivalent of a farm. After it, pamphlets and texts could be produced in days and distributed across Europe in weeks. When Luther nailed his 95 Theses to the door at Wittenberg in October 1517, they were reprinted across Germany within a month. The Reformation was, among other things, the first great media event of the modern age. The church has never had an unambiguous relationship with new communication technology. The printing press was no exception.

The Reformation

1517–1600

On the 31st of October, 1517, a German Augustinian monk nailed a list of academic debate propositions to the door of a castle church in a small university town. He was not trying to start a revolution. He was trying to have an argument with his colleagues. He did not get the argument he intended. He got the Reformation instead.

Martin Luther (1483–1546) had been tormented by a question: How can I be righteous before a holy God? The church's official answer — go to confession, do penance, accumulate merit, purchase indulgences — had failed to still the accusation that roared in his conscience. Then he read Romans. Slowly. Again and again. And in Psalm 31 and Romans 1:17 he found something that reordered everything: the righteousness of God is not what God demands from us, as if to crush us. It is what God gives us in Christ, freely, as a gift received by faith alone. The just shall live by faith. Luther described the discovery as feeling as though the gates of paradise had been thrown open. The 95 Theses were the overflow of that discovery applied to a specific abuse.

The church told Luther to recant. He was summoned before the Emperor at the Diet of Worms in 1521 and given one last chance. His answer — almost certainly edited slightly in the telling but substantially accurate — became one of the most famous sentences in Western history: "Here I stand. I can do no other. God help me." He was declared an outlaw. A friend kidnapped him for his own safety and hid him in a castle, where Luther spent ten months translating the New Testament into German — a translation so vivid it helped shape the modern German language itself.

John Calvin (1509–1564) came to the Reformation slightly later, through a different path, and brought to it a systematic mind that Luther lacked and probably would not have wanted. His Institutes of the Christian Religion — begun when he was twenty-six and revised throughout his life — remain the most comprehensive and rigorous account of Reformed theology ever written. Geneva under Calvin was not always comfortable — Calvin himself would have been the first to admit that the city and its church were imperfect instruments — but it became a school for the Reformed faith that sent pastors across France, the Low Countries, Scotland, and eventually England.

And William Tyndale. This quiet Englishman's ambition was simple: to give the ploughboy the Bible in his own language. "I will cause the boy who drives the plough to know more of the Scripture than you do," he reportedly told a clergyman who mocked him. He produced the first printed English New Testament (1526) and much of the Old Testament, working in hiding while the English crown hunted him. He was eventually caught in Antwerp, strangled at the stake, and his body burned in 1536. His dying prayer was reportedly: "Lord, open the King of England's eyes." Within three years, Henry VIII had authorized an English Bible — built largely on Tyndale's translation — for every English parish church. His prayer was answered with remarkable speed. Much of what the King James Bible sounds like, it sounds like because of William Tyndale.

🕊️ The Reformers' Failures: Luther, Calvin, and the People They Hurt

The Reformation recovered the gospel. It did not produce perfect men. Three failures deserve honest naming.

Martin Luther and the Jews. The early Luther wrote with genuine compassion about Jewish people, hoping that the recovery of the true gospel would bring them to Christ. When they were not persuaded, something curdled. His 1543 treatise On the Jews and Their Lies is one of the most virulent antisemitic documents in the history of Christian writing. He called for the burning of synagogues, the confiscation of Jewish books, the forced labor of Jewish people, and their expulsion from Germany. The text was printed in Nazi Germany in 1934 as propaganda. Julius Streicher, at his Nuremberg trial, cited Luther in his own defense. Luther's recovery of justification by faith is the greatest theological recovery since the apostolic age. His treatise on the Jews is, without qualification, a moral catastrophe. Both things are true of the same man.

John Calvin and Michael Servetus. Servetus was a Spanish physician and theologian who denied the orthodox doctrine of the Trinity and had been a fugitive from Catholic authorities for years when he arrived in Geneva in 1553. Calvin had corresponded with him, found his theology dangerous, and when Servetus appeared in the city, had him arrested. Servetus was burned at the stake on October 27, 1553 — Calvin had requested beheading as a more merciful death and was refused, which is a strange kind of credit. Calvin defended the execution for the rest of his life. Sebastian Castellio, a former friend of Calvin's, wrote in response what became one of the founding documents of religious toleration: "To kill a man is not to defend a doctrine; it is to kill a man." Calvin was wrong. Castellio was right.

The persecution of the Anabaptists. The Anabaptists — radical Reformers who insisted on believer's baptism, the separation of church and state, and pacifism — were persecuted not only by Catholics but by Lutherans and Reformed. Drowning them was called "the third baptism." Zwingli approved of their execution. Thousands died. The irony is nearly unbearable: men who had themselves been hunted for their faith turned around and hunted people for theirs. The Mennonites and Baptists are their theological descendants, and their witness to the separation of church and state, which the Reformers rejected, has turned out to be one of the more durable contributions to Western political thought.

None of this negates what the Reformers recovered. The gospel they preached was true. The courage they showed was real. But the men who recovered sola gratia — grace alone — could not seem to apply it to those they feared. That gap between what we know and what we do is not their peculiar failure. It is the human condition. It is ours.

The Puritan Era — England, Scotland, and a New World

1558–1700

The Puritans have suffered at the hands of their reputation. The word "Puritan" today conjures joyless killjoys in black hats who banned dancing. The actual Puritans — as anyone who has read them discovers with some surprise — were passionate, warm, often funny, ferociously interested in theology, and deeply committed to the idea that the whole of ordinary life could be lived as an act of worship to God. Richard Baxter wrote more warmly about married love than most modern marriage manuals. Thomas Watson could be genuinely witty. John Owen wrote enough books to fill a small library — and you can still pick them up and find them alive.

The Puritan movement grew out of the English Reformation's unfinished business. Henry VIII had broken with Rome in 1534 — primarily for dynastic rather than theological reasons — and the Church of England that emerged was a compromise creature: Protestant in some ways, Catholic in its vestments and episcopal structure. The Puritans wanted to push the reform further, to purify the church of what they saw as remaining Catholic corruptions. Queen Elizabeth I largely resisted them. James I famously threatened to "harry them out of the land." He fulfilled the threat, accidentally, by failing to fulfill it: the pressure he maintained drove a significant portion of Puritan leadership toward the question of whether England could be reformed at all.

Scotland had its own Reformation, fiercer and more thoroughgoing than England's, under the towering figure of John Knox (1514–1572) — who had spent time as a French galley slave for his faith, studied under Calvin in Geneva, and came back to Scotland determined to build a genuinely Reformed church. He reportedly made Mary Queen of Scots weep with his preaching and showed no signs of regret about it. The Church of Scotland that Knox shaped — Presbyterian, doctrinally Reformed, marked by a seriousness about Scripture that bordered on severity — became the training ground for a remarkable generation of theologians and preachers.

The Westminster Assembly (1643–1648) gathered 121 English clergymen and 30 laypeople, along with Scottish commissioners, in the Jerusalem Chamber of Westminster Abbey. Its assignment was to produce a theological standard for the church. What it produced — the Westminster Confession of Faith, the Larger Catechism, and the Shorter Catechism — became the defining confessional documents of the English-speaking Reformed tradition. The Shorter Catechism's opening question — What is the chief end of man? — and its answer — To glorify God, and to enjoy him forever — is perhaps the most beautiful sentence the English theological tradition has ever produced.

John Owen (1616–1683) is the theological giant of the Puritan era — preacher, Vice-Chancellor of Oxford University, Cromwell's chaplain, and author of an astonishing body of work on the Holy Spirit, the atonement, indwelling sin, and the mortification of sin that the Reformed tradition has never surpassed. He wrote sentences like cathedrals — vast, load-bearing, requiring patience to navigate. John Bunyan (1628–1688), his near-contemporary, wrote The Pilgrim's Progress in a Bedford prison after refusing to stop preaching without a license. It became the bestselling book in the English language after the Bible — and remains one of the most honest and vivid accounts of the Christian life ever written.

🕊️ The Puritans' Blind Spots: Dissent, Toleration, and Ireland

The Puritans wrote beautifully about grace and practiced it unevenly toward those who disagreed with them.

Roger Williams (c. 1603–1683) arrived in Massachusetts as a committed Puritan minister and almost immediately began causing problems — specifically, by insisting that the civil government had no right to enforce religious observance and that the colonists had no valid claim to Indigenous land without fair purchase. The Massachusetts Bay authorities banished him in the middle of winter, 1636. He walked south through the snow to found Providence, Rhode Island — the first civil government in the Western world to guarantee religious liberty for all its citizens regardless of faith. He was right. The men who banished him, for all their genuine piety and brilliant theology, were wrong. Anne Hutchinson, whose crime was largely that she was a woman with a following who held Bible studies in her home and was a better theologian than some of her critics, was tried and banished in 1637. She was later killed in an Indigenous raid. The Massachusetts authorities, appallingly, said it was the judgment of God.

In Boston, between 1659 and 1661, four Quakers were executed by the Puritan authorities for returning after banishment. They included a woman, Mary Dyer. She had been offered her life if she would leave and not return. She returned anyway, because she believed God had called her to witness in Boston. She was hanged. John Owen, the man who wrote more richly about the Holy Spirit than anyone in his generation, was chaplain to the government that signed these death warrants.

And then there is Cromwell in Ireland. Oliver Cromwell (1599–1658) is one of the most complex figures in English history — genuinely devoted to Scripture, genuinely committed to his understanding of Providence, and responsible for the massacre at Drogheda in 1649, where his army killed somewhere between 2,500 and 3,500 people, including civilians, Catholic clergy, and soldiers who had surrendered. He justified it as the judgment of God on a Catholic rebellion. The Irish have not forgotten it. Catholics in Ireland today have not forgotten it. The name "Cromwell" still carries a particular weight in that country. He was not wrong about justification by faith. He was wrong about Drogheda.

The lesson is not that the Puritans were uniquely hypocritical. It is that every generation of Christians finds it easiest to apply its theology to those who already agree with it. The hard application is always to the person you fear, the group you have decided is dangerous, the dissenter who won't be quiet. The Puritans wrote the Westminster Standards, which still stand. They also hanged Mary Dyer, which should make us slow to congratulate ourselves for how far we have come.

Puritanism and the Making of America

1620–1800

The people who sailed on the Mayflower in 1620 were not running away from England. They were, in their own self-understanding, building something. John Winthrop's famous sermon aboard the Arabella in 1630 — "We shall be as a city upon a hill" — was not modest. He was describing a covenant community: a people who had made a solemn agreement with God to build a society shaped by his word, and who understood that their failure would be visible to the watching world. That sense of providential calling — of America as a place where something specific and important is happening — has shaped American national identity in ways that persist long after the explicit theology has faded.

The Puritans brought with them more than their theology. They brought their political theory. Calvinist and Reformed thought, with its deep suspicion of concentrated power, its emphasis on covenant (mutual agreement, accountability, consent), and its insistence that Scripture was the norm for all of life including government, fed directly into the democratic republican instincts that would eventually produce the American founding. The Mayflower Compact (1620) — a written agreement among the settlers to govern themselves by consent — is often called the first governing document in American history. It grew naturally from Puritan covenantal thinking. Many of the Founding Fathers, including those who were not orthodox Christians, were shaped by this intellectual and political inheritance even when they rejected its theological premises.

The relationship between Puritan New England and the broader American project is complex and should not be idealized. The same covenantal seriousness that produced representative government also produced the Salem witch trials. The same missionary impulse that drove Puritan pastors deep into wilderness communities to preach the gospel also coexisted, sometimes in the same person, with attitudes toward Indigenous peoples that were far from the gospel they proclaimed. The "city on a hill" vision was simultaneously genuinely noble and, in its application, sometimes provincial and cruel. The American church is still working through that inheritance.

The Great Awakening and the Evangelical Revival

1700–1800

By the early eighteenth century, the spiritual energy of the Puritan generation had largely dissipated. The churches remained. The forms remained. The fire was mostly out. Then, within a few years of each other on opposite sides of the Atlantic, it was lit again.

George Whitefield (1714–1770) is possibly the greatest preacher in the history of the English-speaking world — certainly the most widely heard, in an era before microphones. He preached to crowds of 20,000–30,000 in the open air, his voice reportedly able to carry a quarter-mile in calm weather. He crossed the Atlantic thirteen times in the era of sailing ships, preached in every colony, and was as well-known in Boston as in Bristol. Benjamin Franklin — skeptic, empiricist, not a man easily impressed by religious enthusiasm — calculated the distance he could stand from Whitefield in Philadelphia and still hear him clearly, and reported the result with unconcealed admiration. Whitefield made no secret of the gospel he preached: you must be born again. That conviction shaped American evangelical identity in ways that persist today.

Jonathan Edwards (1703–1758) gave the Great Awakening its theological mind. Pastor of a small congregation in Northampton, Massachusetts, he watched genuine revival break out in his own church in 1734-35 and began the careful work of distinguishing true spiritual transformation from its counterfeits — work that produced A Treatise Concerning Religious Affections, one of the most searching examinations of the inner life ever written. Edwards is often described as America's greatest theologian — a title he probably would have declined, not from false modesty, but from genuine indifference to such things. His mind ran to God. Everything else was a corollary.

In England, John Wesley (1703–1791) and his brother Charles (who gave the church over 6,000 hymns) launched the Methodist movement — a renewal within Anglicanism that eventually became its own denomination. Wesley rode 250,000 miles on horseback and preached 40,000 sermons. He and Whitefield, close friends, parted on the question of predestination — Whitefield Reformed, Wesley Arminian — but shared an absolute conviction that the gospel was for everyone and that the church had no right to remain comfortable while ordinary people lived and died without hearing it.

🕊️ Whitefield, Edwards, and the Question They Got Wrong

George Whitefield preached that every soul was equally precious before God. He also owned slaves and lobbied successfully for the legalization of slavery in the colony of Georgia, arguing that the colony's economy required it. He justified this to himself in a way that the best of his own theology should have prevented: he believed that the gospel he preached to enslaved people was more important than the freedom they did not have. He was wrong. The two could not be separated the way he imagined, and the American church's long, catastrophic entanglement with the defense of slavery was shaped in part by the precedent men like Whitefield set — that you could preach grace on Sunday and hold people in chains on Monday without the contradiction needing to be resolved.

Jonathan Edwards — arguably the most rigorous theological mind the American church has ever produced — also owned slaves. The man who wrote that the redeemed would be "enraptured with joys that are forever increasing" and who understood the character of God with unusual depth held human beings as property. His biographers have wrestled with this. There is no resolution that makes it comfortable. He was not ignorant that slavery existed; he chose it.

Wesley, to his credit, was a fierce abolitionist. His last letter, written three days before he died in 1791, was to William Wilberforce, urging him to continue the fight against what Wesley called "that execrable villainy." The Whitefield-Wesley divide on predestination is the one everyone knows. The Whitefield-Wesley divide on slavery is the one that mattered more to the millions who lived under it.

The point is not to rank the Reformers or abolish our admiration for them. It is to notice that these were men of profound spiritual insight who also had a catastrophic moral blind spot that their own theology, rigorously applied, should have corrected. That is a warning about ourselves. Every generation has its blind spots — areas where we are accommodating something that our theology, if we followed it faithfully, would not allow. The question is not whether we have blind spots. It is whether we will be honest enough to suspect that we do.

The Great Century of Missions

1792–1914 (and beyond)

William Carey (1761–1834) was a cobbler in Northamptonshire who had taught himself Greek, Hebrew, Dutch, and French while mending shoes. In 1792 he stood before a gathering of Baptist ministers and gave what may be the most consequential sermon in the history of Protestant missions: "Expect great things from God; attempt great things for God." A colleague later said that Carey grabbed his coat when he sat down and said, "If we don't do something tonight, nothing will ever be done." Something was done. In 1793, Carey sailed to India, never to return to England. He spent the next forty years translating Scripture into Bengali, Sanskrit, and twenty-seven other languages and dialects. He helped found the modern Protestant missionary movement almost single-handedly.

Hudson Taylor (1832–1905) went further — literally. Not content with the port cities where Western missionaries clustered, he pushed into the interior of China, adopted Chinese dress and customs (scandalizing respectable missionary colleagues), founded the China Inland Mission on faith principles (no guaranteed income, no debt, trust God to provide), and saw the gospel take root in regions no Western missionary had ever visited. His principle: "There is a living God. He has spoken in the Bible. He means what he says and will do all he has promised." He lost his first wife and two children in China and went back.

The missionary movement of the nineteenth century is one of the most remarkable chapters in Christian history — and one of the most complicated. It traveled alongside European colonialism, often shared its assumptions, and sometimes did active damage. The honest account holds both: genuine gospel proclamation that transformed communities, and cultural imperialism that imposed Western forms alongside the faith. The Christianity that exists today in sub-Saharan Africa, South Korea, and across the Global South is in large part the fruit of this era — a fruit the missionaries themselves could not have fully anticipated, which is now making the Western church look thin by comparison.

Amy Carmichael (1867–1951) went to India at twenty-eight and didn't come home for fifty-five years. She spent much of her time rescuing children from temple prostitution, founding the Dohnavur Fellowship, and writing devotional literature of striking beauty from a sickbed. Jim Elliot (1927–1956) was killed at twenty-eight by the Auca people he had gone to reach in Ecuador, leaving a journal whose last complete entry contains the sentence that has become his epitaph: "He is no fool who gives what he cannot keep to gain what he cannot lose." Elisabeth Elliot, his wife, went back to the people who had killed her husband and saw many of them become Christians.

The Modern Church

1900–present

The twentieth century was the worst and best of centuries for the church simultaneously — which is, on reflection, how most centuries have felt from the inside.

In the West, the Fundamentalist-Modernist controversy of the 1910s–1920s split American Protestantism down the middle. The question was stark: could the historic doctrines of Christianity — the virgin birth, the bodily resurrection, the authority of Scripture, the atonement — be maintained in a culture shaped by Darwinism, higher biblical criticism, and liberal progressivism? The fundamentalists said yes and must be. The modernists said the doctrines needed to be reinterpreted or abandoned. J. Gresham Machen (1881–1937), a Princeton scholar of the first rank, argued with devastating precision in Christianity and Liberalism (1923) that liberal theology was not a revised form of Christianity — it was a different religion. He founded Westminster Theological Seminary when Princeton capitulated and the Presbyterian Church eventually defrocked him. He was right about the diagnosis. The mainline denominations that embraced liberalism have spent the subsequent century in demographic collapse.

Dietrich Bonhoeffer (1906–1945) gave the century its most costly witness. A German Lutheran pastor and theologian, he opposed the Nazi regime from its first days, helped found the Confessing Church as an alternative to the German church's capitulation to Hitler, ran an illegal underground seminary, and eventually became involved in the conspiracy to assassinate Hitler. He was hanged in a concentration camp on April 9, 1945 — three weeks before Germany surrendered. His The Cost of Discipleship and Letters and Papers from Prison are essential documents of the century. A man who knew that following Christ cost everything, and who wrote about that cost from inside it.

🕊️ The Century the Church Failed: Germany, Segregation, and Abuse

Bonhoeffer is the century's hero precisely because he stands out so sharply against its failure. The German church's capitulation to National Socialism was not a fringe phenomenon. The Deutsche Christen — the "German Christians" movement — welcomed the Nazi ideology enthusiastically, hung swastika flags in their churches, banned Jewish converts from their congregations, and attempted to purge the Old Testament from their Bibles because of its Jewish origins. These were ordained ministers preaching from pulpits in churches that had confessed the Nicene Creed for centuries. Bonhoeffer did not fight against an external enemy. He fought against his own church.

In America, the church's relationship with racial segregation is its own long catastrophe. Southern churches provided the primary theological justification for slavery before the Civil War, then for Jim Crow after it. "Separate but equal" had its theological defenders. When Martin Luther King Jr. was imprisoned in Birmingham in 1963 and wrote his letter to the white moderate clergy who had urged him to slow down and wait, he was not writing to atheists. He was writing to pastors. His indictment — that the white church had been a "tail-light rather than a headlight" on justice — has not been answered by the church's subsequent history in a way that should make anyone comfortable.

Then there is the abuse crisis — which is not one event but a pattern that has cut across Catholic and Protestant churches alike: the sexual abuse of children and vulnerable people by clergy, enabled by institutions more interested in their own reputation than in the people they were supposed to protect. The details, church by church, denomination by denomination, are available and documented. They are not aberrations. They are the fruit of structures that placed trust in authority without accountability — structures that the church's own theology should have prevented, because Reformed ecclesiology insists that all authority is accountable and that no one, however gifted, is above correction.

These are not things that happened to the church from the outside. They happened from within, by people who read the same Bible and confessed the same creeds. The honest conclusion is not that Christianity is therefore false. It is the one the Bible itself insists on: the gospel is true, and its carriers are broken. The treasure is in clay jars (2 Corinthians 4:7) — and sometimes the jar cracks in ways that cost people their faith, their safety, or their lives. That is not a footnote. It is the condition of the whole enterprise, from Pentecost to the present. We trust the treasure, not the jar. And we hold the jar accountable.

The most important story of the twentieth-century church, however, is not the decline of the West. It is the explosive growth of Christianity in the Global South. In 1900, roughly 80% of the world's Christians lived in Europe and North America. By 2020, the majority lived in Africa, Asia, and Latin America — and the numbers were growing fastest there. The Pentecostal and charismatic movements, largely dismissed by the established churches when they emerged in the early twentieth century, became the fastest-growing expression of Christianity in history. Their theological emphases deserve serious engagement rather than condescension — and the fact of their growth is a striking challenge to any assumption that the future of the faith belongs to the Western church.

The late twentieth century also saw a remarkable Reformed renewal — the recovery, particularly in the English-speaking world, of the confessional Reformed and Puritan tradition through publishers like Banner of Truth, theologians like J.I. Packer, R.C. Sproul, John Piper, and later Tim Keller, and preaching that took both the doctrines of grace and the integration of faith and culture seriously. That renewal is the intellectual water in which this curriculum swims.
