The Holy Spirit

Who He Is and What He Does
The Holy Spirit is the most mentioned and least understood person of the Trinity in popular Christian life. He appears throughout this curriculum — in the Spine's treatment of how faith begins, in the sanctification material of Modules 7–8, in the assurance discussions — but he has never been the primary subject. This page tries to correct that, and to give an honest account of both what the Reformed tradition holds clearly and where genuine disagreement among serious Christians exists.

A Person, Not a Force

Westminster Confession of Faith II.3

"In the unity of the Godhead there be three persons, of one substance, power, and eternity: God the Father, God the Son, and God the Holy Ghost. The Father is of none, neither begotten, nor proceeding; the Son is eternally begotten of the Father; the Holy Ghost eternally proceeding from the Father and the Son."

The most common mistake about the Holy Spirit is treating him as an impersonal power or energy — a force that can be channeled, an atmosphere that descends, a feeling that arrives. The New Testament's portrait is entirely different. The Spirit is a person, fully divine, distinct from the Father and the Son in person while equal to them in substance, power, and glory (Nicene Creed: "the Lord and Giver of life... who together with the Father and Son is worshipped and glorified").

The marks of personhood are consistent in Scripture. The Spirit grieves — "do not grieve the Holy Spirit of God" (Ephesians 4:30). He can be lied to — when Ananias withheld from the early church's common fund, Peter said he had lied not to men but to the Holy Spirit (Acts 5:3). He speaks — "The Spirit said to Philip, 'Go over and join this chariot'" (Acts 8:29); "the Holy Spirit said, 'Set apart for me Barnabas and Saul'" (Acts 13:2). He intercedes — not as a passive conduit but with active agency (Romans 8:26–27). He decides — distributing gifts "as he wills" (1 Corinthians 12:11).

None of this is true of a force. Forces do not grieve. Forces do not decide. A force is not the kind of thing you can lie to. The Spirit is a person — the third person of the Trinity — and treating him as anything less distorts every subsequent question about what he does and how to relate to him.

📖 IN PLAIN TERMS

When people describe the Holy Spirit as "the presence" or "the anointing" or "a wave of peace," they are often describing real experiences of something. The question is whether they are describing an experience of the Spirit himself — a person — or a spiritual atmosphere that they are treating as impersonal. The difference matters because you relate to a person differently than you relate to an environment. You can grieve a person. You can obey or disobey a person. You cannot grieve an atmosphere. The New Testament's consistent language is relational, not atmospheric — the Spirit as the one who dwells in you (1 Corinthians 6:19), not as a power that descends on you when conditions are right.

The Spirit and New Birth

John 3:5–8

"Truly, truly, I say to you, unless one is born of water and the Spirit, he cannot enter the kingdom of God. That which is born of the flesh is flesh, and that which is born of the Spirit is spirit... The wind blows where it wishes, and you hear its sound, but you do not know where it comes from or where it goes. So it is with everyone who is born of the Spirit."

The curriculum's Spine establishes one of the foundational claims of Reformed theology: no one comes to Christ unless the Father draws them (John 6:44), and the very desire to seek is itself a gift. The agent of that prior work — the one who moves first, who creates the capacity for faith where there was none — is the Holy Spirit. New birth is not something a person does or cooperates in. It is something done to a person by the Spirit — which is why Jesus uses the wind as his image. You hear the sound; you cannot control the source.

This is what the Reformed tradition means by regeneration preceding faith: the Spirit creates new life, and the person who has been given new life then believes. Faith is not the cause of the new birth — it is its immediate fruit. This is why the Westminster Confession describes effectual calling as the Spirit working in a person "out of that state of sin and death in which they are by nature... enlightening their minds spiritually and savingly to understand the things of God" (WCF X.1). The Spirit is not assisting a search already underway. He is beginning one that could not have begun any other way.

The pastoral implication is significant for anyone on the edge of faith. If you have any genuine desire to seek God — any honest openness to the questions this curriculum raises — the Reformed tradition's consistent answer is that this desire is not a neutral human achievement. It is itself the Spirit's work. Something has already begun. The beginning of faith is not your movement toward God but God's movement toward you, of which your movement is the evidence.

The Spirit and Scripture

2 Timothy 3:16–17

"All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness, that the man of God may be complete, equipped for every good work."

The Spirit's relationship to Scripture has two distinct dimensions that the Reformed tradition has always held together: inspiration and illumination.

Inspiration is the Spirit's work in the original writing of Scripture — the process by which the biblical authors, writing as real human beings with distinct personalities, styles, and concerns, nevertheless produced what God intended. Peter describes it as being "carried along by the Holy Spirit" (2 Peter 1:21). The result is a text that is authoritative not because the church endorsed it but because the Spirit breathed it — it is, in Paul's phrase, God-breathed (theopneustos, 2 Timothy 3:16). This is a completed work. The canon is closed (see the Canon section of the church history page). The Spirit does not produce new Scripture.

Illumination is the Spirit's ongoing work in the reader of Scripture — the opening of the mind and heart to understand and receive what has already been written. Paul describes the natural person as unable to receive the things of the Spirit of God because they are "spiritually discerned" (1 Corinthians 2:14). The Spirit who inspired the text also opens the text to those who read it. This is why Bible study is not merely an intellectual exercise — it is an act that calls for the Spirit's help, and why the Reformers insisted that Scripture interprets itself under the Spirit's guidance rather than requiring an authoritative external interpreter.

The important boundary here is the one between illumination and new revelation. The Spirit illumines what Scripture says; he does not add to it. Any claim to direct personal revelation that stands alongside or above Scripture — rather than being tested by it — has departed from the Reformed understanding of both the Spirit's work and Scripture's sufficiency (WCF I.6, I.10). The Spirit and the Word are not in competition. They are not two independent authorities to be balanced. The Spirit speaks in and through the Word; the Word is the Spirit's instrument.

The Spirit and Assurance

Romans 8:15–16

"For you did not receive the spirit of slavery to fall back into fear, but you have received the Spirit of adoption as sons, by whom we cry, 'Abba! Father!' The Spirit himself bears witness with our spirit that we are children of God."

The assurance question is one of the most pastorally important in the Christian life, and it runs through this curriculum from the Spine to the responding page. Here the Spirit is directly implicated: assurance is not a conclusion we reach by self-examination alone. It involves the Spirit's own testimony.

The Westminster Confession holds a careful position. Assurance is real and attainable: it is "an infallible assurance of faith, founded upon the divine truth of the promises of salvation, the inward evidence of those graces unto which these promises are made, the testimony of the Spirit of adoption witnessing with our spirits that we are the children of God" (WCF XVIII.2). Three grounds, in order of weight: the objective promise, the inward evidence of grace, and the Spirit's witness. The promise comes first. The Spirit's witness is not a felt emotion that comes and goes — it is the Spirit's actual testimony, which the believer receives and recognizes, confirming what the promise already declares.

In practice this means two things. First, assurance is Christological before it is experiential: it rests on who Christ is and what he has done, not on the felt intensity of our spiritual experience. The strength of our grip on Christ is not what holds us — his grip on us is. Second, the Spirit's witness is real but not always strongly felt. Seasons of spiritual dryness are not seasons in which the Spirit has stopped witnessing. They are seasons in which we cannot feel what remains true. The objective reality of the Spirit's presence and the felt quality of that presence are distinct, and confusing them produces either false assurance (I feel good therefore I am saved) or unnecessary despair (I feel nothing therefore I am not).

⚖️ What Does the Spirit's Witness Feel Like?

This is a genuinely difficult pastoral question, and the Reformed tradition has not always answered it clearly. The witness of the Spirit is not a specific sensation that can be described in advance and then identified. It is more often described as a quiet, settled recognition — the difference between a slave's fear and a child's trust, in Paul's own imagery. Jonathan Edwards's analysis in Religious Affections is the most rigorous attempt to describe genuine spiritual experience versus its counterfeits: he argues that the primary marks of the Spirit's work are not emotional intensity but a transformed disposition toward God and neighbor — love that is genuinely other-oriented, humility that is not performed, a hunger for God that grows rather than satisfies itself.

The practical test Edwards offers: does this experience increase your love for God and your love for people, or does it increase your confidence in yourself and your distance from others? The Spirit's work points outward; its counterfeits tend to circle back toward self.

The Spirit and Growth

Galatians 5:22–25

"But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, self-control... If we live by the Spirit, let us also keep in step with the Spirit."

The fruit of the Spirit is exactly that — fruit, not produce. You do not manufacture it by willpower. You abide in the vine, and fruit emerges from that connection. This distinction — between fruit as the Spirit's work and moralistic self-improvement as the natural human alternative — is one of the most practically important in the curriculum (see Module 7).

What the Spirit actually does in sanctification is worth naming precisely. He convicts of sin — not to condemn but to create the space for repentance and renewed trust. He applies what Christ has accomplished — making real in the believer's experience what is already objectively true of their standing. He works the "willing and doing" that Paul says is God's operation within us (Philippians 2:13). He is the agent of transformation, conforming the believer increasingly to the image of Christ (2 Corinthians 3:18). And crucially, he does this through means — through the Word read and preached, through prayer, through the gathered community, through the sacraments. The Spirit is not a bypass around these ordinary channels. He works in and through them.

This last point matters for a pastoral reason that deserves honest acknowledgment rather than a dismissal. Many Christians in charismatic and Pentecostal traditions have a deep, genuine, and Scripture-rooted hunger for the Spirit's present and active work — a hunger that has driven the fastest-growing expression of Christianity in the world for over a century. That hunger is not a mistake. What the Reformed tradition wants to hold alongside it — rather than against it — is that God has promised to meet his people specifically through certain ordinary channels: the Word read and preached, prayer, the gathered community, the sacraments. These are not restrictions on the Spirit's freedom. They are his chosen instruments. The concern is not about earnestness or intensity of seeking; it is about whether the architecture of a spiritual life is built primarily on the pursuit of felt experiences, or on the faithful use of the means where the Spirit has specifically promised to work. Those two can exist in the same person and often do. The Reformed tradition's instinct is simply: show up to the ordinary places God has promised to meet you, and let the extraordinary remain his to grant in his timing.

The Spirit and Gifts — A Contested Territory

1 Corinthians 12:7–11

"To each is given the manifestation of the Spirit for the common good... All these are empowered by one and the same Spirit, who apportions to each one individually as he wills."

On the question of spiritual gifts — and specifically on whether the miraculous gifts (tongues, prophecy, healing, words of knowledge) continue in the church today or ceased with the apostolic age — this curriculum is honest about where it stands: this is a genuine disagreement among serious, careful, Bible-believing Christians, and the tradition this curriculum stands in has not resolved it with a single voice. The question deserves engagement without condescension in either direction.

The cessationist position holds that the miraculous gifts were given specifically to authenticate the apostolic message before the canon of Scripture was complete, and that they ceased — or largely ceased — when that authenticating function was no longer needed. The primary Reformed confessions lean in this direction, and figures like B.B. Warfield, Richard Gaffin, and more recently Thomas Schreiner have argued it with rigor. What cessationists want to protect: the sufficiency and finality of Scripture as the complete, closed revelation of God — the danger being that ongoing prophecy or revelation implies the canon is still open.

The continuationist position holds that there is no exegetical basis for drawing a line at the close of the apostolic age, that the New Testament describes spiritual gifts as gifts to the church rather than to a transitional era, and that the widespread experience of these gifts across global Christianity in the twentieth and twenty-first centuries is not easily dismissed. Figures like Wayne Grudem, Gordon Fee, and the broader charismatic and Pentecostal traditions hold this view. What continuationists want to protect: the ongoing and active presence of the Spirit in the life of the church — the danger of cessationism being a functional deism in which the Spirit's work is confined to the text rather than present and active.

Both sides are protecting something genuinely biblical. The cessationist is right that the Spirit's work must be normed by Scripture and never placed above it — that any claimed prophecy or revelation must be tested rather than simply received. The continuationist is right that the Spirit is alive and active, and that the church has no warrant to act as though his extraordinary work ceased at a convenient historical boundary. This curriculum's own posture is to hold the cessationist's core concern firmly (Scripture as the norming authority over all claimed experience) while remaining genuinely open to the Spirit's work in ways that may exceed tidy categories — skeptical of specific claims that cannot be tested, but not skeptical of the living God whose Spirit blows where he wills. If you read that as a Reformed Christian's version of "the question is real and I hold it carefully," that is an accurate reading. Wherever you land, let both concerns govern your practice: hold Scripture as the supreme standard, and do not treat the Spirit as a historical relic.

⚖️ Navigating Differences in Your Group

If your small group contains both cessationists and continuationists — which is increasingly common in mixed evangelical congregations — the gifts question can become a source of unnecessary fracture. Module 7 already bracketed it briefly; this page gives you more to work with.

The most useful pastoral frame is to distinguish between the fruits of the Spirit (which both traditions affirm as present and normative) and the gifts of the Spirit (where the disagreement lives). Galatians 5's list — love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, self-control — is not contested. Both traditions agree these are the marks of genuine Spirit-filled life. The gifts question, while real and worth honest engagement, is a secondary matter compared to the primary question of whether a community is being formed in the fruit. A group that is genuinely growing in patience and love and genuine other-centeredness is demonstrating the Spirit's work regardless of where it lands on the gifts debate.

📚 For Further Study — The Holy Spirit
  • Sinclair Ferguson, The Holy Spirit (IVP, 1996) — The best single-volume Reformed treatment. Comprehensive, careful, warmly written. Start here.
  • John Owen, The Holy Spirit (Banner of Truth, abridged ed.) — The classic Puritan treatment. Dense but inexhaustible. The abridged edition edited by R.J.K. Law is a good entry point.
  • Michael Reeves, The Good God: Enjoying Father, Son, and Spirit (Paternoster, 2012) — Places the Spirit within the Trinitarian life of God; particularly good on why the Spirit's role in the Trinity shapes everything else.
  • Wayne Grudem, The Gift of Prophecy in the New Testament and Today (Crossway, 2000) — The most careful continuationist case. Readable and well-argued.
  • Thomas Schreiner, Spiritual Gifts: What They Are and Why They Matter (B&H, 2018) — A cessationist counterpoint; accessible and irenic in tone.

The Spirit and Prayer

The curriculum's Prayer page develops this in full, but it belongs here as well: the Spirit is not merely the subject of prayer but its enabler. Romans 8:26–27 describes the Spirit interceding for believers when they do not know how to pray. Ephesians 6:18 calls believers to pray "in the Spirit on all occasions." Jude 20 speaks of "praying in the Holy Spirit" as a dimension of building oneself up in faith.

The implication is that prayer is not fundamentally a human achievement — a discipline we practice well enough to break through to God. It is a Trinitarian event in which the Spirit is both the one who enables us to address the Father and the one who carries our inadequate words into a form the Father receives. This is the pastoral ground for why prayer is possible at all in seasons of darkness, confusion, or wordlessness: the Spirit does not wait for us to produce well-formed petitions. He is present in the gap between what we have to bring and what the Father receives, bridging it in ways that exceed what we feel.
