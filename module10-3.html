The Wounded Proxy War

"Fighting About Theology When You're Really Fighting About Pain"
← Tactic 2  |  Overview  |  Tactic 4 →
📍 A NOTE BEFORE YOU BEGIN

If you are reading this because you have been hurt in a church context — by leadership, by community, by the institution itself — this entry was written with you specifically in mind. You will likely recognize some of what follows. That recognition is not evidence that your theological concerns are wrong or that your position is merely psychological. It is evidence that the Enemy knows exactly how to make someone's real pain do double damage: first when the wound happens, and again when the wound is used to keep you from the healing that requires community to complete.

Read at your own pace. The 🕊️ Wounded Pilgrim page is there when you need it.

The Presenting Dispute

The theological argument is genuine. The person making it has thought about it, can cite Scripture for it, and holds it with real conviction. They are not being dishonest, and they are not wrong to raise the question. Church discipline, or elder authority, or the role of women in leadership, or institutional accountability — these are real theological issues that deserve careful engagement. The argument they are making deserves to be taken seriously on its merits.

But something about the conversation is off. The emotional intensity is higher than the theological stakes seem to warrant. Every attempt to distinguish the question produces a new escalation. The conversation cannot seem to reach any resting point — every response, however careful, somehow confirms the other person's worst fears about the situation. The person arguing does not seem to be trying to persuade; they seem to be trying to be heard. And no amount of theological engagement seems to be doing it.

This is often the presenting surface of a Wounded Proxy War. The argument is real. And it is also carrying something the argument alone was never built to carry.

The Enemy's Actual Move

Wounded people often cannot say directly what happened to them. Not because they are dishonest, but because the wound is either too raw, too complicated, or too entangled with their sense of identity and faith to be addressed head-on. What they can do — and what the church's genuine seriousness about theology makes possible — is address the wound through the theology. Church governance is a real issue. Pastoral accountability is a real issue. The church's handling of abuse, or discipline, or authority is a real issue. The wounded person's position on these issues is shaped by their wound — but it is not therefore wrong. Their intensity is disproportionate to the theological question in isolation, but entirely proportionate to the combination of the theological question and the unprocessed pain underneath it.

The Enemy's move is to keep both dimensions from being addressed simultaneously. If the theological argument proceeds without the wound being acknowledged, the person never receives what they actually need — to be seen, heard, and believed about what happened to them. They win or lose the argument, but the wound remains, looking for its next address. If someone names the wound — "this seems personal for you" — it can land as dismissal: the implication that their theological concern is nothing more than psychology, that they are too damaged to think clearly, that their position can be safely ignored because they are hurting. The Enemy wins either way, unless someone is skilled and trusted enough to hold both things at once: your theological concern is valid, and I also want to know what happened to you.

The particular cruelty is the timing. Post-scandal congregations — communities that have experienced pastoral moral failure, or institutional cover-up, or the quiet abuse of authority — often carry invisible fracture lines for years afterward. The original wound gets processed incompletely or not at all, because there is no safe container for it. New doctrinal disputes emerge with unusual frequency, and nobody connects them to the original damage. The pattern repeats. The Enemy, as the directive notes, is patient.

The Scriptural Frame Being Exploited

The genuine call to speak truth and defend the oppressed — both real obligations — is exploited here by making the theological argument a substitute for healing rather than a companion to it. The wounded person is genuinely right that the church should be accountable, that leadership should not be unquestioned, that Scripture has things to say about the abuse of authority. Psalm 55:12–14 is David naming exactly this wound: "It is not an enemy who taunts me — then I could bear it. It is you, my equal, my companion, my familiar friend." Betrayal by those who should have been safe is one of the specific forms of human suffering Scripture takes most seriously.

But Scripture also distinguishes between the cry of the wound and the work of the argument. Romans 12:15 — "weep with those who weep" — is not a prelude to theological debate. It is its own complete action. The wound needs that first. The argument can follow. What the Enemy prevents is the sequencing: he keeps the argument running hot enough that the weeping never happens, the person never feels heard, and the theology never gets addressed on its actual merits.

Counting the Cost

Post-Reformation authority debates in wounded communities. Many of the most bitter post-Reformation disputes about church authority — who has it, what it means, whether it can be questioned — were conducted by communities still raw from genuine clerical abuse, inquisitorial violence, and real betrayal by church leadership. The theological heat in these arguments was not fully explained by the theology alone. Communities that had watched bishops protect abusers, or pastors demand obedience to manifestly unjust commands, or church courts function as instruments of social control — these communities brought the temperature of their wounds into their theological arguments about authority. The arguments deserved to be had. They would have gone better if the wounds had been named and addressed alongside the theology.

Post-scandal congregations. Research on congregations that have experienced significant pastoral moral failure — especially cases involving sexual abuse, financial fraud, or the misuse of spiritual authority — consistently finds the same pattern: the immediate crisis produces a period of explicit processing, but the deeper wound distributes into the community and resurfaces in subsequent years as apparently unrelated theological disputes. A congregation that spent two years arguing about elder governance in 2019 may have been arguing, without knowing it, about a pastor's fall from grace in 2012. The connection was never made. The wound was never fully addressed. The Enemy was patient. He did not need to invent a new conflict. He only needed to keep the old one alive in a new container.

The Reframe

The shift that opens a different path is this: separate the validity of a theological position from the intensity with which it is held. These are two different questions. A position can be valid — biblically grounded, worth defending — and simultaneously be held with an intensity that reflects unprocessed pain more than theological conviction. Recognizing this does not invalidate the position. It asks for the wound to be attended to alongside the argument.

The peacemaker's reframe is not "your theology is really just psychology." It is: "I take what you're saying seriously as a theological matter. I also want to understand the story behind why it matters so much to you, if you're willing to share it. Both things can be true at once — the argument can have real merit, and there can also be something personal that deserves to be honored."

The Peacemaker's Move

This is the entry where the Peacemaker's Move is most delicate, because it requires creating safety before it requires anything else. The wounded person has usually already experienced the theological conversation as unsafe — their concerns dismissed, their intensity pathologized, their position used against them. The peacemaker who leads with the tactical observation ("I think this is a proxy war") will almost certainly confirm those fears. The sequence matters.

Step one — validate the substance: "I want to say first that what you're raising is a real question. [Elder authority / church discipline / pastoral accountability — whatever the specific issue is] — that's something Scripture takes seriously, and you're right to raise it. I'm not going to explain it away." Do not skip this. Without it, everything that follows will be heard as dismissal.

Step two — slow down and ask about the story: After the substance is honored, and only then: "Can I ask something, not to change the subject, but because I want to understand? It feels like this matters to you in a way that goes beyond the policy question. Is there something that happened — something that brought you to this point — that would help me understand why it's so important?" You are not required to have this conversation publicly. This is a one-on-one conversation, or a very small trusted group.

When they share the wound, receive it first: Do not immediately move to problem-solving, theological reframing, or "here's what should have happened." Romans 12:15 applies here: weep with those who weep. "That sounds genuinely painful. I'm sorry that happened. You deserved better from that community." Let that be enough for the moment. The theological conversation will still be there when the wound has been acknowledged.

When you are the leader being confronted: "I hear a real concern about [specific issue]. I want to engage it honestly. I also want to ask — and I ask this with respect, not to dismiss what you're raising — is there something specific that happened to you or someone you care about that is behind this? Because if there is, I want to hear that too, not just the policy argument. Both matter to me." A leader who can say this — and mean it — will often find that the theological conversation becomes navigable once the wound is no longer carrying the argument alone.

If you are the wounded person reading this: You do not owe anyone your wound on their timeline. You are allowed to hold both things — your theological conviction and your pain — without separating them on demand. What this entry is asking is simply whether the argument is getting you what you actually need. If the answer is no — if winning the argument would not actually make you feel heard, would not actually address what happened to you — then the argument may not be the right container for what you are carrying. The 🕊️ Wounded Pilgrim page is for exactly this.

⚖️ Going Deeper: Trauma-Informed Pastoral Care — What Lay Peacemakers Need to Know

You do not need a therapy degree to be a helpful presence for someone carrying a church wound. But there are a few things worth understanding — not as clinical categories, but as practical knowledge for the person trying to hold a space between the wound and the argument.

Trauma shapes perception before cognition. A person who has experienced harm in a religious context will often have a neurological response to triggers — words, tones, postures, power dynamics — that precedes their conscious thought. When someone seems to be reacting to a benign situation with what looks like disproportionate fear or anger, they are often responding to something the current situation reminded them of, not primarily to the current situation itself. This is not a character deficiency. It is the body's attempt to protect itself based on what it learned from the past. A peacemaker who understands this will not read the disproportionate reaction as evidence of bad faith; they will slow down and try to understand what the current situation is echoing.

The wound needs to be believed, not solved. The most common well-intentioned mistake when someone discloses church harm is moving too quickly to solution, explanation, or mitigation: "Have you talked to the pastor?" "I'm sure they didn't mean it that way." "There may be more to the story." These responses, however gentle, communicate that the listener's primary goal is to resolve the discomfort the disclosure created, not to hold the person's experience. The wounded person needs, first, to be believed. "That sounds genuinely harmful. I believe you." This is not a legal verdict. It is pastoral acknowledgment that the person's experience was real and mattered.

Institutional loyalty is not a pastoral virtue. A lay peacemaker who is deeply connected to the institution where the harm occurred — who has friendships with the leadership involved, whose own sense of community depends on the institution — will feel a pull toward protecting the institution while appearing to support the wounded person. This pull is understandable and common, and it is the reason why wounded people often do not feel heard by insiders: the insider's loyalty, however unconscious, shapes what they are willing to hear. Naming this to yourself before entering the conversation is an act of integrity: whose interests am I actually serving here?

Know when to refer. Some wounds are not navigable through pastoral conversation alone. If a person is describing harm that rises to the level of abuse — spiritual, physical, sexual, or financial — the appropriate response includes, but is not limited to, connecting them with professional support: a licensed therapist, a survivor advocacy organization, or (where applicable) appropriate legal counsel. Pastoral care and professional support are not alternatives. For significant harm, both are needed, and suggesting professional help is not a rejection. It is an acknowledgment that what the person is carrying deserves more resources than any one community can provide.
