Thinking Carefully About Christian Faith

Seeing and Believing

A resource for those beginning the movement toward faith, those who have already begun and want to understand what they are moving toward, and those who want to think more carefully about questions the church has not always handled well. It draws on historical, psychological, literary, moral, and personal evidence — because the case for Christian faith is not thin enough to rest on just one.

"If anyone's will is to do God's will, he will know whether the teaching is from God."
— John 7:17

What This Site Is

Most introductions to Christian faith start with an argument: weigh the reasons, evaluate the facts, and then decide. This site does not avoid honest argument — but it rests on a deeper observation drawn from John 7:17: understanding follows the intent to obey. Sight follows the first step toward the light.

This is not anti-intellectual. It is a claim about how spiritual perception works — one that every serious Christian across the centuries has reported from experience: faith is not merely a conclusion you reach but a direction you begin to move in. A Belief with Intent, prompted and enabled by the Holy Spirit.

The organizing question is not merely Is Christianity true? but Is Christianity the kind of thing worth orienting your life around — and what happens when you begin to?

This site is not primarily in the business of providing theological answers. Its purpose is to ignite the desire to seek them — and to point, as directly as it can, toward the source from which real answers come: the living Christ, the Word he has given, the Holy Spirit who illumines it, and the Body he has built to carry it. The teachers, pastors, and preachers cited throughout these pages are not here as authorities over Scripture — they are gifts Christ has given to his church for its equipping (Ephesians 4:11–13), and their collective wisdom is part of how he still teaches. The fountain is not here. This is a set of arrows pointing toward it.

At its core is a twelve-module curriculum designed to be read by individuals or used in small groups — a guided journey through the need, the evidence, the hard questions, and the lived practice of Christian faith. It is built especially for those navigating faith outside of a church community: people who are searching, returning, or rebuilding their relationship with God without a gathered context to do it in. It also works well in small group settings, and it hosts standalone resources that go deeper on topics that deserve more careful exploration than a single module can provide: the gospel itself, theological questions where thoughtful Christians disagree, and practical guidance for those beginning or returning to the life of faith.

A Note on Theological Foundations

This curriculum is written from within the Protestant Reformation tradition, and some choices of scope and emphasis reflect that. In the sixteenth century, the Reformers identified five convictions — the Solas — as the load-bearing structure of the gospel recovered from Scripture. They remain the foundation of this curriculum's theology:

  • Sola Scriptura — Scripture alone is the supreme and final authority in all matters of faith and practice. The Bible, as the inspired and inerrant Word of God in its original giving, stands over every tradition, institution, and teacher — including this one.
  • Sola Gratia — Salvation is by grace alone. It is not earned, merited, or cooperated into. Every movement toward God is itself a gift from God.
  • Sola Fide — Justification is received through faith alone — not faith plus works, not faith plus sacrament, but trust in the person and work of Christ alone as the ground of our standing before God.
  • Solus Christus — Christ alone is mediator, redeemer, and Lord. There is no other name under heaven by which we must be saved (Acts 4:12).
  • Soli Deo Gloria — To God alone be the glory. Every dimension of salvation — its origin, execution, and completion — reflects the glory of the God who planned it, accomplished it, and applies it.

This foundation does not mean the curriculum is dismissive of other traditions. Where Catholic, Orthodox, and other streams of Christianity have preserved genuine biblical insight — on the reality of union with Christ, the importance of embodied worship, the place of honest lament, the communal character of faith — those insights are drawn on with gratitude. At the same time, choices do need to be made. Where traditions have added requirements beyond what Scripture plainly teaches, or located authority in structures Scripture does not sanction, this curriculum follows the Reformation principle: semper reformanda — always reforming, always returning to the Word. That principle applies to this curriculum as well. It aspires to nothing more than what Scripture teaches, and invites every reader to test what is written here against that same standard.

Where readers from other traditions encounter framings that differ from their own, the intent is honest engagement and not disrespect. The Reformers themselves — Luther, Calvin, Tyndale, Cranmer — believed that the church they were challenging had real and sincere believers within it, even as they contended for what they understood Scripture to require. This curriculum holds the same posture toward believers of every tradition: genuine common cause in the gospel of Jesus Christ, honest disagreement where Scripture and tradition diverge.

⚖️ How This Curriculum Reviews Its Own Content — A Transparency Note

Every page in this curriculum is evaluated against a structured set of theological standards before publication. This is not a claim of infallibility — it is an attempt to do what the Reading the Bible page describes: rely on the abundance of counselors, not on one person's unaided reading. The standards used are:

The Westminster Standards — the Westminster Confession of Faith, the Larger Catechism, and the Shorter Catechism, produced by the Westminster Assembly (1643–1648). These represent the most thorough systematic expression of Reformed Protestant theology in the English language, and are used here as the primary confessional benchmark.

The Four Ecumenical Creeds — the Apostles', Nicene, Chalcedonian, and Athanasian Creeds. These represent the shared inheritance of the entire Christian tradition on the cardinal doctrines of the Trinity, the two natures of Christ, and the resurrection. Content is checked for consistency with these before anything else.

The Five Solas — listed above. These are the load-bearing convictions of the Reformation and the organizing framework of this curriculum's soteriology.

The TULIP framework — total depravity, unconditional election, limited (definite) atonement, irresistible (effectual) grace, and perseverance of the saints, as developed in the Canons of Dort (1618–1619). Used particularly to check soteriological language for implied Pelagianism, Semi-Pelagianism, or Arminian framing where the curriculum intends to represent the Reformed position.

The broader Reformed and Puritan tradition — represented by figures including Sinclair Ferguson, Timothy Keller, John Owen, J.I. Packer, R.C. Sproul, and Herman Bavinck. This tradition is used as a check on practical and pastoral application — does this reflect how the church has consistently taught this truth? Where these figures disagree with one another, the disagreement is named rather than resolved by pretending it does not exist.

A severity framework is applied to any issue found: creedal or confessional violations (which require correction before publication), significant doctrinal errors (which require correction), imprecise language that implies something the author did not intend (which requires clarification), and ecumenical drift where the main body presents a cross-traditional position as the default Reformed one (which requires toggle-panel relocation or explicit labeling).

This process is not the same as peer review by qualified theologians — and it is no substitute for the review by mature readers that this curriculum repeatedly asks for before broader sharing. It is one layer of protection among several, offered in the spirit of semper reformanda: always willing to be corrected, always returning to the Word. If you find an error, the About page explains how to raise it.

A Note on Tone — and a Word Before You Begin

This curriculum assumes good faith — that everyone engaging with it is genuinely seeking, whether from inside faith or outside it. Disagreement is welcome. Certainty is not required. The goal is honest engagement with the material, not performance of belief.

It is written for real people in real conditions — including people who have been disappointed by the church, people in seasons of spiritual dryness, and people who have prayed many times and wonder whether it has counted. That material is not hidden. It is present throughout every module, marked with the 🕊️ Wounded Pilgrim marker. There is also a dedicated page for those who need it most — not a module, but a place set apart.

Whoever you are bringing to this material — a friend, a spouse, a skeptic you love, yourself — there is one discipline worth naming before the first page: apply what you find here to yourself before anyone else. Not because other people don't need it, but because the gospel has never worked as a curriculum for other people's improvement. It works as an encounter — personal, inconvenient, and aimed directly at whoever is holding it.

Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn, who knew something about evil, wrote that the line dividing good and evil does not run between people but through the heart of every person. Scripture says the same thing more precisely: "The heart is deceitful above all things — who can know it?" (Jeremiah 17:9). Paul spends all of Romans 1 building an indictment of pagan sin that his Roman readers could nod along to with satisfaction — and then opens Romans 2 with the trap: "Therefore you have no excuse, O man, every one of you who judges." The accused was the reader all along. That structural move is not an accident. It is the shape of every honest engagement with Scripture.

The line runs through here. This is not discouraging news — it is the beginning of the only conversation that actually changes anything.
