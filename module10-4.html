The Uniformity Substitution

"Confusing Agreement with Unity"
← Tactic 3  |  Overview  |  Tactic 5 →

The Presenting Dispute

Someone in the congregation does something differently. They arrive at a different conclusion on a secondary question, or they express their faith in a different style, or they raise a concern about a practice the community takes for granted. Others feel unsettled by this — not because the difference is heretical but because it is different, and difference feels like disruption. Over time, the community begins to apply subtle pressure toward conformity: the different person finds themselves excluded from certain conversations, or gently questioned about their commitment, or given feedback that they "don't quite fit" the culture of the group. If they persist in being different, the pressure increases. If they conform, the community relaxes — and calls it unity.

What has actually happened is the Uniformity Substitution: the replacement of the body's natural and necessary diversity with a demand for sameness that Scripture never makes.

The Enemy's Actual Move

Paul's body metaphor in 1 Corinthians 12 is one of the most radical descriptions of community in the New Testament. He does not merely permit diversity of function; he insists on it as necessary to the body's health. "If the whole body were an eye, where would be the sense of hearing?" (12:17). The foot cannot say to the hand "I have no need of you." The parts that seem weaker are indispensable. The parts that seem less honorable are given greater honor. The body's health depends not on all its members being the same, but on each member doing what they were made to do — which requires them to be genuinely different from each other.

The Enemy's substitution replaces this with something that feels like unity but is actually its opposite: a community in which everyone looks the same, sounds the same, and reaches the same conclusions — and calls this health. The warning signs of the substitution are easy to identify once you know what you're looking for: a community where disagreement is experienced as disloyalty; where new members are expected to converge on the community's positions rather than contribute their own; where the word "unity" is deployed to silence questions rather than to name a genuine shared commitment; and where difference is treated as a problem to be solved rather than a resource to be honored.

The Scriptural Frame Being Exploited

Paul's call to "be of one mind" (Philippians 2:2, Romans 15:5) is genuine and important — but its meaning is clarified by the context in which it appears. Philippians 2:2 is followed immediately by the kenotic hymn of Christ's self-emptying (2:5–11): the one-mindedness Paul is describing is the shared posture of humility and other-orientation that characterized Christ, not agreement on secondary matters. Romans 15:5 calls for the "God of endurance and encouragement" to grant one-mindedness "in accordance with Christ Jesus" — again, a shared orientation toward Christ, not uniformity of theological conclusion on non-essential matters. The Enemy removes this context, leaving only the command to agree, and applies it to matters Paul never intended it to govern.

Counting the Cost

The Restorationist movements of the 19th century. The Stone-Campbell movement (which gave us the Christian Church / Churches of Christ / Disciples of Christ) began with one of the most noble impulses in American church history: no creed but Christ, no book but the Bible, no name but Christian — an attempt to achieve unity by clearing away the accumulated theological frameworks that divided Christians and returning to Scripture alone. The impulse was genuine. The result was a movement that fractured repeatedly, and more bitterly than most, over secondary matters: whether to use musical instruments in worship, whether to support missionary societies, whether to use individual communion cups. Each split was justified by the argument that the other side was compromising essential unity. The unity sought through uniformity produced more division, not less.

Mid-20th century fundamentalist separatism. The doctrine of "secondary separation" — developed within fundamentalist circles in the early 20th century — held that a faithful Christian must separate not only from those in error, but from those who fellowship with those in error, and eventually from those who fellowship with those who fellowship with those in error. The logic was impeccable given the premise, and the premise was the Uniformity Substitution. The movement collapsed inward under the weight of its own standard: within two generations, its separatist communities had fractured to the point where meaningful fellowship was impossible with anyone who had not passed through exactly the right sequence of separations. The Enemy did not need to introduce false doctrine. He only needed to let the substitution run to its conclusion.

The Reframe

The question that cuts through the substitution: Are we confusing unity with uniformity? What would genuine unity that includes this difference actually look like?

This question does something important: it separates the goal (unity, which is genuinely important) from the method being used to achieve it (uniformity, which is not working and is not biblical). A community that can ask this question honestly will often discover that the difference they have been treating as a threat is actually an asset — that the person who thinks differently, worships differently, or raises uncomfortable questions is doing for the body what the foot does for the hand: something the rest of the body cannot do, and cannot afford to lose.

The Peacemaker's Move

When you see conformity pressure being applied to someone different: Name what you observe without accusation. "I've noticed [person] tends to get questions about their commitment when they push back. I want to check my reading — does this community have room for people who land differently on [secondary question]? I ask because I think we might be losing something valuable if the answer is no."

When "unity" is being used to silence a question: "Can we be specific about what kind of unity we're calling for here? Paul's unity in Philippians 2 is about shared humility and orientation toward Christ — I don't think he means agreement on [specific matter]. Are we sure this is the kind of unity Scripture is asking for, or is this a uniformity that we've started calling unity because it's more comfortable?"

When you are the person being pressured to conform: You are not required to perform agreement you do not have. You are allowed to say: "I'm committed to this community and to Christ. I'm not convinced I'm committed to [specific position] at the level you're asking. I'd like to understand whether there is room here for someone who holds my position — and if not, I'd like to know that clearly, so we can both make honest decisions."

In a leadership conversation about a "culture fit" concern: "Before we act on this, I want to ask: is this person outside our doctrinal commitments, or are they outside our cultural comfort zone? Those are different situations and they call for different responses. If it's the second, I think we need to ask whether our discomfort with difference is something we should be managing — or something we should be growing through."

⚖️ Going Deeper: Unity and Union — What the Early Church Learned

The early church made a critical distinction that contemporary ecumenical thought has recovered and that local church practice tends to forget: the difference between unity and union. They are not the same thing, and conflating them has driven more unnecessary division than almost any other single conceptual error.

Union is organizational or structural oneness — sharing the same institutional body, the same governance, the same formal membership. The church of the first three centuries was working toward increasing union across the scattered communities of the Mediterranean world, and the ecumenical councils were in part an exercise in establishing structural coherence. Union is real and has value. But it was never the New Testament's primary vision.

Unity — the thing Jesus prays for in John 17 — is relational and purposive coherence: shared orientation toward the same Father, through the same Son, in the same Spirit; mutual love across difference; the kind of visible relatedness that makes the world stop and ask questions. Unity can exist without union. The early church demonstrated this: communities in Antioch and Alexandria and Rome that had significant theological and liturgical differences, that were not under a single organizational roof, that sometimes disagreed sharply on secondary matters — and that nonetheless recognized each other as the same family, sending letters, receiving visitors, collecting for each other's poor.

The Reformers developed their own vocabulary for this distinction. Calvin's Institutes distinguish between the nota ecclesiae — the marks of the church (the Word rightly preached and the sacraments rightly administered) — and the full institutional identity of any given communion. Two communities that share the marks of the church are in genuine unity even if they are not in formal union. The absence of union does not mean the absence of unity; the presence of union does not guarantee its presence.

For the local church, the practical implication is this: the community is not required to achieve organizational uniformity in order to be genuinely united. What it is required to do is maintain the relational coherence — the mutual love, the shared commitment to Christ, the willingness to bear with one another across genuine difference — that the New Testament actually describes. A community that has union without unity has achieved exactly nothing worth having. A community that has unity without union has achieved exactly what Jesus prayed for.
