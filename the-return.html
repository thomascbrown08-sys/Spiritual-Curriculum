Coming Home

For the Discouraged, the Distant, and the One Who Keeps Meaning to Return
On This Page Who This Is For  ·  God Runs First  ·  The Cross Covers This  ·  The Spirit Is Already Working  ·  Discipline, Not Condemnation  ·  Peter  ·  The Danger of Delay  ·  Where to Place Yourself

Who This Page Is For

This page is for the person who once knew God — or thought they did — and now feels distant. It is for the one who drifted gradually, barely noticing, until one day they looked up and realised how far they had come from where they started. It is for the one who fell suddenly and hard, and has been living in the wreckage ever since. It is for the one who keeps meaning to come back and somehow never does — who has made the decision to return more than once in the privacy of their own heart and found it not holding.

It is for two quite different people, and both are honestly named here.

The first is discouraged. They want to come back. The distance itself is painful. But they are not sure they can — not sure the welcome they remember is still available to someone who has done what they have done or gone as far as they have gone. Shame is the main obstacle, not will.

The second is stubborn — or at least, something in them is. There is resistance, rationalisation, a settling-in to distance that makes return feel theoretical. The pull toward home is there, but it keeps losing to something else. Hardness may be forming, though they may not yet be able to name it. They are the prodigal who has not yet come to himself — still in the far country, still making the excuses that keep him there.

This page does not offer a programme or a checklist. What it offers is a portrait of who God is toward the person who has drifted — specifically and from Scripture — because in the tradition's experience, it is the accurate sight of God's character that produces genuine return, not the pressure of obligation or the fear of consequences. You cannot be argued or frightened into genuine love. But you can be shown something that makes you want to come home.

God Runs First

Luke 15:20

"But while he was still a long way off, his father saw him and felt compassion, and ran and embraced him and kissed him."

The most important thing on this page is the first thing: God is not waiting for you to arrive before he begins to move. He is already moving. The father in Luke 15 does not wait until the son reaches the door to decide whether to receive him. He sees him at a distance — while he is still a long way off — and he runs. In first-century culture, a man of his standing did not run. Running was undignified. The father makes himself undignified in order to reach the son before the son can fully rehearse his speech or complete his self-abasement.

Notice what happens next. The son begins his prepared speech: "Father, I have sinned against heaven and before you. I am no longer worthy to be called your son. Make me like one of your hired servants." The speech is not wrong — the self-assessment is accurate. But the father does not respond to the speech. He has already called for the robe, the ring, the sandals, the fatted calf. He is not waiting to evaluate the quality of the repentance before deciding the scale of the welcome. The welcome was decided when the father saw the son at a distance. The restoration is not the reward for adequate contrition. It is the Father's response to the son's return — any return, however ragged.

I will heal their waywardness and love them freely, for my anger has turned away from them.
— Hosea 14:4

Hosea 14 is addressed to a people who have been comprehensively unfaithful — not accidentally but persistently, wilfully, repeatedly. The word God speaks through Hosea is not conditional restoration ("if you perform adequate repentance I will consider receiving you") but the announcement of a character: I will love them freely. The Hebrew word translated "freely" is nedabah — spontaneously, voluntarily, without external pressure or compulsion. This is how God loves the returning wanderer: as an expression of his character, not as a transaction triggered by the quality of the return.

This is the first thing the discouraged backslider needs to hear — and to hear specifically, not in the abstract. The welcome you remember is not smaller than you thought. It is larger. What you have done does not reduce it. Where you have been does not disqualify you from it. The distance does not change who is waiting at the end of it.

The Cross Covers This Too

1 John 1:9

"If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness."

The ground of forgiveness here is striking: God is faithful and just to forgive. Not merely merciful — just. The forgiveness is grounded not in God's indulgence toward sin but in Christ's payment for it. The cross is the event that makes it possible for God to be both just (taking sin seriously, requiring that it be genuinely addressed) and the justifier (welcoming the returning sinner without pretending nothing happened). 1 John 1:9 is addressed to believers — to people who already know the gospel, who have already confessed Christ — and it tells them that the same cross that brought them in the first time is still sufficient for what they have done since.

The question the discouraged backslider often carries is: yes, but does the cross cover this specifically? Does it cover what I did with full knowledge? Does it cover the pattern, not just the single fall? Does it cover the sin I went back to after I promised I wouldn't?

Psalm 51 is the answer the canon provides. David wrote it after adultery and murder — deliberate, sustained, calculated sin, committed by a man who knew God deeply and did it anyway. The psalm does not begin with lengthy self-justification or explanation. It begins with the appeal to God's character: "Have mercy on me, O God, according to your steadfast love; according to your abundant mercy blot out my transgressions." The ground of the appeal is not the quality of David's remorse. It is the character of the God to whom he is returning. And the psalm is in the canon — preserved, inspired, given to the church — precisely so that every generation would have permanent evidence that serious, deliberate, post-knowledge sin is not beyond the reach of restoration.

⚖️ Does the Quality of My Repentance Determine Whether I Am Forgiven?

This is one of the most pastorally important questions on this page, and the tradition's answer is careful: no. The ground of forgiveness is the faithfulness and justice of God, anchored in Christ's atoning work — not the quality, depth, or genuineness of the repentance. If forgiveness were contingent on repentance meeting some threshold of quality, no one would ever know whether they had crossed it, and pastoral despair would be the only logical outcome.

What the tradition does say — and this is important — is that genuine faith includes genuine repentance as its natural direction. Not a performed contrition aimed at triggering forgiveness, but the honest turning of a person who has seen their sin clearly and turns from it toward God. The difference is not in the feeling (which will vary enormously) but in the direction. The person who acknowledges sin while secretly intending to continue in it is not yet repenting. The person who acknowledges sin while desiring — even faintly, even without confidence in their own ability to change — to turn toward God is already in motion in the direction of home. And God meets people in motion.

Charles Spurgeon, who knew the pastoral weight of this question, put it plainly: the threshold for coming to Christ is not a sufficiently broken heart but a heart that is willing to be broken. You do not need to arrive with the right feelings. You need to arrive.

The Spirit Is Already Working in You

There is something significant about the fact that you are reading this page. Not because this page has any power — it does not. But because the desire to return, the discomfort with distance, the restlessness that makes you pick up something like this — these are not self-generated. They are the Spirit's activity in you.

John 6:44: "No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him." The drawing is God's work, not the human being's. Which means that the pull toward home — even if it is faint, even if it is mixed with resistance, even if it keeps losing to other things — is evidence that God has not given up on you. The very fact that you feel the pull is the first sign that grace is already at work. You did not generate that pull. You cannot generate it. Its presence in you is the mark of the one who is drawing you.

The prodigal "came to himself" in Luke 15:17 — and that coming-to-himself is described as something that happened to him as much as something he did. There is a passive quality to genuine awakening: a seeing-clearly that precedes the decision, a recognition more than a resolution. If you are seeing clearly enough to know you have drifted — if the distance itself is registering as distance — then something has already happened in you that you did not make happen. The question is whether you will cooperate with what is already underway.

🕊️ For the One Who Has Been Away a Long Time

The person who has been away for years, or decades, carries a specific burden: not only the guilt of the drift, but the sense that too much has passed, that the gap is now too wide, that they are too settled in a life built around something other than God for return to be practically possible. The life, the relationships, the habits, the identity — all of it has grown up around the absence. Return feels like it would require becoming a different person.

That is exactly right. It would. And that is exactly what the gospel promises: not reform but new creation (2 Corinthians 5:17). Not the improved version of the person who left, but the beginning of something genuinely new. The gap you have placed between yourself and God is real. But it is not a measurement of the distance you would have to travel on your own. The father ran. He covered most of the distance himself. You need only to turn.

And the practical shape of that turning is not the sudden reversal of everything a long drift has built. It is one step: the honest acknowledgment that you want to come home, spoken to God directly, in whatever words you actually have. The rest unfolds from there — not on your initiative but on his.

Discipline Is Not Condemnation

Hebrews 12:5–6

"My son, do not regard lightly the discipline of the Lord, nor be weary when reproved by him. For the Lord disciplines the one he loves, and chastises every son whom he receives."

One of the most practically important distinctions in Scripture for the backslider is the difference between discipline and condemnation. Romans 8:1 is unambiguous: "There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus." That is a statement about legal standing before God — about whether the verdict has been rendered, and what it is. For those in Christ, condemnation is not the verdict, regardless of what has happened since.

But Hebrews 12 adds something that looks uncomfortable from one angle and profoundly comforting from another: God disciplines his children. The consequences of drift — the broken relationships, the damaged trust, the spiritual dryness, the sense of God's absence, the suffering that follows poor decisions — these may be the Father's disciplinary work rather than his rejection. And here is the key: Hebrews 12:8 says that those who are without discipline are not sons. Discipline is evidence of sonship, not evidence of abandonment. The pain you may be carrying from the consequences of where you have been is not God turning his face away. It may be precisely the opposite — the Father pressing toward you in love, using the consequences to do what comfort could not.

This does not make the suffering smaller or the consequences less real. But it changes what the suffering means. It is not the verdict. It is the Father's hand.

Peter

Peter is the New Testament's standing answer to the question of whether restoration from serious, deliberate failure is possible — and what it looks like when it comes.

He was not a naive believer who stumbled accidentally. He had been with Jesus for three years. He had walked on water, witnessed the Transfiguration, confessed that Jesus was the Christ, the Son of the living God. He had been told — specifically and directly — that he would deny Jesus that night, and had insisted that was impossible. And then he denied knowing him three times, publicly, to people who were not in any position to harm him, within earshot of the man he was denying.

John 21 is the restoration. Jesus does not put Peter on probation. He does not require an elaborate performance of contrition. He asks him one question three times — "Simon, do you love me?" — corresponding to the three denials, and at each answer he gives Peter back his calling: "Feed my lambs... tend my sheep... feed my sheep." Peter is restored not to reduced standing but to full responsibility — to the very calling the denial had seemed to forfeit. He went on to become the central figure of the early church, the author of two New Testament epistles, and, according to the tradition, the martyr who asked to be crucified upside down because he was unworthy to die as his Lord died.

The account is in the canon — permanently, by divine intention — so that the church would never run out of evidence that restoration from serious failure is not only possible but complete. Peter's failure is not a footnote to his story. It is part of the story, preserved so that everyone who reads it can see that the call on their life is not cancelled by what they have done.

Come now, let us reason together, says the LORD: though your sins are like scarlet, they shall be as white as snow; though they are red like crimson, they shall become like wool.
— Isaiah 1:18

The Danger of Delay — Stated Pastorally

Hebrews 3:15

"Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts."

This is the most urgent section of this page, and it is addressed primarily to the stubborn backslider — the person who is not primarily discouraged but is primarily resistant, who has been meaning to return and keeps not returning.

Hebrews 3 is not warning that God's patience will eventually run out. That framing is available, but it is not the primary point. The primary point is what delay does to the person — specifically, to their capacity to hear and respond. Verse 13 names it: "exhort one another every day... that none of you may be hardened by the deceitfulness of sin." Hardness is not a single event. It is a process. It grows with time. It is the accumulated effect of the conscience being overridden — of the inner voice that calls you home being consistently not obeyed. Each decision not to return makes the next decision not to return slightly easier. Each suppression of the pull toward God makes the pull slightly fainter. Not because God is withdrawing, but because the capacity to receive what God is offering is being eroded from the inside.

The word today in Hebrews 3:7, 13, and 15 is emphatic and repeated. Three times. It is not incidental. It means: the moment of hearing is the moment of opportunity, and delay is not neutral. The person who says "I will return, but not yet" is not in a stable holding position. They are drifting further, and the drift is doing something to them in the meantime.

This is not a threat. It is a description of a mechanism — a compassionate warning about what delay costs, not an announcement that the door is closing. The door is not closing. But you are changing. And the longer you wait, the harder the person you are becoming will find it to walk through a door that has not moved.

⚖️ On the Fear of Presumption

Some people who have drifted and want to return are held back not by hardness but by a specific theological anxiety: they fear that returning to God on the basis of grace alone — without adequate suffering for what they have done, without a sufficient period of demonstrated change — is presumptuous. They feel they should have to earn their way back, or at least demonstrate that they deserve to be readmitted.

The tradition's answer is careful but unambiguous: this fear, however sincere, is a misunderstanding of the gospel. It is the elder brother's logic applied to oneself — the assumption that relationship with the Father must be earned and maintained by performance. The cross is the sufficient ground of return. There is no waiting period, no demonstration of worthiness required, because the worthiness that matters is Christ's, not yours. Psalm 51's David does not wait until he has suffered adequately before coming to God. He comes immediately, on the basis of God's character. The fear of presumption, when it prevents return, is itself a form of pride — the assumption that your guilt is too large for the cross to cover, which is to say that your sin is greater than God's grace. It is not.

The act of turning toward God is itself the beginning of repentance — not its completion, but its beginning. What the Reformed tradition calls repentance in its fullest sense (the sustained reordering of desires, the deepening grief over sin, the progressive turning of the whole life) is the Spirit's continuing work in the person who has turned. It grows in the relationship, not before it. Come with what you have. The Spirit will do the rest.

And this is worth stating plainly, because it changes what return actually means: repentance is not primarily about what you are leaving. It is about who you are returning to. The grammar of the biblical language has this built in. The primary Old Testament word for repentance is shub — to return, to come back. It is directional toward someone. Hosea 14:1: "Return to the LORD your God." Zechariah 1:3: "Return to me, declares the LORD, and I will return to you." The preposition is not incidental; it is the meaning. The Greek metanoia — the word the New Testament uses — is a change of the whole orientation of the person. And Paul names the direction explicitly in Acts 20:21: "repentance toward God and faith in our Lord Jesus Christ." Toward. That preposition is Paul's own formulation of what repentance is.

This matters pastorally because it means the focus of return is not your sin but the Father. The prodigal's repentance in Luke 15 is described in one movement: "he arose and came to his father." The wasted money, the far country, the hired servants he has been eating with — these are the background from which he turns. The father is the foreground toward which he moves. What makes it repentance is not the quality of his guilt but the direction of his journey. He is going home. And the father, seeing him coming, runs.

The implication for the person reading this page is this: you do not need to spend time examining the adequacy of your remorse before you begin to move. You begin to move. The grief over what you have done, the hatred of the sin, the reordering of the life — these are real, and they will come, but they come as consequences of the return rather than conditions for it. They are what the relationship produces as it deepens. The positive destination is primary. The departure from the far country gets its meaning from where the road leads, not from how wretched the far country was.

Where to Place Yourself

This page does not offer a checklist. But it does name something specific about where God has promised to meet the returning person — not because the following things earn restoration, but because they are the field in which the Spirit characteristically works it.

The single most important thing is to speak to God directly and honestly — in whatever words you actually have. Not a carefully composed prayer designed to sound adequate. Not a recitation of the right theological framework. Whatever you actually have: I have been away. I want to come back. I'm not sure I know how. I don't feel what I think I should feel. But I am here. That is enough to begin. The father does not wait for the son to deliver a polished speech. He runs when he sees him coming.

After that: return to Scripture slowly, not ambitiously. Not a programme to read through the Bible in a year. One passage, read attentively, asking what it says about who God is. The Psalms are the traditional starting point for return — partly because they contain the full range of human experience before God, including the experience of distance and darkness, and partly because they model the kind of honest speech to God that return requires. Psalm 51 specifically. Psalm 42. Psalm 103.

Find one person to tell. Not an audience, not a public declaration — one person who is further along than you and whom you trust. The tradition consistently names the community of believers as essential to return and to staying returned, because isolation is where drift begins and where it continues. The prodigal in the far country was alone. Coming home meant coming back to a household, a family, a community. The return to God and the return to his people are not two separate projects.

And receive the Lord's Supper when you have the opportunity. Not as a reward for having successfully returned, but as the appointed means by which the grace of Christ's death is held before you in concentrated, tangible form — specifically designed for people who need to be reminded of what was done for them at cost.

These are not the steps that produce restoration. They are where the Spirit meets people who are willing to be met. Place yourself there, honestly and without performance, and trust God to do what he has promised to do for the person who returns to him: to heal their waywardness, and love them freely.

🕊️ If the Church Itself Is Part of What You Are Returning From

Some people reading this page have not drifted from God through moral failure or spiritual dryness but through specific harm done to them by a church, a pastor, or a community that presented itself as speaking for God. The drift was a response to an injury, and the injury was real. The person who says "I have drifted from the church" and "I have drifted from God" may need to be helped to separate those two things, because they are genuinely different.

God is not the institution that harmed you. The character of the Father that this page describes is not a portrait of the leader who misused authority, the community that failed to protect you, or the theology that was used as a weapon. The gospel's account of who God is stands on its own terms — in Scripture, not in the behaviour of those who claimed to represent it. Coming home to God does not require returning to the specific community where you were hurt. It requires finding the people of God who are actually living what the gospel describes, and it may take time to find them. The Finding a Healthy Church page names some of what to look for.
