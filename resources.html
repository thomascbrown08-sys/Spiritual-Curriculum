Resources

Consolidated deep dive suggestions for all twelve modules

Each recommendation below is listed with its primary module and a brief note on what it offers. Some appear in multiple modules. The list is a starting point, not a reading programme — begin with what is most relevant to where you are.

Looking for something else? The About page explains what this curriculum is and how it was built. The 🕊️ Wounded Pilgrim page is a separate resource specifically for those coming from a place of church harm or spiritual dryness — not a reading list, but a place to begin.

The Beauty and Desirability of God

Module 1Spine

Michael Reeves, Delighting in the Trinity — The most accessible modern account of why the Trinitarian nature of God changes everything about love, creation, and the gospel. Essential pre-reading for facilitators of Module 1.

Jonathan Edwards, The End for Which God Created the World (in John Piper's edition, God's Passion for His Glory, Crossway) — Edwards' sustained argument that God's glory and the joy of his creatures are not competing aims but one end. The philosophical machinery is demanding; Piper's introductory essays make it navigable. This is the primary source behind the curriculum's Chief End section.

John Piper, Desiring God: Meditations of a Christian Hedonist — Piper's full development of the argument that pursuing joy in God is not only permitted but commanded; that God is most glorified in us when we are most satisfied in him. More accessible than Edwards directly; the most widely read modern treatment of this theme.

John Piper, Captive to Glory: Celebrating the Vision and Influence of Jonathan Edwards (Desiring God, freely available at desiringGod.org) — A shorter collection drawing together the best of Piper's writing on Edwards. The first chapter, "Fifteen Implications," is the most useful single entry point to this material for group leaders.

Thomas Chalmers, The Expulsive Power of a New Affection (sermon, freely available online) — Sin is not conquered by suppression but by a superior attraction. Short, essential, and more practically useful than its 19th-century prose might suggest.

Augustine, Confessions, Books I–III — The most famous exploration of restlessness pointing toward God. Also the most honest account of what years of looking in the wrong places actually feels like from the inside.

The Gospel Story and Its Shape

Module 2

Henri Nouwen, The Return of the Prodigal Son — A sustained meditation on Rembrandt's painting of the parable, exploring all three characters — the prodigal, the elder brother, and the father — with unusual honesty about which one we actually inhabit. One of the most pastorally searching books on this parable available, and essential for facilitators who want to address the elder brother's lostness as seriously as the prodigal's.

Timothy Keller, The Prodigal God — A short, accessible treatment of the parable centered on the elder brother; Keller's argument that religious moralism is as much a form of lostness as prodigal rebellion is particularly valuable for groups with church backgrounds.

N.T. Wright, Simply Good News — On the gospel as announcement rather than advice; one of the clearest short treatments of the euangelion argument available from a major New Testament scholar.

Kenneth Bailey, The Cross and the Prodigal — A Middle Eastern New Testament scholar's reading of the parable in its cultural context; forty years of village life in the region inform his reading of what each gesture in the father's welcome would have meant and cost. Essential for facilitators using the honor-shame register.

C.S. Lewis, Myth Became Fact (essay in God in the Dock) — Lewis's account of why the gospel's mythic resonance combined with historical rootedness matters, and what it felt like to arrive at that conclusion from outside.

Historical Reliability and Archaeology

Module 3

F.F. Bruce, The New Testament Documents: Are They Reliable? — A classic short treatment of manuscript evidence and early dating; still one of the best starting points for the historical credibility question.

Craig Blomberg, The Historical Reliability of the Gospels — A careful scholarly engagement with the full range of critical challenges to the gospel accounts. More current than Bruce and more accessible than Kitchen; a natural complement to both.

K.A. Kitchen, On the Reliability of the Old Testament — A rigorous Egyptologist's case for the historical credibility of the Old Testament. Comprehensive, demanding, and highly rewarding for those who want the full archaeological argument.

John McRay, Archaeology and the New Testament — A thorough scholarly survey of archaeological findings relevant to the New Testament world.

The Resurrection

Module 4

N.T. Wright, The Resurrection of the Son of God — The most comprehensive scholarly treatment of the resurrection from a historical perspective. Eight hundred pages of serious argument; worth the investment.

N.T. Wright, Surprised by Hope — Shorter and more accessible; on resurrection and its implications for how Christians live now.

Gary Habermas & Michael Licona, The Case for the Resurrection of Jesus — A detailed apologetic treatment using minimal facts accepted by the majority of critical scholars regardless of theological commitment.

Timothy Keller, The Reason for God, Chapter 13 — "The Reality of the Resurrection." Pastoral and philosophical; accessible to general readers.

Scripture's Honesty and Lament

Module 5

Walter Brueggemann, The Message of the Psalms — The foundational scholarly treatment of lament as a theological category: the recovery of honest grief as legitimate prayer. Brueggemann has done more than any contemporary scholar to recover the psalms of lament for Christian worship and preaching.

Walter Brueggemann, Praying the Psalms — A shorter, more accessible companion: a practical guide to using the full range of the Psalter in prayer, including the psalms of darkness and complaint. A good starting point for group members new to Brueggemann.

Tremper Longman III, How to Read the Psalms — A clear, scholarly, and accessible guide to reading the Psalms in their literary and theological context.

Suffering, Trauma, and the Church's Healing Role

Module 6

Diane Langberg, Suffering and the Heart of God: How Trauma Destroys and Christ Restores (New Growth Press, 2015) — The most theologically and clinically integrated treatment of trauma from an evangelical perspective. Langberg brings 50 years of clinical experience alongside a deeply Christ-centered theology; her central argument that the cross is the transforming lens for traumatic suffering — not an explanation but an entry — is exactly the frame Module 6 requires. Essential reading for facilitators.

Diane Langberg, When the Church Harms God's People (Brazos, 2024) — For facilitators who will encounter church-hurt in Module 6. Langberg diagnoses how Christian communities cause harm and offers a constructive, hope-filled vision of what the church is designed to be. Pairs directly with the module's Pilgrim box on spiritual abuse.

Trauma Healing Institute, Healing the Wounds of Trauma: How the Church Can Help (American Bible Society) — The foundational curriculum of a Scripture-based, globally-deployed trauma healing program used in 100+ languages. Integrates mental health best practices with biblical truth in a small-group setting led by trained facilitators. For churches wanting to go beyond this curriculum's discussion format into structured trauma healing ministry: traumahealinginstitute.org.

Beth Argot, "When the Brain Can't Hear God" (DTS, 2025) — A helpful introduction to the neurological framework underlying Module 6's trauma section, presented accessibly for a non-clinical audience. Available on YouTube via Dallas Theological Seminary. A useful supplementary entry point before going deeper with Langberg.

Timothy Keller, Walking with God through Pain and Suffering (2013) — The most comprehensive evangelical treatment: intellectually serious, pastorally warm, honest about what cannot be said. Essential for facilitators of Module 6.

C.S. Lewis, A Grief Observed — Lewis's journal after his wife Joy's death. Not a theodicy but an account of what grief actually looks like from the inside — raw, honest, and ultimately irreplaceable as pastoral reading.

Philip Yancey, Where Is God When It Hurts? and Disappointment with God — Accessible, honest, written from personal experience of disillusionment. Yancey's willingness to name his own doubt makes him trustworthy.

D.A. Carson, How Long, O Lord? Reflections on Suffering and Evil — Carson's rigorous biblical-theological treatment. More demanding than Yancey; they complement each other well.

Henri Nouwen, The Wounded Healer — On the pastoral call to minister from one's own wounds rather than from a position of safe competence. Essential for facilitators of Module 6 specifically.

Eleonore Stump, Wandering in Darkness — Philosophically rigorous engagement with God's hiddenness and its compatibility with his goodness. For those who want the intellectual argument at its most serious level.

Spiritual Growth, Sanctification, and the Paradox of Weakness

Modules 7–8Spine

Barbara Duguid, Extravagant Grace: God's Glory Displayed in Our Weakness (P&R, 2013) — The most important contemporary treatment of this module's central reframe: that growing in grace is more about humility, dependence, and exalting Christ than about defeating sin. Duguid draws rigorously on Owen while writing with unusual pastoral warmth and personal honesty. Essential reading for facilitators of Module 7, and for anyone who has been following Christ for years and is still struggling.

John Owen, The Mortification of Sin (1656; modern edition by Crossway) — The theological root of Duguid's reframe. Owen's argument — that the goal of mortification is not to feel like you are winning but to keep sin clearly before Christ — is demanding, precise, and unlike almost anything written in the last century. Chapter 2 alone is worth the investment. Read alongside Duguid for the full picture.

Eugene Peterson, A Long Obedience in the Same Direction — On the pilgrim life as sustained journey; Peterson's reading of the Psalms of Ascent as a curriculum for the long walk complements this module's argument about the slow time of genuine growth.

Dallas Willard, Renovation of the Heart and The Spirit of the Disciplines — The active-effort counterbalance to Duguid's reframe: Willard's argument that we do not become holy by trying harder but by training intentionally in cooperation with the Spirit.

Discipleship and Community

Module 9

Dietrich Bonhoeffer, The Cost of Discipleship — The classic account of what genuine discipleship costs and why cheap grace is a betrayal of the gospel. Read in conjunction with his biography for full effect.

James K.A. Smith, You Are What You Love — On how embodied habits and gathered practices form our desires beneath the level of conscious decision; a contemporary articulation of why liturgy and communal worship matter for the whole person, not only the intellect. A natural companion to Willard and an accessible entry to Smith's larger argument in the Cultural Liturgies series.

Miroslav Volf, Exclusion and Embrace — On love, forgiveness, and reconciliation in a world where people genuinely harm one another. Some of the most important Christian social ethics of the last thirty years.

Lesslie Newbigin, The Gospel in a Pluralist Society — On what it means to be the church as a sign, foretaste, and instrument of God's kingdom in a culture that has moved past it. Chapters 18–20 are especially relevant to Module 9's vision of community as witness.

C.S. Lewis, The Weight of Glory — Particularly "The Weight of Glory" and "Membership": on the glory of ordinary people, the cost and delight of taking them seriously, and what genuine Christian community makes possible that isolated spirituality cannot.

Beth Argot, "Restoring the Body: Knowing God Fully through Whole-Brain Worship" (DTS Chapel, 2025) — On how embodied, whole-person worship creates pathways for healing and encounter that purely propositional engagement cannot replicate. Available through Dallas Theological Seminary's chapel archive. Pairs naturally with the embodied-life section of Module 9.

Church Conflict and Peacemaking

Module 10

Ken Sande, The Peacemaker: A Biblical Guide to Resolving Personal Conflict — The most widely used practical manual for biblical peacemaking in church contexts. Covers the full arc from personal conflict to formal conciliation; especially useful for the Peacemaker's Move sections throughout Module 10.

David Augsburger, Caring Enough to Confront — On the theology and practice of honest, loving confrontation; how to speak truth in ways that honor both the relationship and the reality. A shorter, more pastoral complement to Sande; most directly applicable in Sub-Module 3 and Sub-Module 7, where the confrontation is most delicate.

Dietrich Bonhoeffer, Life Together — The most theologically serious account of what genuine Christian community requires and costs. Bonhoeffer's chapter on confession and the community's capacity to hold weakness is directly relevant to Sub-Module 3: The Wounded Proxy War and Sub-Module 7: The Social Hostage.

Miroslav Volf, Exclusion and Embrace — On forgiveness, identity, and the possibility of genuine reconciliation after real harm. More demanding than Sande, more theologically rich; essential for communities that have experienced significant rupture rather than ordinary disagreement. See especially Sub-Module 3, Sub-Module 7, and Sub-Module 9 for the specific fracture patterns this book equips communities to address.

C.S. Lewis, The Screwtape Letters — The Enemy as tactician: Lewis's portrait of demonic method as applied to a single human soul maps precisely onto Module 10's portrait of those methods applied to a congregation. Read it having read Module 10 and the resonances are extensive.

Rodney Clapp, A Peculiar People: The Church as Culture in a Post-Christian Society — On what genuine Christian community looks like when it is the church rather than a religious support group; the positive ecclesiology that frames Module 10's critique of what fractures it. Most directly pairs with Sub-Module 4: The Uniformity Substitution.

Mark Dever and Paul Alexander, The Deliberate Church — A practical account of how church culture forms and what leadership choices shape it; useful for leaders who want to understand how to build communities that are resistant to the Uniformity Substitution and the Purity Spiral.

Scot McKnight, A Fellowship of Differents — On the New Testament's vision of the church as a community of genuine diversity held together by something stronger than agreement; a direct theological argument for the position Module 10 defends against the Uniformity Substitution.

Heresy, Doctrinal History, and Pattern Recognition

Module 11

Ben Quash and Michael Ward (eds.), Heresies and How to Avoid Them: Why It Matters What Christians Believe — A scholarly but accessible collection of essays on the major heresies, with each essay treating both the ancient form and the modern resonance. The closest direct companion to Module 11; useful for readers who want to go deeper on any of the seven core entries.

Roger Olson, The Story of Christian Theology: Twenty Centuries of Tradition and Reform — A comprehensive and readable survey of Christian doctrinal history from the early church through the 20th century. For readers who want the full narrative of how the church's theology developed — controversies, councils, key figures, and cumulative settlements — this is the single most useful one-volume account at the lay-to-graduate level. Directly relevant to both Module 11 and Module 12.

Gerald Bray, Creeds, Councils and Christ — A clear and historically grounded account of how the great ecumenical creeds were produced and what they established; especially useful for the conciliar background behind Module 12. Bray writes from a Reformed Anglican perspective but gives the material fair and ecumenical treatment.

The Creeds and the Church's Theological Inheritance

Module 12

C.S. Lewis, Mere Christianity (Book II: "What Christians Believe") — The most accessible lay account of orthodox Christology in the modern era. Lewis's famous "Lord, Liar, or Lunatic" trilemma and his explanation of the incarnation and atonement are models of how to make dense theological material both intellectually serious and personally compelling. Pairs directly with Module 12's walkthrough of the creeds' Christological claims, and with Arianism and Docetism in Module 11.

Athanasius of Alexandria, On the Incarnation (St. Vladimir's Seminary Press edition, trans. John Behr, with C.S. Lewis's introduction) — Written by Athanasius while still a young man — before the Nicene controversy was even fully joined — this is one of the most accessible works of early Christian theology and the most sustained argument for why the incarnation had to be real, costly, and bodily. Lewis's introduction makes the case for reading old books in the first place; Athanasius makes the case for the incarnation in language that has aged remarkably well. The human story behind this book — its author exiled five times for refusing to abandon what it argues — is told in Module 12.

Thomas G. Weinandy, Athanasius: A Theological Introduction — For readers who want the full theological account of Athanasius's contribution: his argument structure, his use of Scripture, his response to the Arian position, and his legacy for the Nicene settlement. More demanding than Lewis; more rewarding for those willing to engage it.

Jaroslav Pelikan, The Christian Tradition: A History of the Development of Doctrine, Vol. 1: The Emergence of the Catholic Tradition (100–600) — The definitive scholarly account of how Christian doctrine developed through the patristic period. This is not light reading, but it is the fullest available account of the controversies behind the creeds, told with precision and historical depth. For readers who want to understand the full context of Module 12's story.

General Apologetics

Multiple Modules

Timothy Keller, The Reason for God — The best single-volume contemporary apologetic for Christian faith. Engages the most common intellectual objections seriously and charitably.

C.S. Lewis, Mere Christianity — Still the most widely read apologetic work in the English language; Books I–II are essential, Book III connects to Module 1's diagnostic framework.

Alister McGrath, Mere Apologetics — A short, practical guide to how to engage apologetically; useful for facilitators wanting to develop their own capacity to address questions.

Timothy Keller, Preaching: Communicating Faith in an Age of Skepticism (Viking, 2015) — Essential reading for facilitators who want to understand the architectural principle behind this curriculum's design. Chapter 4 develops the argument — drawn from Sinclair Ferguson's The Whole Christ — that the gospel which saves is the same gospel that sanctifies, and that a mixed room of believers and skeptics is therefore not a problem to manage but a feature: when believers are returned to the gospel as the solution to real problems, skeptics watching from outside faith see it working. That is both edification and evangelism, simultaneously, with the same content. The curriculum's inside-out structure and its refusal to separate a "believer track" from a "seeker track" both reflect this conviction.
