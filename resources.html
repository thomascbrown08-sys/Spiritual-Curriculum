Resources

Consolidated deep dive suggestions for all nine modules

Each recommendation below is listed with its primary module and a brief note on what it offers. Some appear in multiple modules. The list is a starting point, not a reading programme — begin with what is most relevant to where you are.

The Beauty and Desirability of God

Module 1Spine

Michael Reeves, Delighting in the Trinity — The most accessible modern account of why the Trinitarian nature of God changes everything about love, creation, and the gospel. Essential pre-reading for facilitators of Module 1.

Jonathan Edwards, The End for Which God Created the World (in Piper's edition, God's Passion for His Glory) — Edwards' sustained argument that God's beauty and glory is the fountain from which creation and redemption flow. Demanding but rewarding.

Thomas Chalmers, The Expulsive Power of a New Affection (sermon, freely available online) — Sin is not conquered by suppression but by a superior attraction. Short, essential, and more practically useful than its 19th-century prose might suggest.

Augustine, Confessions, Books I–III — The most famous exploration of restlessness pointing toward God. Also the most honest account of what years of looking in the wrong places actually feels like from the inside.

The Gospel Story and Its Shape

Module 2

N.T. Wright, Simply Good News — On the gospel as announcement rather than advice; one of the clearest short treatments available from a major New Testament scholar.

Kenneth Bailey, The Cross and the Prodigal — A Middle Eastern New Testament scholar's reading of the parable in its cultural context. Essential for facilitators using the honor-shame register.

C.S. Lewis, Myth Became Fact (essay in God in the Dock) — Lewis's account of why the gospel's mythic resonance combined with historical rootedness matters.

Historical Reliability and Archaeology

Module 3

F.F. Bruce, The New Testament Documents: Are They Reliable? — A classic short treatment; still one of the best starting points for the historical credibility question.

K.A. Kitchen, On the Reliability of the Old Testament — A rigorous Egyptologist's case for the historical credibility of the Old Testament. Comprehensive and demanding.

John McRay, Archaeology and the New Testament — A thorough scholarly survey of archaeological findings relevant to the New Testament.

Sir Robert Anderson, The Coming Prince — The original detailed chronological calculation for Daniel 9. Methodologically disputed but intellectually serious and historically significant.

The Resurrection

Module 4

N.T. Wright, The Resurrection of the Son of God — The most comprehensive scholarly treatment of the resurrection from a historical perspective. Eight hundred pages of serious argument; worth the investment.

N.T. Wright, Surprised by Hope — Shorter and more accessible; on resurrection and its implications for how Christians live now.

Gary Habermas & Michael Licona, The Case for the Resurrection of Jesus — A detailed apologetic treatment using minimal facts accepted by the majority of critical scholars regardless of theological commitment.

Timothy Keller, The Reason for God, Chapter 13 — "The Reality of the Resurrection." Pastoral and philosophical; accessible to general readers.

Scripture's Honesty and Lament

Module 5

Walter Brueggemann, The Message of the Psalms — On lament as a theological category: the recovery of honest grief as legitimate prayer. Brueggemann has done more than any contemporary scholar to recover the psalms of lament for Christian worship.

Tremper Longman III, How to Read the Psalms — A clear, scholarly, and accessible guide to reading the Psalms in their literary and theological context.

Suffering and the Problem of Evil

Module 6

Timothy Keller, Walking with God through Pain and Suffering (2013) — The most comprehensive evangelical treatment: intellectually serious, pastorally warm, honest about what cannot be said. Essential for facilitators of Module 6.

C.S. Lewis, A Grief Observed — Lewis's journal after his wife Joy's death. Not a theodicy but an account of what grief actually looks like from the inside — raw, honest, and ultimately irreplaceable as pastoral reading.

Philip Yancey, Where Is God When It Hurts? and Disappointment with God — Accessible, honest, written from personal experience of disillusionment. Yancey's willingness to name his own doubt makes him trustworthy.

D.A. Carson, How Long, O Lord? Reflections on Suffering and Evil — Carson's rigorous biblical-theological treatment. More demanding than Yancey; they complement each other well.

Henri Nouwen, The Wounded Healer — On the pastoral call to minister from one's own wounds rather than from a position of safe competence. Essential for facilitators of Module 6 specifically.

Eleonore Stump, Wandering in Darkness — Philosophically rigorous engagement with God's hiddenness and its compatibility with his goodness. For those who want the intellectual argument at its most serious level.

Ron Hutchcraft, A Life That Matters and associated radio ministry — Hutchcraft's pastoral voice, shaped by significant personal loss, offers warmth and accessibility that academic treatments often lack.

Spiritual Growth and Formation

Modules 7–8

Eugene Peterson, A Long Obedience in the Same Direction — On the pilgrim life as a sustained journey rather than dramatic experiences. One of the most practically wise books in contemporary Christian writing.

Dallas Willard, Renovation of the Heart and The Spirit of the Disciplines — Rigorous accounts of spiritual formation as transformation of the whole person. Willard's argument: we do not become holy by trying harder but by training intentionally in cooperation with the Spirit.

Discipleship and Community

Module 9

Dietrich Bonhoeffer, The Cost of Discipleship — The classic account of what genuine discipleship costs and why cheap grace is a betrayal of the gospel. Read in conjunction with his biography for full effect.

Miroslav Volf, Exclusion and Embrace — On love, forgiveness, and reconciliation in a world where people genuinely harm one another. Some of the most important Christian social ethics of the last thirty years.

Lesslie Newbigin, The Gospel in a Pluralist Society — On what it means to be the church as a sign, foretaste, and instrument of God's kingdom in a culture that has moved past it.

General Apologetics

Multiple Modules

Timothy Keller, The Reason for God — The best single-volume contemporary apologetic for Christian faith. Engages the most common intellectual objections seriously and charitably.

C.S. Lewis, Mere Christianity — Still the most widely read apologetic work in the English language; Books I–II are essential, Book III connects to Module 1's diagnostic framework.

Alister McGrath, Mere Apologetics — A short, practical guide to how to engage apologetically; useful for facilitators wanting to develop their own capacity to address questions.
