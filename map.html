Curriculum Map

Twelve modules, one sequence, multiple entry points

The inside-out logic of this sequence: feel the need → encounter the story that meets it → examine the document that tells it → encounter the fact that anchors it → trust the honesty of that document → look at what suffering does to faith and what faith does to suffering → look for its fruit → step through the door → live it out → recognize the Enemy's tactics in community → understand the errors the church corrected → receive the creeds as the church's hard-won voice.

Modules can be taken in sequence or selected individually. Each stands alone. Together they form a cumulative case that no single module carries by itself.

The Modules at a Glance

Module 1

The Honest Mirror

What we were made for, and what we have turned away from. The beauty of the Triune God; the tragedy of the turn; sin as power, guilt, and shame; the gospel as news rather than advice.
Module 2

The Compelling Story

Why the gospel has the shape of truth. The story no one would have invented. The Prodigal Son as a window into God's character. The Spirit working through narrative.
Module 3

The Examined Record

Prophecy, archaeology, and the historical reliability of Scripture. Isaiah 53. The Tel Dan Stele. Why this document deserves serious intellectual engagement.
Module 4

The Resurrection

The most disruptive fact in history. The historical case, the existential stakes, the personal presence of the risen Christ. What it means to live as if this is true.
Module 5

The Unvarnished Record

Scripture's radical honesty about human and institutional failure. Psalm 88 — the psalm that ends in darkness. The prophets against power. Why this honesty is itself evidence.
Module 6

The Problem of Suffering

The intellectual and personal problem of pain. The cross as God entering suffering rather than explaining it. Lament as a theological category. What we can and cannot promise.
Module 7

Signposts of Growth

Seeing God's faithfulness over time. Fruit as expression, not measure. Why inconsistency is a call to return to the vine, not a verdict on whether we were ever connected.
Module 8

Obedience as the Door

John 7:17 in practice. Obedience as natural expression and explicit command. Anchored obedience. Unanswered prayer and the Spirit's intercession. The living experiment.
Module 9

Living It Out

Faith that moves the hands and feet. The Sermon on the Mount. Homecoming and cost — both are true. The church as the visible shape of Trinitarian love in the world.
Module 10

Divided We Fall

The Enemy's playbook in the family of God. Nine tactical patterns that fracture churches — named precisely, with their scriptural roots, their historical costs, and the peacemaker's specific moves for each.
Module 11

Old Errors, New Labels

The heresies that keep coming back. Seven core doctrinal distortions — Arianism, Pelagianism, Gnosticism, Marcionism, Docetism, Modalism, and the Prosperity Gospel — each with its modern rebrand, its diagnostic question, and the church’s hard-won response.
Module 12

We Believe

The creeds as the church’s hard-won voice. The Apostles’ and Nicene Creeds walked through clause by clause. The story of Nicaea, Constantinople, and the Filioque. Extended reading: the Chalcedonian Definition and the Athanasian Creed.

The Sequence Logic

Phase One: The Need and the Story (Modules 1–2)

The curriculum begins with the most personally immediate questions: does this document describe me accurately? And does the story it tells have the shape of something true? No prior commitment to Scripture's authority is required — only honesty about one's own experience, and openness to encountering a narrative. These two modules create the felt need and the initial draw that make the evidential modules personally motivated rather than academically remote.

Phase Two: The Evidence (Modules 3–5)

Having been drawn in by the story, the group's natural question becomes: can I trust the document that tells it? Modules 3 and 5 approach this from outside the text (prophecy, archaeology, historical reliability) and from inside it (Scripture's own unflinching honesty about failure). Module 4 addresses the central historical claim directly: the resurrection, which is the load-bearing fact of the entire structure. Together these three modules make a two-and-a-half session case for why this document deserves sustained, serious attention.

Phase Three: Suffering and Reality (Module 6)

Before moving to the applied material of the later modules, the curriculum pauses to address the most significant intellectual and personal barrier most Western post-Christian people carry: the problem of suffering and evil. This module is placed here deliberately — after the evidence has been examined and before the call to faith is issued, because it would be dishonest to issue that call without acknowledging what it costs and what it asks of people who carry real wounds. This module is the curriculum's most pastorally demanding and most important for people in genuine pain.

Phase Four: The Applied Life (Modules 7–9)

With the case established and the hard questions addressed, the final three modules turn to what following this actually produces in a life: observable growth over time (Module 7), the practice of obedience as John 7:17's living experiment (Module 8), and the full texture of a life shaped by the resurrection and lived in community (Module 9). These modules are the destination the earlier ones point toward — and Modules 8 and 9 in particular will continue to grow as the group brings its own experience to bear on them.

Phase Five: The Community Under Pressure (Module 10)

Module 9 describes what the church is meant to be. Module 10 addresses why it so often isn't — and why the gap between the vision and the reality is not mainly a failure of effort or sincerity. The Enemy's tactics for fracturing church communities are real, identifiable, and ancient; they follow patterns precise enough to name. Module 10 names nine of them, traces their historical costs, and gives the peacemaker practical tools for each. It is appropriate for groups that have completed the main sequence, for lay leaders looking for tools to hand to their communities, and for anyone who has been hurt by a church conflict and wants to understand what actually happened to them.

Phase Six: The Church's Doctrinal Inheritance (Modules 11–12)

Where Module 10 addresses how the Enemy fractures the church through relational and rhetorical tactics, Modules 11 and 12 address how he corrupts it through doctrinal confusion — and how the church has repeatedly, at great cost, found its way back to clarity. Module 11 provides pattern recognition for the errors that recur across every generation: seven major heresies, each with its modern rebrand and its diagnostic question. Module 12 presents the church's responses in their most enduring form — the creeds themselves, walked through clause by clause, with the human stories of the people who paid to produce them. Extended reading sub-pages cover the Chalcedonian Definition and the Athanasian Creed for those who want to go further. These two modules can be taken together or independently; readers drawn primarily to theology and church history may find them the most natural entry point in the curriculum.

A Note on Audience: One Room, One Gospel, Both Directions at Once

This curriculum does not have a separate "believer track" and "seeker track" — and that is a deliberate architectural choice, not an oversight. The design rests on a conviction drawn from Timothy Keller's work on preaching: the gospel that saves and the gospel that sanctifies are the same gospel. When a facilitator returns a believer to the gospel as the solution to a real problem in their life — not by calling them to try harder but by pointing them to what Christ has already accomplished — two things happen simultaneously. The believer is genuinely built up, encountering grace in a new dimension. And any seeker in the room watches faith in Christ actually working in a person they know. That is not a superficial evangelistic moment. It is the most persuasive kind: the gospel demonstrating what it claims, in real time, on a recognizably human problem.

This means facilitators do not need to manage their group into two separate conversations. The inside-out sequence of this curriculum — beginning with felt need, moving through story and evidence, arriving at practice and community — is the same path for both the person who has not yet believed and the person who has believed for years. The gospel addressed at depth serves both, every session, without splitting the room.

🧭 For Facilitators: Module Selection

If your group cannot commit to all twelve sessions, here are suggested short sequences:

Four-session essentials: Modules 1, 2, 4, 9. This covers the diagnosis, the story, the resurrection, and the destination.

Apologetics focus: Modules 3, 4, 5, 6. Historical reliability, the resurrection, Scripture's honesty, and suffering — the four most common intellectual barriers. Pair with Science & Faith for groups where the science-religion question is a first objection.

Pastoral focus: Modules 1, 6, 7, 8. The honest diagnosis, the problem of suffering, the signposts of growth, and the practice of obedience. Best for groups that already have some faith background and are working through doubt or spiritual stagnation. Pair with Prayer for groups wanting deeper roots in the practices of the Christian life.

For the wounded: Modules 1, 5, 6, and the Spine. Begin with honest diagnosis, move through Scripture's own honesty about darkness, and spend significant time on suffering before anything else. The Spine's material on Christological assurance is essential for this group. See also the 🕊️ Wounded Pilgrim page, which addresses those coming from a place of church harm or sustained spiritual dryness directly — and may be the right starting point before any module is opened. Finding a Healthy Church is essential reading for anyone ready to re-engage with community.

For communities in conflict: Module 10 standalone, or Module 9 followed by Module 10. Module 10 can be read independently of the main sequence by anyone dealing with an active church dispute or processing a past one. Sub-Modules 3 (Wounded Proxy War) and 7 (Social Hostage) are designed to be first-contact entries for hurting readers and can be shared directly without the full module context. For conflicts with a genuine doctrinal dimension — disputes over teaching, theological direction, or what the church believes — Module 11 provides pattern-recognition for identifying whether a doctrinal controversy is what it appears to be, or whether it is also carrying unresolved relational or institutional wound.

Theology and doctrine focus: Modules 11 and 12 standalone, or preceded by the Spine. These two modules form a self-contained unit on doctrinal pattern recognition (11) and the creeds (12). Module 11 is well-suited for anyone encountering unfamiliar teaching and wanting tools to evaluate it; Module 12 rewards anyone who recites the creeds without fully understanding them, or who comes from a tradition that doesn't use them at all. Pair with The Atonement and The Holy Spirit for groups wanting deeper doctrinal grounding.

For new Bible readers: Begin with Reading the Bible before Module 1. The hermeneutics page gives first-time readers the tools they need to engage the text throughout the curriculum rather than encountering them for the first time mid-sequence.

Sharing this curriculum with a group? The About page explains what it is, how it was built, and the personal circumstances behind it — useful context for a facilitator introducing the material to people who may want to know where it came from.
